### Jamieson-Fausset-Brown:
1. "If ye were born again of flesh, corruptible seed, ye must also perish again as the grass; but now that from which you have derived life remains eternally, and so also will render you eternal."
2. "The glory is the wisdom, strength, riches, learning, honor, beauty, art, virtue, and righteousness of the NATURAL man, which all are transitory, not OF MAN absolutely, for the glory of man, in his true ideal realized in the believer, is eternal."
3. "The grass is the flesh: 'the flower' its glory."

### Albert Barnes:
1. "All flesh, every human being, is like grass. There is no stability in anything that man does or produces. He himself resembles grass that soon fades and withers; but God and his word endure forever the same."
2. "The comparison of a human being with grass, or with flowers, is very beautiful, and is quite common in the Scriptures. The comparison turns on the fact, that the grass or the flower, however green or beautiful it may be, soon loses its freshness; is withered; is cut down, and dies."
3. "All that man prides himself on - his wealth, rank, talents, beauty, learning, splendor of equipage or apparel."

### Marvin Vincent:
1. "The best texts read 'of it' or 'thereof,' emphasizing the transient nature of man and his accomplishments." 
2. "The use of the aorist tense in 'withered was the grass' creates a sense of sudden and dramatic decay." 
3. "The word 'fell off' in 'the flower fell off' highlights the abrupt and complete loss of beauty and vitality."

### John Calvin:
1. "For as the Prophet there speaks of the restoration of the Church, to prepare the way for it, he reduces men to nothing lest they should flatter themselves."
2. "Until their own emptiness has been shown to men, they are not prepared to receive the grace of God."
3. "He afterwards shows how suddenly everything that seems beautiful in men vanishes, even through the blowing of the Spirit of God; and by this he intimates, that man seems to be something until he comes to God, but that his whole brightness is as nothing in his presence."

### Philip Schaff:
1. "The ‘glory’ of the flesh, by which is meant its goodliest outcome, ‘the most splendid manifestations of man’s life,’ is compared to the still more tender bloom that brightens on the flower only to fall off."
2. "A lifelike picture of the actual occurrence, the tenses used being those of direct narration."
3. "Peter does not bring in this passage as a formal quotation sustaining his statement by an appeal to Scripture, but simply expresses in Old Testament words which come easily to his lips a reason for the incorruptibility which he attributes to the new life."

### John Gill:
1. "All men, as born of corruptible seed, are frail, mortal, and perishing; they spring up like grass, and look beautiful for a while, but are very weak and tender, and in a little time they are cut down by death, and wither away."
2. "All outward things which are in esteem with men, and render them glorious to one another, as riches, honour, wisdom, strength, external righteousness, holiness, and goodness; all which are fading and transitory, like the flower of the field."
3. "The Gospel continues, and reveals durable riches, and honour with Christ; and true wisdom and strength with him, and spiritual knowledge, in comparison of which, all things are dross and dung; and an everlasting righteousness; and true holiness in him."

### John Wesley:
1. "Every human creature is transient and withering as grass."
2. "His wisdom, strength, wealth, righteousness... all the glory of it."
3. "Man is like the grass, and his glory is like a flower that quickly falls off."
