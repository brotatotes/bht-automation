In this Bible verse, the commentator remarks on the transformative power of grief and repentance. They emphasize that the sorrow experienced by the recipients was not mere regret or despair but a sorrow that led to a positive change and reformation. ### Albert Barnes:
1. "When people are brought to repentance under the preaching of the gospel, the ministers of the gospel do not find pleasure in their grief as such."
2. "It was not mere grief; it was not sorrow producing melancholy, gloom, or despair; it was not sorrow which led you to be angry at him who had reproved you for your errors - as is sometimes the case with the sorrow that is produced by reproof; but it was sorrow that led to a change and reformation."
3. "Sinners are gainers in happiness in the end by all the pain of repentance produced by the preaching of the gospel."

### Marvin Vincent:
1. "Repentance is different from regret... indicating a moral change." 
2. "The salutary moral results of the apostle's letter compensated for the sorrow which it caused." 
3. "The epistle which won them to repentance was no damage to them."

### John Calvin:
1. "He means, that he feels no pleasure whatever in their sorrow — nay more, had he his choice, he would endeavor to promote equally their welfare and their joy, by the same means."
2. "But that as he could not do otherwise, their welfare was of so much importance in his view, that he rejoiced that they had been made sorry unto repentance."
3. "Paul declares, that he is not of such a disposition as to employ harsh cures, when not constrained by necessity."

### Philip Schaff:
1. "The sorrow, though sharp, was short and it was salutary." 
2. "Ye were made sorry after a godly sort, that ye might suffer loss by us in nothing." 
3. "Our treatment of you proved to be of God for your good."

### Archibald T. Robertson:
1. "This was the occasion of the noble outburst in 2 Corinthians 2:12-6." 
2. "It is a linguistic and theological tragedy that we have to go on using 'repentance' for μετανοια." 
3. "It was not mere sorrow, but a change in their attitude that counted."

### John Gill:
1. "Their sorrow issued in true evangelical repentance, and this was the ground of his rejoicing."
2. "What gave him so much joy and satisfaction was, that their sorrow was of the right sort; it was a godly sorrow, they sorrowed after; or according to God, according to the will of God, and for sin, as it was committed against him."
3. "What added to his pleasure was, that his writing to them, and the effect it produced, had not been in the least detrimental to them; things had worked so kindly, and this sorrow had wrought in such a manner, that they were not hurt in their souls, but profited."
