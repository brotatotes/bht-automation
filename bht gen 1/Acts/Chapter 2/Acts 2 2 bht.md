# Acts 2:2 Commentary Help Text

## BHT:
In this remarkable event described by various commentators, a powerful and mighty wind rushes through, accompanied by a resonating sound that captivates the senses. The wind symbolizes the invisible yet mighty divine influence of the Holy Spirit, stirring hearts and bringing about a divine change. This manifestation of the Spirit's power was unexpected and awe-inspiring, leaving a lasting impression on those present. The filling of the house showcases the overwhelming presence and inherent holiness of God, while also emphasizing the temple as a sacred space where divine encounters and revelations can occur.

## Choicest Commentary Quotes:
### Henry Alford:
1. "A sound as of a rushing mighty wind." 
2. "It was the chosen vehicle by which the Holy Spirit was manifested to their sense of hearing, as by the tongues of fire to their sense of seeing."
3. "Certainly Luke would not have used this word of a chamber in the Temple, or of the Temple itself, without further explanation."

### Jamieson-Fausset-Brown:
1. "The whole description is so picturesque and striking that it could only come from an eye-witness."
2. "The suddenness, strength, and diffusiveness of the sound strike with deepest awe the whole company, and thus complete their preparation for the heavenly gift."
3. "Wind was a familiar emblem of the Spirit."

### Albert Barnes:
1. "Though they were waiting for the descent of the Spirit, yet it is not probable that they expected it in this manner."
2. "It was proper that the gift of the Holy Spirit should take place in some striking and sensible manner, so as to convince their own minds that the promise was fulfilled."
3. "The wind in the sacred Scriptures is often put as an emblem of a divine influence. It is invisible, yet mighty, and thus represents the agency of the Holy Spirit."

### Marvin Vincent:
1. "A sound [ηχος] . See on Luke 4:37."
2. "Of a rushing mighty wind [φερομενης πνοης βιαιας] . Lit., of a mighty wind born along."
3. "The house. Not merely the room. Compare ch. 1 13."

### John Calvin:
1. "For such is our slothfulness to consider the gifts of God, that unless he awake all our senses, his power shall pass away unknown."
2. "The violence of the wind did serve to make them afraid; for we are never rightly prepared to receive the grace of God, unless the confidence (and boldness) of the flesh be tamed."
3. "The Lord doth show that their voice shall be fiery, that it may inflame the hearts of men; that the vanity of the world being burnt and consumed, it may purge and renew all things."

### Archibald T Robertson:
1. "It was not wind, but a roar or reverberation 'as of the rushing of a mighty wind'." 
2. "An echoing sound as of a mighty wind borne violently." 
3. "They were sitting."

### John Gill:
1. "Which is expressive of the original of the gifts and graces of the Spirit of God, which come from above, from heaven, from the Father of lights."
2. "The Spirit of God is sometimes compared to the wind, because of the freeness of his operations; as that blows where it listeth, so he works when and where, and on whom he pleases."
3. "It makes its way into the heart, and throws down the strong holds of sin and Satan; there it works effectually, though secretly, and is the power of God to salvation."

### John Wesley:
1. "The sound from heaven signifies the imminent arrival of the Son of man for judgment."
2. "The filling of the house indicates the overwhelming presence and power of God in that moment."
3. "This event highlights the significance of the temple as a place where divine encounters and revelations can occur."
