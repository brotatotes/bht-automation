# Acts 24:16 Commentary Help Text

## BHT:
In this verse, the author speaks of a heartfelt hope, one that fuels his unwavering commitment to living a godly life. Undeterred by slander and persecution, he remains devoted to his conscience, which guides him in his pursuit of purity. His firm resolve is complemented by his engagement in spiritual discipline, as he actively exercises himself to develop and strengthen his faith. Anchored in the hope of the resurrection and supported by a community of like-minded believers, he endeavors to live a blameless life before both God and man.

### Henry Alford:

### Jamieson-Fausset-Brown:
1. "On this account, accordingly; that is, looking forward to that awful day."
2. "The 'I' here is emphatic; 'Whatever they do, this is my study.'"
3. "These are the great principles of my life and conduct—how different from turbulence and sectarianism!"

### Albert Barnes:
1. "Paul often appeals to his conscientiousness as the leading habit of his life."
2. "To have always a conscience...implies two things: Toward God and Toward men."
3. "The great and noble aim of Paul was to be pure; and no slander of his enemies, no trials, persecutions, perils, or pains of dying could take away the approving voice of conscience."

### Marvin Vincent:
1. "Exercise myself [ασκω]. Originally, to work raw material, to form: hence, to practice, exercise, discipline; and so, in ecclesiastical language, to mortify the body."
2. "Of the kindred adjective ajskhtikov, our word ascetic is a transcript."
3. "Void of offense [αποσκοπον]. Lit., without stumbling; unshaken. The word is used thus in a passive sense here, as in Philippians 1:10."

### John Calvin:
1. "There is no sharper prick to prick men forward, with all desire to lead a godly and holy life, than the hope of the last resurrection."
2. "An evil conscience is as good as a thousand witnesses to accuse men of blockishness, that they may gather for a certainty that they do not earnestly and thoroughly believe eternal life."
3. "Those who hope for the last resurrection are never weary of well-doing."

### Philip Schaff:
1. "The rule of his life was to struggle to keep himself from sin."
2. "Paul’s belief...was anything but a merely speculative, it was a real and living faith."
3. "He lived, or rather tried to live, as though he believed, and taught others to do the same."

### Archibald T Robertson:
1. "Paul claims to be equal to his accusers in efforts to please God."
2. "Void of offence" can be understood as both "not stumbling" and "not stumbled against."
3. "Certainly his conscience acquitted him of having caused any offence to his countrymen."

### John Gill:
1. "The doctrine of the resurrection of the dead...promotes and engages to a holy life and conversation."
2. "The apostle studied, and laboured, and bent himself...to have always a conscience void of offence toward God and toward man."
3. "By a 'conscience void of offence', is meant a good conversation; which...respects God, lies in a carefulness not to offend him, but to do his will."

### John Wesley:
1. "For this cause" refers to the previous verse where Paul talks about enduring all things for the sake of the elect. It implies that Paul's motivation for enduring hardships is rooted in his desire to see people come to faith in Christ.

2. "I also exercise myself" shows Paul's commitment to actively engage in spiritual disciplines and personal growth. It highlights his intentional efforts to strengthen his faith and deepen his relationship with God.

3. "As well as they" indicates that Paul is not alone in his pursuit of spiritual growth. He recognizes that other believers also strive to grow in their faith and serve the Lord. This statement emphasizes the importance of community and mutual encouragement in the Christian journey.
