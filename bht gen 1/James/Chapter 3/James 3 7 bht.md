### Jamieson-Fausset-Brown:
1. "every nature" (that is, natural disposition and characteristic power).
2. "quadrupeds of every disposition; as distinguished from the three other classes of creation, 'birds, creeping things (the Greek includes not merely 'serpents,' as English Version), and things in the sea.'"
3. "man's characteristic power taming that of the inferior animals."

### Albert Barnes:
1. "Small is the power which nature has given to man; but, by various acts of his superior understanding, he has subdued the tribes of the sea, the earth, and the air."
2. "A portion of the leathered race, as the hen, the goose, the duck, is thoroughly domesticated."
3. "It may be affirmed with confidence that there is no animal which might not, by proper skill and perseverance, be rendered tame, or made obedient to the will of man."

3. "Every nature of beasts is tamed by the nature of man."

### John Calvin:
1. "For he says that there is no animal so savage or fierce, which is not tamed by the skill of man, — that fishes, which in a manner inhabit another world, — that birds, which are so quick and roving — and that serpents, which are so inimical to mankind, are sometimes tamed."
2. "Since then the tongue cannot be restrained, there must be some secret fire of hell hidden in it."

### Philip Schaff:
1. "For every kind: literally, every nature or disposition."
2. "The inferior creation arranged under its usual fourfold classification beasts of the earth, fowls of heaven, creeping things, and fish of the sea."
3. "They may all be subdued."
4. "By human nature."

### Archibald T. Robertson:
1. "Kind (φυσις). Old word from φυω, order of nature (Romans 1:26), here of all animals and man, in 2 Peter 1:4 of God and redeemed men."
2. "The present tense gives the general picture of the continuous process through the ages of man's lordship over the animals as stated in Genesis 1:28."
3. "The four groups are put in two pairs here by the use of τε κα with the first two and the second two."

### John Gill:
1. "Every kind of beasts, and of birds...is tamed, and hath been tamed of mankind." 
2. "By the wit and industry of man; by the various ways, means, and methods devised by man." 
3. "Though some think this is only to be understood of their being mastered and subdued, by one means or another."
