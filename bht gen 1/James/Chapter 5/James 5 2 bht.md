# James 5:2 Commentary Help Text

## BHT:
In this passage, commentators describe the temporary nature and perils of worldly riches. The wealth that people hoard up is portrayed as corruptible and subject to decay, like moth-eaten garments. The commentators emphasize the futility of placing excessive value on material possessions, urging believers to focus on storing up eternal treasures in heaven. The passage warns against the insatiable desire for wealth and the neglect of using resources for the benefit of others. It highlights the perishable nature of earthly treasures, illustrating the need to prioritize what is lasting and truly valuable.

### Jamieson-Fausset-Brown:
1. "Your riches perish without being of any use either to others or even to yourselves." 
2. "Your garments which are moth-eaten in your chests."

### Albert Barnes:
1. "Their hoarded treasures...kept them until they rotted or spoiled."
2. "Such articles of property were often stored up, expecting that they would furnish a supply for many years."
3. "Wealth consisted much in the garments that were laid up for show or for future use."

Note: The commentary provides insight into the concept of hoarding wealth and possessions, emphasizing the negative consequences of accumulating more than necessary and neglecting to use those resources for the benefit of others.

### Marvin Vincent:
1. "The idea here is that the treasures that people accumulate on earth are subject to corruption and decay. They are like garments that have been moth-eaten, losing their value and usefulness over time." 

2. "This verse serves as a reminder that material possessions are temporary and can easily be destroyed or lost. It encourages believers to focus on storing up treasures in heaven, which are eternal and cannot be corrupted." 

3. "The use of the word 'corrupted' emphasizes the perishable nature of earthly treasures. It highlights the futility of placing too much importance on material wealth, as it will ultimately fade away and lose its value."

### John Calvin:
1. "He ridicules their foolish confidence, because the riches in which they placed their happiness were wholly fading, yea, that they could be reduced to nothing by one blast from God." 
2. "He condemns their insatiable avarice, because they heaped together wealth only for this, that they might perish without any benefit." 
3. "He reproves some vices in some, and some vices in others. However, all those are generally condemned who unjustly accumulate riches, or who foolishly abuse them."

### Philip Schaff:
1. "Your riches, in which you prided yourselves, and in which you trusted, will be taken from you."
2. "The word 'corrupted' is evidently a figurative term used to denote the perishable nature of the riches."
3. "The general idea of 'riches' is here specialized as consisting in garments and in treasure silver and gold."

### Archibald T Robertson:

### John Gill:

3. "Your garments are moth eaten; being neither wore by themselves, nor put upon the backs of others, as they should, but laid up in wardrobes, or in chests and coffers, and so became the repast of moths, and now good for nothing."
