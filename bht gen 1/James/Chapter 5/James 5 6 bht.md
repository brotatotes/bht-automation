# James 5:6 Commentary Help Text

## BHT:
In this verse, the enmity and cruelty of the rich and powerful towards the just is highlighted. The unjust actions of the people, including the condemnation and crucifixion of Jesus Christ, are condemned. The verse also emphasizes the meekness and patience of the just in the face of persecution, reminding readers that the Lord will ultimately bring justice to those who have suffered. It serves as a call to patience and hope in the midst of injustice.

### Jamieson Fausset Brown:
1. "Their condemnation of Christ, 'the Just,' is foremost in James' mind."
2. "The justice or righteousness of Jesus and His people is what peculiarly provoked the ungodly great men of the world."

### Albert Barnes:
1. "Their conduct towards his people had been similar to the treatment of the Saviour, and was in fact a condemnation and crucifixion of him afresh."
2. "By their rejection of him in order to live in sin, they in fact condemned him and his religion."
3. "The sins...condemned in these verses...are these four: hoarding up money, keeping back wages, indulging in a life of luxury, and wronging and oppressing good and just men."

### John Calvin:
1. "The rich by their power oppressed and destroyed the poor and weak."
2. "The audacity of the rich was greater; because those whom they oppressed were without any protection."
3. "The more ready and prompt would be the vengeance of God, when the poor have no protection from men."

### Philip Schaff:
1. "He is pre-eminently the Just One; and this appears from the Acts of the Apostles to be a common appellation of our Lord in the primitive Church, and perhaps also of the Messiah among the Jews." 
2. "His murder is ever represented as the crowning sin of the Jewish nation." 
3. "There is here a tacit reference to the vengeance of God, who adopts the cause of the just."

### Archibald T Robertson:
1. "The rich controlled the courts of justice." 
2. "There is probably no direct reference to one individual, though it does picture well the death of Christ and also the coming death of James himself."
3. "The unresisting end of the victim (τον δικαιον) is pictured."

### John Gill:
1. "The apostle is not writing to the Jerusalem Jews, nor to unbelievers, but to professors of religion; though he might say they did it, because their nation did it."
2. "This is to be understood of the poor saints, who were just, through the imputation of Christ's righteousness to them, and lived soberly, righteously, and godly."
3. "They were evil spoken of, censured, and judged, and condemned in a rash and uncharitable manner by their brethren; or were drawn to the judgment seats by the rich, who obtained a judicial process against them, and procured a sentence of condemnation to pass upon them unrighteously."

### John Wesley:
1. "Ye have killed the just" - This refers to the unjust actions of the people in killing many just men, including Jesus Christ himself. It highlights the injustice and violence that was prevalent during that time.

2. "He doth not resist you" - This phrase emphasizes the non-violent nature of Jesus and his followers. Despite the injustices they faced, they did not retaliate or resist their oppressors. It speaks to the power of forgiveness and turning the other cheek.

3. "But the Lord cometh quickly" - This statement reminds the readers that although the unjust may seem secure in their actions, there will come a time when the Lord will return and bring justice. It offers hope and reassurance to those who have suffered at the hands of the unjust.
