# John 2:15 Commentary Help Text

## BHT:
In this verse, Jesus enters the temple and drives out the animals, merchants, and money changers with a scourge made of cords or rushes. His forceful actions served to cleanse the temple and proclaim his authority as the Messiah. Jesus' use of the whip was not just an instrument of physical force, but a symbol of divine anger and punishment. This dramatic act of purification instilled a sense of fear and awe in the people, underscoring the power and authority of Jesus' mission.

### Henry Alford:
1. "He drove all out of the temple, both the sheep and the oxen."
2. "No other way was open with regard to them, than to order them to take their birds away."

### Jamieson-Fausset-Brown:
1. "Not by this slender whip but by divine majesty was the ejection accomplished, the whip being but a sign of the scourge of divine anger" [GROTIUS].
2. "Poured out... overthrew—thus expressing the mingled indignation and authority of the impulse."
3. "The small cords used to tie up the cattle there collected."

### Albert Barnes:
1. "A scourge - A whip."
2. "This whip was made as an emblem of authority."
3. "The original word implies that these 'cords' were made of twisted 'rushes' or 'reeds' - probably the ancient material for making ropes."

### Marvin Vincent:
1. "A scourge [φραγελλιον] . Only here in the New Testament. Only John records this detail."
2. "The A. V. is therefore strictly literal."
3. "Money. See on ver. 14."

### Philip Schaff:
1. "The scourge was made for the expulsion of the animals, but by it Jesus also declared His purpose to the traders themselves."
2. "He drives them from the sacred place by banishing the instruments and means of their unholy traffic."
3. "In a figurative sense Messiah was said to come armed with a scourge."

### Archibald T Robertson:
1. "A scourge of cords (φραγελλιον εκ σχοινιων). The Latin flagellum. In papyri, here only in N.T. and note Latin l becomes ρ in Koine."
2. "He poured out (εξεχεεν). Second aorist active indicative of εκχεω, to pour out."
3. "The changers' money (των κολλυβιστων τα κερματα). 'The small pieces of money (κερματα, cut in pieces, change) of the bankers (κολλυβιστης from κολλυβος, clipped, late word see on Matthew 21:12).' Perhaps he took up the boxes and emptied the money."

### John Gill:
1. "This scourge might be made either of thongs cut out of the hides of beasts slain in sacrifice; or of the cords, with which the owners of the cattle had brought them to this place; or with which they had fastened them in it."
2. "And it seems to be made, and used, not so much for force and terror, as to intimate, that these persons, the violators of the holy place, deserved the scourge of divine wrath and punishment."
3. "He drove them all out of the temple; that is, he drove out 'the men', as the Persic version reads; the merchants, the sellers of oxen, sheep, and doves, and the money changers."

### John Wesley:
1. "Having made a scourge of rushes" - Jesus' use of a makeshift whip made of rushes demonstrates his resourcefulness and determination to cleanse the temple.
2. "He drove all out of the temple" - Jesus' actions were not limited to just the animals, but extended to all those who were conducting business in the temple, emphasizing the need for a complete purification.
3. "A terror from God fell upon them" - The fear that gripped the people in the temple was not a result of physical force, but a divine intervention, highlighting the power and authority of Jesus' actions.
