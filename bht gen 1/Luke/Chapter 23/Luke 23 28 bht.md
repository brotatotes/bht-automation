## BHT:
As we reflect on this Bible verse, we witness a poignant moment where Christ addresses the daughters of Jerusalem while carrying the Cross. The commentators reveal that the impending destruction of Jerusalem hangs over them, urging them to weep for themselves. However, amidst His own sufferings, Christ demonstrates compassion for their future pain. He cautions them, reminding them that while His own sorrows are great, they will soon come to an end, leading to His resurrection and exaltation. In this powerful scene, we see Christ's courage, his divine mission, and his unwavering dedication to our salvation.

### Henry Alford:

2. "Nor again were His sacred sufferings a mere popular tragedy for street-bewailing."
3. "Many of those who now bewailed Him perished in the siege of Jerusalem."

### Jamieson Fausset Brown:
1. "Noble spirit of compassion, rising above His own dread endurances"
2. "In tender commiseration of sufferings yet in the distance and far lighter"
3. "But without His supports and consolations"

### Albert Barnes:
1. "Daughters of Jerusalem - Women of Jerusalem. This was a common mode of speaking among the Hebrews." 
2. "Weep for yourselves ... - This refers to the calamities that were about to come upon them in the desolation of their city by the Romans." 
3. "The call to weep for themselves implies that the impending destruction was not just a physical one, but also a spiritual and emotional one."

### John Calvin:
1. "Christ does not simply reprove them, as if it were improperly and without a cause that they were weeping, but warns them that there will be far greater reason for weeping on account of the dreadful judgment of God which hangs over them."
2. "He intimates, that he was not abandoned to the wickedness of man in such a manner as not to be the object of Divine care."
3. "This doctrine is even now of use to us, when we learn that Christ was not less dear to his Father, because for a moment he was deprived of his aid, but that he set so high a value on our salvation, that he did not even spare his only-begotten Son."

### Philip Schaff:
1. "Daughters of Jerusalem. A natural address, but solemn and pointing to their relation to a doomed city."
2. "He not only endures the cross, but forgets His sorrows, so heavy, to tell the truth to those who manifested for Him only a human sympathy."
3. "Appropriate words for those who even now make of the crucifixion a mere popular tragedy."

### Archibald T Robertson:
1. "Turning (στραφεις). Luke is fond of this second aorist passive participle of στρεφω." 
2. "If he had been still carrying the Cross, he could not have made this dramatic gesture." 
3. "Weep not (μη κλαιετε). Present active imperative with μη, Stop weeping."

### John Gill:
1. "He desired nothing more; this was that he came into the world about; nor was he afraid to die; death was no king of terrors to him; he went to the cross with the greatest courage and intrepidity."
2. "His sufferings, though he knew they would be very great and painful, yet that they would be soon over; nor could he be long held in the power of death, but would be raised again, and go to his Father, and be exalted at his right hand, and which should be matter of joy."
3. "Christ's meaning is, that when things were rightly considered, there would be great reason to assuage their grief, on this account, and rather express it on another."
