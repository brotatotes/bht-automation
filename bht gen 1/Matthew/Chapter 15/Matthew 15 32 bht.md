# Matthew 15:32 Commentary Help Text

## BHT:
In this account, Jesus demonstrates his boundless compassion and provision for the physical and spiritual needs of the multitude. The three-day period serves to symbolize a time of preparation and testing, as well as foreshadow the ultimate sacrifice Jesus would make on the cross. Despite the disciples' initial doubts, Jesus teaches them the importance of perseverance and faith, reminding us to trust in God's abundant provision even in the face of challenges. This miraculous feeding not only satisfies the immediate hunger of the crowd, but also points to the deeper spiritual nourishment available through belief in Jesus.

## Choicest Commentary Quotes:
### Henry Alford:
1. "Every circumstance which could vary, does vary, in the two accounts."
2. "Here the matter is introduced by our Lord Himself with an expression of pity for the multitudes who had continued with Him three days."
3. "Perhaps the strangest reason for supposing the two identical is an imagined difficulty in the question of the disciples, so soon after the former miracle."

### Marvin Vincent:
1. "The A.V. might easily be mistaken for the simple future of the verb send. But two verbs are used: the verb I will expressing Jesus' feeling or disposition."
2. "The Greek order is, and to send them away fasting I am not willing. Therefore Rev. is better: I would not."
3. "Faint (ejkluqwsin). Lit., be unstrung or relaxed."

### John Calvin:
1. "Let us learn from this, that the power of God is not restricted to means or outward assistance, and that it is all one with Him whether there be much or little."
2. "As the blessing of God can make one loaf suffice as well as twenty for satisfying a great multitude, so, if that be wanting, a hundred loaves will not be a sufficient meal for ten men."
3. "The three days’ fasting, of which Christ speaks, must not be understood to mean that they had eaten nothing for three days; but that in desert places they had few conveniences, and must have wanted their ordinary food."

### Philip Schaff:
1. "Our Lord Himself takes the first step... This case was more urgent; the crowd was not composed of those on the way to the Passover, and had been three days with Him."
2. "The third day was passing; so they were hungry and destitute of provisions, but not yet in actual distress."
3. "The Lord’s compassion was called out by their physical want, which, however, resulted from their desire to be near Him."

### Archibald T Robertson:
1. "In the feeding of the five thousand Jesus took compassion on the people and healed their sick (Matthew 14:14)."
2. "Here the hunger of the multitude moves him to compassion (σπλαγχνιζομα, in both instances)."
3. "So he is unwilling (ου θελω) to send them away hungry."

### John Gill:
1. "Christ, our high priest, is a merciful one, and is touched with the feeling of the infirmities of men, of every sort, both of soul and body."
2. "Which shows the diligence and indefatigableness of Christ, as well as the attachment of the people to him."
3. "He could not bear the thoughts of dismissing them in such a condition; having had but very little sustenance all this while, and so might be said to be in a manner fasting during this time, at least now."

### John Wesley:
1. "The three days mentioned here symbolize a period of testing and preparation. Just as Jesus spent three days in the tomb before his resurrection, the disciples were being prepared for a new revelation and understanding of Jesus' power and provision."

2. "This passage highlights the importance of perseverance and faith. Despite the disciples' initial doubts and concerns about feeding the crowd, Jesus teaches them to trust in his ability to provide abundantly. It serves as a reminder that even in the face of challenges, we should hold on to our faith and trust in God's provision."

3. "The three-day period also foreshadows Jesus' ultimate sacrifice on the cross. Just as he would lay down his life for the salvation of humanity, he demonstrates his compassion and willingness to provide for the physical needs of the crowd. This act of feeding the multitude points to the deeper spiritual nourishment that Jesus offers to all who believe in him."
