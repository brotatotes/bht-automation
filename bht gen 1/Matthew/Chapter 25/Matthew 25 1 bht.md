# Matthew 25:1 Commentary Help Text

## BHT:
In this parable, a group of virgins eagerly awaits the arrival of the bridegroom for a wedding celebration. Some have made preparations to ensure their lamps continue to burn, while others have not. The parable emphasizes the blessedness of endurance and the importance of enduring till the end. It symbolizes the vigilant and expectant attitude of faith, highlighting the difference between superficial and genuine believers. Through this parable, we are reminded of the need to be ready and watchful for the return of Christ and the ultimate judgment day.

## Choicest Commentary Quotes:
### Henry Alford:
1. "The difference consists in some having made a provision for feeding the lamps in case of delay, and the others none and the moral of the parable is the blessedness of endurance unto the end." 
2. "The interpretation is these are souls come out from the world into the Church, and there waiting for the coming of the Lord not hypocrites, but faithful souls, bearing their lamps."
3. "All views of this parable which represent the foolish virgins as having only a dead faith, only the lamp without the light, the body without the spirit, &c., are quite beside the purpose."

### Jamieson-Fausset-Brown:
1. "This supplies a key to the parable, whose object is, in the main, the same as that of the last parable—to illustrate the vigilant and expectant attitude of faith."
2. "This entire and beautiful change of figure brings out the lesson of the former parable in quite a new light."
3. "The Virgins and the Bridegroom holding forth all the intended instruction: nor could believers be represented both as Bride and Bridal Attendants without incongruity."

### Albert Barnes:
1. "When the Son of man returns to judgment, it will be as it was in the case of ten virgins in a marriage ceremony."
2. "The marriage relation is the most tender, firm, and endearing of any known on earth, and on this account it suitably represents the union of believers to Christ."
3. "These torches were dipped in oil, and gave a large light. Marriage 'ceremonies' in the East were conducted with great pomp and solemnity."

### Marvin Vincent:
1. "Lamps [λαμπαδας] . Lit., torches." 
2. "Probably a short, wooden stem held in the hand, with a dish at the top."
3. "In which was a piece of cloth dipped in oil or pitch."

### John Calvin:
1. "By this term is meant the condition of the future Church, which was to be collected by the authority and direction of Christ."
2. "He employs this remarkable title, that believers may not deceive themselves by an erroneous opinion that they have arrived at absolute perfection."
3. "The general instruction of the parable consists in this, that it is not enough to have been once ready and prepared for the discharge of duty, if we do not persevere to the end."

### Philip Schaff:
1. "The number of completion among the Jews; this number may have been usual in wedding processions."
2. "To carry out the apt figure of a wedding, this class of persons must be introduced."
3. "Christ, the Bridegroom, comes from a distance, the bride is the Church; but she is not mentioned here, while the 'virgins' represent the individuals making up the Church."

### Archibald T. Robertson:
1. "The scene is apparently centered round the house of the bride to which the bridegroom is coming for the wedding festivities."
2. "But Plummer places the scene near the house of the bridegroom who has gone to bring the bride home."
3. "Probably torches with a wooden staff and a dish on top in which was placed a piece of rope or cloth dipped in oil or pitch."

### John Gill:
1. "The parable is manifestly connected with, and refers to the preceding chapter, which chiefly treats of Jerusalem's destruction."
2. "The design of it is to show, what will be the case of professors at that time; the difference between nominal and real Christians."
3. "The necessity of being ready for it; and how watchful the saints should be, that they be not surprised with it."
