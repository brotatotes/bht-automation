# Matthew 6:34 Commentary Help Text

## BHT:
In this verse, the commentators highlight the importance of living in the present and not being overly consumed by worries about the future. They emphasize the futility of anxiously thinking about tomorrow, as each day has its own cares and challenges. The verse warns against the temptation to borrow troubles from the future and encourages trust in God's providence. Despite the presence of difficulties, the commentators suggest that these trials can bring about growth and ultimately be a beneficial part of our spiritual journey.

## Choicest Commentary Quotes:
### Henry Alford:
1. "For the morrow will care for it, viz. for ἡ αὔριον mentioned above: i.e., will bring care enough about its own matters." 
2. "After all your endeavor to avoid worldly cares, you will find quite enough, and more of them when tomorrow comes, about tomorrow itself." 
3. "Do not then increase those of today by introducing them before their time."

### Jamieson Fausset Brown:
1. "Take therefore no thought—anxious care for the morrow." 
2. "Sufficient unto the day is the evil thereof." 
3. "Every day brings its own cares; and to anticipate is only to double them."

### Albert Barnes:
1. "Christ has here forcibly taught the necessity of charity, of prayer, and of all religious duties."
2. "We see the necessity of sincerity and honesty in our religious duties. They are not to be done to be seen by people. If they are, they cannot be performed acceptably."
3. "Christ has here, in a particular manner, urged the duty of prayer. He has given a model for prayer. Nothing can equal this composition in simplicity, beauty, and comprehensiveness."

### Philip Schaff:
1. "The morrow is here personified."
2. "Not 'take care of itself,' but 'bring its own cares and anxieties,' do not foolishly increase those of today by borrowing from the morrow."
3. "'Evil' may mean natural or moral evil, suffering, or sin. The latter sense is the more usual one, the former suits the context better. Perhaps both may be included, the sin being the want of trust under the suffering."

### Archibald T Robertson:
1. "The last resort of the anxious soul when all other fears are allayed."
2. "The ghost of tomorrow stalks out with all its hobgoblins of doubt and distrust."

### John Gill:
1. "A man cannot promise or assure himself, that he shall have a morrow, and therefore it is great weakness and folly to be anxiously thoughtful about it."
2. "Perhaps tomorrow may not be, and thou wilt be found distressing thyself, for the time which is nothing to thee."
3. "Every day brings along with it fresh care and thought, being attended with fresh wants and troubles; and therefore, it is very unadvisable, to bring the cares and troubles of two days upon one."

### John Wesley:
1. "The morrow shall take thought for itself" - This phrase encourages us to focus on the present moment and not worry excessively about the future.
2. "The evil thereof" - This reminds us that troubles and challenges can ultimately be beneficial for our growth and development.
3. "All trouble is, upon the whole, a real good" - This perspective encourages us to see difficulties as opportunities for God to work in our lives and strengthen our faith.
