# Matthew 9:15 Commentary Help Text

## BHT:
In this Bible verse, Jesus refers to himself as the bridegroom, signifying the fulfillment of Old Testament prophecies. The phrase "sons of the bride-chamber" denotes the joyous celebration and honor surrounding a wedding. Jesus addresses his disciples, assuring them of the festivities and gentleness they would experience while he was present. However, He also foretells the need for fasting and mourning when He is no longer physically with them. This passage reveals the profound connection between Jesus and his followers, portraying them as privileged participants in his divine plan.

### John Calvin:
1. "Christ apologizes for his disciples on the score of the season, alleging that God was still pleased to indulge them in joyous feelings, as if they were present at a marriage."
2. "Let us be satisfied with Christ’s declaration, that he spares his disciples, and treats them with gentleness, so long as he is with them."
3. "When the Lord sometimes endures the weakness of our brethren, and acts towards them with gentleness, while he treats us with greater severity, we have no right to murmur."

### Philip Schaff:
1. "The application is of course to the disciples of Christ; He Himself being the bridegroom." 
2. "Mourn' and 'fast' are used interchangeably; genuine fasting springs from real sorrow."
3. "'How sublime and peaceful is this early announcement by our Lord of the bitter passage before Him' (Alford)."

### Archibald T Robertson:
1. "The sons of the bride-chamber" refers to the wedding guests, specifically the friends of the bridegroom. This phrase highlights the joyous and celebratory nature of a wedding, where everyone comes together to rejoice and honor the couple.

2. The use of this idiom in the Bible, particularly in John 2:29, emphasizes the significance of Jesus as the bridegroom. It suggests that Jesus is the central figure in a divine wedding, symbolizing the union between God and His people.

3. By referring to the disciples as "the sons of the bride-chamber," Jesus implies that they are not just followers or servants, but participants in this divine wedding. They are privileged to be part of the celebration and to witness the fulfillment of God's promises through Jesus.

### John Gill:
1. "By the 'bridegroom' Christ means himself, who stands in such a relation to his church, and to all believers; whom he secretly betrothed to himself from all eternity, in the covenant of grace; and openly espouses in the effectual calling."
2. "The allusion is to a nuptial solemnity, which is a time of joy and feasting, and not of sorrow and fasting; when both bride and bridegroom have their friends attending them, who used to be called 'the children of the bride chamber'."
3. "The time of Christ's being with his disciples, between his entrance on his public ministry, and his death, is the time here referred to, during which the disciples had very little care and trouble: this was their rejoicing time, and there was a great deal of reason for it; they had no occasion to fast and mourn."

### John Wesley:

3. "After I am gone, all my disciples likewise shall be in fastings often."
