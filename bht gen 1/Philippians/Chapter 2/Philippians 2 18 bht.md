# Philippians 2:18 Commentary Help Text

## BHT:
This verse beautifully captures the sacrificial nature of Jesus' death on the cross, as described by John Wesley. The commentators emphasize the paradoxical and voluntary nature of Jesus' sacrifice, highlighting the ultimate purpose and significance of his death. Jesus' willingness to be offered up, akin to the Old Testament sacrificial system, brings redemption and atonement, inviting believers to rejoice in the profound impact of his selfless act. The verse invites us to reflect on the immense love and grace displayed by Jesus in laying down his life for the salvation of humanity.

### Jamieson-Fausset-Brown:
1. "Do ye also rejoice" - This verse encourages believers to share in the joy and celebration of the honor bestowed upon them. It emphasizes the importance of communal rejoicing and unity among believers.

2. "and congratulate me" - The commentary highlights the significance of congratulating and supporting one another in times of blessings and victories. It emphasizes the importance of expressing genuine happiness for the success and well-being of others.

### Albert Barnes:
1. "Because we are united, and what affects one of us should affect both."
2. "Do not grieve at my death. Be not overwhelmed with sorrow, but let your hearts be filled with congratulation."
3. "It will be a privilege and a pleasure thus to die."

### John Calvin:
1. "By the alacrity which he thus discovers, he encourages the Philippians, and enkindles in them a desire to meet death with firmness."
2. "He declares that it is no ground of sorrow; nay, that they have occasion of joy, inasmuch as they will find it to be productive of advantage."
3. "He lets them know that to himself personally death would be a matter of joy."

### Philip Schaff:
1. "There are two offerings in the contemplation of St. Paul: his own, as he is poured out on the sacrifice; the offering of the faithful lives of the Philippians which they themselves are to make." 
2. "He does not intimate whether the power of Rome, or his own continued toils, shall be the agency employed to pour his life forth. But both offerings are subjects for joy."
3. "He cannot restrain his triumph at the one, and in like manner he bids his friends feel equal joy at the offering which he is making and being strengthened to make unto his Master."

### John Gill:
1. "He would not have them be sorrowful, should they hear of his death for the sake of the Gospel, and of his blood being poured out in such a cause, since it was as a libation on their faith, and for the confirmation of it."
2. "They should be so far from indulging grief and sorrow on that account, that they should rather joy and rejoice with him, who was ready to be offered up, or poured out."
3. "He had run out his race, and that not in vain, but to so good a purpose, and especially among them."

### John Wesley:
1. "This verse speaks to the paradoxical nature of Jesus' sacrifice. While it may seem strange to congratulate someone on being offered up for sacrifice, it highlights the ultimate purpose and significance of Jesus' death on the cross."

2. "The phrase 'when I am offered up' emphasizes the voluntary nature of Jesus' sacrifice. It shows that Jesus willingly laid down his life for the salvation of humanity, fully aware of the immense suffering and death that awaited him."

3. "By using the term 'offered up,' Jesus alludes to the Old Testament sacrificial system, where animals were offered as a means of atonement for sin. Jesus, as the ultimate sacrificial Lamb, offers himself as the perfect and final sacrifice, bringing redemption and forgiveness to all who believe in him."
