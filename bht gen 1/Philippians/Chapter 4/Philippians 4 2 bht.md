# Philippians 4:2 Commentary Help Text

## BHT:
In this verse, there are two prominent women in the church who are urged to reconcile their differences. They may have been influential and possibly even deaconesses. The repetition of the plea to each individually emphasizes the importance of their unity. The call for them to be "in the Lord" highlights that their disagreements are inconsistent with their shared faith. The apostle's heartfelt pleas and affectionate entreaties reflect his deep concern for their reconciliation.

## Choicest Commentary Quotes:
### Henry Alford:
1. "Euodia and Syntyche...appear to have needed this exhortation on account of some disagreement, both however being faithful, and fellow-workers with himself in the Gospel." 
2. "The repetition of the verb παρακαλῶ not merely signifies ‘vehementiam affectus’ (Erasm.), but hints at the present separation between them."
3. "He adds ἐν κυρίῳ, both to shew them wherein their unanimity must consist, and perhaps to point out to them that their present alienation was not ἐν κυρίῳ."

### Jamieson-Fausset-Brown:
1. "Euodia and Syntyche were two women who seem to have been at variance; probably deaconesses of the church." 
2. "He repeats, 'I beseech,' as if he would admonish each separately, and with the utmost impartiality." 
3. "In the Lord—the true element of Christian union; for those 'in the Lord' by faith to be at variance, is an utter inconsistency."

### Albert Barnes:
1. "It has been commonly supposed that they were deaconesses, who preached the gospel to those of their own sex; but there is no certain evidence of this."
2. "That they be of the same mind - That they be united, or reconciled. Whether the difference related to doctrine, or to something else, we cannot determine from this phrase."
3. "In the Lord - In their Christian walk and plans. They were doubtless professing Christians, and the apostle exhorts them to make the Lord the great object of their affections, and in their regard for him, to bury all their petty differences and animosities."

### Marvin Vincent:
1. "Euodia, meaning prosperous journey, and Syntyche, meaning happy chance, were prominent women in the Church, possibly deaconesses." 

2. "In Macedonia, the position of women was exceptional, with monuments erected to women by public bodies and records of male proper names formed on the mother's name instead of the father's." 

3. "The repetition of the word 'beseech' with each name makes the exhortation individual and specific."

### John Calvin:
1. "He assigns to them so much honor as to call them fellow-soldiers in the gospel."
2. "Whenever he speaks of agreement, he adds also the bond of it—in the Lord."
3. "Nothing is so disjoined, but that it ought to be reunited in Christ."

### Philip Schaff:
1. "The apostle calls on these sisters by the authority of his office."
2. "It was to the women especially that the first preaching at the proseucha in Philippi was addressed."
3. "If then, remembering this, they seek to their Master for aid, the unity of spirit will be bestowed."

### Archibald T. Robertson:
1. "Euodia and Syntyche, whose names carry meanings of 'prosperous journey' and 'pleasant acquaintance' respectively, may have been influential women in the church in Philippi." 
2. "Some scholars suggest that these women may have had separate church assemblies in their homes, one for Jewish-Christians and the other for Gentile-Christians." 
3. "The presence of even slight bickerings between these two energetic Christian women could have caused a real disturbance in the pure church of Philippi."

### John Gill:
1. "It becomes a very hard and difficult task to reconcile them, though as here entreated in the most tender and importunate manner to agree."
2. "Being drawn aside by false teachers from the simplicity of the Gospel, and their steadfastness in the faith."
3. "That they become of the same mind, and embrace the same truths, and profess and maintain the same principles the church did."

### John Wesley:
1. "I beseech" - He repeats this twice, as if speaking to each face to face, and that with the utmost tenderness.
2. "The apostle here uses the most earnest and affectionate entreaties, as if he were pleading for his own life, or for the lives of those to whom he wrote."
3. "He does not command, but he entreats; he does not use his apostolical authority, but he uses his apostolical affection."
