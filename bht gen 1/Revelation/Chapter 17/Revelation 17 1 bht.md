# Revelation 17:1 Commentary Help Text

## BHT:
In this verse, we encounter a vivid portrayal of the great whore, symbolizing the apostate church. She is seen seated on a scarlet-colored beast, signifying her submission to worldly allurements and her downfall. This corrupt city, often associated with Babylon or Rome, plays a central role in seducing people away from true faith and engaging in worldly pleasures. Through the use of powerful imagery, this verse warns about the dangers of spiritual unfaithfulness and the consequences that follow.

1. "The whore is the apostate Church, just as 'the woman' is the Church while faithful."
2. "Satan having failed by violence, tries to successfully seduce her by the allurements of the world."

### Albert Barnes:
1. "The general idea seems to be, that to those seven angels was entrusted the execution of the last things, or the winding up of affairs introductory to the reign of God, and that the communications respecting those last events were properly made through them."
2. "The expression 'which had the seven vials,' would seem to imply that though they had emptied the vials in the manner stated in the previous chapter, they still retained them in their hands."
3. "It is not uncommon in the Scriptures to represent a city under the image of a woman - a pure and holy city under the image of a virgin or chaste female; a corrupt, idolatrous, and wicked city under the image of an abandoned or lewd woman."

### Marvin Vincent:
1. "Sitteth upon many waters." - This phrase refers to Babylon, symbolizing its wealth and prosperity derived from the Euphrates River and an extensive canal system. (Jeremiah 51:13)

2. "The symbol is interpreted by some commentators as signifying Babylon, by others pagan Rome, Papal Rome, Jerusalem." - Different interpretations exist regarding the symbolic meaning of the phrase, with some associating it with Babylon, pagan Rome, Papal Rome, or Jerusalem.

3. "The Evangelist you pastors had in mind, When she who sitteth upon many waters To fornicate with kings by him was seen." - Dante alludes to this passage in his work "Inferno," using the imagery of the woman sitting on many waters to depict the corrupt relationship between the church and secular powers. (Dante, "Inferno," 19, 106 - 110)

### Philip Schaff:
1. "The judgment spoken of, as appears by the word used in the original, is judgment executed, not in process of execution."
2. "‘Many waters’ are interpreted by the angel in Revelation 17:15 as ‘peoples and multitudes and nations and tongues, and the fourfold division shows that we have a representation of the whole world."
3. "The term ‘harlot’ points to the fact that this city seduced men from the true God to worldliness and sin."

### Archibald T. Robertson:
1. "It is fitting that one of the seven angels that had the seven bowls should explain the judgment on Babylon already pronounced."
2. "The word κριμα is the one used about the doom of Babylon in Jeremiah 51:9."
3. "Babylon got its wealth by means of the Euphrates and the numerous canals for irrigation."

### John Gill:
1. "And there came one of the seven angels that had the seven vials... the seventh and last angel, concerned in the utter destruction of antichrist." 
2. "He conversed with him in a friendly manner... desires him to come nearer to him, and go along with him."
3. "That noted and famous one, known before to John by the names of Jezabel and Babylon, who taught and caused many to commit fornication... no other than Rome Papal."

### John Wesley:
1. "This relation concerning the great whore, and that concerning the wife of the Lamb, Revelation 21:9,10, have the same introduction, in token of the exact opposition between them."
