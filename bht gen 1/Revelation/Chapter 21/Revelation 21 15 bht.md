# Revelation 21:15 Commentary Help Text

## BHT:
The Bible verse being described is about the measuring of the city, its walls, and its gates with a golden reed. Commentators explain that, through this measurement, the city's noble proportions, conformity to God's word, and glorious state are revealed. The use of a golden reed signifies the divine standard. These measurements symbolize the perfect and just nature of God's judgment, as well as the preparation of the city for a multitude of inhabitants in a state of glory and spiritual bodies.

### Jamieson-Fausset-Brown:
1. "the non-measuring of the outer courts of the temple implied its being given up to secular and heathen desecration."
2. "the city being measured implies the entire consecration of every part, all things being brought up to the most exact standard of God's holy requirements."
3. "God's accurate guardianship henceforth of even the most minute parts of His holy city from all evil."

### Albert Barnes:
1. "The reed, or measuring rod, here, is of gold, because all about the city is of the most rich and costly materials."
2. "The rod is thus suited to the personage who uses it, and to the occasion."
3. "The object of this measuring is to show that the city has proper architectural proportions."

### Marvin Vincent:
1. "A golden reed" - This symbolizes the divine standard of measurement, representing the perfection and righteousness of God's judgment. It reminds us that God's judgment is always just and accurate, leaving no room for error or partiality.

2. "Add metron as a measure" - The use of the word "metron" emphasizes the precise and exact measurement that God employs in His judgment. It signifies that God's judgment is not arbitrary or subjective, but rather based on an objective and unchanging standard.

3. "Compare Ezekiel 40:5" - This reference to Ezekiel 40:5 highlights the connection between the measuring reed in Revelation and the vision of the future temple in Ezekiel. It suggests that the measuring reed in Revelation symbolizes the establishment of God's perfect and eternal dwelling place, where His righteousness and justice will reign supreme.

### Philip Schaff:
1. "The city is to be measured, in order that its noble and fair proportions may be seen."
2. "The angel measures it with a golden reed, the metal of the reed corresponding in dignity and value to the city itself."
3. "A measuring reed, though not of gold, is used in Ezekiel 40:3."

### Archibald T Robertson:
1. "The rod of gold was in keeping with the dignity of the service of God."
2. "Μετρον is an old word, kin to μητηρ (mother, moulder, manager), an instrument for measuring."
3. "Regular imperfect here, no longer εχων."

### John Gill:
1. "One of the seven angels... had a golden reed; not a common measuring reed, but one covered with gold, denoting the present glorious state of the church." 
2. "To measure the city... to show how exactly conformable this church state will be to the rule of God's word, even to perfection."
3. "And the gates thereof... to signify who had a right to enter into it, and who not."

### John Wesley:
1. "By this measure is understood the greatness of the city, with the exact order and just proportion of every part of it; to show, figuratively, that this city was prepared for a great number of inhabitants, how small soever the number of real Christians may sometimes appear to be."
2. "The reed therefore was as great as was the stature of that human form in which the angel appeared."
3. "The gold, the pearls, the precious stones, the walls, foundations, gates, are undoubtedly figurative expressions; seeing the city itself is in glory, and the inhabitants of it have spiritual bodies: yet these spiritual bodies are also real bodies, and the city is an abode distinct from its inhabitants, and proportioned to them who take up a finite and a determinate space."
