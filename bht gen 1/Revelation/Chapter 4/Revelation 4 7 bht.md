### Albert Barnes:
1. "The lion is the monarch of the woods, the king of beasts, and he becomes thus the emblem of dominion, of authority, of government in general."
2. "A young bullock was worshipped in Egypt as a god... the image appears to have been adopted early and extensively to represent the divinity."
3. "The aspect of a man here would denote intelligence - for it is this which distinguishes man from the creation beneath him... the divine administration is conducted with intelligence and wisdom."
4. "The eagle is distinguished, among the feathered race, for the rapidity, the power, and the elevation of its flight... the rapidity with which the commands of God are executed."
5. "The government of God is energetic, firm, intelligent, and that in the execution of its purposes it is rapid like the unobstructed flight of an eagle, or protective like the care of the eagle for its young."

### Marvin Vincent:
1. "From this passage is derived the familiar symbolism of the four Evangelists; Mark seated on a lion, Luke on a steer, Matthew on a man, and John on an eagle."
2. "These are varied, however. Irenaeus attributes the lion to John, and the eagle to Mark. Augustine attributes the lion to Matthew and the man to Mark."
3. "Calf [μοσχω] is used in the Septuagint for an ox or steer, as seen in Exodus 22:1 and Ezekiel 1:10."
4. "Eagle [αετω] can be seen in Matthew 24:28 as well."

### Archibald T Robertson:
1. "The four forms represent whatever is noblest, strongest, wisest, and swiftest in nature" (Swete).
2. "Μοσχος is first a sprout, then the young of animals, then a calf (bullock or heifer) as in Luke 15:23; Luke 15:27; Luke 15:30, or a full-grown ox (Ezekiel 1:10)."
3. "Shows that the likeness in each instance extended only to the face."

### John Gill:
1. "This figure expresses the strength of the ministers of the word, the lion being the strongest among beasts, to do the work they are called to."
2. "It denotes their courage and boldness in preaching the Gospel of Christ, without fearing the faces of men."
3. "The second beast like a calf...designs the laboriousness of Christ's faithful ministers in treading out the corn of Gospel truth."
4. "It also represents their humility, meekness, and patience in bearing insults, reproaches, and sufferings for Christ."
5. "The third beast had a face as a man...points at the humanity and tender-heartedness, the wisdom, prudence, knowledge, and understanding, and the use of the reasoning faculty, together with a manly spirit in abiding by the Gospel at any rate."

### John Wesley:
1. "Just such were the four cherubim in Ezekiel, who supported the moving throne of God."
2. "Each of those that overshadowed the mercy-seat in the holy of holies had all these four faces."
3. "A late great man supposes them to have been emblematic of the Trinity, and the incarnation of the second Person."
