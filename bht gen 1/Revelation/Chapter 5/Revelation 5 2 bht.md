### Henry Alford:
1. "The voice is the vehicle, or investiture, of the thing proclaimed."
2. "No one was able, in heaven, nor yet upon the earth, nor yet under the earth, to open the book, nor yet to look on it."
3. "The claim to open the book must be founded on a claim of worthiness to see that which was contained in it."

### Jamieson-Fausset-Brown:
1. "His voice penetrated heaven, earth, and Hades." 
2. "His voice was powerful and all-encompassing, reaching every corner of existence." 
3. "The strength of his voice was a testament to his authority and power."

### Albert Barnes:
1. "An angel endowed with great strength, as if such strength was necessary to enable him to give utterance to the loud voice of the inquiry."
2. "The design undoubtedly is to impress the mind with a sense of the importance of the disclosures about to be made, and at the same time with a sense of the impossibility of penetrating the future by any created power."
3. "Who is 'worthy' in the sense of having a rank so exalted, and attributes so comprehensive, as to authorize and enable him to do it."

### Marvin Vincent:
1. "Strong, either as being of higher rank, or with reference to the great voice."
2. "Worthy, as in John 1:27."
3. "Morally entitled."

### Philip Schaff:
1. "The angel of this verse is strong, and his voice is great, because his cry has to be heard in every region of the universe, in heaven, in earth, and in Hades."
2. "That an 'angel' raises the cry may remind us of the interest taken by angels in the plan of redemption and in the fortunes of the Church."
3. "At the same time, it may be nothing more than a part of that imagery of this book of which we have already spoken."

### Archibald T. Robertson:
1. "One needed 'whose call could reach to the farthest limits of the universe' and so 'with a great voice.'" 
2. "Worthy to open and to loose, worthy by rank and character as well as by ability."
3. "The articles here refer to the book and the seals. It is a husteron-proteron, since the loosing of the seals precedes the opening of the book."

### John Gill:
1. "Angels are said to be mighty, and to excel in strength: this is called so here, chiefly with respect to his voice, which he, by reason of his great strength, exerted so loudly, as to be heard by all the creatures in heaven, and in earth, and under the earth."
2. "He must not only be one of power and ability, but of dignity and authority, a person of greatness and worth, from whence his ability arises; as he ought to be that can engage his heart to draw nigh to God, and take a book out of his right hand."
3. "This proclamation was made to stir up an earnest desire in John, and all the saints he represents, to know what was in this book; and to show the impotence of all creatures to make any discovery of it; and to illustrate, and set off with a greater foil, the glory, excellency, ability, and worth of Jesus Christ."

### John Wesley:
1. "This proclamation to every creature was too great for a man to make, and yet not becoming the Lamb himself."
2. "It was therefore made by an angel, and one of uncommon eminence."

These quotes highlight the significance of the strong angel in delivering the proclamation to every creature. They emphasize that the magnitude of the message required a messenger of great stature, surpassing the capabilities of a human but still distinct from the Lamb.
