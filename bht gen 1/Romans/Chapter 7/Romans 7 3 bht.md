1. "The Apostle is insisting on the fact, that DEATH DISSOLVES LEGAL OBLIGATION."
2. "Death has dissolved the legal obligation between man and wife: therefore the wife is at liberty to be married to another."
3. "Death has dissolved the legal obligation between the law and us: therefore we are at liberty to be married to another."

### Jamieson-Fausset-Brown:
1. "Marriage is not just a legal contract, but a sacred union where two individuals become one. It is a joining of hearts, minds, and souls."
2. "The word 'married' implies a deep level of commitment and intimacy. It signifies a bond that goes beyond mere physical union."
3. "In Romans 7:4, Paul uses the analogy of marriage to illustrate our relationship with Christ. Just as a married woman is bound to her husband, we are bound to Christ in a covenant of love and faithfulness."

### Albert Barnes:
1. "The word used here χρηματίσει chrēmatisei is often used to denote being called by an oracle or by divine revelation."
2. "But it is here employed in the simple sense of being commonly called."
3. "or of being so regarded."

### Marvin Vincent:
1. "The term 'χρηματισει' used here carries the connotation of divine appointment and purpose. It signifies that the name 'Christian' is not a mere label, but a recognition of God's calling and commissioning of His people."

2. "Being called a Christian means being identified with Christ, not only in name but also in character and conduct. It implies a life that reflects the teachings and example of Jesus."

3. "The name 'Christian' is not self-proclaimed, but given by others as a testimony to the visible manifestation of Christ's presence and influence in the lives of believers."

### Philip Schaff:
1. "Shall be called an adulteress. This is the formal sentence, with a definite penalty stoning."
2. "Free from that law; lit., ‘the law,’ in so far as it binds her to the husband, the binding effect of the law as respects the marriage relation."
3. "So that she is not an adulteress. This clause may express either the result (‘so that’) or the purpose, ‘in order that’ The latter is perhaps grammatically more exact; the purpose of this freedom was to prevent the woman from being an adulteress in case of a second marriage."

### Archibald T. Robertson:
1. "She shall be called (χρηματισε). Future active indicative of χρηματιζω, old verb, to receive a name as in Acts 11:26, from χρημα, business, from χραομα, to use, then to give an oracle, etc." 

2. "An adulteress (μοιχαλις). Late word, in Plutarch, LXX. See on Matthew 12:39." 

3. "So that she is no adulteress (του μη εινα αυτην μοιχαλιδα). It is a fact that του and the infinitive is used for result as we saw in Romans 1:24. Conceived result may explain the idiom here."

### John Gill:
1. "True indeed it is, that whilst her husband is alive, if she be married to another man, she shall be called an adulteress; she will be noted and accounted of as such by everybody."
2. "But if her husband be dead; then there can be no exception to her marriage: she is free from the law of marriage, by which she was before bound."
3. "Hence it appears that second marriages are lawful."
