# 1 Corinthians 16:12 Commentary Help Text

## BHT:
Apollos, a renowned preacher and well-respected by the Corinthians, was urged by Paul to accompany him to Corinth in hopes that he could help resolve their conflicts. However, Apollos, being aware of the divisive atmosphere, was reluctant to go at that time. Nevertheless, Paul assured the Corinthians that Apollos would come when the opportune moment presented itself.

## Choicest Commentary Quotes:
### Henry Alford:
1. "On the construction of περὶ.… ἀδ., see on 1 Corinthians 16:1."
2. "παρεκάλ . ἵνα ἔλθῃ] ἵνα denotes the aim, not only the purport of the exhortation. See remarks on ch. 1 Corinthians 14:13."
3. "θέλημα] Evidently the will of Apollos, not, as Theophyl.: τουτέστιν, ὁ θεὸς οὐκ ἤθελεν."

### Jamieson-Fausset-Brown:
1. "Paul's noble freedom from all selfish jealousy led him to urge Apollos to go"
2. "Apollos, having heard of the abuse of his name at Corinth to party purposes, perseveringly refused to go"
3. "Apollos did return to Corinth when their divisions were moderated"

### Albert Barnes:
1. "His will was not at all to come at this time - It is probable that there were matters which detained him, or which required his presence in Ephesus."
2. "For the same reason he might not be induced to return there while those dissensions lasted and there might be employment which he had where he then was which rendered his presence there important."
3. "He will come when he shall have convenient thee - The Greek word means, when he should have leisure, or a good opportunity. He might then be engaged; or he might be unwilling to go while their contentions lasted."

### John Calvin:
1. "He now states a reason why he does not come with the others, and he states the reason of this, in order that the Corinthians may not suspect that he had been hindered by him."
2. "They might, at least, be prepared to inquire among themselves: 'Why has he sent these persons to us rather than Apollos?' He answers, that it was not owing to him, inasmuch as he entreated him; but he promises that he will come as soon as he has opportunity."
3. "That St. Paul feeling offended with the Corinthians, had intentionally brought it about, that Apollos should not go to them."

### Philip Schaff:
1. "The apostle’s wish that he should go, and the declinature of Apollos, were alike honourable to the high principle and brotherly affection of both." 
2. "Considering the jealousies which their names had respectively aroused at Corinth."
3. "Apollos consented, as we shall presently see, to go at a future time; but this time seemed inopportune."

### Archibald T. Robertson:
1. "And it was not at all his will to come now (κα παντως ουκ ην θελημα ινα νυν ελθη). Adversative use of κα = 'but.' Apollos had left Corinth in disgust over the strife there which involved him and Paul (1 Corinthians 16:1)."
2. "He had had enough of partisan strife over preachers."

### John Gill:
1. "Who was a senior man to Timothy, an eloquent preacher, one who had been at Corinth, and was well known to the saints there, and greatly approved by them."
2. "He greatly importuned him to go along with them, knowing how acceptable he would be among them, and hoping he might be of great use to them in composing their differences, and rectifying their disorders."
3. "However, for their encouragement it is added, but he will come when he shall have convenient time."

### John Wesley:
1. "I besought him much — To come to you."
2. "With the brethren — Who were then going to Corinth."
3. "Yet he was by no means willing to come now — Perhaps lest his coming should increase the divisions among them."
