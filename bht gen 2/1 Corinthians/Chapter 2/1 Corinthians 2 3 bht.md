# 1 Corinthians 2:3 Commentary Help Text

## BHT:
In my early days of preaching among you, I appeared weak in both my physical presence and my voice, which made me unimpressive as a preacher. Despite this, I approached my ministry with humility and a deep concern not to offend anyone.

## Choicest Commentary Quotes:
### Henry Alford:
1. "The weakness and fear and much trembling must not be exclusively understood of his manner of speech as contrasted with the rhetorical preachers, but partly of this, and principally of his internal deep and humble persuasion of his own weakness and the mightiness of the work which was entrusted to him." 
2. "The ἀσθένεια may have reference to the παρουσία σώματος ἀσθενής of 2 Corinthians 10:10, but in the places to which he refers, it has a far wider meaning, viz. infirmities, including those resulting from persecution."

### Jamieson-Fausset-Brown:
1. "I—the preacher: as 'Christ crucified'." 
2. "Weakness—personal and bodily."
3. "Trembling—not personal fear, but a trembling anxiety to perform a duty."

### Albert Barnes:
1. "In weakness - In conscious feebleness; diffident of my own powers, and not trusting to my own strength."
2. "And in fear, and in much trembling - Paul was sensible that he had many enemies to encounter...and he, therefore, delivered his message with deep and anxious solicitude as to the success."
3. "If Paul was conscious of weakness, well may other ministers be; and if Paul sometimes trembled in deep solicitude about the result of his message, well may other ministers tremble also...Notwithstanding all Paul’s fears, he was successful there."

### Marvin Vincent:
1. "I fell into a state of weakness, etc., after I had come among you."
2. "With you, i e., in intercourse with."
3. "The implication is that his condition grew out of the circumstances in which he found himself in Corinth."

### John Calvin:
1. "He shows that the power of God shone out the more conspicuously in this, that he could effect so much, while sustained by no human helps."
2. "The term weakness he employs here, and in several instances afterwards, as including everything that can detract from a person’s favor and dignity in the opinion of others."
3. "Paul, therefore, was not devoid of the influence of fear, but that fear he controlled in such a manner as to go forward, notwithstanding, with intrepidity through the midst of dangers."

### Philip Schaff:
1. "It is remarkable that nowhere else does the apostle so speak, nor does he seem to have anywhere else felt such an oppressive consciousness of his insufficiency." 
2. "The historian of the Acts refers to these very feelings at Corinth in the following unusual terms: 'But when Silas and Timothy came down from Macedonia (to Corinth), Paul was constrained by the word (as the true text is), testifying to the Jews that Jesus was the Christ.'"

### Archibald T. Robertson:
1. "I not only eschewed all affectation of cleverness or grandiloquence, but I went to the opposite extreme of diffidence and nervous self-effacement."
2. "Paul had been in prison in Philippi, driven out of Thessalonica and Beroea, politely bowed out of Athens. It is a human touch to see this shrinking as he faced the hard conditions in Corinth."
3. "Cool complacency is not the mood of the finest preaching."

### John Gill:
1. "The weakness of his bodily presence, the contemptibleness of his voice, and the mean figure he made as a preacher among them."
2. "The lowly and humble deportment among them, not exerting the power and authority Christ had given him as an apostle."
3. "The violence of men against him, threatening his life, and lying in wait for it."

### John Wesley:
1. "And I was with you — At my first entrance."
2. "In weakness — Of body."
3. "And in fear - Lest I should offend any."
