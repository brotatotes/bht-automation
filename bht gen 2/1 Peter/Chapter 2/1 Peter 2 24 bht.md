# 1 Peter 2:24 Commentary Help Text

## BHT:
Christ willingly took upon Himself the work of redemption and bore the guilt and punishment of our sins in His own body on the cross. His sufferings were not insignificant, but rather a noble expression of His love towards us. By offering Himself as a sacrifice, He became the perfect sin offering and made atonement for sins. His death and resurrection enabled us to be delivered from the guilt and power of sin, so that we might live and walk in righteousness. Through the stripes He endured, healing and wholeness are made available to all who believe in Him.

## Choicest Commentary Quotes:
### Henry Alford:
1. "He was not only negatively innocent, but suffered in the pursuance of the noblest purpose of love, and that love towards us."
2. "He bore our sins in His own body on the tree, took them to the tree and offered them up on it."
3. "By whose stripe ye were healed."

### Jamieson-Fausset-Brown:
1. "His voluntary undertaking of the work of redemption is implied."
2. "In offering or presenting in sacrifice...His body, Christ offered in it the guilt of our sins upon the cross...that it might be expiated in Him, and so taken away from us."
3. "Ye servants...often bear the strife; but it is not more than your Lord Himself bore; learn from Him patience in wrongful sufferings."

### Albert Barnes:
1. "He did it in his own proper person; he did not make expiation by offering a bloody victim, but was himself the sacrifice."
2. "He put himself in the place of sinners, and bore that which those sins deserved; that is, that he endured in his own person that which, if it had been inflicted on the sinner himself, would have been a proper expression of the divine displeasure against sin, or would have been a proper punishment for sin."
3. "The sufferings of the Saviour were in all respects made as nearly like the sufferings of the most guilty, as the sufferings of a perfectly innocent being could be."

### Marvin Vincent:
1. "Bare up to the cross, as to an altar, and offered himself thereon."
2. "Having become off unto sins; not becoming separate from sins, but having ceased to exist as regards them."
3. "Such a sight we feel sure, as we read this descriptive passage, St. Peter's eyes beheld on the body of his Master, and the flesh so dreadfully mangled made the disfigured form appear in his eyes like one single bruise."

### John Calvin:
1. "Had he commended nothing in Christ’s death except as an example, it would have been very frigid: he therefore refers to a fruit much more excellent."
2. "Christ’s death was a sacrifice for the expiation of our sins; for being fixed to the cross and offering himself a victim for us, he took on himself our sin and our punishment."
3. "He [Peter] intended to set forth the same thing by the words of this verse, even that we are reconciled to God on this condition, because Christ made himself before his tribunal a surety and as one guilty for us, that he might suffer the punishment due to us."

### Philip Schaff:
1. "The vicariousness of His sufferings adds to His example a power and grandeur higher still than it receives from the qualities already instanced in it."
2. "The statement... involves the two ideas of sacrifice and substitution."
3. "The moral sickness of sin is translated into the health of righteousness by the pain of the Sinless."

### Archibald T. Robertson:
1. "Jesus is the perfect sin offering... For Christ's body as the offering..."

### John Gill:
1. "The apostle here explains the nature and end of Christ's sufferings, which were to make atonement for sins, and which was done by bearing them."
2. "He took them to himself, as one may take the debt of another, and make himself answerable for it; or as a man takes up a burden, and lays it on his shoulders."
3. "Christ is the only physician, and his blood the balm and sovereign medicine; this cleanses from all sin; through it is the remission of sin, which is meant by healing."

### John Wesley:
1. "Who himself bore our sins — That is, the punishment due to them. In his afflicted, torn, dying body on the tree - The cross, whereon chiefly slaves or servants were wont to suffer."
2. "That we being dead to sin — Wholly delivered both from the guilt and power of it: indeed, without an atonement first made for the guilt, we could never have been delivered from the power."
3. "Might live to righteousness — Which is one only. The sins we had committed, and he bore, were manifold."
