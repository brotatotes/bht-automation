# Acts 13:11 Commentary Help Text

## BHT:
The punishment administered to him was not only a just consequence of his actions but also had a merciful purpose of leading him to repentance. The temporary blindness was meant to awaken reflection and remorse in him. It is fascinating how swiftly God can humble us and strip away our senses, reminding us of our complete dependence on Him.

## Choicest Commentary Quotes:
### Henry Alford:
1. "The punishment was only temporary, being accompanied with a gracious purpose to the man himself, to awaken repentance in him."
2. "In the same precise and gradual manner is the healing of the lame man, ch. Acts 3:8, described: ἔστη (first), κ . περιεπάτει. So here, first a dimness came on him, then total darkness."

### Jamieson-Fausset-Brown:
1. "The hand of the Lord is upon thee, and thou shalt be blind for a season—the judgment being mercifully designed to lead him to repentance."
2. "The tradition that it did is hardly to be depended on."
3. "There fell on him a mist, &c.—This is in Luke's medical style."

### Albert Barnes:
1. "His wickedness deserved such punishment; and at the same time that due punishment was inflicted, it was designed that the gospel should be extended by this means."
2. "It was, however, in mercy ordained that the blindness should not be permanent and final; and though it was a punishment, it was at the same time benevolent, for nothing would be more likely to lead him to reflection and repentance than such a state of blindness."
3. "How soon can God bring down the pride of man, and make him as helpless as an infant! How easily can He touch our senses, the organs of our most exquisite pleasures, and wither away all our enjoyments!"

### Marvin Vincent:
1. "Mist [αχλυς] . Only here in New Testament."
2. "The word is used by medical writers as a name for a disease of the eyes."
3. "The mention of the successive stages, first dimness, then total darkness, are characteristic of the physician."
4. "The first miracle which Paul performed was the infliction of a judgment; and that judgment the same which befell himself when arrested on his way to Damascus" (Gloag).

### John Calvin:
1. "For as they did excel in power of the Spirit to help the faithful with miracles, so had they the whip in their hand to tame the rebellious and obstinate withal."
2. "But because miracles ought, for the most part, to resemble the nature of Christ, who is all gentle, sweet, bountiful, and merciful; therefore he would seldom have the apostles to show examples of the contrary power."
3. "This visible punishment which was once laid upon the sorcerer, and such as this, are perpetual examples of God’s wrath against all those who are not afraid either to corrupt and deprave, or openly with slanders to resist the pure doctrine of the gospel."

### Philip Schaff:
1. "Miracles of punishment are very rare in the New Testament... it was not simple unbelief which was punished, but a course of conduct which... set the example of religious hypocrisy... and gave its sanction to a self-indulgent, evil life."
2. "Elymas was punished for a deliberate using of talents and power to persuade men to be enemies of righteousness, and haters of the pure life loved by the Lord."
3. "The miraculous power with which [Paul] was invested was not under his own control, but under the control and direction of Him who bestowed that power."

### Archibald T. Robertson:
1. "He has not humbled himself under the mighty hand of God."
2. "The blindness was to be 'for a season,' if it should please God to restore his sight."
3. "He went about seeking some one to lead him by the hand."

### John Gill:
1. "The power of God was just ready to be exerted on him in a way of punishment."
2. "Blind men need one to hold them by the hand, and lead them."
3. "The striking this man with blindness is an instance of the power the apostles were endued with, for the punishing of offenders."

### John Wesley:
1. And immediately a mist — Or dimness within, and darkness without, fell upon him.
