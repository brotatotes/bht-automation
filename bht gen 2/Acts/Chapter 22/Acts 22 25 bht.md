# Acts 22:25 Commentary Help Text

## BHT:
When they stretched him forward, Paul, a Roman citizen, knew that he could not be bound with thongs or subjected to scourging according to the laws of Rome. Therefore, he questioned the centurion who stood by, reminding him of his rights and privileges as a Roman citizen.

## Choicest Commentary Quotes:
### Henry Alford:
1. "The προ in both verbs allude to the position of the prisoner, which was, bent forward, and tied with a sort of gear made of leather to an inclined post."
2. "De W. and others render τοῖς ἱμᾶσιν, 'for the scourge' (dat. commodi); but why should μάστιξιν be varied? and can it be shewn (as Dr. B. asks) that the word in the plural will bear this meaning?"
3. "The 'centurio supplicio præpositus' of Tacitus and Seneca, standing by to superintend the punishment."

### Jamieson-Fausset-Brown:
1. "Paul said to the centurion that stood by—to superintend the torture and receive the confession expected to be wrung from him."
2. "Is it lawful for you to scourge a man that is a Roman, &c."

### Albert Barnes:
1. "Bound him with thongs - With cords, preparatory to scourging."
2. "Is it lawful ... - It was directly contrary to the Roman law to bind and scourge a Roman citizen."
3. "See the notes on Acts 16:36-37."

### Marvin Vincent:
1. "They stretched forward, in allusion to the position of the victim for scourging."
2. "It is better... to render stretched him out for (or before) the thongs."
3. "The word is used... of a shoe-latchet."

### John Calvin:
1. "For nothing was then more heinous than to do anything which was contrary to the liberty of the people of Rome."
2. "The privilege was so (sure and) holy, that they thought it to be not only a deadly offense, but also such an offense as could not be purged, that a citizen of Rome should be beaten."
3. "But we must know that he did so allege the right and privilege of the city, that the chief captain was brought to believe him, because his words should not have been credited unless he had used some proof."

### Philip Schaff:
1. "And as they stretched him out for the scourge; that is, the apostle was bound to a post or pillar in order to be exposed to the blows of the scourge, and in a suitable position to receive the torture."
2. "Once before at Philippi had Paul saved himself from the dread infliction by the same plea; now again the execution of the sentence was at once delayed, and the officer who was in charge of the prisoner at once reported to his commander the words of the sufferer."
3. "The claim of Roman citizenship was instantly allowed. There was no fear of imposture in such a case: the assertion, if false, was punishable with death."

### Archibald T. Robertson:
1. "When they stretched him forward." 
2. "Paul was bent forward and tied by the thongs to a post in front to expose his back the better to the scourges."
3. "A Roman and uncondemned."

### John Gill:
1. "The word here used signifies an extension, or distension; perhaps the stretching out of the arms to the pillar, and a bending forward of the whole body, which fitly expresses the stooping inclining posture of the person scourged, and was a very proper one for such a punishment."
2. "Though the apostle puts this by way of question, yet he knew full well what the Roman laws were in such cases; he did not put this through ignorance, or for information, but to let them know who he was, and to put them in mind of these laws, and of their duty."
3. "According to the Porcian law, Roman citizens were not to be beaten. Hence, says Cicero, 'it is a heinous sin to bind a Roman citizen, it is wickedness to beat him, it is next to parricide to kill him, and what shall I say to crucify him?'"

### John Wesley:
1. "A freeman of Rome might be bound with a chain and beaten with a staff: but he might not be bound with thongs, neither scourged, or beaten with rods."
2. "Paul said to the centurion - The captain, who stood by to see the orders of the tribune executed."
