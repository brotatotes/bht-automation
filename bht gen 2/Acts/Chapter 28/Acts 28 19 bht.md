# Acts 28:19 Commentary Help Text

## BHT:
Paul explains that his appeal to Caesar was not made out of hatred or a desire to accuse his own people. Instead, it was a necessary step to protect himself from those who sought to harm him unjustly. He had no ill intentions towards his nation, but rather sought to defend his innocence and preserve his own life. Despite the unjust treatment he had received, Paul harbored no resentment or animosity towards those who had wronged him.

## Choicest Commentary Quotes:
### Henry Alford:
1. "My appeal was a defensive and necessary step not an offensive one, to complain of my nation."
2. "The inf. aor. of the rec. would point to some one definite charge."
3. "κατηγορεῖν means 'to play the accuser against my nation in any thing,' indicating the habit."

### Jamieson-Fausset-Brown:
1. "I am here not as their accuser, but as my own defender, and this not of choice but necessity." 
2. "His object in alluding thus gently to the treatment he had received from the Jews was plainly to avoid whatever might irritate his visitors at the first."
3. "Especially as he was not aware whether any or what information against him had reached their community."

### Albert Barnes:
1. "I was constrained - By a regard to my own safety and character."
2. "To appeal unto Caesar - See the notes on Acts 25:11."
3. "Paul had been unjustly accused and injured; yet, with the true spirit of the Christian religion, he here says that he cherished no unkind feelings toward those who had done him wrong."

### John Calvin:
1. "This appeal was full of hatred and envy for this cause, because the authority and liberty of the Jewish nation did seem to be sore opprest, who could have been content to have lived with their own laws."
2. "He is excused by necessity, because he had no other way to escape death."
3. "He promiseth that he will so handle his matter hereafter, − that he will not labor against the Jews."

### Philip Schaff:
1. "He presses this point upon them, being most anxious to show them he was there not as an accuser of, or an enemy to, ‘the people’ the people whom he loved better than life."
2. "It was to do them no harm that he had appealed to the Cæsar at Rome: it was his last resort to save himself from judicial murder or assassination."
3. "We must bear in mind that here, as in the other reports of Paul’s sermons and speeches, we only possess the barest outline of the original."

### Archibald T. Robertson:
1. "The word is a mild one to describe the bitter enmity of the Jews."
2. "Paul was compelled to appeal to Caesar, unless Paul was willing to be the victim of Jewish hate when he had done no wrong."
3. "Paul says εθνος instead of λαος as in Acts 24:17; Acts 26:4."

### John Gill:
1. "I was constrained to appeal to Caesar; to prevent the design of the Jews upon him, which was to way lay him and kill him, or by what ways they could, right or wrong, take away his life; and to provide for his own safety."
2. "Not that I had ought to accuse my nation of; meaning, that he had no ill design in this appeal against his country, to expose them, and bring them under reproach and censure, but to vindicate himself, defend his own innocence, and preserve his character and life."
3. "Suggesting, that what he did was not of choice, but by constraint, and with reluctance; being no friend to Heathen tribunals, nor any enemy to the Jewish nation."

### John Wesley:
1. "He speaks tenderly of them, not mentioning their repeated attempts to murder him."
2. "Not that I had any thing to accuse my nation of — Not that I had any design to accuse others, but merely to defend myself."
