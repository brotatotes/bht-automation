# Colossians 4:11 Commentary Help Text

## BHT:
These Jewish helpers were the few who demonstrated support for Paul's gospel work, in contrast to the broader Jewish community at Rome, which generally opposed Christ. These workers were specifically those who had the necessary gifts to promote the gospel. Their assistance and comfort were invaluable to Paul in the face of affliction and suffering.

## Choicest Commentary Quotes:
### Henry Alford:
1. "The surname Justus (צדוק) was common among the Jews."
2. "These alone who are of the circumcision (the construction is of the nature of an anacoluthon, οἱ ὄντες ἐκ π. being equivalent to 'of those of the circumcision.'"
3. "The judaistic teachers were for the most part in opposition to St. Paul."

### Jamieson-Fausset-Brown:
1. "Justus—that is, righteous; a common name among the Jews; Hebrew, 'tzadik' (Acts 1:23)."
2. "This implies that Epaphras, Luke, and Demas (Colossians 4:12; Colossians 4:14) were not of the circumcision."
3. "For the Jewish teachers were generally opposed to the apostle of the Gentiles (Philippians 1:15)."

### Albert Barnes:
1. "The word 'only' here, probably refers to the fact that they only of all the Jews who were at Rome assisted Paul in his work."
2. "These only are my fellow-workers unto the kingdom of God."
3. "Which have been a comfort unto me - The more so because they were Jews."


### Marvin Vincent:
1. "Jesus Justus. Not mentioned elsewhere."
2. "Parhgoria comfort, only here in the New Testament. Properly, an address, an exhortation : an exhortation for the purpose of encouraging : hence a comfort."
3. "This word, and the kindred adjectives parhgorikov and parhgorhtikov soothing, are common in medical writings."

### John Calvin:
1. "He means, therefore, that there were few Jews at Rome who shewed themselves to be helpers to the gospel, nay more, that the whole nation was opposed to Christ."
2. "At the same time, by workers he means those only who were endowed with gifts that were necessary for promoting the gospel."
3. "He calls the gospel the kingdom of God, for it is the scepter by which God reigns over us, and by means of it we are singled out to life eternal."

### Philip Schaff:
1. "Otherwise unknown; not the person mentioned in Acts 18:7, since the latter was a proselyte, not a born Jew, and moreover was called 'Titus Justus.'" 
2. "Many disconnect this clause from what precedes, and render: 'Of those who are of the circumcision, only these are my fellow-workers,' etc. This is undoubtedly the correct sense, since others, who were not Jews, had labored with him and been a comfort."
3. "This indicates the general antagonism of the Jewish Christians; comp. Philippians 1:15."

### Archibald T. Robertson:
1. "The surname Justus is the Latin Justus for the Greek Δικαιος and the Hebrew Zadok and very common as a surname among the Jews."  
2. "Jewish Christians certainly, but not necessarily Judaizers like those so termed in Acts 11:3 (ο εκ περιτομης. Cf. Acts 35:1; Acts 35:5)."  
3. "Παρηγορια is an old word (here only in N.T.) from παρηγορεω, to make an address) and means solace, relief. A medical term. Curiously enough our word paregoric comes from it (παρηγορικος)."

### John Gill:
1. "Jesus, called Justus, a surname that was sometimes given to men remarkable for holiness and righteousness."
2. "These are my fellow workers unto the kingdom of God, who assisted him in preaching the Gospel and promoting the honor and interest of Christ."
3. "They have been a comfort unto me under my afflictions and sufferings."

### John Wesley:
1. "Of all my Jewish fellowlabourers."
2. "The only fellowworkers unto the kingdom of God."
3. "Who have been a comfort to me."
