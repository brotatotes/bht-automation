# Luke 1:68 Commentary Help Text

## BHT:
God's gracious intervention among His chosen people is portrayed in this passage. The mention of God's visitation emphasizes His desire to bless and glorify Him, acknowledging His divine attributes and mighty works. The concept of redemption is also highlighted, with it being not just a political release, but also a profound and everlasting spiritual deliverance. Through His visitation and redemption, God bestows abundant blessings upon His people, demonstrating His steadfast love and faithfulness.

## Choicest Commentary Quotes:
### Henry Alford:
1.  68. ] After  ἐπεσκέψατο (for Hebraistic sense of which see reff.) must be understood, as an object, τὸν λαὸν αὐτοῦ , contained in the following dative.

### Jamieson-Fausset-Brown:
1. "There is not a word in this noble burst of divine song about his own child; like Elisabeth losing sight entirely of self, in the glory of a Greater than both." 
2. "In the Old Testament, God is said to 'visit' chiefly for judgment, in the New Testament for mercy."
3. "But this Old Testament phraseology, used at first with a lower reference, is, when viewed in the light of a loftier and more comprehensive kingdom of God, equally adapted to express the most spiritual conceptions of the redemption that is in Christ Jesus."

### Albert Barnes:
1. "God 'looked upon' the world - he saw it miserable - he came to relieve it, and brought salvation."
2. "The literal translation of this passage is, 'He hath made a 'ransom' for his people."
3. "The 'blood of Jesus' - that is, his death in the place of sinners constitutes such a ransom."

### John Calvin:
1. "The reason why the Lord, to whose government the whole world is subject, is here called the God of Israel, will more fully appear from what follows, that to the seed of Abraham, in a peculiar manner, the Redeemer had been promised."
2. "The word ἐπεσκέψατο , he hath visited, contains an implied contrast: for the face of God had been turned away for a time from the unhappy children of Abraham."
3. "God looked upon ( ἐπεσκέψατο ) his people, that he might redeem them Now, as those whom God redeems must be prisoners, and as this redemption is spiritual in its nature, we conclude from this passage, that even the holy fathers were made free from the yoke of sin and the tyranny of death, only through the grace of Christ."

### Philip Schaff:
1. "For he hath visited, etc. The past tenses throughout are used because the eye of prophecy regards these certain future events as having already taken place."
2. "Wrought redemption. This sums up the benefits bestowed by the Messiah, regarding them from the priestly point of view."
3. "His people. Comp, the previous clause: 'the God of Israel.'"

### Archibald T. Robertson:
1. "Hath visited (επεσκεψατο). An old Greek word with a Hebraic colouring to look into with a view to help."
2. "The papyri have plenty of examples of the verb in the sense of inspecting, examining."
3. "Redemption (λυτρωσιν) here originally referred to political redemption, but with a moral and spiritual basis."

### John Gill:
1. "To bless God...signify to confer a blessing on him, but to praise and glorify him, on account of the perfections of his nature, and the works of his hands."
2. "The act of 'visiting' them...the foundation and security of their salvation."
3. "The 'redemption' of them...is a plenteous, full, complete, and everlasting one."
