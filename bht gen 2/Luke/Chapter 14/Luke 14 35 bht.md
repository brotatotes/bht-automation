# Luke 14:35 Commentary Help Text

## BHT:
Professors of religion and ministers without the grace of God are like useless things, offering no benefit but causing harm to the church and the world. They ought to be cast out of the churches and will be excluded from the kingdom of heaven. The significance of this message is emphasized by the phrase He that hath ears to hear, let him hear, indicating that it applies to all who are willing to listen and has profound implications.

## Choicest Commentary Quotes:
### Philip Schaff:
1. "Neither for the land, nor for the dunghill. Fuller than Matthew 5:13: 'good for nothing.' It is not useful directly or indirectly."
2. "Men cast it out (emphatically), because it is thus useless."
3. "He that hath ears to hear, etc. This common formula calls attention to the importance of what had been said, implying that it has an application to all the hearers, and admonishing them to make that application to their own hearts."

### Archibald T. Robertson:
1. "Dunghill (κοπριαν). Later word in the Koine vernacular."
2. "Men cast it out (εξω βαλλουσιν αυτο). Impersonal plural."
3. "This saying about salt is another of Christ's repeated sayings (Matthew 5:13; Mark 9:50)."

### John Gill:
1. "Just such useless things, are a mere external profession of religion, and professors of it, and ministers of the word, without the grace of God; they are of no use, but hurtful to the church, and to the world."
2. "And so such graceless professors and ministers, are to be cast out of the churches of Christ now, and will be excluded the kingdom of heaven hereafter."
3. "He that hath ears to hear, let him hear; this being a point of great importance and consequence."
