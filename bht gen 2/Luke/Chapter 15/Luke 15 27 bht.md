# Luke 15:27 Commentary Help Text

## BHT:
The mention of the father killing the fatted calf in response to the prodigal son's return may be significant because it is closely connected with the music and dancing that would ensue. This detail stands out among the robe and ring, highlighting the joyful celebration that takes place when someone who was lost is found and welcomed back into the family.

## Choicest Commentary Quotes:
### Marvin Vincent:
1. "How nice is the observance of all the lesser proprieties of the narration."
2. "He sees into the deep of his joy, that he is receiving him now indeed a son, once dead but now alive; once lost to him and to God, but now found alike by both."
3. "But the servant confines himself to the more external features of the case, to the fact that, after all he has gone through of excess and hardship, his father has yet received him safe and sound."

### Philip Schaff:
1. "The servant states the case as it impresses him."
2. "He says nothing of the condition in which the prodigal returned, but simply that the father had received him safe and sound."
3. "No special interpretation is to be put upon this verse."

### Archibald T. Robertson:
1. "Is come (ηκε). Present indicative active, but a stem with perfect sense..." 
2. "Hath killed (εθυσεν). Aorist active indicative and literally means, did kill."
3. "Hath received (απελαβεν). Second aorist active indicative with similar difficulty of translation."

### John Gill:
1. "And thy father... hath killed the fatted calf... expresses the large and rich entertainment God makes for souls, when they are brought home to him by repentance."
2. "The elect of God... through efficacious grace in calling, are found, received, and enjoyed again."
3. "He was received 'safe'... God's elect... being redeemed by him, are safe; so that the law cannot lay hold on them, nor sin, nor any thing else condemn them, nor Satan destroy them."

### John Wesley:
1. "Thy father hath killed the fatted calf — Perhaps he mentions this rather than the robe or ring, as having a nearer connection with the music and dancing."
