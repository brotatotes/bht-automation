# Luke 19:11 Commentary Help Text

## BHT:
The disciples had believed that the kingdom of God, a magnificent and earthly realm, would manifest itself without delay. However, Jesus sought to counter this belief through the parable, showing that there would be a period of separation before the establishment of his kingdom.

## Choicest Commentary Quotes:
### Henry Alford:
1. "The distance of Jericho from Jerusalem was 150 stadia = 18 English miles and 6 furlongs."
2. "They imagined that the present journey to Jerusalem, undertaken as it had been with such publicity and accompanied with such wonderful miracles, was for the purpose of revealing and establishing the Messianic kingdom."

### Albert Barnes:
1. "To do that, he tells them of a man who had a right to the kingdom, yet who, 'before' taking possession of it, went into another kingdom to receive a confirmation of his title, thus intimating that 'he' would also go away 'before' he would completely set up his kingdom."
2. "To 'correct' that notion seems to have been the main design of this parable."
3. "He spoke the parable to 'correct' that expectation."

### Marvin Vincent:
1. Appear [αναφαινεσθαι] . Only here and Acts 21:3. It means to be brought to light; shown forth. The common phrase show up [ανα] represents it.

### John Calvin:
1. "It was next to a prodigy that the disciples, after having been so frequently warned as to the approaching death of Christ, flew aside from it to think of his kingdom... They judged of the kingdom of God according to their own carnal sense."
2. "Hence it appears how slight and obscure their faith was... that knowledge immediately degenerates into vain imaginations, which either overturn or obscure the power of his kingdom."
3. "But the strangest thing of all was, that so many warnings should have passed away from their recollection without yielding any advantage... they not only remained unconcerned, but rushed forward, as if to a joyful triumph."

### Philip Schaff:
1. "The parable was designed to controvert the idea that the glory of the Messianic kingdom would appear at once, without a previous separation of the Master from His servants, to whom He would return as King."
2. "This public journey to Jerusalem, attended by so many miracles and impressive discourses, was regarded as introductory to a Messianic kingdom of temporal splendor."
3. "The parable was spoken in the house, probably from the open room looking into the court, where a good part of the multitude that had followed Him... had doubtless remained and murmured. To them the parable was addressed."

### Archibald T. Robertson:
1. "He added and spake. It is a Hebrew idiom... This undoubted Hebraism occurs in the N.T. in Luke only, probably due to the influence of the LXX on Luke the Greek Christian." 
2. "To appear. Present passive infinitive of an old verb to be made manifest, to be shown up."
3. "In the N.T. only here and Acts 21:3."

### John Gill:
1. "The scope and design of the following parable is to refute the notion of a temporal kingdom, and its near approach."
2. "His servants and disciples had a great deal of business to transact for him, and must not think of pomp and grandeur, but of labour and service."
3. "The Jews were so far from receiving any advantages by his kingdom, that they would not submit to his government, and would be treated as enemies, and utterly destroyed."

### John Wesley:
1. They thought the kingdom of God — A glorious temporal kingdom, would immediately appear.
