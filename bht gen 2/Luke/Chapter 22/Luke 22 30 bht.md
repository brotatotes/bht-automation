# Luke 22:30 Commentary Help Text

## BHT:
As believers, our ultimate destiny is to be seated at the table of the Lord, enjoying the highest happiness and fulfillment. This passage speaks of the honor and dignity bestowed upon the apostles, and by extension, all followers of Christ. The language used here is figurative, representing both their spiritual growth and the eternal blessings they will experience in the kingdom of grace and glory. They will have the privilege of judging, admonishing, and revealing the truth to others, fulfilling their role as faithful servants and disciples of Christ.

### Albert Barnes:
1. See the notes at Matthew 19:28.

### Philip Schaff:
1. "The enjoyments of their reign, with Him in the kingdom appointed by His father, are thus set forth." 
2. "Ye shall lit. A direct promise."
3. "Not 'twelve thrones,' as Matthew 19:28, possibly on account of Judas."

### Archibald T. Robertson:
1. "The picture seems to be that given in Matthew 19:28 when Jesus replied to Peter's inquiry."
2. "It is not clear how literally this imagery is to be taken."
3. "But there is the promise of honour for the loyal among these in the end."

### John Gill:
1. "In the ultimate glory, when the Lamb shall feed them, and shall lead them to fountains of water; and they shall never hunger nor thirst more, but shall have fulness of joy, and be satiated with pleasures that will never fade nor end."
2. "Expressive of the great honour and dignity they were raised to, both in this, and the other world, from a low and mean estate, being before as beggars on the dunghill, now among princes, and on thrones, even on the same throne with Christ."
3. "Judging the twelve tribes of Israel; doctrinally and ministerially; accusing the Jews, and arraigning them for the crucifixion of Christ; passing sentence upon them, and condemning them, and declaring that they should be damned for their disbelief and rejection of him."

### John Wesley:
1. "That ye may eat and drink at my table — That is, that ye may enjoy the highest happiness, as guests, not as servants."
2. "These expressions seem to be primarily applicable to the twelve apostles, and secondarily, to all Christ's servants and disciples."
3. "Their spiritual powers, honors, and delights, are here represented in figurative terms, with respect to their advancement both in the kingdom of grace and of glory."
