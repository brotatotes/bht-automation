# Luke 9:51 Commentary Help Text

## BHT:
The approaching fulfillment of the days signifies the time drawing near for Jesus' ascension, his exaltation to the Father. This voluntary sacrifice reveals His determination and unwavering purpose to give His life for the sins of people. Jesus, with unwavering courage and firmness of mind, resolutely set His face like a flint towards Jerusalem, fearless of the dangers and ready to face his enemies. His journey to death and subsequent glorification was marked by a steadfast resolve to fulfill his ministry and accomplish all that was foretold and expected of him.

## Choicest Commentary Quotes:
### Henry Alford:
1. "When the days were being fulfilled: i.e. approaching their fulfilment."
2. "His assumption, i.e. ascension into heaven."
3. "Implies determinate fixed purpose: cf. Isaiah 50:7, the sense of which, as prophetic of the Messiah going to his sufferings, seems to be referred to in this expression."

### Jamieson-Fausset-Brown:
1. "the days were being fulfilled," or approaching their fulfilment.
2. "that he should be received up," meaning His exaltation to the Father; a sublime expression, taking the sweep of His whole career, as if at one bound He was about to vault into glory.
3. "He steadfastly set his face" - the "He" here is emphatic—"He Himself then." See His own prophetic language, "I have set my face like a flint" (Isaiah 50:7).

### Albert Barnes:
1. "The word here translated 'received up' means literally a removal from a lower to a higher place, and here it refers evidently to the solemn ascension of Jesus to heaven."
2. "And from this we learn that Jesus made a voluntary sacrifice; that he 'chose' to give his life for the sins of people."
3. "The expression here means only that he 'resolved' to go, and it implies that he was not appalled by the dangers - that he was determined to brave all, and go up into the midst of his enemies - to die."

### Marvin Vincent:
1. "When the time was come [εν τω συμπληρουσθαι τας ημερας] . Lit., in the fulfilling of the days. This means when the days were being fulfilled; not when they were fulfilled : when the time was drawing near." 
2. "That he should be received up [της αναλημψεως αυτου] . Lit., the days of his being taken up : his ascension into heaven."
3. "Luke is speaking of a period beginning with the first announcement of his sufferings, and extending to the time of his being received up."

### John Calvin:
1. "It describes the divine courage and firmness of Christ in despising death."
2. "What deadly enmities are produced by differences about religion."
3. "By the example of Christ we are called to the exercise of meekness."

### Philip Schaff:
1. "When the days were being fulfilled... not when the time itself had come." 
2. "The clause cannot mean that the days of His favorable reception in Galilee were at an end... the Evangelist does not imply an immediate ascension, but rather regards the history from this point as a journey to death and subsequent glorification." 
3. "He not only had but showed the fixed purpose, to go to Jerusalem... He saw what was before Him there, and went to meet it."

### Archibald T. Robertson:
1. "The language here makes it plain that Jesus was fully conscious of the time of his death as near."
2. "Not only in John's Gospel does Jesus reveal a yearning for a return to the Father, but it is in the mind of Christ here as evidently at the Transfiguration and later in Luke 12:49."
3. "This look on Christ's face as he went to his doom is noted later in Mark 10:32."

### John Gill:
1. "when he had fulfilled his ministry, and finished all his sayings, and wrought all the miracles he was to work in those parts"
2. "set his face like a flint...denoting...boldness, courage, constancy and firmness of mind"
3. "he was resolutely bent on going thither, and accordingly prepared for his journey"

### John Wesley:
1. "The days are fulfilled that he should be received up — That is, the time of his passion was now at hand."
2. "St. Luke looks through this, to the glory which was to follow."
3. "He steadfastly set his face — Without fear of his enemies, or shame of the cross, Hebrews 12:2."
