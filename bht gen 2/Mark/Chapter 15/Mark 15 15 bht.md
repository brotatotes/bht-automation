Pilate, in a difficult position, wanted to appease the noisy and threatening crowd who demanded the release of Jesus. He feared the consequences if he did not comply and decided to satisfy and calm the crowd by releasing Barabbas. In doing so, Pilate disregarded the law and justice, acting against his own conscience, all to fulfill the people's desires.

### Marvin Vincent:
1. To content [το ικανον ποιησαι] . Lit., to do the sufficient thing. Compare the popular phrase, Do the right thing. A Latinism, and used by Mark only. Wyc., to do enough to the people.

### Philip Schaff:
1. "The word 'wishing' points to a decision, a determination, neither a hearty desire, nor a mere permission."
2. "Pilate wanted to release Jesus, but in the dilemma (of his own making) concluded to gratify the mob."
3. "On the scourging see on Matthew 27:26."

### Archibald T. Robertson:
1. "To content the multitude (τω οχλω το ικανον ποιησα). A Latin idiom (satisfacere alicui), to do what is sufficient to remove one's ground of complaint."
2. "This same phrase occurs in Polybius, Appian, Diogenes Laertes, and in late papyri."
3. "Pilate was afraid of this crowd now completely under the control of the Sanhedrin. He knew what they would tell Caesar about him."

### John Gill:
1. "To satisfy and make them easy, who were become very noisy and tumultuous, and fearing the consequences of their resentment, should he not comply."
2. "After he had washed his hands, to testify his innocence in the matter, he released Barabbas unto them."
3. "He acted contrary to law and justice, to the violation of his own conscience, and merely to gratify the humour of the people."
