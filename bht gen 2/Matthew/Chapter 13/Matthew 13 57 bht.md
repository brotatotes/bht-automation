# Matthew 13:57 Commentary Help Text

## BHT:
It was a stumbling block for them to accept him because they couldn't understand how he acquired such wisdom and power, especially since he didn't learn it from scholars or receive it from his family. Instead, they chose to entertain suspicions that he gained his abilities from an evil source, which caused them to turn against him. This reflects a common tendency for people to reject those in their hometown, disregarding their true worth.

## Choicest Commentary Quotes:
### Albert Barnes:
1. "They were envious of his wisdom, and were too proud to be taught by him."
2. "Jesus advances it as a general truth. There might be some exceptions to it, but He was not an exception."
3. "A case remarkably similar to this occurs in the history of the discovery of America."

### John Calvin:
1. "It may, no doubt, be a general proverb, that those who are distinguished by eminent gifts are nowhere held in less estimation than in their own country."
2. "This manifests the ingratitude of men, who, in proportion to the greater familiarity with which God exhibits himself to them, are the more bold to reject him in the influences of his Spirit."
3. "There were good grounds for the charge which he brings against them, that, instead of being the first to accept the grace offered to them, as they ought to have been, they drive him to a distance from them."

### Philip Schaff:
1. "And they were offended in him, made to stumble. They were led into error and sin with regard to Him."
2. "A prophet is not without honor, etc. The rejection is accounted for by a proverbial expression, verified by human experience."
3. "‘Familiarity breeds contempt,’ ‘Distance lends enchantment to the view,’ are still more general expressions of the same principle."

### Archibald T. Robertson:
1. "They stumbled at him," "They were repelled by him" (Moffatt), "They turned against him" (Weymouth).
2. "It was unpardonable for Jesus not to be commonplace like themselves."
3. "Not without honour."

### John Gill:
1. "It was a stumbling to them, how he came by his wisdom and power; since he had not these things from men of learning, and could not have them from his relatives."
2. "They chose to indulge at least a suspicion, that he had them from the devil, and so were offended in him."
3. "Showing, that a prophet, or any teacher, or preacher, generally speaking, is more esteemed among strangers, who have no personal pique, nor prejudices against him, and who judge of him, not by what he has been, but by his present abilities, doctrine, and conduct, than among his countrymen."

### John Wesley:
1. They were offended at him — They looked on him as a mean, ignoble man, not worthy to be regarded. John 4:44; Luke 7:23.
