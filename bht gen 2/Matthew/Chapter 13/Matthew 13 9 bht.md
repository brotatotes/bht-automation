# Matthew 13:9 Commentary Help Text

## BHT:
Whoever has ears to hear, let him hear. These words were spoken by Jesus as a way of calling attention to the importance of understanding the parable being shared. It serves as a reminder that true understanding comes not just from external hearing, but from internal comprehension. Jesus used this phrase to engage his listeners and encourage them to earnestly reflect upon the deeper meaning of his teachings. This call to attentive listening is especially significant in the case of parables, which can require careful consideration. It even impacted Jesus' own disciples, as seen in the Gospel of Mark.

### John Calvin:
1. "He that hath ears to hear, let him hear. These words were intended partly to show that all were not endued with true understanding to comprehend what he said."
2. "Indeed, he makes a distinction among the hearers, by pronouncing some to have ears, and others to be deaf."
3. "If it is next inquired, how it comes to pass that the former have ears, Scripture testifies in other passages, that it is the Lord who pierces the ears, and that no man obtains or accomplishes this by his own industry."

### Philip Schaff:
1. "He that hath, etc. Comp. chap. Matthew 11:15. A peculiarly appropriate ending to a parable."
2. "It here refers to the understanding of the parable; the parable itself, as our Lord shows, signified the outward hearing."
3. "The former would in this case imply the latter."

### Archibald T. Robertson:
1. "He that hath ears let him hear"
2. "It is comforting to teachers and preachers to observe that even Jesus had to exhort people to listen and understand his sayings, especially his parables."
3. "They will bear the closest thought and are often enigmatical."

### John Gill:
1. "Who hath ears to hear, let him hear. Not externally only, but internally; he that has ears given him to hear, so as to understand, let him make use of them, and seriously consider of, and diligently attend to the use and importance of this parable."
2. "It is a way of speaking used by Christ, when anything of moment was delivered, and not so easy to be understood, on purpose to quicken the attention of his auditors, and stir up in them a desire of understanding what was said."
3. "Which effect this had upon his disciples; see Mark 4:10."
