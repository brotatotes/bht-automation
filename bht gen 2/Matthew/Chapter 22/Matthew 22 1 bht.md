### Albert Barnes:
1. "He made a still further statement, to show how the gospel would be received and treated by them."
2. "The real answer here, as is frequently the case in the New Testament, refers to what was passing in the mind, or to the conduct of those who were addressed, not to what they said."

### John Calvin:
1. "The design which Matthew had in view was, to point out the reasons why the scribes were excited to the highest pitch of fury; and therefore he properly placed it in the midst of those discourses which were hateful to them, and interwove it with those discourses, without attending to the order of time."
2. "It is by no means probable, that the guest and friend of a Pharisee broke out into this exclamation from any sincere feeling of piety."
3. "And his words make it manifest, that he had nothing in view beyond what was gross and earthly; for he did not employ the phrase, eat bread, as a metaphor for enjoying eternal life, but appears to have dreamed of I know not what state, filled with prosperity and abundance of all things."

### Philip Schaff:
1. Matthew 22:1.  Answered. See above.
	 Again in parables; not necessarily, in a number of parables, but in parabolic discourse.
	

### Archibald T. Robertson:
1. "Matthew has already given two on this occasion (The Two Sons, The Wicked Husbandmen). He alone gives this Parable of the Marriage Feast of the King's Son." 
2. "Hence some scholars consider this merely Matthew's version of the Lucan parable in the wrong place because of Matthew's habit of grouping the sayings of Jesus. But that is a gratuitous indictment of Matthew's report which definitely locates the parable here by παλιν."
3. "Some regard it as not spoken by Jesus at all, but an effort on the part of the writer to cover the sin and fate of the Jews, the calling of the Gentiles, and God's demand for righteousness. But here again it is like Jesus and suits the present occasion."

### John Gill:
