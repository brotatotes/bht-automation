# Matthew 26:50 Commentary Help Text

## BHT:
In the midst of his suffering, Jesus exhibited a heroic and composed demeanor that captured the attention of all who witnessed it. Despite Judas' betrayal, Jesus calmly met him and accepted his treacherous kiss. He surrendered himself with dignity to his enemies, demonstrating his superiority over them even as he allowed himself to be captured. This awe-inspiring display of composure and strength in the face of betrayal is a testament to Jesus' divine nature.

### Henry Alford:
1. "That variation shewing perhaps that one of the accounts is not from an eye-witness."
2. "ὁ ἑταιρος οὐ πάντως φίλος."
3. "No such use of the simple relative ὅς has ever been adduced."

### Marvin Vincent:
1. "Literally it is, that for which thou art here; and the mind is to supply do or be about." 
2. "The Lord spurns the traitor's embrace, and says, in effect, 'Enough of this hypocritical fawning. Do what you are here to do.'"
3. "So Rev., Do that for which thou art come."

### John Calvin:
1. "For Christ does not employ an ironical address when he calls him friend, but charges him with ingratitude, that, from being an intimate friend, who sat at his table, he had become a traitor."
2. "Let us know that this evil, which Christ once sustained in his own person, is an evil to which the Church will always be exposed—that of cherishing traitors in her bosom."
3. "We are all commanded to kiss the Son of God, and we ought, therefore, to see that no one give him a traitor’s kiss, otherwise it will cost us dear to have been elevated to so great an honor."

### Philip Schaff:
1. "Our Lord did not turn away, in holy indignation, from this Judas kiss. His meekness and gentleness under the greatest provocation, surpasses even the standard which He holds up for His disciples." 
2. "Do that for which thou art come! The expression is elliptical, and may be either an exclamation or a question: 'Is it this for which thou art come?' The former accords much better with the emotion natural at such a time."
3. "Laid their hands, etc. This does not imply undue violence. He was probably not bound until afterwards."

### Archibald T. Robertson:
1. "Do your errand."
2. "εφ' ο in late Greek having the interrogative sense of επ τ."
3. "Jesus exposes the pretence of Judas and shows that he does not believe in his paraded affection."

### John Gill:
1. "Not in an ironical and sarcastic way, but because he pretended to be his friend, by saluting and kissing him..."
2. "This he said, to let him know he knew him, and therefore he calls him by name; and that he knew his design in kissing him, and that what he was doing was against light and knowledge..."
3. "though not till Christ had given them a specimen of his power, in striking them to the ground; to let them know, that Judas could never have put him into their hands, nor could they have laid hold on him, had he not thought fit to surrender himself to them."

### John Wesley:
1. "The heroic behaviour of the blessed Jesus, in the whole period of his sufferings, will be observed by every attentive eye, and felt by every pious heart."
2. "With what composure does he go forth to meet the traitor! With what calmness receive that malignant kiss!"
3. "With what dignity does he deliver himself into the hands of his enemies! Yet plainly showing his superiority over them, and even then leading as it were captivity captive!"
