# Matthew 26:65 Commentary Help Text

## BHT:
When faced with the false claim that Jesus had committed blasphemy in claiming to be the Son of God, the high priest reacted with great indignation. By tearing his garments, the high priest expressed his grief and concern at the profanation of God's holy name and his abhorrence of the alleged blasphemy, while also symbolically foreshadowing the upcoming change within the priesthood. Despite Jesus not actually committing blasphemy, the high priest's extreme reaction highlighted the severity of the situation and his dedication to defending the honor and glory of God.

## Choicest Commentary Quotes:
### Henry Alford:
1. "In Leviticus 21:10 (see also Lev 10:6) the High-priest is ordered not to rend his clothes; but that appears to apply only to mourning for the dead." 
2. "In 1Ma 11:71, and in Josephus, B. J. ii. 15. 4, we have instances of High-priests rending their clothes." 
3. "On rending the clothes at hearing blasphemy, see 2 Kings 18:37."

### Albert Barnes:
1. "By that was probably intended the robes of his priestly office."
2. "This was done on this occasion to denote the great grief of the high priest that so great a sin as blasphemy had been committed in his presence."
3. "If he had not been the Messiah, the charge would have been true; but the question was whether he had not given evidence that he was the Messiah, and that therefore his claims were just."

### John Calvin:
1. "By this we see how little advantage was derived by wicked men from the miracles by which Christ had proved his Divinity."
2. "The rending of the clothes plainly shows how boldly and wickedly those who profanely despise God make false pretensions of zeal."
3. "But while he refused to make inquiry, he contrived an unfounded charge of blasphemy."

### Philip Schaff:
1. "Rending the clothes was a sign of mourning or of indignation." 
2. "Instances of the high-priests using this sign of indignation occur in the first Book of the Maccabees and Josephus." 
3. "The high-priest assumes that they all agree with him, the whole verdict being spoken in hot haste."

### Archibald T. Robertson:
1. "Jesus had incriminated himself by claiming under oath to be the Messiah, the Son of God."
2. "Now it would not be blasphemy for the real Messiah to make such a claim, but it was intolerable to admit that Jesus could be the Messiah of Jewish hope."
3. "At the Triumphal Entry Jesus let the Galilean crowds hail him as Messiah, knowing what the effect would be."

### John Gill:
1. "This he did, to show his zeal for the honour and glory of God, his grief and concern at the profanation of his holy name by a false oath, and his abhorrence of, and indignation at the blasphemy he supposed Christ to be guilty of, in asserting himself to be the Son of God."
2. "The observation, that some learned men have made, that the high priest's rending his garments, was, though without his intention, an emblem and presage, of the rending of the priesthood from him, and his brethren, and the entire change of it."
3. "but also the Son of God; hereby making himself equal with God, which is the sense in which the Jews always understood this phrase; and he appearing to them to be but a mere man, they charged it as blasphemy against God, to assume such a character and relation to himself."

### John Wesley:
1. "Though the high priest was forbidden to rend his clothes (that is, his upper garment) in some cases where others were allowed to do it, Leviticus 21:10; yet in case of blasphemy or any public calamity, it was thought allowable."
2. "Caiaphas hereby expressed, in the most artful manner, his horror at hearing such grievous blasphemy."
