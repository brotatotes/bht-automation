They were both awestruck and overjoyed by what they had witnessed, making their emotions as natural as their quick departure. ### Jamieson-Fausset-Brown:
1. "They departed quickly...with fear and great joy—How natural this combination of feelings!"
2. "Neither said they anything to any man [by the way]; for they were afraid."
3. "This appearance is recorded only by Matthew."

### Albert Barnes:
1. "The news was too good to be credited at once, yet they had sufficient faith in it to fill them with great and unexpected joy."
2. "Perhaps no language could better express the state of their minds - the mingled awe and rejoicing - than that which is used here."
3. "They ran to announce what they had seen to the disciples."

### John Calvin:
1. "By these words Matthew means that they were indeed gladdened by what the angel told them, but, at the same time, they were struck with fear, so that they were held in suspense between joy and perplexity."
2. "For there are sometimes opposite feelings in the hearts of the godly, which move them alternately in opposite directions, until at length the peace of the Spirit brings them into a settled condition."
3. "This was an astonishing instance of goodness, that Christ manifested his heavenly glory to a wretched woman, who had been possessed by seven devils, and, intending to display the light of a new and eternal life, began where there was nothing in the eyes of man but what was base and contemptible."

### Philip Schaff:
1. "With fear and great joy. A natural state of mingled feeling, in view of what they had seen and heard."
2. "Fear at what they had seen, joy at what they had heard, and both mingled because the latter seemed too good to be true."
3. "The same state of mind is indicated in all the accounts."

### Archibald T. Robertson:
1. "With fear and great joy. A touch of life was this as the excited women ran quickly." 
2. "They had the greatest piece of news that it was possible to have. Mark calls it fear and ecstasy. Anything seemed possible now."
3. "But these mingled emotions of ecstasy and dread need cause no surprise when all things are considered."

### John Gill:
1. "a mixture of both these; with fear and dread, because of the vision they had seen, and with joy at the news of Christ's resurrection"
2. "However, between both joy and fear, they set out, and did run to bring his disciples word"
3. "Their fright, as well as their joy, and their regard to the angel's order, might cause them to run, and make the quicker dispatch."
