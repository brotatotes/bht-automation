# Matthew 8:25 Commentary Help Text

## BHT:
The disciples urgently cried out, awakening Jesus with their loud and desperate pleas, calling him Master, Master! Their repeated calls expressed their distress, impatience, and desire for immediate deliverance. They fervently pleaded, saying, Save us, we perish, signifying their deep fear and recognition of their lost and desperate condition.

## Choicest Commentary Quotes:
### Henry Alford:
1. "Una eademque sententia est excitantium Dominum, volentiumque salvari; nec opus est quærere quid horum potius Christo dictum sit."
2. "Sive enim alliquid horum trium dixerint, sive alia verba quæ nullus Evangelistarum commemoravit, tantundem tamen valentia ad eandem sententiæ veritatem, quid ad rem interest?"
3. "Quamquam et hoc fieri potuit, ut pluribus eum simul excitantibus, omnia hæc, aliud ab alio, dicerentur."

### Albert Barnes:
1. "Save us - Save our lives. We perish - We are in danger of perishing."
2. "This showed great confidence in the Savior."
3. "There is none that can save from the storms of divine wrath but the Son of God."

### John Calvin:
1. "Certainly, I have no doubt that they attached too much importance to the bodily presence of their Master."
2. "Is every kind of fear sinful and contrary to faith? First, he does not blame them simply because they fear, but because they are timid."
3. "We ought to be aware that it is not every kind of fear which indicates a want of faith, but only that dread which disturbs the peace of the conscience in such a manner that it does not rest on the promise of God."

### Philip Schaff:
1. Matthew 8:25.  Save, we perish, or, ‘are perishing.’ Disconnected language of anxiety or terror, as in the parallels (Mark 4:33; Luke 8:24).
	

### Archibald T. Robertson:
1.  Save, Lord; we perish  (Κυριε, σωσον, απολλυμεθα). More exactly, "Lord, save us at once (aorist), we are perishing (present linear)." 

### John Gill:
1. "They awoke him by their loud cries, and repeated calls; for in Luke, the form of address is doubled, 'Master, Master!' expressing their distress, importunity, and haste for deliverance." 
2. "Saying, 'save us, we perish,' or 'we are lost': which shows the apprehensions they had of their condition; not only that they were in danger of being lost, or were ready to be lost, but were lost."
