# Philippians 1:4 Commentary Help Text

## BHT:
Paul's heartfelt prayers for the Philippians demonstrate his love and care for them. He consistently remembered them in his prayers, and his joy in doing so is evident. The repetition of the word 'all' emphasizes his equal love for every individual in the church. Prayer was not a duty but a joyful expression of his deep concern for their spiritual growth. Paul's gratitude in his requests to God reflected his recognition of past blessings. This passage encourages believers to approach prayer with the same fervor, remembering others and expressing gratitude as they seek God's presence.

## Choicest Commentary Quotes:
### Henry Alford:
1. "here we have the overflowings of a full heart."
2. "Render always in every prayer of mine making my prayer for you all with joy."
3. "...the sense is, that every time he prayed, he joyfully offered up that portion of his prayers which was an intercession for them."

### Jamieson-Fausset-Brown:
1. "The frequent repetition in this Epistle of 'all' with 'you,' marks that Paul desires to declare his love for all alike, and will not recognize any divisions among them."
2. "Joy gives especial animation to prayers. It marked his high opinion of them, that there was almost everything in them to give him joy, and almost nothing to give him pain."

### Albert Barnes:
1. "Paul labors to show them that he never forgot them; that he always remembered them in his prayers."
2. "The person or object that we remember in every prayer must be very dear to the heart."
3. "He industriously repeats the word 'all,' that he might show that he loved them all equally well, and that he might the more successfully excite them to the manifestation of the same love and benevolence."

### John Calvin:
1. "Always presenting prayer for you all in every prayer of mine."
2. "For as he had said before, that the remembrance of them was an occasion of joy to him, so he now subjoins, that they come into his mind as often as he prays."
3. "Thus it becomes us always to rejoice in the blessings received from God in such a manner, as to remember to ask from him those things that we are still in need of."

### Philip Schaff:
1. "Feeling this, St. Paul comes to God with supplication, that as they now stand, so they may continue, and only change to a greater advancement in grace and holiness."
2. "They are all become his brethren in Christ, and therefore all have a claim, a right, to a share in his prayers."
3. "The joy would have many sources. It would come in part from the retrospect of the history of the Philippian church to which he has just alluded; and in part from the most recent manifestations of their love during his present imprisonment."

### Archibald T. Robertson:
1. "With joy. Keynote of the Epistle."
2. "Paul is a happy prisoner as in Philippi when he and Silas sang praises at midnight though in prison (Acts 16:25)."

### John Gill:
1. "The apostle was a praying believer, and a praying minister: notwithstanding all his gifts, and graces, and high attainments, he was not above the work and duty of prayer"
2. "He was constant and assiduous at the throne of grace, and was concerned for others, as well as himself, for all the churches, and for this church, and all the saints in it."
3. "Requests are to be made known to God with thanksgiving. When we request a favour of him, it becomes us to return thanks for what we have received from him."

### John Wesley:
1. "After the epistle to the Ephesians, wherein love reigns, follows this, wherein there is perpetual mention of joy."
2. "Joy peculiarly enlivens prayer."
3. "The sum of the whole epistle is, I rejoice. Rejoice ye."
