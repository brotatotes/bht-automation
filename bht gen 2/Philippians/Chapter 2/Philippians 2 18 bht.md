# Philippians 2:18 Commentary Help Text

## BHT:
Because Paul is willing to be offered up for the sake of the Gospel, he encourages the Philippians to rejoice and congratulate him instead of grieving. He sees his own death as an honor and gain for both himself and the Philippians. His readiness to pour out his life in service to the Gospel is a cause for celebration and joy, and he invites his friends to join him in this spirit of triumph.

## Choicest Commentary Quotes:
### Henry Alford:
1. "On the same account, do ye rejoice and congratulate me."
2. "The contrast is only in the reciprocity."
3. "This is your honour and my joy."

### Jamieson-Fausset-Brown:
1. 18. "Do ye also rejoice"
	at this honor to you, "and congratulate me" on my blessed
	"gain" (Philippians 1:21).

### Albert Barnes:
1. "Because we are united, and what affects one of us should affect both."
2. "It will be a privilege and a pleasure thus to die."
3. "So Paul felt that there was occasion for him, and for all whom he loved, to rejoice, if he was permitted to die in the cause of others, and in such a manner that his death would benefit the world."

### John Calvin:
1. "By the alacrity which he thus discovers, he encourages the Philippians, and enkindles in them a desire to meet death with firmness."
2. "He declares that it is no ground of sorrow; nay, that they have occasion of joy, inasmuch as they will find it to be productive of advantage."
3. "He lets them know that to himself personally death would be a matter of joy."

### Philip Schaff:
1. "He does not intimate whether the power of Rome, or his own continued toils, shall be the agency employed to pour his life forth." 
2. "But both offerings are subjects for joy." 
3. "He cannot restrain his triumph at the one, and in like manner he bids his friends feel equal joy at the offering which he is making and being strengthened to make unto his Master."

### John Gill:
1. "He would not have them be sorrowful, should they hear of his death for the sake of the Gospel, and of his blood being poured out in such a cause."
2. "They should be so far from indulging grief and sorrow on that account, that they should rather joy and rejoice with him, who was ready to be offered up, or poured out."
3. "Since he had run out his race, and that not in vain, but to so good a purpose, and especially among them."

### John Wesley:
1. Congratulate me — When I am offered up.
