# Revelation 1:4 Commentary Help Text

## BHT:
May the abundant favor and tranquility of God be upon you, encompassing all blessings in this life and in eternity. The eternal and unchanging nature of God is emphasized through His designation as the everlasting One, existing beyond the confines of time. The reference to the seven spirits signifies the Holy Spirit, who though one in essence, manifests Himself in various gifts and graces. This depiction of the Holy Spirit parallels other instances in the book of Revelation, affirming His divine presence and influence.

## Choicest Commentary Quotes:
### Jamieson-Fausset-Brown:
1. "For none but he (supposing the writer an honest man) would thus sign himself nakedly without addition."
2. "These seven represent the universal Church of all times and places."
3. "The seven Spirits which are before his throne—The oldest manuscripts omit 'are.' One in His own essence, manifold in His gracious influences."

### Albert Barnes:
1. "The word 'Asia' is used in quite different senses... That part of Asia which Attalus III, king of Pergamos, gave to the Romans... In the New Testament, usually the southwestern part of Asia Minor..." 
2. "From him who is, and who was, and who is to come - From him who is everlasting - embracing all duration, past, present, and to come."
3. "A third opinion is, that the reference is to seven attending and ministering presence-angels - angels represented as standing before the throne of God."

### Marvin Vincent:
1. "Seven is the number of every grace and benefit bestowed upon Israel; which is thus marked as flowing out of the covenant, and a consequence of it."
2. "Grace - peace. For grace [χαρις] , see on Luke 1:30. Both words are used by Paul in the salutations of all his Epistles, except the three Pastorals."
3. "The whole salutation is given in the name of the Holy Trinity: the Father (Him which is, and was, and is to come), the Spirit (the seven spirits), the Son (Jesus Christ)."

### Archibald T. Robertson:
1. "John is writing, but the revelation is from God and Christ through an angel."
2. "It is evidently on purpose to call attention to the eternity and unchangeableness of God."
3. "A difficult symbolic representation of the Holy Spirit here on a par with God and Christ, a conclusion borne out by the symbolic use of the seven spirits in Revelation 3:1; Revelation 4:5; Revelation 5:6."

### John Gill:
1. "Grace [be] unto you, and peace; which is the common salutation of the apostles in all their epistles, and includes all blessings of grace, and all prosperity, inward and outward." 
2. "The phrases are expressive both of his eternity, he being God from everlasting to everlasting; and of his immutability, he being now what he always was, and will be what he now is, and ever was, without any variableness, or shadow of turning." 
3. "By these seven spirits are intended the Holy Spirit of God, who is one in his person, but his gifts and graces are various; and therefore he is signified by this number, because of the fulness and perfection of them."

### John Wesley:
1. "Grace be unto you, and peace — The favour of God, with all temporal and eternal blessings."
2. "By these seven spirits, not seven created angels, but the Holy Ghost is to be understood."
3. "He is called the seven spirits, not with regard to his essence, which is one, but with regard to his manifold operations."
