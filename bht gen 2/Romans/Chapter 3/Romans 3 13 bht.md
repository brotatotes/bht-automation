# Romans 3:13 Commentary Help Text

## BHT:
The speech of the wicked is likened to an open sepulchre, indicating its destructive and insatiable nature. Their words are like a poison, infecting others and causing harm. The communication of wicked men corrupts good manners and is as dangerous as a venomous serpent. Just as an open grave symbolizes perdition, the mouth of the wicked is filled with treachery and deceit, causing destruction and robbing others of peace and happiness.

## Choicest Commentary Quotes:
### Henry Alford:
1. "The open sepulchre is an emblem of perdition." 
2. "Their throat, as the instrument of their speech, is compared to the open sepulchre."

### Jamieson-Fausset-Brown:
1. "from the sole of the foot even to the head there is no soundness" in us.
2. "What proceeds out of their heart, and finds vent in speech and action through the throat, is like the pestilential breath of an open grave."
3. "That tongue which is man's glory is prostituted to the purposes of deception."
4. "Those lips which should 'drop as honeycomb,' and 'feed many,' and 'give thanks unto His name' are employed to secrete and to dart deadly poison."

### Albert Barnes:
1. "The sentiment here may be, as the grave is ever open to receive all into it, that is, into destruction, so the mouth or the throat of the slanderer is ever open to swallow up the peace and happiness of all."
2. "In their conversation, their promises, etc., they have been false, treacherous, and unfaithful."
3. "The words of the slanderer are deadly, pestiferous, quickly destroying the reputation and happiness of man. They are as subtle, as insinuating, and as deadly to the reputation, as the poison of the adder is to the body."

### Marvin Vincent:
1. "Lit., a sepulchre opened or standing open."
2. "Some explain the figure by the noisome exhalations from a tomb."
3. "Guile is contrasted with violence in the previous clause."

### John Calvin:
1. "Their throat is an open grave; that is, a gulf to swallow up men."
2. "It is an intimation of extreme barbarity, when the throat is said to be so great a gulf, that it is sufficient to swallow down and devour men whole and entire."
3. "Their tongues are deceitful, and the poison of asps is under their lips, import the same thing."

### Philip Schaff:
1. "Their throat is an open sepulchre. The figure is either from the noxious odor, or from the insatiableness of an open grave. In either case, the reference is to the corrupting character of the speech."
2. "They have used deceit. Habitual, continued action is expressed. Hebrew: ‘their tongues they make smooth.’"
3. "The poison of asps, etc. The reference is to the malice which is behind the cunning of their tongues. Perhaps the thought of the poison bag under the serpent’s fangs suggests the figure."

### Archibald T. Robertson:
1. "Their mouth (words) like the odour of a newly opened grave."
2. "Some portions of Greek and Roman literature stink like a newly opened grave."
3. "The poison of the asp lies in a bag under the lips."

### John Gill:
1. "The throat is said to be an open sepulchre, as in Psalms 5:9, so called, for its voracity and insatiableness."
2. "The words of the wicked are devouring ones."
3. "The evil communications of wicked men, as they corrupt good manners, are dangerous and hurtful."

### John Wesley:
1. "Their throat — Is noisome and dangerous as an open sepulchre. Observe the progress of evil discourse, proceeding out of the heart, through the throat, tongue, lips, till the whole mouth is filled therewith."
2. "The poison of asps — Infectious, deadly backbiting, tale-bearing, evil-speaking, is under (for honey is on) their lips."
3. "An asp is a venomous kind of serpent. Psalm 5:9; Psalm 140:3."
