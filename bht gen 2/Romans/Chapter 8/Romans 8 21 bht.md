# Romans 8:21 Commentary Help Text

## BHT:
The creation has a hopeful expectation for the deliverance it will experience. This deliverance is not destruction, but rather a transition into a glorious liberty. This liberty refers to the excellent state in which the creatures were originally created. It is important to understand that deliverance entails being freed from corruption, not from existence itself.

## Choicest Commentary Quotes:
### Jamieson-Fausset-Brown:
1. "Even the creation itself shall be delivered from the bondage of corruption." 
2. "The creation itself shall, in a glorious sense, be delivered into that freedom from debility and decay in which the children of God, when raised up in glory, shall expatiate."
3. "Into this freedom from corruptibility the creation itself shall, in a glorious sense, be delivered."

### Albert Barnes:
1. "It is the purpose of God that deliverance shall be granted, and this supports the Christian amidst the trials to which he is subjected here."
2. "The identical person shall be admitted to heaven without essential change."
3. "The highest 'freedom' in the universe is that which is enjoyed in heaven, where the redeemed are under the sovereignty and government of their king, but where they do that, and that only, which they desire."

### Marvin Vincent:
1. "The creation was subjected in the hope that," etc.
2. "Nature possesses in the feeling of her unmerited suffering, a sort of presentiment of her future deliverance."
3. "Liberty is one of the elements of the glorious state and is dependent upon it."

### John Calvin:
1. "He shows how the creation has in hope been made subject to vanity; that is, inasmuch as it shall some time be made free..."
2. "It is then indeed meet for us to consider what a dreadful curse we have deserved, since all created things in themselves blameless... undergo punishment for our sins."
3. "But he means not that all creatures shall be partakers of the same glory with the sons of God; but that they, according to their nature, shall be participators of a better condition..."

### Philip Schaff:
1. "The phrase ‘the creation itself’ is repeated in contrast with ‘children of God.’ To attribute such a hope to the creation is in accordance with the thought of the entire passage."
2. "The corruption results from the vanity to which the creation was subjected; it is borne ‘not willingly,' and hence is termed ‘bondage.’"
3. "Not only delivered from bondage, but transferred into this freedom, which consists in, or at least results from, a share in the glory of the children of God."

### John Gill:
1. "The earnest expectation of the creature waiteth for the manifestation of the sons of God, in hope that the creature itself also shall be delivered from the bondage of corruption."
2. "They hope for a deliverance... into the glorious liberty of the children of God."
3. "Which designs either the liberty of grace the children of God have here... or the liberty of glory the saints shall enjoy in the other world."

### John Wesley:
1. "The creation itself shall be delivered."
2. "Destruction is not deliverance: therefore whatsoever is destroyed, or ceases to be, is not delivered at all."
3. "Into the glorious liberty - The excellent state wherein they were created."
