# Romans 9:2 Commentary Help Text

## BHT:
The apostle expresses his profound sorrow and anguish that weighs heavily on his heart. It is a grief caused by the unbelief and rejection of his fellow Jews, which he knows will lead to their destruction. Yet, amidst this sorrow, he maintains a sense of spiritual joy, knowing that God’s plan is at work. He wants to assure his readers that his words are not driven by prejudice, but by genuine concern and compassion for the spiritual well-being of his countrymen.

## Choicest Commentary Quotes:
### Jamieson-Fausset-Brown:
1. "That I have great grief (or, sorrow) and unceasing anguish in my heart." 
2. "The bitter hostility of his nation to the glorious Gospel."
3. "The awful consequences of their unbelief, weighing heavily and incessantly upon his spirit."

### Albert Barnes:
1. "Great heaviness - Great grief."
2. "The word rendered 'continual' here must be taken in a popular sense."
3. "The cause of this grief, Paul does not expressly mention, though it is implied in what he immediately says."

### Marvin Vincent:
1. "Heaviness, sorrow."
2. "Some derive it from the root ed eat, as indicating, consuming pain."
3. "Only here and 1 Timothy 6:10, Heart."

### John Calvin:
1. He dexterously manages so to cut short his sentence as not yet to express what he was going to say; for it was not as yet seasonable openly to mention the destruction of the Jewish nation.
2. It may be added, that he thus intimates a greater measure of sorrow, as imperfect sentences are for the most part full of pathos.
3. We hence learn that the obedience we render to God’s providence does not prevent us from grieving at the destruction of lost men, though we know that they are thus doomed by the just judgment of God.

### Philip Schaff:
1. "The cause of this grief obviously is the unbelief of his countrymen, their practical exclusion from the Messianic salvation."
2. "This feeling was respecting those who had for years persecuted him with relentless hatred, and who, shortly after this time occasioned him a long imprisonment."
3. "Thus becoming the immediate cause of his martyrdom."

### Archibald T. Robertson:
1. "We may compare the grief of a Jew writing after the fall of Jerusalem" (Sanday and Headlam).
2. "Unceasing pain in my heart" (αδιαλειπτος οδυνη τη καρδια). Like angina pectoris.
3. "Two rare words together and both here only in N.T. and I and II Timothy (some small argument for the Pauline authorship of the Pastoral Epistles)."

### John Gill:
1. "These two words, 'heaviness' and 'sorrow', the one signifies grief, which had brought on heaviness on his spirits; and the other such pain as a woman in travail feels."
2. "The trouble of his mind expressed by both, is described by its quantity, 'great', it was not a little, but much; by its quality it was internal, it was in his 'heart', it did not lie merely in outward show, in a few words or tears, but was in his heart, it was a heart sorrow."
3. "It was because of the obstinacy of his countrymen the Jews, the hardness of their hearts, and their wilful rejection of the Messiah... their trusting to their own righteousness, to the neglect and contempt of the righteousness of Christ, which he knew must unavoidably issue in their eternal destruction."

### John Wesley:
1. "A high degree of spiritual sorrow and of spiritual Joy may consist together." 
2. "He shows that what he was now about to speak, he did not speak from any prejudice to them."
