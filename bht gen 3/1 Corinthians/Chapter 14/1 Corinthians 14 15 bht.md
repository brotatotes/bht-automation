# 1 Corinthians 14:15 Commentary Help Text

## BHT:
In worship, Paul urges believers to pray and sing with both the spirit and the understanding. He cautions against praying without the use of intellect, emphasizing that prayer should be an exercise of the mind. Similarly, when singing, Paul values the engagement of the intellect alongside stirring emotions. He warns that solos without understanding lose their value in communal worship. Therefore, in order to edify others, believers should strive to pray and sing in a way that appeals to both the spirit and the understanding.

### Albert Barnes:
1. "I deem it of vast importance to pray with the spirit in such a way that the 'heart' and the 'affections' may be engaged."
2. "I will make the appropriate use of the intellect, so that it may convey ideas, and make suitable impressions on the minds of others."
3. "There should be clear enunciation as well as in prayer and preaching, since the design of sacred music in the worship of God is not only to utter praise, but it is to impress the sentiments which are sung on the heart by the aid of musical sounds and expression more deeply than could otherwise be done."

### Marvin Vincent:
1. "The noun yalmov psalm (Ephesians 5:19; Colossians 3:16; 1 Corinthians 14:26), which is etymologically akin to this verb, is used in the New Testament of a religious song in general, having the character of an Old Testament psalm." 
2. "Some think that the verb has here its original signification of singing with an instrument. This is its dominant sense in the Septuagint, and both Basil and Gregory of Nyssa define a psalm as implying instrumental accompaniment." 
3. "The verb is used here in the general sense of singing praise."

### Philip Schaff:
1. "What is it then? 'As the result of all I have said, how am I to act, supposing me thus gifted?'"
2. "I will pray with the spirit, and I will pray with the understanding also: I will sing with the spirit, and I will sing with the understanding also so pray and sing (in the spirit) as that others may get the sense of my utterances."

### Archibald T. Robertson:
1. "Paul is distinctly in favour of the use of the intellect in prayer. Prayer is an intelligent exercise of the mind."
2. "But Paul prefers singing that reaches the intellect as well as stirs the emotions."
3. "Solos that people do not understand lose more than half their value in church worship."

### John Gill:
1. "Shall I not pray with the Spirit at all, because my understanding, or that which I understand, is of no use to others, being not understood by them?"
2. "I will pray with the Spirit; meaning not with the human breath, or spirit only, vocally, with an articulate voice, and distinct sounds, so as to be understood; nor with his own spirit, or in a spiritual way, with a spirit of devotion and fervency, with his whole heart and soul engaged in such service, though this is necessary to it; nor with the common and ordinary assistance of the Spirit of God, though without this prayer cannot be performed aright, with faith and fervency, freedom and boldness; but with the extraordinary gift of the Spirit, so as to pray in an extraordinary manner, with divers tongues, in an unknown language; this, as the apostle was capable of, he determined to use at proper times, and on proper occasions."
3. "I will sing with the understanding also; not to his own understanding, or by or with the understanding of what is sung, though that is absolutely needful; but to the understanding of others, and in a language also which may be understood by others, and in which they could join with him in that service."

### John Wesley:
1. "I will pray with the spirit, but I will pray with the understanding also."
2. "I will use my own understanding, as well as the power of the Spirit."
3. "I will not act so absurdly, as to utter in a congregation what can edify none but myself."
