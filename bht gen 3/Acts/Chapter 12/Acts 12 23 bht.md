# Acts 12:23 Commentary Help Text

## BHT:
The circumstances surrounding the death of Herod Agrippa I illustrate the displeasure of God and the consequences of pride. His death, caused by an angel of God and accompanied by excruciating pain and the infestation of worms, served as a punishment for his arrogance and acceptance of idolatrous worship. This demise, similar to that of Nebuchadnezzar, demonstrates the downfall of those who disregard God's glory and trust in their own strength. The horrifying nature of Herod's death serves as a warning to all, particularly rulers, not to exceed their rightful authority and to humble themselves before God.

### Henry Alford:
1. "The fact may be correctly related by Josephus: but our narrative alleges the cause of what happened to have been the displeasure of God, and the stroke to have been inflicted by His angel." 
2. "He was eaten of worms. Another additional particular; and one to be expected from a physician."
3. "In several cases of deaths by divine judgment we have accounts of this loathsome termination of the disease."

### Albert Barnes:
1. "Because he gave not God the glory - Because he was willing to receive the worship due to God. It was the more sinful in him as he was a Jew, and was acquainted with the true God, and with the evils of idolatry."
2. "Hence, he was judged; and God vindicated his own insulted honor by inflicting severe pains on him, and by a most awful death."
3. "The most proud, and mighty, and magnificent princes have no security of their lives. God can in a moment [...] touch the seat of life, and turn them to loathsomeness and putrefaction."

### Marvin Vincent:

3. "For on her return to Egypt from Libya, directly after taking vengeance on the people of Barca, she was overtaken by a most horrid death. Her body swarmed with worms, which ate her flesh while she was still alive."

### John Calvin:

2. "For God intended to make choice of a kind of punishment wherewith he might repress the cruelty of a proud man with extreme ignominy...he is handled according to his deserts."

### Philip Schaff:
1. "Josephus speaks of violent and torturing pains."

3. "After the death of Herod Agrippa I., Jerusalem was never ruled again by a native prince."

### Archibald T. Robertson:
1. "Like Nebuchadnezzar (Daniel 4:30) pride went before a fall."
2. "He accepted the impious flattery instead of giving God the glory."
3. "Josephus gives further details, one a superstitious sight of an owl sitting on one of the ropes of the awning of the theatre while the people flattered him, an omen of his death to him."

### John Gill:
1. "Because he reproved not the flatterers, nor rejected their impious flattery, but tacitly took that to himself, which belonged to God."
2. "Their tongues swelled and fell upon their navels, and worms came out of their tongues and went into their navels, and out of their navels they went into their tongues."
3. "So that the worms rose up out of the body of this wicked man, and whiles he lived in sorrow and pain, his flesh fell away, and the filthiness of his smell was noisome to all his army."

### John Wesley:
1. "God does not delay to vindicate his injured honour."
2. "An angel of the Lord brought out Peter; an angel smote Herod. Men did not see the instruments in either case. These were only known to the people of God."
