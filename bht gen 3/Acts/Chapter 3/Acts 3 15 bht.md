### Albert Barnes:
1. "The word rendered 'prince' denotes properly 'a military leader or commander.' Hence, in Hebrews 2:10, it is translated 'captain:' 'It became him ... to make the 'Captain of their salvation' perfect through sufferings.'"
2. "In Hebrews 12:2 it is properly rendered author, 'Looking unto Jesus, the author and finisher of our faith.' The word 'author,' or 'giver,' would express the meaning of the word here."
3. "Jesus is here called the Prince of life in contrast with him whom the Jews demanded in his place, Barabbas. He was a murderer, one who had destroyed life, and yet they demanded that he whose character it was to destroy life should be released, and the Author of life be put to death."

### Marvin Vincent:
1. "Ye demanded a murderer, but the Prince of life ye killed."
2. "The Greek brings out by the position of these words what Bengel calls 'the magnificent antithesis' between a murderer and the Prince of life."
3. "Archgov, though sometimes rendered prince, means, primarily, beginning, and thence originator, author."

### Philip Schaff:
1. "Life here, in its highest sense, is intended eternal life."
2. "The words may contain an allusion to the great miracle, which was the immediate cause of the enmity of the rulers to Jesus."
3. "And of this we are witnesses, for we have seen Him risen."

### Archibald T. Robertson:
1. "Peter here draws between their asking for a murderer and killing the Prince (or Author) of life."
2. "Peter pictures Jesus as the source of all life."
3. "But God raised him from the dead in contrast to what they had done."

### John Gill:
1. "And killed the Prince of life... who not only is the living God, and has life in himself."
2. "His death is laid to them because it was at their request, and through their instigation."
3. "Whom God hath raised from the dead...so that they had not their whole will, and all their end."
