# Colossians 4:6 Commentary Help Text

## BHT:
Let your speech always be full of grace, seasoned with salt. Grace, reflecting the grace from above, and salt, symbolizing the vital briskness of the Holy Spirit, should permeate our words and actions. Our conversation should be cheerful without levity and serious without gloom, manifesting fresh and lively spiritual wisdom. Just as salt renders palatable, grace and salt combined create an agreeable and pleasant taste. With grace as the subject matter, our speech should be both useful and beneficial, showcasing piety, understanding, and edification.

## Choicest Commentary Quotes:
### Henry Alford:
1. "Let your speech be always in grace, gracious, and winning favour."
2. "Seasoned with salt, not insipid and void of point, which can do no man any good."
3. "Grace, the grace which is conferred on us from above, and which our words and actions should reflect, and salt, as symbolizing the unction, freshness, and vital briskness which characterizes the Spirit's presence and work in a man."

### Jamieson-Fausset-Brown:
1. "Even the smallest leaf of the believer should be full of the sap of the Holy Spirit."
2. "His conversation should be cheerful without levity, serious without gloom."
3. "The savor of fresh and lively spiritual wisdom and earnestness, excluding all 'corrupt communication,' and also tasteless insipidity."

### Albert Barnes:
1. "It rather means that our conversation should be such as to show that we are governed by the principles of religion, and that there is unfeigned piety in the heart."
2. "If it be not imbued with the spirit of piety, it is flat, insipid, unprofitable, injurious. The spirit of piety will make it what it should be - useful, agreeable, beneficial to mankind."
3. "Be imbued with the spirit of piety, that you may not utter any thing that would be rash and foolish, but be prepared to answer anyone who may question you about your religion in a way that will show that you understand its nature, and that will tend to edification."

### Marvin Vincent:
1. "The metaphor is from the office of salt in rendering palatable."
2. "In Greek and Latin authors, salt was used to express the pungency and wittiness of speech."
3. "Grace and salt are combined... because, mingled with most things, it makes them agreeable and pleasant to the taste."

### John Calvin:

2. "He indirectly prohibits believers from the practice and familiar use of witticisms."
3. "The man who has accustomed himself to caution in his communications will not fall into many absurdities."
