# John 18:31 Commentary Help Text

## BHT:
Pilate's practical shrewdness is shown as he forces the Jews to admit their desire for Jesus' death. By turning the case over to the Sanhedrin, he draws out their admission and exposes their true intentions. The Jews lacked the power of life and death, so they could not carry out their desire for capital punishment, allowing the divine purpose to proceed calmly to its fulfillment.

2. "From the time when Archelaus was deposed...the Jews lost the power of life and death."
3. "There was there a Procurator with full powers, who exercised the right of judicial cognizance. Jerusalem however possessed the privilege of judging all lighter causes...so that none but these reserved cases remained for the Procurator."

### Albert Barnes:
1. "They still, therefore, pressed it on his attention, and refused to obey his injunction to judge him."
2. "The Jews were accustomed to put persons to death still in a popular tumult, but they had not the power to do it in any case in a regular way of justice."
3. "Thus, a tumult might be avoided, and the odium of putting him to death which they expected would fall, not on themselves, but upon Pilate!"

### Marvin Vincent:
1. "Pilate's words display great practical shrewdness in forcing the Jews to commit themselves to the admission that they desired Christ's death."
2. "Take him yourselves, and judge him according to your law."
3. "By our law, he ought to die. But this penalty they could not inflict."

### John Calvin:
1. "This form of condemnation, which they were eager to carry into effect, was at variance with the common law of all nations and with the feelings of mankind." 
2. "Were he in your power, he would instantly be executed, without being heard in his own defense; and, is this the equity of your Law, to condemn a man without any crime?" 
3. "Those who think that the Jews refuse an offer, which Pilate had made to them, are mistaken; but rather, knowing that he had said to them in mockery, Take you him, they reply, 'You would not allow it; and since you are the judge, execute your office.'"

### Philip Schaff:
1. "Pilate will have nothing to do with merely religious squabbles, and he remits the whole matter to the Jews themselves." 
2. "Thus the Jews are compelled to declare their purpose, and their self-confessed humiliation begins."
3. "But, while they are thus degraded, the Divine purpose proceeds calmly to its accomplishment."

### Archibald T. Robertson:
1. "Pilate shrewdly turns the case over to the Sanhedrin in reply to their insolence."
2. "He drew out at once the admission that they wanted the death of Jesus, not a fair trial for him."
3. "Pilate's approval of their purpose to kill him."

### John Gill:
1. "Either ironically, knowing that they did not, or it was not in their power, to judge in capital causes; or seriously, and with some indignation, abhorring such a method of procedure they would have had him gone into, to condemn a man without knowing his crime, and having evidence of it."
2. "Not that they were of such tender consciences, that they could not put him to death, or that they had no law to punish him with death, provided he was guilty; but because judgments in capital cases had ceased among them."
3. "But at this time, their power was not entirely gone; but the true reason of their saying these words is, that they might wholly give up Christ to the Roman power, and throw off the reproach of his death from themselves."

### John Wesley:
1. "The power of inflicting capital punishment had been taken from them that very year."
2. "So the sceptre was departed from Judah, and transferred to the Romans."
