## Choicest Commentary Quotes:
### Henry Alford:
1. "In the economy of God's Providence, his suffering had its place and aim, and this was to bring out the works of God in his being healed by the Redeemer."
2. "De Wette denies this interpretation, and refers the saying merely to the view of our Lord to bring out his own practical design, to make use of this man to prove His divine power."
3. "The choice of the word implies attention and habit."

### Jamieson-Fausset-Brown:
1. "Is not this he that sat and begged—Here are a number of details to identify the newly seeing with the long-known blind beggar."


### Albert Barnes:
1. "The number of persons totally blind in any community is very small, and it is possible that this was the only blind beggar in Jerusalem."
2. "The case was one, therefore, likely to attract attention, and one where there could be no imposture, as he was generally known."
3. "The neighbours... This man seems to have been one who attracted considerable attention."

### Marvin Vincent:
1. Blind. The best texts substitute prosaithv, a beggar. 
That sat and begged [ο καθημενος και προσαιτων] . Literally, the one sitting and begging. Denoting something customary.


### John Calvin:
1. "This circumstance — of the man being known — contributed to make many people acquainted with the fame of the miracle."
2. "But, as impiety is ingenious in obscuring the works of God, many thought that it was not the same man, because a new power of God openly appeared in him."
3. "But the doubts of those men aided in proving the miracle, for, in consequence of those doubts, the blind man celebrated more highly the grace of Christ by his testimony."

### Philip Schaff:
1. "The fact that he was a beggar has not been mentioned before."
2. "Stress is laid on it here rather than on his blindness..."
3. "...because it was from his frequenting the spot for the purpose of begging that he had become well known."

### Archibald T. Robertson:
1. "Neighbours (γειτονες). From γη (land), of the same land, old word."
2. "Saw him (θεωρουντες). Present active participle of θεωρεω, who used to observe him."
3. "Is not this he that sat and begged? (Ουχ ουτος εστιν ο καθημενος κα προσαιτων?)"

### John Gill:
1. "the blind man was not a stranger, one that came out of the country to the city to beg; but a native of Jerusalem, that had long lived in a certain neighbourhood in it, and was well known to be what he was." 
2. "they particularly remark his begging posture; he was not laid all along, as the lame man in Acts 3:2; nor did he go from door to door, as others were used to do, but he sat in some certain place, as blind men generally did."
3. "is this not he that sat and begged?"
