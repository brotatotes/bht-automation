## BHT:
Jesus assures his disciples not to fear those who can harm their bodies but not their souls. He calls them my friends to emphasize their close bond. He urges them to be courageous in speaking the truth and not be influenced by fear or intimidation. He assures them that while their enemies may have power to kill, that power ends there; the soul remains untouched.

### Jamieson-Fausset-Brown:
1. "Be it so but, 'My friends, there their power ends.'" 
2. "He calls them 'my friends' here, not in any loose sense."
3. "He and they were going to be affectingly one with each other."

### Marvin Vincent:
1. "Unto you, the friends of me."
2. "Be not afraid... from the hands of."

### Archibald T. Robertson:
1. "Unto you my friends (υμιν τοις φιλοις). As opposed to the Pharisees and lawyers in Luke 11:43; Luke 11:46; Luke 11:53."
2. "Be not afraid of (μη φοβηθητε απο). First aorist passive subjunctive with μη, ingressive aorist, do not become afraid of, with απο and the ablative like the Hebrew min and the English 'be afraid of.'"
3. "Have no more that they can do (μη εχοντων περισσοτερον τ ποιησα). Luke often uses the infinitive thus with εχω, a classic idiom."

### John Gill:
1. "Be not afraid of them that kill the body; and after that have no more that they can do."
2. "He would have them know them, and avoid their hypocrisy, and guard against it; but not fear them."
3. "The soul is out of their reach, is an immortal spirit, and cannot be hurt or destroyed by them."

### John Wesley:
1. But I say to you, Fear not — Let not the fear of man make you act the hypocrite, or conceal any thing which I have commissioned you to publish.
