# Matthew 19:24 Commentary Help Text

## BHT:
The saying of Jesus about a camel passing through the eye of a needle is meant to illustrate the greatest human impossibility and to magnify divine grace. Although some have attempted to explain it away, Jesus points out the impossibility, comparing it to an eastern proverb. This proverb was widely used in the Jewish and other eastern cultures to signify difficulties or impossibilities. Thus, Jesus is emphasizing the insurmountable challenge for a rich man to enter the kingdom of God under his own power.

## Choicest Commentary Quotes:
### Henry Alford:
1. "The object here was to set forth the greatest human impossibility."
2. "To magnify divine grace, which could accomplish even that."
3. "Lightfoot brings instances from the Talmud of similar proverbial expressions."

### Marvin Vincent:
1. "The reason why the camel was substituted for the elephant was because the proverb was from the Babylonian Talmud, and in Babylon the elephant was common, while in Palestine it was unknown."
2. "The Koran has the same figure: 'The impious shall find the gates of heaven shut; nor shall he enter there till a camel shall pass through the eye of a needle.'"
3. "A needle's eye is not too narrow for two friends, nor is the world wide enough for two enemies."

### Philip Schaff:
1. "A strong declaration of impossibility (comp. Matthew 19:26)."
2. "The literal sense is not too strong, as both the context and abundant facts show."
3. "The camel was more familiar to the hearers of the Saviour than the elephant, and on account of the hump on its back, it was especially adapted to symbolize earthly wealth as a heavy load and serious impediment to entrance through the narrow gate of the kingdom of heaven."

### Archibald T. Robertson:
1. "Jesus, of course, means by this comparison, whether an eastern proverb or not, to express the impossible."
2. "All these [efforts to explain it away] are hopeless, for Jesus pointedly calls the thing 'impossible.'" 
3. "The Jews in the Babylonian Talmud did have a proverb that a man even in his dreams did not see an elephant pass through the eye of a needle."

### John Gill:
1. "it is easier for a camel to go through the eye of a needle, than for a rich man to enter into the kingdom of God."
2. "Thus, when the Jews would express anything that was rare and unusual, difficult and impossible, they used a like saying with this."
3. "And not only among the Jews, but in other eastern nations, this proverbial way of speaking was used, to signify difficulties or impossibilities."

### John Wesley:
1. "It is easier for a camel to go through the eye of a needle, (a proverbial expression,) than for a rich man to go through the strait gate."
2. "Rich man! tremble! feel this impossibility; else thou art lost for ever!"
3. "Humanly speaking, it is an absolute impossibility."
