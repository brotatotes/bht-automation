### Henry Alford:
1. "Cautions them, referring perhaps especially to Judas, but with a view to all."
2. "Many first should be last, and last first."

### John Calvin:
1. "And many that are first shall be last. This sentence was added in order to shake off the indolence of the flesh."
2. "But Christ exhorts those who have begun well to vigorous perseverance, and at the same time gives warning, that it will be of no avail to runners to have begun with alacrity, if they lose courage in the midst of the course."
3. "As often, therefore, as we call to mind the heavenly crown, we ought, as it were, to feel the application of fresh spurs, that we may not be more indolent for the future."

### Philip Schaff:
1. "But many shall be last that are first,; and first that are last."
2. "A general truth in proverbial form; here a caution against trusting to appearances or to the permanence of present circumstances and conditions."
3. "The promise must be accompanied by a caution, especially in view of the coming apostasy of Judas."

### Archibald T. Robertson:
1. "This paradoxical enigma... refers to ranks in the kingdom."
2. "There are many other possible applications."
3. "The following parable illustrates it."

### John Gill:
1. "But many that are first shall be last... and the last shall be first." 
2. "The apostles, who were last in this world, being poor, mean, and abject, should be the first in the other."
3. "The Jews, who were first in outward privileges, would be rejected of God for their unbelief, and contempt of the Messiah; and the Gentiles, who were last called, should be first, or chief, in embracing the Messiah, professing his Gospel, and supporting his interest."

### John Wesley:
1. "Many of those who were first called, shall be last - Shall have the lowest reward: those who came after them being preferred before them."
2. "Possibly both the first and the last may be saved, though with different degrees of glory."
3. "Though with different degrees of glory."
