### Albert Barnes:
1. "He who should be on the house-top when this calamity came upon the city 'should flee without delay; He should not even take time to secure any article of apparel from his house."

### Marvin Vincent:
1. "From roof to roof there might be a regular communication, called by the Rabbis 'the road of the roofs.'" 
2. "Thus a person could make his escape passing from roof to roof, till, at the last house, he would descend the stairs on the outside of the house, but within the exterior court." 
3. "The urgency of the flight is enhanced by the fact that the stairs lead into this court. 'Though you must pass by the very door of your room, do not enter to take anything out. Escape for your life.'"

### Philip Schaff:
1. "On the house-top. The flat roofs of eastern dwellings were a favorite place of resort."
2. "What is distinctly forbidden is to go down to take the things out of his house."
3. "Extreme haste is enjoined; and being hindered by motives of selfishness or convenience is prohibited."

### Archibald T. Robertson:
1. "They could escape from roof to roof and so escape, 'the road of the roofs,' as the rabbis called it."
2. "There was need for haste."

### John Gill:
1. "Let him not come down to take anything out of his house."
2. "They had two ways of going out of, and into their houses."
3. "Let him not go into his house to take any of his goods, or money, or food along with him necessary for his sustenance in his flight."

### John Wesley:
1. "Let not him that is on the house top come down to take any thing out of his house."
2. "It may be remembered that their stairs used to be on the outside of their houses."
