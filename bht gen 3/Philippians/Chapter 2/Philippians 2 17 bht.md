# Philippians 2:17 Commentary Help Text

## BHT:
Paul considers his apostolic work a sacrifice, ready to pour out his blood as a libation upon it. He sees his service to the Philippians as an honor and a boast, rejoicing at the opportunity to be poured out for their faith. Paul's life-blood symbolizes the sacrifice of the Philippians' faith offered to God. His willingness to give his life for the cause of Christ exemplifies his selflessness, inspiring others to similarly dedicate themselves.

### Henry Alford:
1. "He represents his whole apostolic work for the faith of the Philippians, as a sacrifice: if he is put to death in the course of it, he will be, by the shedding of his blood, poured out as a libation upon this sacrifice."
2. "Your faith is the sacrifice, which I, as a priest, offer to God. The image is precisely as in Rom. xv. 16, where he is the priest, offering up the Gentiles to God."
3. "I rejoice for myself and congratulate you on the fact that I have been thus poured out for your faith, which would be an honour and a boast for you."

### Jamieson-Fausset-Brown:
1. "He had assumed the possibility of his being found alive at Christ's coming... he here puts a supposition which he regards as more likely, namely, his own death before Christ's coming."
2. "I am made a libation. I am poured out... He represents his Philippian converts, offered through faith, as the sacrifice, and his blood as the libation 'poured upon' it."
3. "I congratulate you all... on the honor occurring to you by my blood being poured out on the sacrifice of your faith."

### Albert Barnes:
1. "He had labored for their salvation. He had exposed himself to peril that they and others might have the gospel. On their account he had suffered much... Yet he says that, even if this should happen, he would not regret it, but it would be a source of joy." 
2. "Paul may have regarded himself as a victim prepared for the sacrifice... Paul...might be called to pour out his blood, or to offer up his life as if he were a sacrifice, or an offering to God."
3. "If, in order to render that offering what it should be - to make it as complete and acceptable to God as possible - it were necessary for him to die, pouring out his blood, and strength, and life...he would not refuse to do it, but would rejoice in the opportunity."
