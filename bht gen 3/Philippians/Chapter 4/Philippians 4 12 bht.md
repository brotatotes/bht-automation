## BHT:

### Henry Alford:
1. "I know also how to abound...in every thing...I have been taught the lesson both to be satiated and to hunger, both to abound and to be in need."
2. "I know...how to be brought low...I know also how to abound...in all things I have been taught the lesson both to be satiated and to hunger, both to abound and to be in need."
3. "I have been taught the lesson both to be satiated and to hunger, both to abound and to be in need."

### Jamieson-Fausset-Brown:
1. "abased—in low circumstances"
2. "everywhere—rather, 'in each, and in all things'"
3. "instructed—in the secret"

### Albert Barnes:
1. "Adversity, of itself, does something to keep the mind in a right state; prosperity does nothing."
2. "Paul says that he had been initiated into the lessons taught by trials and by prosperity. The secret and important lessons which these schools of adversity are fitted to teach, he had had an ample opportunity of learning; and he had faithfully embraced the doctrines thus taught."
3. "Hence, new grace is imparted for this new form of trial, and new traits of Christian character are developed in these rapid transitions in life."

### Marvin Vincent:
1. "I have learned the secret."
2. "The metaphor is from the initiatory rites of the pagan mysteries."
3. "To be full."

### John Calvin:
1. "I know, says he,to be abased — that is, to endure abasement with patience." 
2. "If a man knows to make use of present abundance in a sober and temperate manner, with thanksgiving, prepared to part with everything whenever it may be the good pleasure of the Lord, giving also a share to his brother, according to the measure of his ability, and is also not puffed up, that man has learned to excel, and to abound."
3. "Let all who wish to be Christ’s disciples exercise themselves in acquiring this knowledge which was possessed by Paul, but in the mean time let them accustom themselves to the endurance of poverty in such a manner that it will not be grievous and burdensome to them when they come to be deprived of their riches."

### Philip Schaff:
1. "I know how to be abased... and he employs the same expression where he is speaking to the Corinthians of preaching the Gospel without being chargeable unto them. 'Did I commit a sin in abasing myself... because I preached to you the Gospel of God for nought?'"
2. "In what way to use abundance, when it comes to me, as now, in such wise as to glorify God thereby."
3. "In each particular state into which I may be brought, and the changes in my life have been so various that I may say I have known all states; in each and all I have learned the secret."

### Archibald T. Robertson:
1. "I know how (οιδα). Followed by the infinitive οιδα has this sense. So here twice, with ταπεινουσθα, to be humbled, from ταπεινος, and with περισσευειν, to overflow."
2. "Have I learned the secret (μεμυημα). Perfect passive indicative of μυεω, old and common word from μυω, to close (Latin mutus), and so to initiate with secret rites, here only in N.T. The common word μυστηριον (mystery) is from μυστης (one initiated) and this from μυεω, to initiate, to instruct in secrets. Paul draws this metaphor from the initiatory rites of the pagan mystery-religions."
3. "To be filled (χορταζεσθα). Old verb from χορτος (grass, hay) and so to fatten like an animal. To be hungry (πειναιν). Old verb from πεινα (hunger) and kin to πενης, poor man who has to work for his living (πενομα)."

### John Gill:
1. "I know how to be abased... and I know how to abound... every where... and in all things... I am instructed."
2. "I know how to behave under all this; not to be depressed and cast down, or to fret, repine, and murmur."
3. "I am initiated, as he was by the Gospel; and, ever since he embraced it, was taught this lesson of contentment, and inured to the exercise of it."

### John Wesley:
1. "I know how to be abased... and to abound." 
2. "I am initiated in that mystery, unknown to all but Christians." 
3. "Both to be full and to be hungry... both to abound and to want."
