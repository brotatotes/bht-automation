# Revelation 18:22 Commentary Help Text

## BHT:
The once lively city shall no longer resonate with the melodies of harpers and musicians, the skillful craftsmanship of artisans, or the grinding sound of the millstone. These sounds, intertwined with the corrupt practices of a fallen Christendom, have been desecrated. The absence of music, art, and skilled labor in this desolate state symbolizes utter decay. Rome's impending destruction will result in a famine, leaving no inhabitants to even partake in the grinding of corn, signifying the end of rich feasts and luxurious living.

### Henry Alford:
1. "The sound of harpers and musicians and flute-players shall never be heard in thee any more."
2. "Every artisan of every art shall never be found in thee any more."
3. "The sound of the millstone shall never be heard in thee any more."

### Jamieson-Fausset-Brown:

	Christendom.

### Albert Barnes:
1. "Of course, in a great and 'fun' city, there would be all kinds of music; and when it is said that this would be heard there no more it is a most striking image of utter desolation."
2. "The general idea is, that the sound of music, as an indication of festivity and joy, would cease."
3. "The word used here - τεχνίτης technitēs - would include all artisans or mechanics, all who were engaged in any kind of trade or craft. The meaning here is, that all these would disappear, an image, of course, of utter decay."

### Marvin Vincent:
1. "The primitive education at Athens consisted of two branches: gymnastics for the body, music for the mind."
2. "During the middle of the fifth century B.C. at Athens, there came thus to be found among the musical teachers men of the most distinguished abilities and eminence."
3. "The female flute-players, usually dissolute characters, were indispensable attendants at the Greek banquets."

### Archibald T. Robertson:
1. "The voice (φωνη). Cf. Ezekiel 26:13. Or 'sound' as in 1 Corinthians 14:8 with σαλπιγξ (trumpet). For this song of judgment see Jeremiah 25:10."
2. "Of flute-players (αυλητων). Old word (from αυλεω, to play on a flute, Matthew 11:17, αυλος, flute, 1 Corinthians 14:7), in N.T. only here and Matthew 9:23."
3. "Shall be heard no more at all (ου μη ακουσθη). First aorist passive subjunctive of ακουω with the double negative as below, with φωνη μυλου (sound of the millstone),... and as in verse Revelation 18:21 with ου μη ευρεθη and again with πας τεχνιτης (craftsman)."

### John Gill:
1. "There will be no more bells, nor organs, or any other instruments of music; no more chanters, and sub-chanters, choristers, singing men and boys." 
2. "There will be a famine at the time that Rome is besieged... and after it is destroyed, there will be no corn to grind, nor inhabitants to eat it, and so no use of the millstone."
3. "This may also refer to feasts and rich entertainments, for which spices were ground and prepared by a hand mill in the house; and so may signify here that there would be no more of such entertainments and rich living."

### John Wesley:
1. "No artificer — Arts of every kind, particularly music, sculpture, painting, and statuary, were there carried to their greatest height. No, nor even the sound of a mill-stone shall be heard any more in thee."
2. "All these expressions denote absolute and eternal desolation."
3. "The desolation of Rome is therefore described in such a manner, as to show that neither rich nor poor, neither persons of middle rank, nor those of the lowest condition, should be able to live there any more."
