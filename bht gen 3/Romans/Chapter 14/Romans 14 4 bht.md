# Romans 14:4 Commentary Help Text

## BHT:
Who art thou that judgest another man's servant? He shall be made to stand, for God is able to make him stand. The Apostle Paul addresses the weak who harshly judge the strong, doubting their faithfulness to God in the face of temptation. However, Paul reminds them that the power to judge belongs to Christ alone, as the strong are His servants. The Lord's mighty strength is sufficient to uphold them, keeping them firm in their faith and standing in the true fellowship of the Church despite any criticisms.

### Henry Alford:
1. "Who art thou that judgest the servant of another? [...] But he shall be made to stand, for the Lord is able to make him stand." 
2. "This argument is entirely directed to the weak, who uncharitably judges the strong. The weak imagines that the strong cannot be a true servant of God, nor retain his faith amidst such temptation."
3. "To this the Apostle answers, (1) that such judgment belongs only to Christ, whose servant he is: (2) that the Lord's almighty Power is able to keep him up, and will do so."

### Albert Barnes:
1. "That judgest ... - This is a principle of common sense and common propriety. It is not ours to sit in judgment on the servant of another man." 
2. "To his own master - The servant is responsible to his master only. So it is with the Christian in regard to God."
3. "Yea, he shall be holden up - This is spoken of the Christian only...God has power to make him stand; to hold him; to keep him from error, and from condemnation."

### Marvin Vincent:
1. "Who art thou? Thou, first in the Greek order and peculiarly emphatic."
2. "He shall be holden up. Rev., shall be made to stand; better, both because the rendering is more truthful, and because it corresponds with the kindred verb stand - he standeth, make him stand."
3. "Is able. Stronger than dunatai can. The sense is, is mighty. Hence Rev., hath power."

### John Calvin:
1. "It belongs not to you to prescribe to him what to do and what not to do, nor is it necessary for him to live according to your law."
2. "We ought to leave the man, whatever he may be, to the judgment of God; but as to his deeds we may indeed form a decisive opinion, though not according to our own views, but according to the word of God."
3. "He not only bids us to abstain from condemning, but also exhorts us to mercy and kindness, so as ever to hope well of him, in whom we perceive anything of God."
