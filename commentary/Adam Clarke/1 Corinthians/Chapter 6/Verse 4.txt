<p>Verse <span class="scriptRef" com="acc" lang="eng" ref="1co+6:4">1 Corinthians 6:4</span>. <i><b>Things pertaining to this life</b></i> — They could examine all civil cases among themselves, which they were permitted to determine without any hinderance from the heathen governments under which they lived.</p>
<p><i><b>Who are least esteemed in the Church.</b></i> — τους εξουθενημενους, Those who were in the <i>lowest order of judges</i>; for the apostle may refer here to the <i>order</i> in the Jewish benches, as Dr. Lightfoot conjectures, of which there were <i>five</i>, viz:-</p>
<p>1. The great <i>Sanhedrin</i>, consisting of seventy-two elders, which presided in Jerusalem.</p>
<p>2. The little <i>Sanhedrin</i> of twenty-five, in large cities, out of Jerusalem.</p>
<p>3. The Bench of <i>Three</i> in every synagogue.</p>
<p>4. The <i>Authorized</i>, or <i>Authentic</i> Bench.</p>
<p>5. The Bench <i>not authorized</i>, εξουθενημενος. This latter bench was so called because it received not its authority <i>immediately</i> from the <i>Sanhedrin</i>, but was chosen by the parties between whom the controversy depended. The apostle certainly does not mean persons of <i>no repute</i>, but such as these <i>arbitrators</i>, who were chosen for the purpose of settling private differences, and preventing them from going before the regular magistrates.
