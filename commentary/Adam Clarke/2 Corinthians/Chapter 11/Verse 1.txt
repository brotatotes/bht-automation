<p>Verse <span class="scriptRef" com="acc" lang="eng" ref="2co+11:1">2 Corinthians 11:1</span>. <i><b>Would to God ye could bear with me</b></i> — οφελον ηνειχεσθε μου μικρον. As the word <i>God</i> is not mentioned here, it would have been much better to have translated the passage literally thus: <i>I</i> <i>wish ye could bear a little with me</i>. The too frequent use of this sacred name produces a <i>familiarity</i> with it that is not at all conducive to <i>reverence</i> and <i>godly fear</i>.</p>
<p><i><b>In</b></i><b> my </b><i><b>folly</b></i> — In my <i>seeming</i> folly; for, being obliged to vindicate his ministry, it was necessary that he should speak much of himself, his sufferings, and his success. And as this would appear like <i>boasting</i>; and boasting is always the effect of an <i>empty, foolish mind</i>; those who were not acquainted with the <i>necessity</i> that lay upon him to make this defence, might be led to impute it to <i>vanity</i>. As if he had said: Suppose you allow this to be <i>folly</i>, have the goodness to bear with me; <i>for though I glory, I</i> <i>should not be a fool</i>, <span class="scriptRef" com="acc" lang="eng" ref="2co+12:6">2 Corinthians 12:6</span>. <i>And let no man think me a fool for my boasting</i>, <span class="scriptRef" com="acc" lang="eng" ref="2co+11:16">2 Corinthians 11:16</span>.</p>
