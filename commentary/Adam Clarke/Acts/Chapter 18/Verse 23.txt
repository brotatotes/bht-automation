<p>Verse <span class="scriptRef" com="acc" lang="eng" ref="ac+18:23">Acts 18:23</span>. <i><b>Went over</b></i><b> all </b><i><b>the country of Galatia and Phrygia</b></i> — Both were provinces of Asia Minor: <span class="scriptRef" com="acc" lang="eng" ref="ac+2:10">Acts 2:10</span>.</p>
<p><i><b>In order</b></i> — καθεξης, A word peculiar to St. Luke; see his Gospel, <span class="scriptRef" com="acc" lang="eng" ref="lu+1:3">Luke 1:3</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="acc" lang="eng" ref="lu+8:1">Luke 8:1</span>; and his history of the Acts, <span class="scriptRef" com="acc" lang="eng" ref="ac+3:24">Acts 3:24</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="acc" lang="eng" ref="ac+11:4">Acts 11:4</span>, and the place above; the only places where this word occurs in the New Testament. It properly signifies, <i>in order, distinctly,</i> <i>particularly</i>; from κατα, <i>according to</i>, and εξη, <i>order</i>, as opposed to <i>confusion</i>, indistinctness, c. If St. Paul went up to Jerusalem at this time, which we are left to <i>infer</i>, for Luke has not expressed it, (<span class="scriptRef" com="acc" lang="eng" ref="ac+18:22">Acts 18:22</span>,) it was his <i>fourth</i> journey thither and this is generally supposed to have been the twenty-first year after his conversion. His <i>first</i> journey is mentioned <span class="scriptRef" com="acc" lang="eng" ref="ac+9:26">Acts 9:26</span>; his <i>second</i>, <span class="scriptRef" com="acc" lang="eng" ref="ac+11:30">Acts 11:30</span>; his <i>third</i>, <span class="scriptRef" com="acc" lang="eng" ref="ac+15:4">Acts 15:4</span>; and his <i>fourth</i>, <span class="scriptRef" com="acc" lang="eng" ref="ac+18:22">Acts 18:22</span>, the place above.</p>
