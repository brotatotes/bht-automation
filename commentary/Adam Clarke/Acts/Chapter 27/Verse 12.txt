<p>Verse <span class="scriptRef" com="acc" lang="eng" ref="ac+27:12">Acts 27:12</span>. <i><b>Might attain to Phoenice</b></i> — It appears that the <i>Fair</i> <i>Havens</i> were at the <i>eastern</i> end of the island, and they wished to reach <i>Phoenice</i>, which lay farther towards the <i>west</i>.</p>
<p><i><b>Toward the south-west and north-west.</b></i> — κατα λιβα και κατα χωρον. The <i>libs</i> certainly means the <i>south-west</i>, called <i>libs</i>, from <i>Libya</i>, from which it blows to. wards the AEgean Sea. The <i>chorus</i>, or <i>caurus</i>, means a <i>north-west</i> wind. Virgil mentions this, Geor. iii. ver. 356. </p>
<p style="margin-left: 0.9em; "> <i>Semper hyems, semper spirantes frigora</i> cauri.</p>
<p style="margin-left: 0.9em; "> </p>
<p style="margin-left: 0.9em; "> "It is always <i>winter</i>; and the <i>cauri</i>, the <i>north-westers</i>,</p>
<p style="margin-left: 0.9em; "> ever blowing <i>cold</i>."</p>
<p style="margin-left: 0.9em; "><br/> </p>
<p style="margin-left: 0.9em; "> Dr. Shaw lays down this, and other winds, in a Greek compass, on his map, in which he represents the drifting of St. Paul's vessel from Crete, till it was wrecked at the island of Melita. Travels, p. 331, 4to. edit.</p>
