<p>Verse 29. <i><b>Let no corrupt communication</b></i> — πασ λογος σαπρος. <i>Kypke</i> observes that λογος σαπρος signifies a <i>useless, putrid,</i> <i>unsavoury</i>, and <i>obscene word</i> or <i>conversation</i>.</p>
<p>1. <i>Useless</i>, particularly that which has been rendered so by old age and corruption.</p>
<p>2. <i>Putrid, impure</i>; so Aristophanes in Lysistrat., p. 859, calls a <i>bad woman</i> σαπραεμοισυλουτρονωσαπρα. <i>Tune</i>, <i>Spurca! balneum mihi parabis</i>?</p>
<p>3. <i>Calumnious</i>, or <i>reproachful</i>; whatever has a tendency to <i>injure</i> the <i>name, fame</i>, or <i>interest</i> of another.</p>
<p>In short, it appears to mean any word or thing <i>obscene</i>, any thing that <i>injures virtue, countenances vice</i>, or <i>scoffs</i> at <i>religion</i>. In the parallel place, <span class="scriptRef" com="acc" lang="eng" ref="col+4:6">Colossians 4:6</span>, the apostle exhorts that our speech may be <i>seasoned with salt</i>, to preserve it from <i>putrefaction</i>. See <i>Kypke</i> and <i>Macknight</i>.</p>
<p><i><b>But that which is good to the use of edifying</b></i> — To be <i>good for a</i> <i>thing</i> is a <i>Graecism</i>, as well as an <i>Anglicism</i>, for, to be <i>fit,</i> <i>proper, suitable</i>, c. so <i>Achilles Tatius</i>, lib. iv. p. 231: αγαθον εις φιλιαν οιδα σε. <i>I know thee to be good</i> (formed) <i>for</i> <i>friendship</i>. And <i>Appian</i>, de <i>Bell. Hisp</i>., p. 439, terms both the Scipios, ανδρας ες παντα αγαθους γενομενουχ, <i>men who were good</i> (suitable) <i>for all things</i>. And also <i>Lucian</i>, in <i>Toxari</i>, p. 53: ου μονον αρα τοξευειν αγαθοι ησαν σκυθαι. <i>The Scythians were not</i> <i>good</i> (expert) <i>in archery only</i>. See <i>Kypke</i>, from whom I quote.</p>
<p><i><b>That it may minister grace</b></i> — ινα δω χαριν. This may be understood thus:</p>
<p>1. Let your conversation be pure, wise, and holy, that it may he the means of conveying grace, or Divine influences, to them that hear.</p>
<p>2. Let it be such as to be <i>grateful</i> or <i>acceptable to the hearers</i>. This is the meaning of ινα δω χαριν in some of the most correct Greek writers. Never wound modesty, truth, or religion with your discourse; endeavour to <i>edify</i> those with whom you converse; and if possible, speak so as to <i>please</i> them.</p>
