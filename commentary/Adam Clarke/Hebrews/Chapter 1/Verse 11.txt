<p>Verse <span class="scriptRef" com="acc" lang="eng" ref="heb+1:11">Hebrews 1:11</span>. <i><b>They shall perish</b></i> — Permanently fixed as they seem to be, a time shall come when they shall be <i>dissolved</i>, and afterward <i>new heavens</i> and a <i>new earth</i> be formed, in which righteousness alone shall dwell. See <span class="scriptRef" com="acc" lang="eng" ref="1pe+3:10-13">1 Peter 3:10-13</span>.</p>
<p><i><b>Shall wax old as doth a garment</b></i> — As a garment by long using becomes unfit to be longer used, so shall all visible things; they shall <i>wear old</i>, and <i>wear out</i>; and hence the necessity of their being <i>renewed</i>. It is remarkable that our word <i>world</i> is a contraction of <i>wear old</i>; a term by which our ancestors expressed the sentiment contained in this verse. That the word was thus compounded, and that it had this sense in our language, may be proved from the most competent and indisputable witnesses. It was formerly written [Anglo-Saxon] <i>weorold</i>, and [Anglo-Saxon] <i>wereld</i>. This <i>etymology</i> is finely alluded to by our excellent poet, <i>Spencer</i>, when describing the primitive age of innocence, succeeded by the age of depravity: - </p>
<p><i><b>Thou remainest</b></i> — Instead of διαμενεις, some good MSS. read διαμενεις, the first, without the circumflex, being the present tense of the indicative mood; the latter, with the circumflex, being the <i>future-thou shalt remain</i>. The difference between these two readings is of little importance.</p>
