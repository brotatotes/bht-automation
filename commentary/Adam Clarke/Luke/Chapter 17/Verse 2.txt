<p>Verse <span class="scriptRef" com="acc" lang="eng" ref="lu+17:2">Luke 17:2</span>. <i><b>A mill-stone</b></i> — That drowning a person with a stone tied about the neck was an ancient mode of punishment, see proved in the note on <span class="scriptRef" com="acc" lang="eng" ref="mt+18:6">Matthew 18:6</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="acc" lang="eng" ref="mt+18:7">Matthew 18:7</span>, to which let the following be added. To have a <i>mill-stone hanged about the neck</i>, was a common proverb. "Samuel saith, A man may marry, and after that addict himself to the study of the law. Rab. Jochanan saith, No: shall he addict himself to the study of the law with a mill-stone about his neck?"</p>
<p>The place in Aristophanes, to which the reader is referred in the note on <span class="scriptRef" com="acc" lang="eng" ref="mt+18:6">Matthew 18:6</span>, is the following: -</p>
<p style="margin-left: 0.9em; "> Αραν μετεωρον εις το βαραθρον εμβαλω,</p>
<p style="margin-left: 0.9em; "> Εκ του λαρυγγος εκκρεμασας ὑπερβολον</p>
<p style="margin-left: 0.9em; "><br/> </p>
<p>"Lifting him up into the air, I will plunge him into the deep: a <i>great stone</i> being hung about his neck." Aristoph. in Equit. ver. 1359.</p>
