<p>Verse <span class="scriptRef" com="acc" lang="eng" ref="lu+17:6">Luke 17:6</span>. <i><b>As a grain of mustard seed</b></i> — A faith that <i>increases</i> and <i>thrives</i> as that is described to do, <span class="scriptRef" com="acc" lang="eng" ref="mt+13:32">Matthew 13:32</span>, where see the note. See also <span class="scriptRef" com="acc" lang="eng" ref="mt+17:20">Matthew 17:20</span>.</p>
<p><i><b>This sycamine</b></i> — The words seem to intimate that they were standing by such a tree. The <i>sycamine</i> is probably the same as the <i>sycamore</i>. Sycamore with us, says Mr. <i>Evelyn</i>, is falsely so called, being our <i>acer majus</i>, greater maple. The true <i>sycamore</i> is the <i>ficus Pharaonis</i> or <i>AEgyptia, Pharaoh's</i>, or <i>Egyptian fig-tree</i>; called also, from its similitude in leaves and fruit, <i>morosyous</i>, or mulberry fig-tree. The Arabians call it <i>guimez</i>: it grows in Cyprus, Caria, Rhodes, and in Judea and Galilee, where our Lord at this time was: see <span class="scriptRef" com="acc" lang="eng" ref="lu+17:11">Luke 17:11</span>. St. Jerome, who was well acquainted with these countries, translates the word <i>mulberry-tree</i>.</p>
<p><i><b>Be thou plucked up by the root</b></i> — <span class="_0000ff"><span class="scriptRef" com="acc" lang="eng" ref="mt+21:21">Matthew 21:21</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="acc" lang="eng" ref="mt+21:21">Matthew 21:21</span></span>, where it is shown that this mode of speech refers to the accomplishment of things very difficult, but not impossible.</p>
