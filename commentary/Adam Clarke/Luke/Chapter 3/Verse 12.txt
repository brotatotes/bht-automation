<p>Verse <span class="scriptRef" com="acc" lang="eng" ref="lu+3:12">Luke 3:12</span>. <i><b>Then came also publicans</b></i> â€” He next instructs the <i>tax-gatherers</i> in the proper discharge of their duty: though it was an office detested by the Jews at large, yet the Baptist does not condemn it. It is only the <i>abuse</i> of it that he speaks against. If <i>taxes</i> be necessary for the support of a state, there must be <i>collectors</i> of them; and the collector, if he properly discharge his duty, is not only a useful, but also a respectable officer. But it seems the Jewish tax-gatherers exacted much more from the people than government authorized them to do, <span class="scriptRef" com="acc" lang="eng" ref="lu+3:13">Luke 3:13</span>, and the surplus they pocketed. See the conduct of many of our <i>surveyors</i> and <i>assessors</i>. They are oppressors of the people, and enrich <i>themselves</i> by unjust <i>surcharges</i>. This, I am inclined to think, is too common an evil; and the executive government is often the <i>people's scape-goat</i>, to bear the crimes of its officers, crimes in which it has no concern.
