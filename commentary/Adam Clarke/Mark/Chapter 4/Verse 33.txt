<p>Verse 33. <i><b>With many such parables</b></i> — πολλαις, <i>many</i>, is omitted by L, <i>sixteen</i> others; the <i>Syriac</i>, both the <i>Persic</i>, one <i>Arabic,</i> <i>Coptic, Armenian, AEthiopic</i>, and two of the <i>Itala</i>. <i>Mill</i> approves of the omission, and <i>Griesbach</i> leaves it doubtful. It is probably an interpolation: the text reads better without it.</p>
<p><i><b>As they were able to hear</b></i> — ακουειν, or to <i>understand</i> always suiting his teaching to the <i>capacities</i> of his hearers. I have always found that preacher most useful, who could adapt his phrase to <i>that</i> of the people to whom he preached. Studying different <i>dialects</i>, and <i>forms</i> of <i>speech</i>, among the common people, is a more <i>difficult</i> and a more <i>useful</i> work than the study of <i>dead languages.</i> The one a man <i>should do</i>, and the other he <i>need</i> not leave <i>undone</i>.</p>
