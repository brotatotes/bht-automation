<p>Verse <span class="scriptRef" com="acc" lang="eng" ref="ro+6:10">Romans 6:10</span>. <i><b>He died unto sin once</b></i> — On this clause Rosenmuller speaks thus: "Τῃ ἁμαρτιᾳ απεθανεν εφαπαξ· <i>propter peccatum mortuus</i> <i>est semel</i>, et quidem misera morte. Τῃ ἁμαρτιᾳ, i.e. ὑπερ της ἁμαρτιας, <i>ad expianda peccata</i>; res ipsa docet aliter <i>homines</i> αποθνησκειν τῃ ἁμαρτιᾳ, <i>aliter</i> Christum: amat Paulus parallelismum, in quo interpretando multa cautione opus est." "<i>He</i> <i>died unto sin once</i>: i.e. <i>he died on account of sin</i>, and truly a miserable death. Τῃ ἁμαρτιᾳ, is the same as ὑπερ της ἁμαρτιας, <i>for the expiation of sin</i>. Common sense teaches us that <i>men die to</i> <i>sin</i> in <i>one</i> sense; <i>Christ</i> in <i>another</i>: St. Paul loves parallelisms, in the interpretation of which there is need of much caution." From the whole scope of the apostle's discourse it is plain that he considers the death of Christ as a death or sacrifice for sin; a sin-offering: in this sense no <i>man</i> has ever died <i>for</i> sin, or ever can die.</p>
