<p>Verse <span class="scriptRef" com="acc" lang="eng" ref="ro+6:2">Romans 6:2</span>. <i><b>God forbid</b></i> — Μη γενοιτο, <i>Let it not be; by no means;</i> <i>far from it; let not such a thing be mentioned</i>!-Any of these is the meaning of the Greek phrase, which is a strong expression of surprise and disapprobation: and is not properly rendered by our <i>God forbid</i>! for, though this may express the <i>same thing</i>, yet it is not proper to make the sacred NAME SO familiar on such occasions.</p>
<p><i><b>How shall we, that are dead to sin</b></i> — The phraseology of this verse is common among Hebrews, Greeks, and Latins. To DIE <i>to a</i> <i>thing</i> or <i>person</i>, is to <i>have nothing to do with it</i> or <i>him</i>; to be <i>totally separated</i> from them: and <i>to live to a thing</i> or <i>person</i> is to be <i>wholly given up to them</i>; to have the most <i>intimate</i> <i>connection</i> with them. So Plautus, Clitell. iii. 1, 16: <i>Nihil</i> <i>mecum tibi</i>, MORTUUS TIBI SUM. <i>I have nothing to do with thee; I</i> <i>am</i> DEAD <i>to thee</i>. Persa, i. 1, 20: <i>Mihi quidem tu jam</i> MORTUUS ERAS, <i>quia te non visitavi. Thou wast</i> DEAD <i>to me because I</i> <i>visited thee not</i>. So AElian, Var. Hist. iii. 13: Ὁτι φιλοινοτατον εθνος το των Ταπυρων, τοσουτον, ὡστε ζῃν αυτους εν οινῳ, και το πλειστον του βιου εν τῃ προς αυτον ὁμιλιᾳ καταναλισκειν· "<i>The Tapyrians are such lovers of wine, that they</i> LIVE <i>in wine; and the principal part of their</i> LIFE <i>is</i> DEVOTED <i>to</i> <i>it</i>." <i>They live to wine</i>; they are insatiable drunkards. See more examples in <i>Wetstein</i> and <i>Rosenmuller</i>.</p>
