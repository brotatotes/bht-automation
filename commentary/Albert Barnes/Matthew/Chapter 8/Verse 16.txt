<p style=" "><b>When the even was come ... - </b>The fame of the miracles of Jesus would probably draw together a crowd, and those who had friends that were afflicted would bring them. All that were brought to him he healed. This was proof of two things: first, of his great benevolence; and, secondly, of his divine mission. He might have established the latter by miracles that would do no good. None of his miracles were performed, however, merely to make a display of power, unless the cursing of the barren fig-tree be an exception. Compare <span class="scriptRef" com="bnb" lang="eng" ref="mr+11:11-14">Mark 11:11-14</span>. What is here recorded occurred on the evening of the Sabbath, <span class="scriptRef" com="bnb" lang="eng" ref="mr+1:21-32">Mark 1:21-32</span>. The Jews kept the Sabbath from evening to evening, <span class="scriptRef" com="bnb" lang="eng" ref="le+23:32">Leviticus 23:32</span>. On the Sabbath they would not even bring their sick to be healed <span class="scriptRef" com="bnb" lang="eng" ref="lu+13:14">Luke 13:14</span>; but as soon as it was closed, on the evening of the same day, they came in multitudes to be cured.</p>
