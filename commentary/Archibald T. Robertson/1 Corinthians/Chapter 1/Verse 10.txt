<p><b> Now I beseech you </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">παρακαλω δε υμας</span></span>). Old and common verb, over 100 times in N.T., to call to one's side. Corresponds here to <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ευχαριστω</span></span>,</p>
<p><b>I thank </b>, in verse <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="1co+1:4">1 Corinthians 1:4</span>. Direct appeal after the thanksgiving.</p>
<p><b>Through the name </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">δια του ονοματος</span></span>). Genitive, not accusative (cause or reason), as the medium or instrument of the appeal (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="2co+10:1">2 Corinthians 10:1</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ro+12:1">Romans 12:1</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ro+15:30">Romans 15:30</span>).</p>
<p><b>That </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ινα</span></span>). Purport (sub-final) rather than direct purpose, common idiom in <i>Koine</i> (Robertson, <i>Grammar</i>, pp.991-4) like <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mt+14:36">Matthew 14:36</span>. Used here with <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">λεγητε, ηι, ητε κατηρτισμενο</span></span>, though expressed only once.</p>
<p><b>All speak </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">λεγητε παντες</span></span>). Present active subjunctive, that ye all keep on speaking. With the divisions in mind. An idiom from Greek political life (Lightfoot). This touch of the classical writers argues for Paul's acquaintance with Greek culture.</p>
<p><b>There be no divisions among you </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">μη η εν υμιν σχισματα</span></span>). Present subjunctive, that divisions may not continue to be (they already had them). Negative statement of preceding idea. <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">Σχισμα</span></span> is from <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">σχιζω</span></span>, old word to split or rend, and so means a rent (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mt+9:16">Matthew 9:16</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mr+2:21">Mark 2:21</span>). Papyri use it for a splinter of wood and for ploughing. Here we have the earliest instance of its use in a moral sense of division, dissension, see also <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="1co+11:18">1 Corinthians 11:18</span> where a less complete change than <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">αιρεσεις</span></span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="1co+12:25">1 Corinthians 12:25</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="joh+7:43">John 7:43</span> (discord); <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="1co+9:16">1 Corinthians 9:16</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="1co+10:19">1 Corinthians 10:19</span>. "Here, faction, for which the classical word is <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">στασις</span></span>: division within the Christian community" (Vincent). These divisions were over the preachers (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="1co+1:12-4">1 Corinthians 1:12-4</span>), immorality (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="1co+5:1-13">1 Corinthians 5:1-13</span>), going to law before the heathen (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="1co+6:1-11">1 Corinthians 6:1-11</span>), marriage (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="1co+7:1-40">1 Corinthians 7:1-40</span>), meats offered to idols (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="1co+1:1">1 Corinthians 1:1</span>), conduct of women in church (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="1co+11:1-16">1 Corinthians 11:1-16</span>), the Lord's Supper (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="1co+11:17-34">1 Corinthians 11:17-34</span>), spiritual gifts (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="1co+1:1">1 Corinthians 1:1</span>), the resurrection (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="1co+1:1">1 Corinthians 1:1</span>).</p>
<p><b>But that ye be perfected together </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ητε δε κατηρτισμενο</span></span>). Periphrastic perfect passive subjunctive. See this verb in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mt+4:21">Matthew 4:21</span> (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mr+1:19">Mark 1:19</span>) for mending torn nets and in moral sense already in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="1th+3:10">1 Thessalonians 3:10</span>. Galen uses it for a surgeon's mending a joint and Herodotus for composing factions. See <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="2co+13:11">2 Corinthians 13:11</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ga+6:1">Galatians 6:1</span>.</p>
<p><b>Mind </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">νο</span></span>),</p>
<p><b>judgment </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">γνωμη</span></span>). "Of these words <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">νους</span></span> denotes the frame or state of mind, <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">γνωμη</span></span> the judgment, opinion or sentiment, which is the outcome of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">νους</span></span>" (Lightfoot). </p>
