<p><b> Were our examples </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">τυπο ημων εγενηθησαν</span></span>). More exactly, examples for us (objective genitive <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ημων</span></span>, not subjective genitive, of us). The word <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">τυπο</span></span> (our types) comes from <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">τυπτω</span></span>, to strike, and meant originally the mark of a blow as the print of the nails (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="joh+20:25">John 20:25</span>), then a figure formed by a blow like images of the gods (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ac+7:43">Acts 7:43</span>), then an example to be imitated (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="1pe+5:3">1 Peter 5:3</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="1ti+4:12">1 Timothy 4:12</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="1th+1:7">1 Thessalonians 1:7</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="2th+3:9">2 Thessalonians 3:9</span>), or to be avoided as here, and finally a type in a doctrinal sense (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ro+5:14">Romans 5:14</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="heb+9:24">Hebrews 9:24</span>).</p>
<p><b>To the intent we should not lust after </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εις το μη εινα ημας επιθυμητας</span></span>). Purpose expressed by <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εις</span></span> with the articular infinitive <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">το εινα</span></span> and the accusative of general reference with <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">επιθυμητας</span></span> (lusters) in the predicate. </p>
