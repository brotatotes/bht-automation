<p><b> God hath set some </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ους μεν εθετο ο θεος</span></span>). See verse <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="1co+12:18">1 Corinthians 12:18</span> for <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εθετο ο θεος</span></span>. Note middle voice (for his own use). Paul begins as if he means to say <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ους μεν αποστολουσ, ους δε προφητας</span></span> (some apostles, some prophets), but he changes the construction and has no <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ους δε</span></span>, but instead <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">πρωτον, δευτερον, επειτα</span></span> (first, second, then, etc.).</p>
<p><b>In the church </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εν τη εκκλησια</span></span>). The general sense of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εκκλησια</span></span> as in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mt+16:18">Matthew 16:18</span> and later in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="col+1:18">Colossians 1:18</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="col+1:24">Colossians 1:24</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="eph+5:23">Ephesians 5:23</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="eph+5:32">Ephesians 5:32</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="heb+12:23">Hebrews 12:23</span>. See list also in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="eph+4:11">Ephesians 4:11</span>. See on <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mt+10:2">Matthew 10:2</span> for <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">αποστολους</span></span>, the official title given the twelve by Jesus, and claimed by Paul though not one of the twelve.</p>
<p><b>Prophets </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">προφητας</span></span>). For-speakers for God and Christ. See the list of prophets and teachers in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ac+13:1">Acts 13:1</span> with Barnabas first and Saul last. Prophets are needed today if men will let God's Spirit use them, men moved to utter the deep things of God.</p>
<p><b>Teachers </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">διδασκαλους</span></span>). Old word from <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">διδασκω</span></span>, to teach. Used to the Baptist (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="lu+3:12">Luke 3:12</span>), to Jesus (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="joh+3:10">John 3:10</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="joh+13:13">John 13:13</span>), and of Paul by himself along with <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">αποστολος</span></span> (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="1ti+2:7">1 Timothy 2:7</span>). It is a calamity when the preacher is no longer a teacher, but only an exhorter. See <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="eph+4:11">Ephesians 4:11</span>.</p>
<p><b>Then miracles </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">επειτα δυναμεις</span></span>). Here a change is made from the concrete to the abstract. See the reverse in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ro+12:7">Romans 12:7</span>. See these words (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">δυναμεισ, ιαμητων, γλωσσων</span></span>) in verses <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="1co+12:9">1 Corinthians 12:9</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="1co+12:10">1 Corinthians 12:10</span> with <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">γλωσσων</span></span>, last again. But these two new terms (helps, governments).</p>
<p><b>Helps </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">αντιλημψεις</span></span>). Old word, from <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">αντιλαμβανομα</span></span>, to lay hold of. In LXX, common in papyri, here only in N.T. Probably refers to the work of the deacons, help rendered to the poor and the sick.</p>
<p><b>Governments </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">κυβερνησεις</span></span>). Old word from <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">κυβερναω</span></span> (cf. <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">Κυβερνητης</span></span> in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ac+27:11">Acts 27:11</span>) like Latin <i>gubernare</i>, our govern. So a governing. Probably Paul has in mind bishops (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">επισχοπο</span></span>) or elders (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">πρεσβυτερο</span></span>), the outstanding leaders (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ο προισταμενο</span></span> in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="1th+5:12">1 Thessalonians 5:12</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ro+12:8">Romans 12:8</span>; <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ο ηγουμενο</span></span> in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ac+15:22">Acts 15:22</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="heb+13:7">Hebrews 13:7</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="heb+13:17">Hebrews 13:17</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="heb+13:24">Hebrews 13:24</span>). Curiously enough, these two offices (pastors and deacons) which are not named specifically are the two that survive today. See <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="php+1:1">Philippians 1:1</span> for both officers. </p>
