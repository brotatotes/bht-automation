<p><b> Hath set forth us the apostles last </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ημας τους αποστολους εσχατους απεδειξεν</span></span>). The first aorist active indicative of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">αποδεικνυμ</span></span>, old verb to show, to expose to view or exhibit (Herodotus), in technical sense (cf. <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="2th+2:4">2 Thessalonians 2:4</span>) for gladiatorial show as in <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εθηριομαχησα</span></span> (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="1co+15:32">1 Corinthians 15:32</span>). In this grand pageant Paul and other apostles come last (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εσχατους</span></span>, predicate accusative after <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">απεδειξεν</span></span>) as a grand finale.</p>
<p><b>As men doomed to die </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ως επιθανατιους</span></span>). Late word, here alone in N.T. The LXX (Bel and the Dragon 31) has it for those thrown daily to the lions. Dionysius of Halicarnassus (<i>A.R</i>. vii. 35) uses it of those thrown from the Tarpeian Rock. The gladiators would say <i>morituri salutamus</i>. All this in violent contrast to the kingly Messianic pretensions of the Corinthians.</p>
<p><b>A spectacle </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">θεατρον</span></span>). Cf. <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="heb+11:33-40">Hebrews 11:33-40</span>. The word, like our theatre, means the place of the show (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ac+19:29">Acts 19:29</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ac+19:31">Acts 19:31</span>). Then, it means the spectacle shown there (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">θεαμα</span></span> or <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">θεα</span></span>), and, as here, the man exhibited as the show like the verb <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">θεατριζομενο</span></span>, made a spectacle (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="heb+10:33">Hebrews 10:33</span>). Sometimes it refers to the spectators (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">θεατα</span></span>) like our "house" for the audience. Here the spectators include "the world, both to angels and men" (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">τω κοσμω κα αγγελοις κα ανθρωποις</span></span>), dative case of personal interest. </p>
