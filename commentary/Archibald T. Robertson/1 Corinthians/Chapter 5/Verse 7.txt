<p><b> Purge out </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εκκαθαρατε</span></span>). First aorist (effective) active imperative of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εκκαθαιρω</span></span>, old verb to cleanse out (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εκ</span></span>), to clean completely. Aorist tense of urgency, do it now and do it effectively before the whole church is contaminated. This turn to the metaphor is from the command to purge out the old (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">παλαιαν</span></span>, now old and decayed) leaven before the passover feast (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ex+12:15">Exodus 12:15</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ex+13:7">Exodus 13:7</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="zep+1:12">Zephaniah 1:12</span>). Cf. modern methods of disinfection after a contagious disease.</p>
<p><b>A new lump </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">νεον φυραμα</span></span>). Make a fresh start as a new community with the contamination removed. <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">Νεος</span></span> is the root for <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">νεανισκος</span></span>, a young man, not yet old (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">γηραιος</span></span>). So new wine (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">οινον νεον</span></span> <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mt+9:17">Matthew 9:17</span>). <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">Καινος</span></span> is fresh as compared with the ancient (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">παλαιος</span></span>). See the distinction in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="col+3:10">Colossians 3:10</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="eph+4:22">Ephesians 4:22</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="2co+5:17">2 Corinthians 5:17</span>.</p>
<p><b>Unleavened </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">αζυμο</span></span>). Without (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">α</span></span> privative) leaven, the normal and ideal state of Christians. Rare word among the ancients (once in Plato). They are a new creation (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">καινη κτισις</span></span>), "exemplifying Kant's maxim that you should treat a man as if he were what you would wish him to be" (Robertson and Plummer).</p>
<p><b>For our passover also hath been sacrificed, even Christ </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">κα γαρ το πασχα ημων ετυθη Χριστος</span></span>). First aorist passive indicative of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">θυω</span></span>, old verb to sacrifice. Euphony of consonants, <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">θ</span></span> to <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">τ</span></span> because of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">-θη</span></span>. Reference to the death of Christ on the Cross as the Paschal Lamb (common use of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">πασχα</span></span> as <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mr+14:12">Mark 14:12</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="lu+22:7">Luke 22:7</span>), the figure used long before by the Baptist of Jesus (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="joh+1:29">John 1:29</span>). Paul means that the Lamb was already slain on Calvary and yet you have not gotten rid of the leaven. </p>
