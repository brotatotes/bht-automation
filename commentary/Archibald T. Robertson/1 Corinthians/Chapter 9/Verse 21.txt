<p><b> To them that are without law </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">τοις ανομοις</span></span>). The heathen, those outside the Mosaic law (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ro+2:14">Romans 2:14</span>), not lawless (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="lu+22:37">Luke 22:37</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ac+2:23">Acts 2:23</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="1ti+1:9">1 Timothy 1:9</span>). See how Paul bore himself with the pagans (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ac+14:15">Acts 14:15</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ac+17:23">Acts 17:23</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ac+24:25">Acts 24:25</span>), and how he quoted heathen poets. "Not being an outlaw of God, but an inlaw of Christ" (Evans, Estius has it <i>exlex, inlex</i>, <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">μη ων ανομος θεου, αλλ' εννομος Χριστου</span></span>). The genitive case of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">θεου</span></span> and <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">Χριστου</span></span> (specifying case) comes out better thus, for it seems unusual with <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ανομος</span></span> and <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εννομος</span></span>, both old and regular adjectives. </p>
