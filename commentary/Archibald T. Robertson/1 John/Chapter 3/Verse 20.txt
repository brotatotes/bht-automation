<p><b> Whereinsoever our heart condemn us </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">οτ εαν καταγινωσκη ημων η καρδια</span></span>). A construction like <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">οτ αν</span></span>, whatever, in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="joh+2:5">John 2:5</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="joh+14:13">John 14:13</span>. <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">Καταγινωσκω</span></span> occurs only three times in the N.T., here, verse <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="1jo+3:21">1 John 3:21</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ga+2:11">Galatians 2:11</span>. It means to know something against one, to condemn.</p>
<p><b>Because God is greater than our heart </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">οτ μειζων εστιν της καρδιας ημων</span></span>). Ablative <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">καρδιας</span></span> after the comparative <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">μειζων</span></span>.</p>
<p><b>And knoweth all things </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">κα γινωσκε παντα</span></span>). Just so Peter replied to Jesus in spite of his denials (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="joh+21:17">John 21:17</span>). God's omniscience is linked with his love and sympathy. God knows every secret in our hearts. This difficult passage strikes the very centre of Christian truth (Brooke). </p>
