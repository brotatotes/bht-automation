<p><b> Hereby know ye </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εν τουτω γινωσκετε</span></span>). Either present active indicative or imperative. The test of "the Spirit of God" (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">το πνευμα του θεου</span></span>) here alone in this Epistle, save verse <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="1jo+4:13">1 John 4:13</span>. With the clamour of voices then and now this is important. The test (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εν τουτω</span></span>, as in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="1jo+3:19">1 John 3:19</span>) follows.</p>
<p><b>That Jesus Christ is come in the flesh </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">Ιησουν Χριστον εν σαρκ εληλυθοτα</span></span>). The correct text (perfect active participle predicate accusative), not the infinitive (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εληλυθενα</span></span>, B Vg). The predicate participle (see <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="joh+9:22">John 9:22</span> for predicate accusative with <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ομολογεω</span></span>) describes Jesus as already come in the flesh (his actual humanity, not a phantom body as the Docetic Gnostics held). See this same idiom in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="2jo+1:7">2 John 1:7</span> with <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ερχομενον</span></span> (coming). A like test is proposed by Paul for confessing the deity of Jesus Christ in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="1co+12:3">1 Corinthians 12:3</span> and for the Incarnation and Resurrection of Jesus in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ro+10:6-10">Romans 10:6-10</span>. </p>
