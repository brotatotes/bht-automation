<p><b> Wherefore </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">διο</span></span>). "Because of which thing," the glorious free grace opened for Gentiles and Jews in Christ (verses <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="1pe+1:3-12">1 Peter 1:3-12</span>).</p>
<p><b>Girding up </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">αναζωσαμενο</span></span>). First aorist middle participle of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">αναζωννυμ</span></span>, late and rare verb (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="jdg+18:16">Judges 18:16</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="pr+29:35">Proverbs 29:35</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="pr+31:17">Proverbs 31:17</span>), here only in N.T., vivid metaphor for habit of the Orientals, who quickly gathered up their loose robes with a girdle when in a hurry or starting on a journey.</p>
<p><b>The loins </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">τας οσφυας</span></span>). Old word for the part of the body where the girdle (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ζωνη</span></span>) was worn. Metaphor here as in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="lu+12:35">Luke 12:35</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="eph+6:14">Ephesians 6:14</span>.</p>
<p><b>Mind </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">διανοιας</span></span>). Old word for the faculty of understanding, of seeing through a thing (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">δια, νοεω</span></span>) as in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mt+22:37">Matthew 22:37</span>.</p>
<p><b>Be sober </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">νηφοντες</span></span>). "Being sober" (present active participle of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">νηφω</span></span>, old verb, but in N.T. always as metaphor (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="1th+5:6">1 Thessalonians 5:6</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="1th+5:8">1 Thessalonians 5:8</span>, etc., and so in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="1pe+4:7">1 Peter 4:7</span>).</p>
<p><b>Perfectly </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">τελειως</span></span>). Adverb, old word (here alone in N.T.), from adjective <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">τελειος</span></span> (perfect), connected with <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ελπισατε</span></span> (set your hope, first aorist active imperative of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ελπιζω</span></span>) in the Revised Version, but Bigg, Hort, and most modern commentators take it according to Peter's usual custom with the preceding verb, <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">νηφοντες</span></span> ("being perfectly sober," not "hope perfectly").</p>
<p><b>That is to be brought </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">την φερομενην</span></span>). Present passive articular participle of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">φερω</span></span>, picturing the process, "that is being brought." For "revelation" (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">αποκαλυψε</span></span>) see end of verse <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="1pe+1:7">1 Peter 1:7</span>. </p>
