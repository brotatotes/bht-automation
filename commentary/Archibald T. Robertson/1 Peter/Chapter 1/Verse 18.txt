<p><b> Knowing </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ειδοτες</span></span>). Second perfect active participle of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">οιδα</span></span>, causal participle. The appeal is to an elementary Christian belief (Hort), the holiness and justice of God with the added thought of the high cost of redemption (Bigg).</p>
<p><b>Ye were redeemed </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ελυτρωθητε</span></span>). First aorist passive indicative of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">λυτροω</span></span>, old verb from <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">λυτρον</span></span> (ransom for life as of a slave, <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mt+20:28">Matthew 20:28</span>), to set free by payment of ransom, abundant examples in the papyri, in N.T. only here, <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="lu+24:21">Luke 24:21</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="tit+2:14">Titus 2:14</span>. The ransom is the blood of Christ. Peter here amplifies the language in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="isa+52:3">Isaiah 52:3</span>.</p>
<p><b>Not with corruptible things </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ου φθαρτοις</span></span>). Instrumental case neuter plural of the late verbal adjective from <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">φθειρω</span></span> to destroy or to corrupt, and so perishable, in N.T. here, verse <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="1pe+1:23">1 Peter 1:23</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="1co+9:25">1 Corinthians 9:25</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="1co+15:53">1 Corinthians 15:53</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ro+1:23">Romans 1:23</span>. <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">Αργυριω η χρυσιω</span></span> (silver or gold) are in explanatory apposition with <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">φθαρτοις</span></span> and so in the same case. Slaves were set free by silver and gold.</p>
<p><b>From your vain manner of life </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εκ της ματαιας υμων αναστροφης</span></span>). "Out of" (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εκ</span></span>), and so away from, the pre-Christian <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">αναστροφη</span></span> of verse <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="1pe+1:15">1 Peter 1:15</span>, which was "vain" (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ματαιας</span></span>. Cf. <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="eph+4:17-24">Ephesians 4:17-24</span>).</p>
<p><b>Handed down from your fathers </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">πατροπαραδοτου</span></span>). This adjective, though predicate in position, is really attributive in idea, like <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">χειροποιητου</span></span> in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="eph+2:11">Ephesians 2:11</span> (Robertson, <i>Grammar</i>, p. 777), like the French idiom. This double compound verbal adjective (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">πατερ, παρα, διδωμ</span></span>), though here alone in N.T., occurs in Diodorus, Dion. Halic, and in several inscriptions (Moulton and Milligan's <i>Vocabulary</i>; Deissmann, <i>Bible Studies</i>, pp. 266f.). The Jews made a wrong use of tradition (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mt+15:2">Matthew 15:2</span>), but the reference here seems mainly to Gentiles (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="1pe+2:12">1 Peter 2:12</span>). </p>
