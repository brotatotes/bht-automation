<p><b> Blessed be </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ευλογητος</span></span>). No copula in the Greek (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εστω</span></span>, let be, or <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εστιν</span></span>, is, or <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ειη</span></span>, may be). The verbal adjective (from <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ευλογεω</span></span>) occurs in the N.T. only of God, as in the LXX (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="lu+1:68">Luke 1:68</span>). See also <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="2co+1:3">2 Corinthians 1:3</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="eph+1:3">Ephesians 1:3</span>.</p>
<p><b>The God and Father of our Lord Jesus Christ </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ο θεος κα πατηρ του κυριου ημων Ιησου Χριστου</span></span>). This precise language in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="2co+1:3">2 Corinthians 1:3</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="2co+1:3">2 Corinthians 1:3</span>; and part of it in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="2co+11:31">2 Corinthians 11:31</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ro+15:6">Romans 15:6</span>. See <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="joh+20:17">John 20:17</span> for similar language by Jesus.</p>
<p><b>Great </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">πολυ</span></span>). Much.</p>
<p><b>Begat us again </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">αναγεννησας ημας</span></span>). First aorist active articular (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ο</span></span>, who) participle of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">αναγενναω</span></span>, late, and rare word to beget again, in Aleph for <i>Sirach</i> (<i>Prol</i>. 20), in Philo, in Hermetic writings, in N.T. only here and verse <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="1pe+1:23">1 Peter 1:23</span>. "It was probably borrowed by the New Paganism from Christianity" (Bigg). The Stoics used <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">αναγεννησις</span></span> for <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">παλινγενεσια</span></span> (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="tit+3:5">Titus 3:5</span>). If <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ανωθεν</span></span> in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="joh+3:3">John 3:3</span> be taken to mean "again," the same idea of regeneration is there, and if "from above" it is the new birth, anyhow.</p>
<p><b>Unto a living hope </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εις ελπιδα ζωσαν</span></span>). Peter is fond of the word "living" (present active participle of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ζαω</span></span>) as in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="1pe+1:23">1 Peter 1:23</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="1pe+2:4">1 Peter 2:4</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="1pe+2:5">1 Peter 2:5</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="1pe+2:24">1 Peter 2:24</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="1pe+4:5">1 Peter 4:5</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="1pe+4:6">1 Peter 4:6</span>. The Pharisees cherished the hope of the resurrection (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ac+23:6">Acts 23:6</span>), but the resurrection of Jesus gave it proof and permanence (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="1co+15:14">1 Corinthians 15:14</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="1co+15:17">1 Corinthians 15:17</span>). It is no longer a dead hope like dead faith (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="jas+2:17">James 2:17</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="jas+2:26">James 2:26</span>). This revival of hope was wrought "by the resurrection of Jesus Christ" (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">δια αναστασεως</span></span>). Hope rose up with Christ from the dead, though the disciples (Peter included) were slow at first to believe it. </p>
