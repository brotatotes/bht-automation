<p><b> Which in time past </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ο ποτε</span></span>). "Who once upon a time."</p>
<p><b>No people </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ου λαος</span></span>). This phrase from <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ho+2:23">Hosea 2:23</span>. Note use of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ου</span></span> (not <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ουδεις</span></span>) with <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">λαος</span></span> like Hebrew negative.</p>
<p><b>Which had not obtained mercy </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ο ουκ ελεημενο</span></span>). Perfect passive articular participle of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ελεεω</span></span> and the emphatic negative <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ου</span></span>, with which compare Paul's use of <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="1pe+2:1">1 Peter 2:1</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="1pe+2:2">1 Peter 2:2</span> in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ro+9:25">Romans 9:25</span>, which may have been known to Peter or not.</p>
<p><b>But now have obtained mercy </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">νυν δε ελεηθεντες</span></span>). Change to first aorist passive participle from "the long antecedent state" to "the single event of conversion which ended it" (Hort). </p>
