<p><b> Not rendering evil for evil </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">μη αποδιδοντες κακον αντ κακου</span></span>). <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">Μη</span></span> and the present active participle of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">αποδιδωμ</span></span>, to give back. The same phrase in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ro+12:17">Romans 12:17</span> and the same idea in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="1th+5:15">1 Thessalonians 5:15</span>. Peter may have obtained it from Paul or both from <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="pr+17:13">Proverbs 17:13</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="pr+20:22">Proverbs 20:22</span>, "an approximation to Christ's repeal of the <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">λεξ ταλιονις</span></span> (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mt+5:38">Matthew 5:38</span>) which Plato first opposed among the Greeks" (Hart). Common use of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">αντ</span></span> for exchange.</p>
<p><b>Reviling for reviling </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">λοιδοριαν αντ λοιδοριας</span></span>). Allusion to <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="1pe+2:23">1 Peter 2:23</span> (Christ's own example).</p>
<p><b>But contrariwise blessing </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">τουναντιον δε ευλογουντες</span></span>). Adverbial accusative and crasis (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">το εναντιον</span></span>) of the neuter article and the adjective <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εναντιος</span></span> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εν, αντιος</span></span>, opposite, <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mt+14:24">Matthew 14:24</span>), "on the contrary." For <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ευλογουντες</span></span> (present active participle of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ευλογεω</span></span>) see <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="lu+6:28">Luke 6:28</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ro+12:14">Romans 12:14</span> (imperative <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ευλογειτε</span></span>).</p>
<p><b>For hereunto were ye called </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">οτ εις τουτο εκληθητε</span></span>). See <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="1pe+2:21">1 Peter 2:21</span> for this verb and use of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εις τουτο</span></span> (pointing to the preceding argument).</p>
<p><b>That ye should inherit a blessing </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ινα ευλογιαν κληρονομησητε</span></span>). Purpose clause with <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ινα</span></span> and the first aorist active subjunctive of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">κληρονομεω</span></span>, a plain reference to Esau, who wanted "to inherit the blessing" (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="heb+12:17">Hebrews 12:17</span>) after he had sold his birthright. Christians are the new Israel (both Gentiles and Jews) and are the spiritual descendants of Isaac (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ga+4:22">Galatians 4:22</span>). </p>
