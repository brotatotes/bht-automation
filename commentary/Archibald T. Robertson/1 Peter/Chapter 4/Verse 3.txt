<p><b> Past </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">παρεληλυθως</span></span>). Perfect active participle of the compound verb <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">παρερχομα</span></span>, old verb, to go by (beside) as in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mt+14:15">Matthew 14:15</span> with <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ωρα</span></span> (hour).</p>
<p><b>May suffice </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">αρκετος</span></span>). No copula in the Greek, probably <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εστιν</span></span> (is) rather than <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">δυνατα</span></span> (can). Late and rare verbal adjective from <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">αρκεω</span></span>, to suffice, in the papyri several times, in N.T. only here and <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mt+6:34">Matthew 6:34</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mt+10:25">Matthew 10:25</span>, apparently referring to Christ's words in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mt+6:34">Matthew 6:34</span> (possibly an axiom or proverb).</p>
<p><b>To have wrought </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">κατειργασθα</span></span>). Perfect middle infinitive of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">κατεργαζομα</span></span>, common compound (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">κατα, εργον</span></span> work) as in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="1co+5:3">1 Corinthians 5:3</span>.</p>
<p><b>The desire </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">το βουλημα</span></span>). Correct text, not <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">θελημα</span></span>. Either means the thing desired, willed. Jews sometimes fell in with the ways of Gentiles (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ro+2:21-24">Romans 2:21-24</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ro+3:9-18">Romans 3:9-18</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="eph+2:1-3">Ephesians 2:1-3</span>) as today some Christians copy the ways of the world.</p>
<p><b>And to have walked </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">πεπορευμενους</span></span>). Perfect middle participle of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">πορευομα</span></span> in the accusative plural of general reference with the infinitive <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">κατειργασθα</span></span>. Literally, "having walked or gone."</p>
<p><b>In lasciviousness </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εν ασελγειαις</span></span>). All these sins are in the locative case with <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εν</span></span>. "In unbridled lustful excesses" (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="2pe+2:7">2 Peter 2:7</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="2co+12:21">2 Corinthians 12:21</span>).</p>
<p><b>Lusts </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">επιθυμιαις</span></span>). Cf. <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="1pe+2:11">1 Peter 2:11</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="1pe+4:2">1 Peter 4:2</span>.</p>
<p><b>Winebibbings </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">οινοφλυγιαις</span></span>). Old compound (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">οινος</span></span>, wine, <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">φλυω</span></span>, to bubble up), for drunkenness, here only in N.T. (also in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="de+21:20">Deuteronomy 21:20</span>).</p>
<p><b>Revellings </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">κομοις</span></span>). Old word (from <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">κειμα</span></span>, to lie down), rioting drinking parties, in N.T. here and <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ga+5:21">Galatians 5:21</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ro+13:13">Romans 13:13</span>.</p>
<p><b>Carousings </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ποτοις</span></span>). Old word for drinking carousal (from <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">πινω</span></span>, to drink), here only in the N.T. In the light of these words it seems strange to find modern Christians justifying their "personal liberty" to drink and carouse, to say nothing of the prohibition law. The Greeks actually carried lust and drunkenness into their religious observances (Aphrodite, for instance).</p>
<p><b>Abominable idolatries </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">αθεμιτοις ειδωλολατριαις</span></span>). To the Christian all "idolatry," (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ειδωλον, λατρεια</span></span>), worship of idols, is "abominable," not allowed (alpha privative and <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">θεμιτος</span></span>, <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">θεμιστος</span></span> the old form, verbal of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">θεμιζω</span></span>, to make lawful), but particularly those associated with drinking and licentiousness. The only other N.T. example of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">αθεμιτος</span></span> is by Peter also (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ac+10:28">Acts 10:28</span>) and about the Mosaic law. That may be the idea here, for Jews often fell into idolatrous practices (Deissmann, <i>Bible Studies</i>, p. 274). </p>
