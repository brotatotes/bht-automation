<p><b> Being bereaved of you </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">απορφανισθεντες αφ' υμων</span></span>). First aorist passive participle of the rare compound verb (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">απορφανιζω</span></span>, in Aeschylus, but nowhere else in N.T.). Literally,</p>
<p><b>being orphaned from you </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">αφ' υμων</span></span>, ablative case). Paul changes the figure again (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">τροφος</span></span> or mother nurse in verse <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="1th+2:7">1 Thessalonians 2:7</span>, <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">νηπιος</span></span> or babe in verse <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="1th+2:7">1 Thessalonians 2:7</span>, <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">πατηρ</span></span> or father in verse <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="1th+2:11">1 Thessalonians 2:11</span>) to</p>
<p><b>orphan </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ορφανος</span></span>). He refers to the period of separation from them,</p>
<p><b>for a short season </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">προς καιρον ωρας</span></span>) for a season of an hour. This idiom only here in N.T., but <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">προς καιρον</span></span> in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="lu+8:13">Luke 8:13</span> and <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">προς ωραν</span></span> in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="2co+7:8">2 Corinthians 7:8</span>. But it has seemed long to Paul. Precisely how long he had been gone we do not know, some months at any rate.</p>
<p><b>In presence, not in heart </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">προσωπω ου καρδια</span></span>). Locative case. <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">Προσωπον</span></span>, old word (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">προσ, οπς</span></span>, in front of the eye, face) for face, look, person. Literally,</p>
<p><b>in face or person </b>. His heart was with them, though they no longer saw his face. Heart, originally <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">καρδια</span></span>, is the inner man, the seat of the affections and purposes, not always in contrast with intellect (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">νους</span></span>). "Out of sight, not out of mind" (Rutherford).</p>
<p><b>Endeavoured the more exceedingly </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">περισσοτερως εσπουδασαμεν</span></span>). Ingressive aorist active indicative of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">σπουδαζω</span></span>, old word to hasten (from <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">σπουδη, σπευδω</span></span>).</p>
<p><b>We became zealous </b>. Comparative adverb <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">περισσοτερως</span></span> from <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">περισσον</span></span>, more abundantly than before being orphaned from you.</p>
<p><b>Your face </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">το προσωπον υμων</span></span>). Cf. his</p>
<p><b>face </b> above.</p>
<p><b>With great desire </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εν πολλη επιθυμια</span></span>).</p>
<p><b>In much longing </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">επιθυμια</span></span> from <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">επ</span></span> and <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">θυμος</span></span>, <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">επιθυμεω</span></span>, to run after, to yearn after, whether good or bad). </p>
