<p><b> The elders that rule well </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ο καλως προεστωτες πρεσβυτερο</span></span>). See verse <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="1ti+5:1">1 Timothy 5:1</span> for ordinary sense of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">πρεσβυτερος</span></span> for "older man." But here of position in same sense as <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">επισκοπος</span></span> (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="1ti+3:2">1 Timothy 3:2</span>) as in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="tit+1:5">Titus 1:5</span> = <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">επισκοπος</span></span> in verse <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="1ti+5:7">1 Timothy 5:7</span>. Cf. Luke's use of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">πρεσβυτερος</span></span> (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ac+20:17">Acts 20:17</span>) = Paul's <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">επισκοπους</span></span> (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ac+20:28">Acts 20:28</span>). <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">Προεστωτες</span></span> is second perfect active participle of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">προιστημ</span></span> (intransitive use) for which see <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="1ti+3:4">1 Timothy 3:4</span>.</p>
<p><b>Let be counted worthy </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">αξιουσθωσαν</span></span>). Present passive imperative of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">αξιοω</span></span>, to deem worthy (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="2th+1:11">2 Thessalonians 1:11</span>). With genitive case here.</p>
<p><b>Of double honour </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">διπλης τιμης</span></span>). Old and common contract adjective (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">διπλοος</span></span>, two-fold, in opposition to <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">απλοος</span></span>, single fold). But why "of double honour"? See <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="1ti+6:1">1 Timothy 6:1</span> for "of all honour." White suggests "remuneration" rather than "honour" for <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">τιμης</span></span> (a common use for price or pay). Liddon proposes "honorarium" (both honour and pay and so "double"). Wetstein gives numerous examples of soldiers receiving double pay for unusual services. Some suggest twice the pay given the enrolled widows.</p>
<p><b>Especially those who labour in word and teaching </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">μαλιστα ο κοπιωντες εν λογω κα διδασκαλια</span></span>). Either those who work hard or toil (usual meaning of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">κοπιαω</span></span>, <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="2ti+2:6">2 Timothy 2:6</span>) in preaching and teaching (most probable meaning. See verse <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="1ti+5:18">1 Timothy 5:18</span>) or those who teach and preach and not merely preside (a doubtful distinction in "elders" at this time). See <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="tit+1:8">Titus 1:8</span>. See both <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">κοπιαω</span></span> and <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">προισταμα</span></span> used for same men (elders) in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="1th+5:12">1 Thessalonians 5:12</span> and the use of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">κοπιαω</span></span> in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="1co+15:10">1 Corinthians 15:10</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="1co+16:16">1 Corinthians 16:16</span>. </p>
