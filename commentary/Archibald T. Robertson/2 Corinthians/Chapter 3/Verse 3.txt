<p><b> An epistle of Christ </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">επιστολη Χριστου</span></span>). He turns the metaphor round and round. They are Christ's letter to men as well as Paul's.</p>
<p><b>Not with ink </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ου μελαν</span></span>). Instrumental case of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">μελας</span></span>, black. Plato uses <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">το μελαν</span></span> for ink as here. See also <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="2jo+1:12">2 John 1:12</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="3jo+1:13">3 John 1:13</span>.</p>
<p><b>Of stone </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">λιθιναις</span></span>). Composed of stone (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">λιθος</span></span> and ending <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">-ινος</span></span>).</p>
<p><b>Of flesh </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">σαρκιναις</span></span>). "Fleshen" as in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="1co+3:1">1 Corinthians 3:1</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ro+7:14">Romans 7:14</span>. </p>
