<p><b> Wherefore we faint not </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">διο ουκ εγκακουμεν</span></span>). Repeats from verse <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="2co+4:1">2 Corinthians 4:1</span>.</p>
<p><b>Our outward man </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ο εξω ημων ανθρωπος</span></span>),</p>
<p><b>our inward man </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ο εσω ημων</span></span>). In <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ro+7:22">Romans 7:22</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="col+3:9">Colossians 3:9</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="eph+4:22">Ephesians 4:22</span>, we have the inward man and the outward for the higher and the lower natures (the spirit and the flesh). "Here the decay (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">διαφθειρετα</span></span>) of the bodily organism is set over against the growth in grace (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ανακαινουτα</span></span>, is refreshed) of the man himself" (Bernard). Plato (<i>Republ</i>. ix, p. 589) has <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ο εντος ανθρωπος</span></span>. Cf. "the hidden man of the heart" (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="1pe+3:4">1 Peter 3:4</span>).</p>
<p><b>Day by day </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ημερα κα ημερα</span></span>). This precise idiom is not in LXX nor rest of N.T. It may be colloquial use of locative in repetition. </p>
