<p><b> I thought </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εγησαμην</span></span>). Epistolary aorist again. See <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="php+2:25">Philippians 2:25</span> for the expression here.</p>
<p><b>Go before </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">προελθωσιν</span></span>). Second aorist active of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">προερχομα</span></span>. Go to you before I come.</p>
<p><b>Make up beforehand </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">προκαταρτισωσ</span></span>). Late and rare double compound verb <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">προκαταρτιζω</span></span> (in Hippocrates). Only here in N.T. See <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">καταρτιζω</span></span> in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="1co+1:10">1 Corinthians 1:10</span>.</p>
<p><b>Your afore-promised bounty </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">την προεπηγγελμενην ευλογιαν υμων</span></span>). "Blessing" (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ευλογια</span></span>) literally, but applied to good deeds also as well as good words (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ge+33:11">Genesis 33:11</span>). Note third use of "pro" before. He literally rubs it in that the pledge was overdue.</p>
<p><b>That the same might be ready </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ταυτην ετοιμην εινα</span></span>). Here the infinitive alone (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εινα</span></span>) is used to express purpose without <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ωστε</span></span> or <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εις το</span></span> or <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">προς το</span></span> with the accusative of general reference (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ταυτην</span></span>). The feminine form <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ετοιμην</span></span> is regular (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="1pe+1:5">1 Peter 1:5</span>) though <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ετοιμος</span></span> also occurs with the feminine like the masculine (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mt+25:10">Matthew 25:10</span>).</p>
<p><b>And not of extortion </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">κα μη ως πλεονεξιαν</span></span>). "And not as covetousness." Some offerings exhibit covetousness on the part of the giver by their very niggardliness. </p>
