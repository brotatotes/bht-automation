<p><b> Simon Peter </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">Σιμων Πετρος</span></span>). Aleph A K L P have <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">Σψμεων</span></span> as in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ac+15:14">Acts 15:14</span>, while B has <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">Σιμων</span></span>. The two forms occur indifferently in I Macc. 2:3, 65 for the same man.</p>
<p><b>Servant and apostle </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">δουλος κα αποστολος</span></span>). Like <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ro+1:1">Romans 1:1</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="tit+1:1">Titus 1:1</span>.</p>
<p><b>To them that have obtained </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">τοις λαχουσιν</span></span>). Dative plural articular participle second aorist active of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">λαγχανω</span></span>, old verb, to obtain by lot (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="lu+1:9">Luke 1:9</span>), here with the accusative (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">πιστιν</span></span>) as in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ac+1:17">Acts 1:17</span>.</p>
<p><b>Like precious </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ισοτιμον</span></span>). Late compound adjective (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ισος</span></span>, equal, <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">τιμη</span></span>, honor, price), here only in N.T. But this adjective (Field) is used in two ways, according to the two ideas in <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">τιμη</span></span> (value, honor), either like in value or like in honor. This second idea is the usual one with <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ισοτιμος</span></span> (inscriptions and papyri, Josephus, Lucian), while <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">πολυτιμος</span></span> has the notion of price like <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">τιμη</span></span> in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="2pe+1:7">2 Peter 1:7</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="2pe+1:19">2 Peter 1:19</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="2pe+2:4">2 Peter 2:4</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="2pe+2:6">2 Peter 2:6</span>. The faith which they have obtained is like in honor and privilege with that of Peter or any of the apostles.</p>
<p><b>With us </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ημιν</span></span>). Associative-instrumental case after <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ισοτιμον</span></span>. Equal to <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">τη ημων</span></span> (the faith of us).</p>
<p><b>In the righteousness </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εν δικαιοσυνη</span></span>). Definite because of the preposition <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εν</span></span> and the following genitive even though anarthrous. The O.T. sense of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">δικαιοσυνη</span></span> applied to God (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ro+1:17">Romans 1:17</span>) and here to Christ.</p>
<p><b>Of our God and Saviour Jesus Christ </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">του θεου ημων κα σωτηρος Ιησου Χριστου</span></span>). So the one article (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">του</span></span>) with <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">θεου</span></span> and <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">σωτηρος</span></span> requires precisely as with <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">του κυριου ημων κα σωτηρος Ιησου Χριστου</span></span> (of our Lord and Saviour Jesus Christ), one person, not two, in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="2pe+1:11">2 Peter 1:11</span> as in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="2pe+2:20">2 Peter 2:20</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="2pe+3:2">2 Peter 3:2</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="2pe+3:18">2 Peter 3:18</span>. So in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="1pe+1:3">1 Peter 1:3</span> we have <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ο θεος κα πατηρ</span></span> (the God and Father), one person, not two. The grammar is uniform and inevitable (Robertson, <i>Grammar</i>, p. 786), as even Schmiedel (Winer-Schmiedel, <i>Grammatik</i>, p. 158) admits: "Grammar demands that one person be meant." Moulton (<i>Prol.</i>, p. 84) cites papyri examples of like usage of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">θεος</span></span> for the Roman emperors. See the same idiom in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="tit+2:13">Titus 2:13</span>. The use of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">θεος</span></span> by Peter as a predicate with Jesus Christ no more disproves the Petrine authorship of this Epistle than a like use in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="joh+1:1">John 1:1</span> disproves the Johannine authorship of the Fourth Gospel and the same use in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="tit+2:13">Titus 2:13</span> disproves the genuineness of Titus. Peter had heard Thomas call Jesus God (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="joh+20:28">John 20:28</span>) and he himself had called him the Son of God (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mt+16:16">Matthew 16:16</span>). </p>
