<p><b> It has happened </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">συμβεβηκεν</span></span>). Perfect active indicative of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">συμβαινω</span></span>, for which see <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="1pe+4:12">1 Peter 4:12</span>.</p>
<p><b>According to the true proverb </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">το της αληθους παροιμιας</span></span>). "The word (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">το</span></span> used absolutely, the matter of, as in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mt+21:21">Matthew 21:21</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="jas+4:14">James 4:14</span>) of the true proverb" (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">παροιμια</span></span> a wayside saying, for which see <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="joh+10:6">John 10:6</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="joh+16:25">John 16:25</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="joh+16:29">John 16:29</span>). The first proverb here given comes from <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="pr+26:11">Proverbs 26:11</span>. <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">Εξεραμα</span></span> is a late and rare word (here only in N.T., in Diosc. and Eustath.) from <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εξεραω</span></span>, to vomit.</p>
<p><b>The sow that had washed </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">υς λουσαμενη</span></span>). <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">Hυς</span></span>, old word for hog, here only in N.T. Participle first aorist direct middle of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">λουω</span></span> shows that it is feminine (anarthrous). This second proverb does not occur in the O.T., probably from a Gentile source because about the habit of hogs. Epictetus and other writers moralize on the habit of hogs, having once bathed in a filthy mud-hole, to delight in it.</p>
<p><b>To wallowing </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εις κυλισμον</span></span>). "To rolling." Late and rare word (from <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">κυλιω</span></span>, <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mr+9:20">Mark 9:20</span>), here only in N.T.</p>
<p><b>In the mire </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">βορβορου</span></span>). Objective genitive, old word for dung, mire, here only in N.T. J. Rendel Harris (<i>Story of Ahikar</i>, p. LXVII) tells of a story about a hog that went to the bath with people of quality, but on coming out saw a stinking drain and went and rolled himself in it. </p>
