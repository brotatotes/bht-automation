<p><b> The day of the Lord </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ημερα κυριου</span></span>). So Peter in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ac+2:20">Acts 2:20</span> (from <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="joe+3:4">Joel 3:4</span>) and Paul in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="1th+5:2">1 Thessalonians 5:2</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="1th+5:4">1 Thessalonians 5:4</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="2th+2:2">2 Thessalonians 2:2</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="1co+5:5">1 Corinthians 5:5</span>; and day of Christ in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="php+2:16">Philippians 2:16</span> and day of God in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="2pe+2:12">2 Peter 2:12</span> and day of judgment already in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="2pe+2:9">2 Peter 2:9</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="2pe+3:7">2 Peter 3:7</span>. This great day will certainly come (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ηξε</span></span>). Future active of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ηκω</span></span>, old verb, to arrive, but in God's own time.</p>
<p><b>As a thief </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ως κλεπτης</span></span>). That is suddenly, without notice. This very metaphor Jesus had used (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="lu+12:39">Luke 12:39</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mt+24:43">Matthew 24:43</span>) and Paul after him (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="1th+5:2">1 Thessalonians 5:2</span>) and John will quote it also (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="re+3:3">Revelation 3:3</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="re+16:15">Revelation 16:15</span>).</p>
<p><b>In the which </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εν η</span></span>). The day when the Lord comes.</p>
<p><b>Shall pass away </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">παρελευσοντα</span></span>). Future middle of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">παρερχομα</span></span>, old verb, to pass by.</p>
<p><b>With a great noise </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ροιζηδον</span></span>). Late and rare adverb (from <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ροιζεω, ροιζος</span></span>)-- Lycophron, Nicander, here only in N.T., onomatopoetic, whizzing sound of rapid motion through the air like the flight of a bird, thunder, fierce flame.</p>
<p><b>The elements </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">τα στοιχεια</span></span>). Old word (from <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">στοιχος</span></span> a row), in Plato in this sense, in other senses also in N.T. as the alphabet, ceremonial regulations (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="heb+5:12">Hebrews 5:12</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ga+4:3">Galatians 4:3</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ga+5:1">Galatians 5:1</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="col+2:8">Colossians 2:8</span>).</p>
<p><b>Shall be dissolved </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">λυθησετα</span></span>). Future passive of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">λυω</span></span>, to loosen, singular because <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">στοιχεια</span></span> is neuter plural.</p>
<p><b>With fervent heat </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">καυσουμενα</span></span>). Present passive participle of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">καυσοω</span></span>, late verb (from <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">καυσος</span></span>, usually medical term for fever) and nearly always employed for fever temperature. Mayor suggests a conflagration from internal heat. Bigg thinks it merely a vernacular (Doric) future for <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">καυσομενα</span></span> (from <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">καιω</span></span>, to burn).</p>
<p><b>Shall be burned up </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">κατακαησετα</span></span>). Repeated in verse <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="2pe+3:12">2 Peter 3:12</span>. Second future passive of the compound verb <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">κατακαιω</span></span>, to burn down (up), according to A L. But Aleph B K P read <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ευρεθησετα</span></span> (future passive of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ευρισκω</span></span>, to find) "shall be found." There are various other readings here. The text seems corrupt. </p>
