<p><b> Who </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">οιτινες</span></span>). Qualitative use, such as. Vanishing in papyri though surviving in Paul (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="1co+3:17">1 Corinthians 3:17</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ro+1:25">Romans 1:25</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ga+4:26">Galatians 4:26</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="php+4:3">Philippians 4:3</span>).</p>
<p><b>Shall suffer punishment </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">δικην τισουσιν</span></span>). Future active of old verb <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">τινω</span></span>, to pay penalty (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">δικην</span></span>, right, justice), here only in N.T., but <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">αποτινω</span></span> once also to repay <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="phm+1:19">Philemon 1:19</span>. In the papyri <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">δικη</span></span> is used for a case or process in law. This is the regular phrase in classic writers for paying the penalty.</p>
<p><b>Eternal destruction </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ολεθρον αιωνιον</span></span>). Accusative case in apposition with <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">δικην</span></span> (penalty). This phrase does not appear elsewhere in the N.T., but is in IV Macc. 10:15 <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">τον αιωνιον του τυραννου ολεθρον</span></span> the eternal destruction of the tyrant (Antiochus Epiphanes). Destruction (cf. <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="1th+5:3">1 Thessalonians 5:3</span>) does not mean here annihilation, but, as Paul proceeds to show, separation</p>
<p><b>from the face of the Lord </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">απο προσωπου του κυριου</span></span>) and from the</p>
<p><b>glory of his might </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">κα απο της δοξης της ισχυος αυτου</span></span>), an eternity of woe such as befell Antiochus Epiphanes. <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">Αιωνιος</span></span> in itself only means age-long and papyri and inscriptions give it in the weakened sense of a Caesar's life (Milligan), but Paul means by age-long</p>
<p><b>the coming age </b> in contrast with</p>
<p><b>this age </b>, as</p>
<p><b>eternal </b> as the New Testament knows how to make it. See on <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mt+25:46">Matthew 25:46</span> for use of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">αιωνιος</span></span> both with <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ζωην</span></span>, life, and <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">κολασιν</span></span>, punishment. </p>
