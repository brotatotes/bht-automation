<p><b> Give diligence </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">σπουδασον</span></span>). First aorist active imperative of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">σπουδαζω</span></span>, old word, as in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="1th+2:17">1 Thessalonians 2:17</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ga+2:10">Galatians 2:10</span>.</p>
<p><b>To present </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">παραστησα</span></span>). First aorist active infinitive of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">παριστημ</span></span> as in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="col+1:22">Colossians 1:22</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="col+1:28">Colossians 1:28</span>.</p>
<p><b>Approved unto God </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">δοκιμον τω θεω</span></span>). Dative case <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">θεω</span></span> with <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">δοκιμον</span></span>, predicate accusative, old adjective (from <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">δεχομα</span></span>), for which see <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="1co+11:19">1 Corinthians 11:19</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="2co+10:18">2 Corinthians 10:18</span>.</p>
<p><b>A workman </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εργατην</span></span>). See <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="2co+11:3">2 Corinthians 11:3</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="php+3:2">Philippians 3:2</span>.</p>
<p><b>That needeth not to be ashamed </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ανεπαισχυντον</span></span>). Late double compound verbal adjective (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">α</span></span> privative, <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">επαισχυνω</span></span>), in Josephus and here alone.</p>
<p><b>Handling aright </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ορθοτομουντα</span></span>). Present active participle of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ορθοτομεω</span></span>, late and rare compound (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ορθοτομος</span></span>), cutting straight, <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ορθος</span></span> and <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">τεμνω</span></span>), here only in N.T. It occurs in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="pr+3:6">Proverbs 3:6</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="pr+11:5">Proverbs 11:5</span> for making straight paths (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">οδους</span></span>) with which compare <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="heb+12:13">Hebrews 12:13</span> and "the Way" in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ac+9:2">Acts 9:2</span>. Theodoret explains it to mean ploughing a straight furrow. Parry argues that the metaphor is the stone mason cutting the stones straight since <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">τεμνω</span></span> and <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ορθος</span></span> are so used. Since Paul was a tent-maker and knew how to cut straight the rough camel-hair cloth, why not let that be the metaphor? Certainly plenty of exegesis is crooked enough (crazy-quilt patterns) to call for careful cutting to set it straight. </p>
