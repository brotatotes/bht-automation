<p><b> He charged </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">παρηγγειλεν</span></span>). First aorist active indicative as in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ac+1:4">Acts 1:4</span>. There Jesus is the subject and so probably here, though Page insists that <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ο θεος</span></span> (God) is here because of verse <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ac+10:40">Acts 10:40</span>.</p>
<p><b>To testify </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">διαμαρτυρασθα</span></span>). First aorist middle infinitive. See on <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ac+2:40">Acts 2:40</span>.</p>
<p><b>Ordained </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ωρισμενος</span></span>). Perfect passive participle of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">οριζω</span></span>, old verb, to mark out, to limit, to make a horizon.</p>
<p><b>Judge </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">κριτης</span></span>). The same point made by Peter in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="1pe+4:5">1 Peter 4:5</span>. He does not use the word "Messiah" to these Gentiles though he did say "anointed" (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εχρισεν</span></span>) in verse <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ac+10:38">Acts 10:38</span>. Peter's claim for Jesus is that he is the Judge of Jew and Gentile (living and dead). </p>
