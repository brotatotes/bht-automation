<p><b> Of all guile </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">παντος δολου</span></span>). From <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">δελω</span></span>, to catch with bait, old word, already seen in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mt+26:4">Matthew 26:4</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mr+7:22">Mark 7:22</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mr+14:1">Mark 14:1</span>. Paul denounces Elymas as a trickster.</p>
<p><b>All villainy </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">πασης ραιδιουργιας</span></span>). Late compound from <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ραιδιουργος</span></span> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ραιδιος</span></span>, easy, facile, <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εργον</span></span>, deed, one who does a thing adroitly and with ease). So levity in Xenophon and unscrupulousness in Polybius, Plutarch, and the papyri. Only here in the N.T., though the kindred word <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ραιδιουργημα</span></span> occurs in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ac+18:14">Acts 18:14</span>. With deadly accuracy Paul pictured this slick rascal.</p>
<p><b>Thou son of the devil </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">υιε διαβολου</span></span>). Damning phrase like that used by Jesus of the Pharisees in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="joh+8:44">John 8:44</span>, a slanderer like the <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">διαβολος</span></span>. This use of son (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">υιος</span></span>) for characteristic occurs in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ac+3:25">Acts 3:25</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ac+4:36">Acts 4:36</span>, a common Hebrew idiom, and may be used purposely by Paul in contrast with the name Barjesus (son of Jesus) that Elymas bore (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ac+13:6">Acts 13:6</span>).</p>
<p><b>Enemy of all righteousness </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εχθρε πασης δικαιοσυνης</span></span>). Personal enemy to all justice, sums up all the rest. Note triple use of "all" (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">παντοσ, πασησ, πασης</span></span>), total depravity in every sense.</p>
<p><b>Wilt thou not cease? </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ου παυση</span></span>). An impatient rhetorical question, almost volitive in force (Robertson, <i>Grammar</i>, p. 874). Note <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ου</span></span>, not <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">μη</span></span>,</p>
<p><b>To pervert </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">διαστρεφων</span></span>). Present active participle describing the actual work of Elymas as a perverter or distorter (see verse <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ac+13:8">Acts 13:8</span>). More exactly, Wilt thou not cease perverting?</p>
<p><b>The right ways of the Lord </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">τας οδους του κυριου τας ευθειας</span></span>). The ways of the Lord the straight ones as opposed to the crooked ways of men (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="isa+40:4">Isaiah 40:4</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="isa+42:16">Isaiah 42:16</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="lu+3:5">Luke 3:5</span>). The task of John the Baptist as of all prophets and preachers is to make crooked paths straight and to get men to walk in them. This false prophet was making even the Lord's straight ways crooked. Elymas has many successors. </p>
