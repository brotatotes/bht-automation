<p><b> To this agree </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">τουτω συμφωνουσιν</span></span>). Associative instrumental case (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">τουτω</span></span>) after <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">συμφωνουσιν</span></span> (voice together with, symphony with, harmonize with), from <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">συμφωνεω</span></span>, old verb seen already in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mt+18:19">Matthew 18:19</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="lu+5:36">Luke 5:36</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ac+5:9">Acts 5:9</span> which see. James cites only <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="am+9:11">Amos 9:11</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="am+9:12">Amos 9:12</span> from the LXX as an example of "the words of the prophets" (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ο λογο των προφητων</span></span>) to which he refers on this point. The somewhat free quotation runs here through verses <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ac+15:16-18">Acts 15:16-18</span> of <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ac+15:15">Acts 15:15</span> and is exceedingly pertinent. The Jewish rabbis often failed to understand the prophets as Jesus showed. The passage in Amos refers primarily to the restoration of the Davidic empire, but also the Messiah's Kingdom (the throne of David his father," <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="lu+1:32">Luke 1:32</span>). </p>
