<p><b> But they that conducted Paul </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ο δε καθιστανοντες τον Παυλον</span></span>). Articular present active participle of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">καθιστανω</span></span> (late form in A B of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">καθιστημ</span></span> or <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">καθισταω</span></span>), an old verb with varied uses to put down, to constitute, to conduct, etc. This use here is in the LXX (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="jos+6:23">Joshua 6:23</span>) and old Greek also.</p>
<p><b>To Athens </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εως Αθηνων</span></span>). To make sure of his safe arrival.</p>
<p><b>That they should come to him with all speed </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ινα ως ταχιστα ελθωσιν προς αυτον</span></span>). Note the neat Greek idiom <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ως ταχιστα</span></span> as quickly as possible (good Attic idiom). The indirect command and purpose (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ινα-ελθωσιν</span></span>, second aorist active subjunctive) is also neat Greek (Robertson, <i>Grammar</i>, p. 1046).</p>
<p><b>Departed </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εξηιεσαν</span></span>). Imperfect active of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εξειμ</span></span>, old Greek word, but rare in N.T.
