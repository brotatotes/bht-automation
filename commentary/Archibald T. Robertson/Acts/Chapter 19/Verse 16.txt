<p><b> Leaped on them </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εφαλομενος επ' αυτους</span></span>). Second aorist (ingressive) middle participle of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εφαλλομα</span></span>, old verb to spring upon like a panther, here only in the N.T.</p>
<p><b>Mastered </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">κατακυριευσας</span></span>). First aorist (effective) active participle of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">κατακυριευω</span></span>, late verb from <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">κατα</span></span> and <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">κυριος</span></span>, to become lord or master of.</p>
<p><b>Both </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">αμφοτερων</span></span>). Papyri examples exist where <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">αμφοτερο</span></span> means "all" or more than "two" (Robertson, <i>Grammar</i>, p. 745). So here <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">αμφοτερο</span></span> includes all seven. "Both" in old English was used for more than two.</p>
<p><b>So that </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ωστε</span></span>). Another example (verses <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ac+19:10">Acts 19:10</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ac+19:11">Acts 19:11</span>) of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ωστε</span></span> with the infinitive for result.</p>

<p><b>Wounded </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">τετραυματισμενους</span></span>). Perfect passive participle of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">τραυματιζω</span></span>, old verb to wound, from <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">τραυμα</span></span> (a wound). In the N.T. only here and <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="lu+20:12">Luke 20:12</span>. </p>
