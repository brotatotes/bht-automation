<p><b> Were parted from them </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">αποσπασθεντας απ' αυτων</span></span>). First aorist passive participle of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">αποσπαω</span></span> same verb as in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ac+20:30">Acts 20:30</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="lu+22:41">Luke 22:41</span>.</p>
<p><b>Had set sail </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">αναχθηνα</span></span>). First aorist passive of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">αναγω</span></span>, the usual verb to put out (up) to sea as in verse <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ac+21:2">Acts 21:2</span> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ανηχθημεν</span></span>).</p>
<p><b>We came with a straight course </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ευθυδρομησαντες ηλθομεν</span></span>). The same verb (aorist active participle of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ευθυδρομεω</span></span>) used by Luke in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ac+16:11">Acts 16:11</span> of the voyage from Troas to Samothrace and Neapolis, which see.</p>
<p><b>Unto Cos </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εις την Κο</span></span>). Standing today, about forty nautical miles south from Miletus, island famous as the birthplace of Hippocrates and Apelles with a great medical school. Great trading place with many Jews.</p>
<p><b>The next day </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">τη εξης</span></span>). Locative case with <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ημερα</span></span> (day) understood. The adverb <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εξης</span></span> is from <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εχω</span></span> (future <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εξω</span></span>) and means successively or in order. This is another one of Luke's ways of saying "on the next day" (cf. three others in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ac+20:15">Acts 20:15</span>).</p>
<p><b>Unto Rhodes </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εις την Ροδον</span></span>). Called the island of roses. The sun shone most days and made roses luxuriant. The great colossus which represented the sun, one of the seven wonders of the world, was prostrate at this time. The island was at the entrance to the Aegean Sea and had a great university, especially for rhetoric and oratory. There was great commerce also.</p>
<p><b>Unto Patara </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εις Παταρα</span></span>). A seaport on the Lycian coast on the left bank of the Xanthus. It once had an oracle of Apollo which rivalled that at Delphi. This was the course taken by hundreds of ships every season. </p>
