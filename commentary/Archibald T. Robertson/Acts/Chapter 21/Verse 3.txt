<p><b> When we had come in sight of Cyprus </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">αναφαναντες την Κυπρον</span></span>). First aorist active participle of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">αναφαινω</span></span> (Doric form <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">-φαναντες</span></span> rather than the Attic <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">-φηναντες</span></span>), old verb to make appear, bring to light, to manifest. Having made Cyprus visible or rise up out of the sea. Nautical terms. In the N.T. only here and <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="lu+19:11">Luke 19:11</span> which see.</p>
<p><b>On the left hand </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ευωνυμον</span></span>). Compound feminine adjective like masculine. They sailed south of Cyprus.</p>
<p><b>We sailed </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">επλεομεν</span></span>). Imperfect active of common verb <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">πλεω</span></span>, kept on sailing till we came to Syria.</p>
<p><b>Landed at Tyre </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">κατηλθομεν εις Τυρον</span></span>). Came down to Tyre. Then a free city of Syria in honour of its former greatness (cf. the long siege by Alexander the Great).</p>
<p><b>There </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εκεισε</span></span>). Thither, literally. Only one other instance in N.T., <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ac+22:5">Acts 22:5</span> which may be pertinent = <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εκε</span></span> (there).</p>
<p><b>Was to unlade </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ην αποφορτιζομενον</span></span>). Periphrastic imperfect middle of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">αποφορτιζω</span></span>, late verb from <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">απο</span></span> and <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">φορτος</span></span>, load, but here only in the N.T. Literally, "For thither the boat was unloading her cargo," a sort of "customary" or "progressive" imperfect (Robertson, <i>Grammar</i>, p. 884).</p>
<p><b>Burden </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">γομον</span></span>). Cargo, old word, from <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">γεμω</span></span>, to be full. Only here and <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="re+18:11">Revelation 18:11</span> in N.T. Probably a grain or fruit ship. It took seven days here to unload and reload. </p>
