<p><b> I am </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">Εγω μεν ειμ</span></span>). In contrast with the wild guess of Lysias Paul uses <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">μεν</span></span> and <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">δε</span></span>. He tells briefly who he is:</p>
<p><b>a Jew </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">Ιουδαιος</span></span>) by race,</p>
<p><b>of Tarsus in Cilicia </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">Ταρσευς της Κιλικιας</span></span>) by country, belonging to Tarsus (this adjective <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">Ταρσευς</span></span> only here and <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ac+9:11">Acts 9:11</span>), and proud of it, one of the great cities of the empire with a great university.</p>
<p><b>A citizen of no mean city </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ουκ ασημου πολεως πολιτης</span></span>). Litotes again, "no mean" (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ασημος</span></span>, old adjective, unmarked, <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">α</span></span> privative and <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">σημα</span></span>, mark, insignificant, here only in the N.T.). This same litotes used by Euripides of Athens (<i>Ion</i> 8). But Paul calls himself a citizen (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">πολιτης</span></span>) of Tarsus. Note the "effective assonance" (Page) in <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">πολεως πολιτης</span></span>. Paul now (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">δε</span></span>) makes his request (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">δεομα</span></span>) of Lysias.</p>
<p><b>Give me leave </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">επιτρεψον μο</span></span>). First aorist active imperative of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">επιτρεπω</span></span>, old and common verb to turn to, to permit, to allow. It was a strange request and a daring one, to wish to speak to this mob howling for Paul's blood. </p>
