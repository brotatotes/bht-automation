<p><b> When I had returned </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">μο υποστρεψαντ</span></span>),</p>
<p><b>while I prayed </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">προσευχομενου μου</span></span>),</p>
<p><b>I fell </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">γενεσθα με</span></span>). Note dative <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">μο</span></span> with <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εγενετο</span></span> as in verse <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ac+22:6">Acts 22:6</span>, genitive <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">μου</span></span> (genitive absolute with <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">προσευχομενου</span></span>), accusative of general reference <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">με</span></span> with <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">γενεσθα</span></span>, and with no effort at uniformity, precisely as in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ac+15:22">Acts 15:22</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ac+15:23">Acts 15:23</span> which see. The participle is especially liable to such examples of anacolutha (Robertson, <i>Grammar</i>, p. 439). </p>
