<p><b> Seeing that thou canst take knowledge </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">δυναμενου σου επιγνωνα</span></span>). Genitive absolute again. The same word and form (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">επιγνωνα</span></span>) used by Tertullus, if in Greek, in verse <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ac+24:8">Acts 24:8</span> to Felix. Paul takes it up and repeats it.</p>
<p><b>Not more than twelve days </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ου πλειους ημερα δωδεκα</span></span>). Here <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">η</span></span> (than) is absent without change of case to the ablative as usually happens. But this idiom is found in the <i>Koine</i> (Robertson, <i>Grammar</i>, p. 666).</p>
<p><b>Since </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">αφ' ης</span></span>). Supply <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ημερας</span></span>, "from which day."</p>
<p><b>To worship </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">προσκυνησων</span></span>). One of the few examples of the future participle of purpose so common in the old Attic. </p>
