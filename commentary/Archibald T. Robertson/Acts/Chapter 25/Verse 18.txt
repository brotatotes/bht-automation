<p><b> Brought </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εφερον</span></span>). Imperfect active of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">φερω</span></span>, referring to their repeated charges.</p>
<p><b>Of such evil things as I supposed </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ων εγω υπενοουν πονηρων</span></span>). Incorporation of the antecedent <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">πονηρων</span></span> into the relative clause and change of the case of the relative from the accusative <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">α</span></span> object of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">υπενοουν</span></span> to the genitive like <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">πονηρων</span></span> (Robertson, <i>Grammar</i>, p. 719). Note the imperfect active <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">υπενοουν</span></span> of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">υπονοεω</span></span> to emphasize Festus's state of mind about Paul before the trial. This old verb only three times in the N.T. (here, <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ac+13:25">Acts 13:25</span> which see; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ac+27:27">Acts 27:27</span>). </p>
