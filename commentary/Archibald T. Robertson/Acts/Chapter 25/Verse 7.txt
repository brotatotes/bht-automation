<p><b> When he was come </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">παραγενομενου αυτου</span></span>). Genitive absolute of common verb <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">παραγινομα</span></span> (cf. <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ac+24:24">Acts 24:24</span>).</p>
<p><b>Which had come down </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ο καταβεβηκοτες</span></span>). Perfect active participle of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">καταβαινω</span></span>. They had come down on purpose at the invitation of Festus (verse <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ac+25:5">Acts 25:5</span>), and were now ready.</p>
<p><b>Stood round about him </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">περιεστησαν αυτον</span></span>). Second aorist (ingressive) active (intransitive) of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">περιιστημ</span></span>, old verb, "Took their stand around him," "<i>periculum intentantes</i>" (Bengel). Cf. <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="lu+23:10">Luke 23:10</span> about Christ. They have no lawyer this time, but they mass their forces so as to impress Festus.</p>
<p><b>Bringing against him </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">καταφεροντες</span></span>). Bearing down on. See on <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ac+20:9">Acts 20:9</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ac+26:10">Acts 26:10</span>, only N.T. examples of this ancient verb.</p>
<p><b>Many and grievous charges </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">πολλα κα βαρεα αιτιωματα</span></span>). This word <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">αιτιωμα</span></span> for old form <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">αιτιαμα</span></span> is found in one papyrus (Moulton and Milligan's <i>Vocabulary</i>) in sense of "blame." But the charges were no "heavier" than those made by Tertullus (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ac+24:5-8">Acts 24:5-8</span>). Paul's reply proves this and they were also probably on court record (Furneaux). See this adjective <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">βαρυς</span></span> (heavy) used with <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">λυκο</span></span> (wolves) in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ac+20:29">Acts 20:29</span>.</p>
<p><b>Which they could not prove </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">α ουκ ισχυον αποδειξα</span></span>). Imperfect active of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ισχυω</span></span>, to have strength or power as in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ac+19:16">Acts 19:16</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ac+19:20">Acts 19:20</span>. Repetition and reiteration and vehemence took the place of proof (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">αποδειξα</span></span>, first aorist active infinitive of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">αποδεικνυμ</span></span>, to show forth, old verb, in N.T. only here, <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ac+2:22">Acts 2:22</span> which see and <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="1co+4:9">1 Corinthians 4:9</span>). </p>
