<p><b> When the ship was caught </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">συναρπασθεντος του πλοιου</span></span>). Genitive absolute again with first aorist passive of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">συναρπαζω</span></span>, old word, in N.T. only <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="lu+8:29">Luke 8:29</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ac+6:12">Acts 6:12</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ac+19:29">Acts 19:29</span>, and here. Graphic picture as if the ship was seized by a great monster.</p>
<p><b>Face the wind </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">αντοφθαλμειν τω ανεμω</span></span>). Dative case with the vivid infinitive of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">αντοφθαλμεω</span></span> from <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">αντοφθαλμος</span></span>, looking in the eye, or eye to eye (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">αντ</span></span>, facing and <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">οπθαλμος</span></span>, eye). Eyes were painted on the prows of vessels. The ship could not face the wind enough to get to Phoenix. Modern sailors talk of sailing into the eye of the wind. We were not able to look the wind in the eye. <i>Koine</i> verb used by Polybius. Some MSS. have it in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ac+6:11">Acts 6:11</span>, but only here in N.T. In Wisdom of Sol. 12:14 it is used of a prince who cannot look God in the face. Clement of Rome 34 uses it of an idle workman not able to look his employer in the face (Milligan and Moulton's <i>Vocabulary</i>).</p>
<p><b>We gave way </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">επιδοντες</span></span>). Second aorist active participle of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">επιδιδωμ</span></span>, giving way to the wind.</p>
<p><b>Were driven </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εφερομεθα</span></span>). Imperfect passive of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">φερω</span></span>, "we were being borne along." We "scudded before the gale" (Page). "The suddenness of the hurricane gave no time to furl the great mainsail" (Furneaux). </p>
