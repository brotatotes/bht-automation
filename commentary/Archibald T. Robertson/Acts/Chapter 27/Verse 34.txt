<p><b> For this is for your safety </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">τουτο γαρ προς της υμετερας σωτηριας υπαρχε</span></span>). Note <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">σωτηρια</span></span> in sense of "safety," literal meaning, not spiritual salvation. This is the only instance in the N.T. of the use of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">προς</span></span> with the ablative meaning "from the side of" your safety, though a classic idiom (Robertson, <i>Grammar</i>, p. 623), an example of Luke's literary style.</p>
<p><b>Perish </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">απολειτα</span></span>). Future middle (intransitive) of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">απολλυμ (-υω)</span></span>, to destroy. So the oldest MSS. rather than <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">πεσειτα</span></span> from <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">πιπτω</span></span>, to fall. This proverbial expression occurs also in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="lu+21:18">Luke 21:18</span> which see and in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="1sa+14:45">1 Samuel 14:45</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="2sa+14:11">2 Samuel 14:11</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="1ki+1:52">1 Kings 1:52</span>. </p>
