<p><b> Which had wintered </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">παρακεχειμακοτ</span></span>). Perfect active participle of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">παραχειμαζω</span></span>, to pass the winter. Old verb, in N.T. only <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ac+27:12">Acts 27:12</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ac+28:11">Acts 28:11</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="1co+16:6">1 Corinthians 16:6</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="tit+3:12">Titus 3:12</span>. The locative case agreeing with <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">πλοιω</span></span>. Navigation in the Mediterranean usually opened up in February (always by March), spring beginning on Feb. 9 (Page).</p>
<p><b>Whose sign was the Twin Brothers </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">παρασημω Διοσκουροις</span></span>). The word <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">παρασημω</span></span> can be either a substantive (as Revised Version has it) or an adjective "marked by the sign," examples of both uses common in ancient Greek. <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">Διοσκουροις</span></span> is in apposition with <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">παρασημω</span></span>. The word means the twin sons (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">κουρος</span></span> or <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">κορος</span></span>) of Zeus (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">Διος</span></span>, genitive of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">Ζευς</span></span>) and Leda, viz., Castor and Pollux. The Attic used the dual, <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">τω Διοσκορω</span></span>. Castor and Pollux were the tutelary deities of sailors whose figures were painted one on each side of the prow of the ship. This sign was the name of the ship. So they start in another grain ship of Alexandria bound for Rome. </p>
