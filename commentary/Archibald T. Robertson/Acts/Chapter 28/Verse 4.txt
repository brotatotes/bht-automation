<p><b> The beast </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">το θηριον</span></span>). Diminutive of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">θηρ</span></span> and so little beast. See on <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mr+1:13">Mark 1:13</span>. Aristotle and the medical writers apply the word to venomous serpents, the viper in particular (Knowling), as Luke does here. Vincent calls attention to the curious history of our word "<i>treacle</i>" for molasses (Latin <i>theriaca</i>) from <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">θηριακη</span></span>, an antidote made from the flesh of vipers. Coverdale translates <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="jer+8:22">Jeremiah 8:22</span>: "There is no more treacle in Gilead." Jeremy Taylor: "We kill the viper and make treacle of him."</p>
<p><b>Hanging from his hand </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">κρεμαμενον εκ της χειρος αυτου</span></span>). Vivid picture of the snake dangling from Paul's hand. Present middle participle of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">κρεμαμα</span></span>, late form for <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">κρεμαννυμ</span></span>, to hang up, to suspend (cf. <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ga+3:13">Galatians 3:13</span>).</p>
<p><b>No doubt </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">παντως</span></span>). Literally, By all means, old adverb. Cf. <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ac+21:22">Acts 21:22</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="lu+4:23">Luke 4:23</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="1co+9:22">1 Corinthians 9:22</span>. Only by Luke and Paul in the N.T. "They <i>knew</i> that he was a prisoner being taken to Rome on some grave charge, and <i>inferred</i> that the charge was murder" (Page).</p>
<p><b>Though he hath escaped </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">διασωθεντα</span></span>). First aorist passive participle of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">διασωζω</span></span> (same verb used in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ac+24:43">Acts 24:43</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ac+24:44">Acts 24:44</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ac+28:1">Acts 28:1</span>), so-called concessive use of the participle (Robertson, <i>Grammar</i>, p. 1129).</p>
<p><b>Yet Justice </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">δικη</span></span>). An abstraction personified like the Latin <i>Justitia</i> (Page). The natives speak of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">Δικη</span></span> as a goddess, but we know nothing of such actual worship in Malta, though the Greeks worshipped abstractions as in Athens.</p>
<p><b>Hath not suffered </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ουκ ειασεν</span></span>). Did not suffer. They look on Paul as a doomed man as good as dead. These people thought that calamity was proof of guilt, poor philosophy and worse theology. </p>
