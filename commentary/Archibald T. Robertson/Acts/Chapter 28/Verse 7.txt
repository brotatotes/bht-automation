<p><b> To the chief man of the island </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">τω πρωτω της νησου</span></span>). An official title correct in Malta (Ramsay, <i>St. Paul</i>, p. 343). An inscription in Malta calls Prudens "Primate of the Maltese" (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">πρωτος Μελιταιων</span></span>). Here it is plainly a title and not the common use seen in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ac+13:50">Acts 13:50</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ac+25:2">Acts 25:2</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ac+28:17">Acts 28:17</span>.</p>
<p><b>Publius </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">Ποπλιω</span></span>). This Greek name (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">πραενομεν</span></span>) can be derived either from <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">Ποπιλιυς</span></span> or <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">Πυβλιυς</span></span> (cf. <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">πυβλιχυς</span></span> for <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ποπυλιχυς</span></span> from <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ποπυλυς</span></span>). Entertained us (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εξενισεν ημας</span></span>). Paul and his companions (Luke and Aristarchus). Was Julius included? On <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ξενιζω</span></span> see <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ac+10:23">Acts 10:23</span>.</p>
<p><b>Courteously </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">φιλοφρονως</span></span>). This old adverb from <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">φιλοφρων</span></span> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">φιλοσ, φρεν</span></span>, friendly mind) occurs here alone in the N.T. In a kindly or friendly manner, all the more so because of the original suspicion of Paul as a criminal. </p>
