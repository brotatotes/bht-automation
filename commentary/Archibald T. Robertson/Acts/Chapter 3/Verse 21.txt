<p><b> Restoration </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">αποκαταστασεως</span></span>). Double compound (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">απο, κατα, ιστημ</span></span>), here only in the N.T., though common in late writers. In papyri and inscriptions for repairs to temples and this phrase occurs in Jewish apocalyptic writings, something like the new heaven and the new earth of <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="re+21:1">Revelation 21:1</span>. Paul has a mystical allusion also to the agony of nature in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ro+8:20-22">Romans 8:20-22</span>. The verb <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">αποκαθιστημ</span></span> is used by Jesus of the spiritual and moral restoration wrought by the Baptist as Elijah (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mt+17:11">Matthew 17:11</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mr+9:12">Mark 9:12</span>) and by the disciples to Jesus in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ac+1:6">Acts 1:6</span>. Josephus uses the word of the return from captivity and Philo of the restitution of inheritances in the year of jubilee. As a technical medical term it means complete restoration to health. See a like idea in <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">παλινγενεσια</span></span> (renewal, new birth) in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mt+19:28">Matthew 19:28</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="tit+3:5">Titus 3:5</span>. This universalism of Peter will be clearer to him after Joppa and Caesarea. </p>
