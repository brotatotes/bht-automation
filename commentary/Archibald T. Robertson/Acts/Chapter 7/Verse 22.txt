<p><b> Was instructed </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">επαιδευθη</span></span>). First aorist passive indicative of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">παιδευω</span></span>, to train a child (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">παις</span></span>), the usual idea in ancient Greek as here. The notion of chastisement (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="heb+12:6">Hebrews 12:6</span>) is also in the old Greek and especially in the LXX and the N.T. Here with instrumental case (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">παση σοφια</span></span>) or the locative. The accusative would usually be retained after this verb. The priestly caste in Egypt was noted for their knowledge of science, astronomy, medicine, and mathematics. This reputation was proverbial (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="1ki+4:30">1 Kings 4:30</span>). Modern discoveries have thrown much light on the ancient civilization of Egypt. Moses, like Paul, was a man of the schools.</p>
<p><b>Mighty in his words and works </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">δυνατος εν λογοις κα εργοις αυτου</span></span>). The same phrase used of Jesus in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="lu+24:19">Luke 24:19</span>. The adjective <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">δυνατος</span></span> is employed of Apollos as an interpreter of the Scriptures (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ac+18:24">Acts 18:24</span>). Moses did not have the rhetorical skill or eloquence of Aaron (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ex+4:10">Exodus 4:10</span>), but his words like his deeds carried weight and power. </p>
