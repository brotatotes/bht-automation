<p><b> The day following </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">τη επιουση ημερα</span></span>). Locative case, "on the following day" (from <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">επειμ</span></span>, to come upon, to approach, present active participle <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">επιων -ουσα, -ον</span></span>). Common phrase in old Greek both with <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ημερα</span></span> (day) as here and without as <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ac+16:11">Acts 16:11</span>. Only in Acts in the N.T.</p>
<p><b>Appeared </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ωφθη</span></span>). First aorist passive indicative of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">οραω</span></span> not with idea that only a vision but rather that it was sudden or unexpected.</p>
<p><b>As they strove </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">μαχομενοις</span></span>). Present middle participle of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">μαχομα</span></span>, actually fighting.</p>
<p><b>Would have set them at one again </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">συνηλλασσεν αυτους εις ειρηνεν</span></span>). Better, he tried to reconcile them (or change them into peace). It is the conative imperfect active as in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mt+3:14">Matthew 3:14</span> of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">συναλλασσω</span></span>, only here in the N.T. though common in the old Greek. Vulgate has <i>reconciliabat</i>. The usual word in the N.T. for reconcile is <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">καταλλασσω</span></span>.</p>
<p><b>Do ye wrong one to another </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">αδικειτε αλληλους</span></span>). The same word used in verse <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ac+7:24">Acts 7:24</span> of the wrong done one of the Hebrews by the Egyptian, but here both are "brethren." </p>
