<p><b> Kneeled down </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">θεις τα γονατα</span></span>). Second aorist active participle of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">τιθημ</span></span>, placing the knees (on the ground). This idiom is not in the old Greek for kneeling, but Luke has it five times (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="lu+22:41">Luke 22:41</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ac+7:60">Acts 7:60</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ac+9:40">Acts 9:40</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ac+22:36">Acts 22:36</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ac+21:5">Acts 21:5</span>) and Mark once (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ac+15:19">Acts 15:19</span>). Jesus was standing at the right hand of God and Stephen knelt before him in worship and called on him in prayer.</p>
<p><b>Lay not this sin to their charge </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">μη στησηις αυτοις ταυτην την αμαρτιαν</span></span>). First aorist (ingressive) active subjunctive with <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">μη</span></span>, regular Greek idiom, Place not to them or against them (dative <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">αυτοις</span></span>) this sin. The very spirit of Jesus towards his enemies as he died upon the Cross (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="lu+23:34">Luke 23:34</span>).</p>
<p><b>He fell asleep </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εκοιμηθη</span></span>). First aorist passive indicative of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">κοιμαω</span></span>, to put to sleep. Old verb and the metaphor of sleep for death is common in all languages, but it is peculiarly appropriate here as Jesus used it of Lazarus. See also <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ac+13:36">Acts 13:36</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="1co+15:18">1 Corinthians 15:18</span>, etc. Our word cemetery (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">κοιμητηριον</span></span>) is the sleeping place of the dead. Knowling calls <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εκοιμηθη</span></span> here "a picture word of rest and calmness which stands in dramatic contrast to the rage and violence of the scene." </p>
