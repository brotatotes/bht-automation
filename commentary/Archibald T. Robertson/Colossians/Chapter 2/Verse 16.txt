<p><b> Let no one judge you </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">μη τις υμας κρινετω</span></span>). Prohibition present active imperative third singular, forbidding the habit of passing judgment in such matters. For <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">κρινω</span></span> see on <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mt+7:1">Matthew 7:1</span>. Paul has here in mind the ascetic regulations and practices of one wing of the Gnostics (possibly Essenic or even Pharisaic influence). He makes a plea for freedom in such matters on a par with that in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="col+2:1">Colossians 2:1</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="col+2:14">Colossians 2:14</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="col+2:15">Colossians 2:15</span>. The Essenes went far beyond the Mosaic regulations. For the Jewish feasts see on <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ga+4:10">Galatians 4:10</span>. Josephus (<i>Ant</i>. III. 10, 1) expressly explains the "seventh day" as called "<i>sabbata</i>" (plural form as here, an effort to transliterate the Aramaic <i>sabbathah</i>). </p>
