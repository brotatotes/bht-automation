<p><b> And might reconcile </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">κα αποκαταλλαξη</span></span>). Final clause with <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ινα</span></span> understood of first aorist active subjunctive of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">αποκαταλλασσω</span></span> for which see <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="col+1:20">Colossians 1:20</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="col+1:22">Colossians 1:22</span>.</p>
<p><b>Them both </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">τους αμφοτερους</span></span>). "The both," "the two" (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">τους δυο</span></span>), Jew and Gentile.</p>
<p><b>In one body </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εν εν σωματ</span></span>). The "one new man" of verse <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="eph+2:15">Ephesians 2:15</span> of which Christ is Head (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="eph+1:23">Ephesians 1:23</span>), the spiritual church. Paul piles up metaphors to express his idea of the Kingdom of God with Christ as King (the church, the body, the commonwealth of Israel, oneness, one new man in Christ, fellow-citizens, the family of God, the temple of God).</p>
<p><b>Thereby </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εν αυτω</span></span>). On the Cross where he slew the enmity (repeated here) between Jew and Gentile. </p>
