<p><b> Under a curse </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">υπο καταραν</span></span>). Picture of the curse hanging over them like a Damocles' blade. Cf. <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ro+3:9">Romans 3:9</span> "under sin" (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">υφ' αμαρτιαν</span></span>). The word for "curse" (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">καταρα</span></span>) is an old one (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">κατα</span></span>, down, <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">αρα</span></span>, imprecation), often in LXX, in N.T. only here and <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ga+3:13">Galatians 3:13</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="jas+3:10">James 3:10</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="2pe+2:14">2 Peter 2:14</span>. Paul quotes <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="de+27:26">Deuteronomy 27:26</span>, the close of the curses on Mt. Ebal. He makes a slight explanatory modification of the LXX changing <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">λογοις</span></span> to <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">γεγραμμενοις εν τω βιβλιω</span></span>. The idea is made clearer by the participle (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">γεγραμμενοις</span></span>) and <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">βιβλιω</span></span> (book). The curse becomes effective only when the law is violated.</p>
<p><b>Cursed </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">επικαταρατος</span></span>). Verbal adjective from <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">επικαταραομα</span></span>, to imprecate curses, late word, common in LXX. In N.T. only here and verse <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ga+3:13">Galatians 3:13</span>, but in inscriptions also (Deissmann, <i>Light from the Ancient East</i>, p. 96). The emphasis is on "continueth" (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εμμενε</span></span>) and "all" (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">πασιν</span></span>). </p>
