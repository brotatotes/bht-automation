<p><b> Let us consider one another </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">κατανοωμεν αλληλους</span></span>). Present (keep on doing so) active volitive subjunctive of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">κατανοεω</span></span>. The verb used about Jesus in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="heb+3:1">Hebrews 3:1</span>.</p>
<p><b>To provoke </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εις παροξυσμον</span></span>). Our very word "paroxysm," from <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">παροξυνω</span></span> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">παρα, οξυνω</span></span> from <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">οξυς</span></span>, sharp), to sharpen, to stimulate, to incite. So here in good sense (for incitement to), but in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ac+15:39">Acts 15:39</span> the word is used of irritation or contention as in the LXX and Demosthenes. Hippocrates uses it for "paroxysm" in disease (so in the papyri).</p>
<p><b>Unto love and good works </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">αγαπης κα καλων εργων</span></span>). Objective genitive. So Paul seeks to stir up the Corinthians by the example of the Macedonians (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="2co+8:1-7">2 Corinthians 8:1-7</span>). </p>
