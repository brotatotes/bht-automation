<p><b> Consider </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">αναλογισασθε</span></span>). First aorist middle imperative of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">αναλογιζομα</span></span>, old word to reckon up, to compare, to weigh, only here in the N.T. See <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">κατανοησατε</span></span> in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="heb+3:1">Hebrews 3:1</span>. Understanding Jesus is the key to the whole problem, the cure for doubt and hesitation.</p>
<p><b>Endured </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">υπομεμενηκοτα</span></span>). Perfect active participle of the same verb <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">υπομενω</span></span> used in verse <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="heb+12:2">Hebrews 12:2</span>.</p>
<p><b>Gainsaying </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">αντιλογιαν</span></span>). Old word from <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">αντιλογος</span></span> (from <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">αντιλεγω</span></span>), already in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="heb+6:16">Hebrews 6:16</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="heb+7:7">Hebrews 7:7</span>.</p>
<p><b>Of sinners </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">υπο των αμαρτωλων</span></span>). "By sinners."</p>
<p><b>Against themselves </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εις εαυτους</span></span>). Against their better selves if a genuine reading. But <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εις εαυτον</span></span> (against himself), against Christ, is far more likely correct.</p>
<p><b>That ye wax not weary </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ινα μη καμητε</span></span>). Negative final clause with <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ινα μη</span></span> and the second aorist active subjunctive of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">καμνω</span></span>, old verb to be weary as here or sick as in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="jas+5:15">James 5:15</span>.</p>
<p><b>Fainting in your souls </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ταις ψυχαις υμων εκλυομενο</span></span>). Present passive participle of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εκλυω</span></span>, old verb to loosen out, to set free, and in passive to be enfeebled, to be tired out (here in soul with locative case), as in verse <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="heb+12:5">Hebrews 12:5</span>. The rest of the Epistle drives home the argument. </p>
