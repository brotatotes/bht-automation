<p><b> Brotherly love </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">φιλαδελφια</span></span>). Late word from <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">φιλαδελφος</span></span> (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="1pe+3:8">1 Peter 3:8</span>). See <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="1th+4:9">1 Thessalonians 4:9</span>. It is always in order in a church.</p>
<p><b>To show love unto strangers </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">της φιλοξενιας</span></span>). Old word for hospitality, from <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">φιλοξενος</span></span> (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="1ti+3:2">1 Timothy 3:2</span>), in N.T. only here and <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ro+12:3">Romans 12:3</span>. In genitive case with <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">επιλανθανεσθε</span></span> (present middle imperative, cf. <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="heb+6:10">Hebrews 6:10</span>).</p>
<p><b>Have entertained angels unawares </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ελαθον ξενισαντες αγγελους</span></span>). Second aorist active indicative of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">λανθανω</span></span>, old verb to escape notice and first aorist active participle of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ξενιζω</span></span>, old verb to entertain a guest (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ξενος</span></span>, stranger), according to a classic idiom seen with <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">λανθανω, τυγχανω, φθανω</span></span>, by which the chief idea is expressed by the participle (supplementary participle), here meaning, "some escaped notice when entertaining angels." The reference is to <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="heb+13:18">Hebrews 13:18</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="heb+13:19">Hebrews 13:19</span> (Abraham and Sarah did this very thing). </p>
