<p><b> Then </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ειτα</span></span>). The next step.</p>
<p><b>The lust </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">η επιθυμια</span></span>). Note article, the lust (verse <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="jas+1:14">James 1:14</span>) which one has.</p>
<p><b>When it hath conceived </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">συλλαβουσα</span></span>). Second aorist active participle of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">συλλαμβανω</span></span>, old word to grasp together, in hostile sense (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ac+26:21">Acts 26:21</span>), in friendly sense of help (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="php+4:3">Philippians 4:3</span>), in technical sense of a woman taking a man's seed in conception (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="lu+1:24">Luke 1:24</span>), here also of lust (as a woman), "having conceived." The will yields to lust and conception takes place.</p>
<p><b>Beareth sin </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">τικτε αμαρτιαν</span></span>). Present active indicative of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">τικτω</span></span> to bring forth as a mother or fruit from seed, old verb, often in N.T., here only in James. Sin is the union of the will with lust. See <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ps+7:14">Psalms 7:14</span> for this same metaphor.</p>
<p><b>The sin </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">η αμαρτια</span></span>). The article refers to <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">αμαρτια</span></span> just mentioned.</p>
<p><b>When it is full-grown </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">αποτελεσθεισα</span></span>). First aorist passive participle of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">αποτελεω</span></span>, old compound verb with perfective use of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">απο</span></span>, in N.T. only here and <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="lu+13:32">Luke 13:32</span>. It does not mean "full-grown" like <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">τελειοω</span></span>, but rather completeness of parts or functions as opposed to rudimentary state (Hort) like the winged insect in contrast with the chrysalis or grub (Plato). The sin at birth is fully equipped for its career (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ro+6:6">Romans 6:6</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="col+3:5">Colossians 3:5</span>).</p>
<p><b>Bringeth forth death </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">αποκυε θανατον</span></span>). Late compound (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">κυεω</span></span> to be pregnant, perfective use of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">απο</span></span>) to give birth to, of animals and women, for normal birth (papyrus example) and abnormal birth (Hort). A medical word (Ropes) rather than a literary one like <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">τικτω</span></span>. The child of lust is sin, of sin is death, powerful figure of abortion. The child is dead at birth. For death as the fruit of sin see <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ro+6:21-23">Romans 6:21-23</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ro+8:6">Romans 8:6</span>. "The birth of death follows of necessity when one sin is fully formed" (Hort). </p>
