<p><b> Gift </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">δοσις</span></span>)</p>
<p><b>--boon </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">δωρημα</span></span>). Both old substantives from the same original verb (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">διδωμ</span></span>), to give. <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">Δοσις</span></span> is the act of giving (ending <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">-σις</span></span>), but sometimes by metonymy for the thing given like <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">κτισις</span></span> for <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">κτισμα</span></span> (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="col+1:15">Colossians 1:15</span>). But <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">δωρημα</span></span> (from <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">δωρεω</span></span>, from <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">δωρον</span></span> a gift) only means a gift, a benefaction (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ro+5:16">Romans 5:16</span>). The contrast here argues for "giving" as the idea in <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">δοσις</span></span>. Curiously enough there is a perfect hexameter line here: <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">πασα δο / σις αγα / θη κα / παν δω / ρημα τε / λειον</span></span>. Such accidental rhythm occurs occasionally in many writers. Ropes (like Ewald and Mayor) argues for a quotation from an unknown source because of the poetical word <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">δωρημα</span></span>, but that is not conclusive.</p>
<p><b>From above </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ανωθεν</span></span>). That is, from heaven. Cf. <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="joh+3:31">John 3:31</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="joh+19:11">John 19:11</span>.</p>
<p><b>Coming down </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">καταβαινον</span></span>). Present active neuter singular participle of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">καταβαινω</span></span> agreeing with <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">δωρημα</span></span>, expanding and explaining <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ανωθεν</span></span> (from above).</p>
<p><b>From the Father of lights </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">απο του πατρος των φωτων</span></span>). "Of the lights" (the heavenly bodies). For this use of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">πατηρ</span></span> see <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="job+38:28">Job 38:28</span> (Father of rain); <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="2co+1:3">2 Corinthians 1:3</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="eph+1:17">Ephesians 1:17</span>. God is the Author of light and lights.</p>
<p><b>With whom </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">παρ' ω</span></span>). For <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">παρα</span></span> (beside) with locative sense for standpoint of God see <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">παρα τω θεω</span></span> (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mr+10:27">Mark 10:27</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ro+2:11">Romans 2:11</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ro+9:14">Romans 9:14</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="eph+6:9">Ephesians 6:9</span>.</p>
<p><b>Can be no </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ουκ εν</span></span>). This old idiom (also in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ga+3:28">Galatians 3:28</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="col+3:11">Colossians 3:11</span>) may be merely the original form of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εν</span></span> with recessive accent (Winer, Mayor) or a shortened form of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ενεστ</span></span>. The use of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εν εν</span></span> in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="1co+6:5">1 Corinthians 6:5</span> argues for this view, as does the use of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εινε</span></span> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εινα</span></span>) in Modern Greek (Robertson, <i>Grammar</i>, p. 313).</p>
<p><b>Variation </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">παραλλαγη</span></span>). Old word from <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">παραλλασσω</span></span>, to make things alternate, here only in N.T. In Aristeas in sense of alternate stones in pavements. Dio Cassius has <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">παραλλαξις</span></span> without reference to the modern astronomical parallax, though James here is comparing God (Father of the lights) to the sun (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mal+4:2">Malachi 4:2</span>), which does have periodic variations.</p>
<p><b>Shadow that is cast by turning </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">τροπης αποσκιασμα</span></span>). <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">Τροπη</span></span> is an old word for "turning" (from <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">τρεπω</span></span> to turn), here only in N.T. <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">Αποσκιασμα</span></span> is a late and rare word (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">αποσκιασμος</span></span> in Plutarch) from <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">αποσκιαζω</span></span> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">απο, σκια</span></span>) a shade cast by one object on another. It is not clear what the precise metaphor is, whether the shadow thrown on the dial (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">αποσκιαζω</span></span> in Plato) or the borrowed light of the moon lost to us as it goes behind the earth. In fact, the text is by no means certain, for Aleph B papyrus of fourth century actually read <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">η τροπης αποσκιασματος</span></span> (the variation of the turning of the shadow). Ropes argues strongly for this reading, and rather convincingly. At any rate there is no such periodic variation in God like that we see in the heavenly bodies. </p>
