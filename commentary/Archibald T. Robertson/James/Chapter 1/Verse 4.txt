<p><b> Let have </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εχετω</span></span>). Present active imperative of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εχω</span></span>, let it keep on having.</p>
<p><b>Perfect </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">τελειον</span></span>). See <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ro+5:3">Romans 5:3</span> for a like chain of blessings. Carry on the work to the end or completion (from <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">τελος</span></span>, end) as in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="joh+17:4">John 17:4</span> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">το εργον τελειωσας</span></span>, having finished the work).</p>
<p><b>That ye may be </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ινα ητε</span></span>). Purpose clause with <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ινα</span></span> and present active subjunctive of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ειμ</span></span>. This is the goal of patience.</p>
<p><b>Perfect and entire </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">τελειο κα ολοκληρο</span></span>). Perfected at the end of the task (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">τελος</span></span>) and complete in all parts (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ολοκληρο</span></span>, <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ολος</span></span> whole and <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">κληρος</span></span> lot or part). "Perfected all over." These two adjectives often occur together in Philo, Plutarch, etc. See <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ac+3:16">Acts 3:16</span> for <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ολοκληριαν</span></span> (perfect soundness).</p>
<p><b>Lacking in nothing </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εν μηδεν λειπομενο</span></span>). Present passive participle of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">λειπω</span></span> to leave. Negative statement of the preceding positive as often in James (cf. <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="jas+1:6">James 1:6</span>). There is now a digression (verses <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="jas+1:5-8">James 1:5-8</span>) from the discussion of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">πειρασμος</span></span>, which is taken up again in verse <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="jas+1:9">James 1:9</span>. The word <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">λειπομενο</span></span> (lacking) suggests the digression. </p>
