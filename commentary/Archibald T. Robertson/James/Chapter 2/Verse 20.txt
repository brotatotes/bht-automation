<p><b> But wilt thou know? </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">θελεις δε γνωναι?</span></span>). "But dost thou wish to know?" Ingressive aorist active infinitive of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">γινοσκω</span></span> (come to know). James here introduces a new argument like <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ro+13:3">Romans 13:3</span>.</p>
<p><b>O vain man </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ω ανθρωπε κενε</span></span>). Goes on with the singular objector and demolishes him. For "empty" (deficient) Paul uses <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">αφρων</span></span> (fool) in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="1co+15:36">1 Corinthians 15:36</span> and just <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ανθρωπε</span></span> in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ro+2:1">Romans 2:1</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ro+9:20">Romans 9:20</span>.</p>
<p><b>Barren </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">αργε</span></span>). See <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="2pe+1:8">2 Peter 1:8</span> (not idle nor unfruitful) and <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mt+12:36">Matthew 12:36</span>, but Hort urges "inactive" as the idea here, like money with no interest and land with no crops. </p>
