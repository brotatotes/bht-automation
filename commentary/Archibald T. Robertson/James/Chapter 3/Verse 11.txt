<p><b> The fountain </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">η πηγη</span></span>). Old word for spring (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="joh+4:14">John 4:14</span>).</p>
<p><b>Opening </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">οπης</span></span>). Old word for fissure in the earth, in N.T. only here and <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="heb+11:38">Hebrews 11:38</span> (caves).</p>
<p><b>Send forth </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">βρυε</span></span>). Present active indicative of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">βρυω</span></span>, old verb, to bubble up, to gush forth, here only in N.T. The use of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">μητ</span></span> shows that a negative answer is expected in this rhetorical question.</p>
<p><b>The sweet and the bitter </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">το γλυκυ κα το πικρον</span></span>). Cognate accusatives with <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">βρυε</span></span>. Separate articles to distinguish sharply the two things. The neuter singular articular adjective is a common way of presenting a quality. <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">Γλυκυς</span></span> is an old adjective (in N.T. only here and <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="re+10:9">Revelation 10:9</span>), the opposite of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">πικρον</span></span> (from old root, to cut, to prick), in N.T. only here and verse <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="jas+3:14">James 3:14</span> (sharp, harsh). </p>
