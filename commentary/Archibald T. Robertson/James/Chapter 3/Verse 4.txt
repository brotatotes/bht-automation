<p><b> The ships also </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">κα τα πλοια</span></span>). Old word from <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">πλεω</span></span>, to sail (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mt+4:21">Matthew 4:21</span>). Another metaphor like "horses" (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ιππο</span></span>). "There is more imagery drawn from mere natural phenomena in the one short Epistle of James than in all St. Paul's epistles put together" (Howson).</p>
<p><b>Though they are so great </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">τηλικαυτα οντα</span></span>). Concessive participle of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ειμ</span></span>. The quantitative pronoun <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">τηλικουτος</span></span> occurs in the N.T. only here, <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="2co+1:10">2 Corinthians 1:10</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="heb+2:3">Hebrews 2:3</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="re+16:18">Revelation 16:18</span>. If James had only seen the modern mammoth ships. But the ship on which Paul went to Malta carried 276 persons (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ac+27:37">Acts 27:37</span>).</p>
<p><b>And are driven </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">κα ελαυνομενα</span></span>). Present passive participle of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ελαυνω</span></span>, old verb, in this sense (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="2pe+2:17">2 Peter 2:17</span>) for rowing (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mr+6:48">Mark 6:48</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="joh+6:19">John 6:19</span>).</p>
<p><b>Rough </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">σκληρον</span></span>). Old adjective (from <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">σκελλω</span></span>, to dry up), harsh, stiff, hard (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mt+25:24">Matthew 25:24</span>).</p>
<p><b>Are yet turned </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">μεταγετα</span></span>). Present passive indicative of the same verb, <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">μεταγω</span></span>, in verse <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="jas+3:3">James 3:3</span>. James is fond of repeating words (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="jas+1:13">James 1:13</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="jas+2:14">James 2:14</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="jas+2:16">James 2:16</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="jas+2:21">James 2:21</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="jas+2:25">James 2:25</span>).</p>
<p><b>By a very small rudder </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">υπο ελαχιστου πηδαλιου</span></span>). For the use of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">υπο</span></span> (under) with things see <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="lu+8:14">Luke 8:14</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="2pe+2:7">2 Peter 2:7</span>. There is possibly personification in the use of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">υπο</span></span> for agency in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="jas+1:14">James 1:14</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="jas+2:9">James 2:9</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="col+2:18">Colossians 2:18</span>. <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">Πηδαλιου</span></span> (from <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">πηδον</span></span>, the blade of an oar) is an old word, in N.T. only here and <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ac+27:40">Acts 27:40</span>. <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">Ελαχιστου</span></span> is the elative superlative as in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="1co+4:3">1 Corinthians 4:3</span> (from the Epic <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ελαχυς</span></span> for <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">μικρος</span></span>).</p>
<p><b>The impulse </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">η ορμη</span></span>). Old word for rapid, violent motion, here of the hand that worked the rudder, in N.T. only here and <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ac+14:5">Acts 14:5</span> (rush or onset of the people).</p>
<p><b>Of the steersman </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">του ευθυνοντος</span></span>). Present active genitive articular participle of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ευθυνω</span></span>, old verb, to make straight (from <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ευθυς</span></span>, straight, level, <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mr+1:3">Mark 1:3</span>), in N.T. only here and <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="joh+1:23">John 1:23</span>. Used also of the shepherd, the charioteer, and today it would apply to the chauffeur. "The twin figure of the control of horse and of ship are frequently found together in later Greek writers" (Ropes). As in Plutarch and Philo.</p>
<p><b>Willeth </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">βουλετα</span></span>). Present middle indicative of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">βουλομα</span></span>, common verb to will. Here intention of the steersman lies back of the impact of the hand on the rudder. </p>
