<p><b> Kind </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">φυσις</span></span>). Old word from <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">φυω</span></span>, order of nature (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ro+1:26">Romans 1:26</span>), here of all animals and man, in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="2pe+1:4">2 Peter 1:4</span> of God and redeemed men.</p>
<p><b>Of beasts </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">θηριων</span></span>). Old word diminutive from <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">θηρ</span></span> and so "little beasts" originally, then wild animals in general (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mr+1:13">Mark 1:13</span>), or quadrupeds as here. These four classes of animals come from <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ge+9:2">Genesis 9:2</span>.</p>
<p><b>Birds </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">πετεινων</span></span>). Old word for flying animals (from <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">πετομα</span></span>, to word from <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ερπω</span></span>, to crawl (Latin <i>serpo</i>), hence serpents.</p>
<p><b>Things in the sea </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εναλιων</span></span>). Old adjective (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εν, αλς</span></span>, sea, salt) in the sea, here only in N.T. The four groups are put in two pairs here by the use of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">τε κα</span></span> with the first two and the second two. See a different classification in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ac+10:12">Acts 10:12</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ac+11:6">Acts 11:6</span>.</p>
<p><b>Is tamed </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">δαμαζετα</span></span>). Present passive indicative of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">δαμαζω</span></span>, old verb kin to Latin <i>dominus</i> and English tame, in N.T. only in this passage and <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mr+5:4">Mark 5:4</span>. The present tense gives the general picture of the continuous process through the ages of man's lordship over the animals as stated in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ge+1:28">Genesis 1:28</span>.</p>
<p><b>Hath been tamed </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">δεδαμαστα</span></span>). Perfect passive indicative of the same verb, repeated to present the state of conquest in some cases (domestic animals, for instance).</p>
<p><b>By mankind </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">τη φυσε τη ανθρωπινη</span></span>). Instrumental case with repeated article and repetition also of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">φυσις</span></span>, "by the nature the human." For <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ανθρωπινος</span></span> see <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ac+17:25">Acts 17:25</span>. </p>
