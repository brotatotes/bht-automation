<p><b> No one </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ουδεις</span></span>). Especially his own tongue and by himself, but one has the help of the Holy Spirit.</p>
<p><b>A restless evil </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ακαταστατον κακον</span></span>). Correct reading, not <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ακατασχετον</span></span>, for which see <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="jas+1:8">James 1:8</span>. The tongue is evil when set on fire by hell, not evil necessarily.</p>
<p><b>Full of deadly poison </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">μεστη ιου θανατηφορου</span></span>). Feminine adjective agreeing with <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">γλωσσα</span></span>, not with <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">κακον</span></span> (neuter). <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">Ιου</span></span> (poison here, as in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ro+3:13">Romans 3:13</span>, but rust in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="jas+5:3">James 5:3</span>, only N.T. examples), old word. Genitive case after <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">μεστη</span></span> (full of). <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">Θανατηφορου</span></span>, old compound adjective (from <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">θανατος</span></span>, death, <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">φερω</span></span>, to bear or bring), death-bringing. Here only in N.T. Like the restless death-bringing tongue of the asp before it strikes. </p>
