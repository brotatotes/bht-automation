<p><b> Whereas ye know not </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">οιτινες ουκ επιστασθε</span></span>). The longer relative <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">οστις</span></span> defines here more precisely (like Latin <i>qui</i>) <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ο λεγοντες</span></span> (ye who say) of verse <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="jas+4:13">James 4:13</span> in a causal sense, as in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ac+10:47">Acts 10:47</span>, "who indeed do not know" (present middle indicative of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">επισταμα</span></span>).</p>
<p><b>What shall be on the morrow </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">της αυριον</span></span>). Supply <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ημερας</span></span> (day) after <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">αυριον</span></span>. This is the reading of B (Westcott) "on the morrow" (genitive of time), but Aleph K L cursives have <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">το της αυριον</span></span> ("the matter of tomorrow"), while A P cursives have <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">τα της αυριον</span></span> ("the things of tomorrow"). The sense is practically the same, though <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">το της αυριον</span></span> is likely correct.</p>
<p><b>What is your life? </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ποια η ζωη υμων</span></span>). Thus Westcott and Hort punctuate it as an indirect question, not direct. <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">Ποια</span></span> is a qualitative interrogative (of what character).</p>
<p><b>As vapour </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ατμις</span></span>). This is the answer. Old word for mist (like <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ατμος</span></span>, from which our "atmosphere"), in N.T. only here and <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ac+2:19">Acts 2:19</span> with <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">καπνου</span></span> (vapour of smoke (from <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="joe+2:30">Joel 2:30</span>).</p>
<p><b>For a little time </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">προς ολιγον</span></span>). See same phrase in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="1ti+4:8">1 Timothy 4:8</span>, <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">προς καιρον</span></span> in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="lu+8:13">Luke 8:13</span>, <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">προς ωραν</span></span> in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="joh+5:35">John 5:35</span>.</p>
<p><b>That appeareth and then vanisheth away </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">φαινομενη επειτα κα αφανιζομενη</span></span>). Present middle participles agreeing with <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ατμις</span></span>, "appearing, then also disappearing," with play on the two verbs (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">φαινομαι, αφανιζω</span></span> as in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mt+6:19">Matthew 6:19</span>, from <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">αφανης</span></span> hidden <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="heb+4:13">Hebrews 4:13</span>) with the same root <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">φαν</span></span> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">φαινω, α-φαν-ης</span></span>). </p>
