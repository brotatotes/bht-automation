<p><b> Because ye ask amiss </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">διοτ κακως αιτεισθε</span></span>). Here the indirect middle does make sense, "ye ask for yourselves" and that is "evilly" or amiss (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">κακως</span></span>), as James explains.</p>
<p><b>That ye may spend it in your pleasures </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ινα εν ταις ηδοναις υμων δαπανησητε</span></span>). Purpose clause with <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ινα</span></span> and the first aorist subjunctive of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">δαπαναω</span></span>, old verb from <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">δαπανη</span></span>, cost (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="lu+14:28">Luke 14:28</span> only in N.T.), to squander (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="lu+15:14">Luke 15:14</span>). God does not hear prayers like this. </p>
