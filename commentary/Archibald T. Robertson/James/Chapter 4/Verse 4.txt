<p><b> Ye adulteresses </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">μοιχαλιδες</span></span>). <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">Μοιχο κα</span></span> (ye adulterers) is spurious (Syrian text only). The feminine form here is a common late word from the masculine <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">μοιχο</span></span>. It is not clear whether the word is to be taken literally here as in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ro+7:3">Romans 7:3</span>, or figuratively for all unfaithful followers of Christ (like an unfaithful bride), as in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="2co+11:1">2 Corinthians 11:1</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="eph+5:24-28">Ephesians 5:24-28</span> (the Bride of Christ). Either view makes sense in this context, probably the literal view being more in harmony with the language of verses <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="jas+4:2">James 4:2</span>. In that case James may include more than Christians in his view, though Paul talks plainly to church members about unchastity (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="eph+5:3-5">Ephesians 5:3-5</span>).</p>
<p><b>Enmity with God </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εχθρα του θεου</span></span>). Objective genitive <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">θεου</span></span> with <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εχθρα</span></span> (predicate and so without article), old word from <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εχθρος</span></span>, enemy (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ro+5:10">Romans 5:10</span>), with <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εις θεον</span></span> (below and <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ro+8:7">Romans 8:7</span>).</p>
<p><b>Whosoever therefore would be </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ος εαν ουν βουληθη</span></span>). Indefinite relative clause with <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ος</span></span> and modal <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εαν</span></span> and the first aorist passive (deponent) subjunctive of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">βουλομα</span></span>, to will (purpose).</p>
<p><b>A friend of the world </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">φιλος του κοσμου</span></span>). Predicate nominative with infinitive <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εινα</span></span> agreeing with <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ος</span></span>. See <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="jas+2:23">James 2:23</span> for <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">φιλος θεου</span></span> (friend of God).</p>
<p><b>Maketh himself </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">καθιστατα</span></span>). Present passive (not middle) indicative as in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="jas+3:6">James 3:6</span>, "is constituted," "is rendered."</p>
<p><b>An enemy of God </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εχθρος του θεου</span></span>). Predicate nominative and anarthrous and objective genitive (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">θεου</span></span>). </p>
