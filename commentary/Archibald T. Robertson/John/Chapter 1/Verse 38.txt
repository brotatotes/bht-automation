<p><b> Turned </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">στραφεις</span></span>). Second aorist passive participle of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">στρεφω</span></span>, vividly picturing the sudden act of Jesus on hearing their steps behind him.</p>
<p><b>Beheld </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">θεασαμενος</span></span>). First aorist middle participle of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">θεαομα</span></span> (verse <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="joh+1:32">John 1:32</span>). Both participles here express antecedent action to <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">λεγε</span></span> (saith).</p>
<p><b>Following </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ακολοθουντας</span></span>). Present active participle of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ακολουθεω</span></span> (verse <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="joh+1:37">John 1:37</span>). It was Christ's first experience of this kind and the two came from the Baptist to Jesus.</p>
<p><b>What seek ye? </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">Τ ζητειτε;</span></span>). Not "whom" (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">τινα</span></span> <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="joh+18:4">John 18:4</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="joh+20:15">John 20:15</span>), but "what purpose have you." The first words of Jesus preserved in this Gospel. See <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="lu+2:49">Luke 2:49</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mt+3:15">Matthew 3:15</span> for words spoken before this and <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mr+1:15">Mark 1:15</span> for Mark's first report in the Galilean ministry.</p>
<p><b>Rabbi </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">Ραββε</span></span>). Aramaic title for "Teacher" which John here translates by <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">Διδασκαλε</span></span> as he is writing late and for general readers. Luke, a Greek Christian, does not use it, but John recalls his first use of this term to Jesus and explains it. Matthew has it only in the greeting of Judas to the Master (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mt+26:25">Matthew 26:25</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mt+26:49">Matthew 26:49</span>) and Mark once by Judas (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mr+14:45">Mark 14:45</span>) and twice by Peter (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mr+9:5">Mark 9:5</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mr+11:21">Mark 11:21</span>). John's Gospel has the disciples at first addressing Jesus by Rabbi while others address him by <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">Κυριε</span></span> (Lord or Sir) as in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="joh+4:11">John 4:11</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="joh+4:49">John 4:49</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="joh+5:7">John 5:7</span>. Peter uses <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">Κυριε</span></span> in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="joh+6:68">John 6:68</span>. In the end the disciples usually say <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">Κυριε</span></span> (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="joh+13:6">John 13:6</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="joh+13:25">John 13:25</span>, etc.), but Mary Magdalene says <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">Ραββουνε</span></span> (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="joh+20:16">John 20:16</span>).</p>
<p><b>Being interpreted </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">μεθερμημευομενον</span></span>). Present passive participle of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">μεθερμηνευω</span></span>, late compound of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">μετα</span></span> and <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ερμηνευω</span></span>, to explain (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="joh+1:42">John 1:42</span>), old word from <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">Hερμες</span></span>, the god of speech (hermeneutics). John often explains Aramaic words (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="joh+1:38">John 1:38</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="joh+1:41">John 1:41</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="joh+1:42">John 1:42</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="joh+4:25">John 4:25</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="joh+9:7">John 9:7</span>, etc.).</p>
<p><b>Where abidest thou? </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">Που μενεισ;</span></span>). They wished a place for quiet converse with Jesus. </p>
