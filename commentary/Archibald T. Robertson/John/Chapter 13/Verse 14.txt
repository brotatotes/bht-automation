<p><b> If I then </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ε ουν εγω</span></span>). Argumentative sense of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ουν</span></span> (therefore). Condition of first class, assumed to be true, with first aorist active indicative of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">νιπτω</span></span>, "If I, being what I am, washed your feet" (as I did).</p>
<p><b>Ye also ought </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">κα υμεις οφειλετε</span></span>). The obligation rests on you <i>a fortiori</i>. Present active indicative of the old verb <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">οφειλω</span></span>, to owe a debt (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mt+18:30">Matthew 18:30</span>). The mutual obligation is to do this or any other needed service. The widows who washed the saints' feet in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="1ti+5:10">1 Timothy 5:10</span> did it "as an incident-of their hospitable ministrations" (Bernard). Up to 1731 the Lord High Almoner in England washed the feet of poor saints (<i>pedilavium</i>) on Thursday before Easter, a custom that arose in the fourth century, and one still practised by the Pope of Rome. </p>
