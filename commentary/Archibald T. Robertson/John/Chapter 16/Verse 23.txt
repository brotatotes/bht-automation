<p><b> Ye shall ask me nothing </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εμε ουκ ερωτησετε</span></span>). Either in the sense of question (original meaning of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ερωταω</span></span>) as in verses <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="joh+16:19">John 16:19</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="joh+16:30">John 16:30</span> since he will be gone or in the sense of request or favours (like <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">αιτεω</span></span> in this verse) as in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="joh+14:16">John 14:16</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ac+3:2">Acts 3:2</span>. In verse <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="joh+16:26">John 16:26</span> both <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">αιτεω</span></span> and <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ερωταω</span></span> occur in this sense. Either view makes sense here.</p>
<p><b>If ye shall ask </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">αν τ αιτησητε</span></span>). Third-class condition, <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">αν</span></span> like <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εαν</span></span> with first aorist active subjunctive of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">αιτεω</span></span>. Note <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="joh+14:26">John 14:26</span> for "in my name." </p>
