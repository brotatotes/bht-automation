<p><b> A custom </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">συνηθεια</span></span>). Old word for intimacy, intercourse, from <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">συνηθης</span></span> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">συν, ηθος</span></span>), in N.T. only here, <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="1co+8:7">1 Corinthians 8:7</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="1co+11:16">1 Corinthians 11:16</span>. This custom, alluded to in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mr+15:6">Mark 15:6</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mt+27:15">Matthew 27:15</span>, is termed necessity (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">αναγκη</span></span>) in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="lu+23:17">Luke 23:17</span> (late MSS., not in older MSS.). All the Gospels use the verb <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">απολυω</span></span> (release, set free). Then <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ινα απολυσω</span></span> is a subject clause (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ινα</span></span> and first aorist active subjunctive) in apposition with <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">συνηθεια</span></span>.</p>
<p><b>Will ye therefore that I release? </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">βουλεσθε ουν απολυσω;</span></span>). Without the usual <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ινα</span></span> before <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">απολυσω</span></span>, asyndeton, as in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mr+10:36">Mark 10:36</span>, to be explained either as parataxis or two questions (Robertson, <i>Grammar</i>, p. 430) or as mere omission of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ινα</span></span> (<i>ibid</i>., p. 994). There is contempt and irony in Pilate's use of the phrase "the king of the Jews." </p>
