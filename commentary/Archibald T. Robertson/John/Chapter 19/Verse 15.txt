<p><b> Away with him, away with him </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">αρον, αρον</span></span>). First aorist active imperative of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">αιρω</span></span>. See <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">αιρε</span></span> in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="lu+23:18">Luke 23:18</span>. This thing has gotten on the nerves of the crowd. Note the repetition. In a second-century papyrus letter (Moulton and Milligan's <i>Vocabulary</i>) a nervous mother cries "He upsets me; away with him" (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">αρρον αυτον</span></span>). Pilate weakly repeats his sarcasm: "<b> Your king shall I crucify? </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">Τον βασιλεα υμων σταυρωσω;</span></span>).</p>
<p><b>But Caesar </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ε μη καισαρα</span></span>). The chief priests (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ο αρχιερεις</span></span>) were Sadducees, who had no Messianic hope like that of the Pharisees. So to carry their point against Jesus they renounce the principle of the theocracy that God was their King (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="1sa+12:12">1 Samuel 12:12</span>). </p>
