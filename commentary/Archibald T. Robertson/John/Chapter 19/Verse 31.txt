<p><b> The Preparation </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">παρασκευη</span></span>). Friday. See verse <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="joh+19:14">John 19:14</span>.</p>
<p><b>Might not remain </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">μη μεινη</span></span>). Negative final clause with <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ινα μη</span></span> and first aorist active (constative) subjunctive of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">μενω</span></span>.</p>
<p><b>A high day </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">μεγαλη</span></span>). A "great" day, since "the sabbath day following synchronized with the first day of unleavened bread which was a 'great' day" (Bernard). A double reason therefore for wanting the bodies removed before sunset when the Sabbath began.</p>
<p><b>That their legs might be broken </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ινα κατεαγωσιν αυτον τα σκελη</span></span>). Purpose clause with <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ινα</span></span> and the second aorist passive subjunctive of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">καταγνυμ</span></span> with the augment retained in the subjunctive, a "false augment" common in later Greek as in the future in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mt+12:20">Matthew 12:20</span> with this verb (Robertson, <i>Grammar</i>, p. 365). This <i>crurifragium</i> was done with a heavy mallet and ended the sufferings of the victim.</p>
<p><b>Legs </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">σκελη</span></span>). Old word, here only in N.T.</p>
<p><b>Might be taken away </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">αρθωσιν</span></span>). First aorist passive subjunctive of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">αιρω</span></span> with <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ινα</span></span> also. </p>
