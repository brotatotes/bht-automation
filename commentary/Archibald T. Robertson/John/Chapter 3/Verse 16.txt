<p><b> For so </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ουτως γαρ</span></span>). This use of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">γαρ</span></span> is quite in John's style in introducing his comments (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="joh+2:25">John 2:25</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="joh+4:8">John 4:8</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="joh+5:13">John 5:13</span>, etc.). This "Little Gospel" as it is often called, this "comfortable word" (the Anglican Liturgy), while not a quotation from Jesus is a just and marvellous interpretation of the mission and message of our Lord. In verses <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="joh+3:16-21">John 3:16-21</span> John recapitulates in summary fashion the teaching of Jesus to Nicodemus.</p>
<p><b>Loved </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ηγαπησεν</span></span>). First aorist active indicative of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">αγαπαω</span></span>, the noble word so common in the Gospels for the highest form of love, used here as often in John (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="joh+14:23">John 14:23</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="joh+17:23">John 17:23</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="1jo+3:1">1 John 3:1</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="1jo+4:10">1 John 4:10</span>) of God's love for man (cf. <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="2th+2:16">2 Thessalonians 2:16</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ro+5:8">Romans 5:8</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="eph+2:4">Ephesians 2:4</span>). In <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="joh+21:15">John 21:15</span> John presents a distinction between <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">αγαπαω</span></span> and <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">φιλεω</span></span>. <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">Αγαπαω</span></span> is used also for love of men for men (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="joh+13:34">John 13:34</span>), for Jesus (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="joh+8:42">John 8:42</span>), for God (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="1jo+4:10">1 John 4:10</span>).</p>
<p><b>The world </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">τον κοσμον</span></span>). The whole cosmos of men, including Gentiles, the whole human race. This universal aspect of God's love appears also in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="2co+5:19">2 Corinthians 5:19</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ro+5:8">Romans 5:8</span>.</p>
<p><b>That he gave </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ωστε εδωκεν</span></span>). The usual classical construction with <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ωστε</span></span> and the indicative (first aorist active) practical result, the only example in the N.T. save that in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ga+2:13">Galatians 2:13</span>. Elsewhere <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ωστε</span></span> with the infinitive occurs for actual result (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mt+13:32">Matthew 13:32</span>) as well as purpose (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mt+10:1">Matthew 10:1</span>), though even this is rare.</p>
<p><b>His only begotten Son </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">τον υιον τον μονογενη</span></span>). "The Son the only begotten." For this word see on <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="joh+1:14">John 1:14</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="joh+1:18">John 1:18</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="joh+3:18">John 3:18</span>. The rest of the sentence, the purpose clause with <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ινα-εχη</span></span> precisely reproduces the close of <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="joh+3:15">John 3:15</span> save that <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εις αυτον</span></span> takes the place of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εν αυτω</span></span> (see <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="joh+1:12">John 1:12</span>) and goes certainly with <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">πιστευων</span></span> (not with <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εχη</span></span> as <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εν αυτω</span></span> in verse <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="joh+3:15">John 3:15</span>) and the added clause "should not perish but" (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">μη απολητα αλλα</span></span>, second aorist middle subjunctive, intransitive, of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">απολλυμ</span></span>, to destroy). The same contrast between "perish" and "eternal life" (for this world and the next) appears also in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="joh+10:28">John 10:28</span>. On "perish" see also <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="joh+17:12">John 17:12</span>. </p>
