<p><b> He that was healed </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ο ιαθεις</span></span>). First aorist passive articular participle of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ιαομα</span></span> (John's usual word).</p>
<p><b>Who it was </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">τις εστιν</span></span>). Present tense preserved in indirect question.</p>
<p><b>Had conveyed himself away </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εξενευσεν</span></span>). First aorist active indicative of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εκνεω</span></span>, old verb to swim out, to slip out, or from <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εκνευω</span></span>, to turn out, to turn the head to one side (to one side with which compare <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ενενευον</span></span>, they nodded, <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="lu+1:62">Luke 1:62</span>). Either of these verbs can explain the form here. The aorist tense simply states an antecedent action without being a pastperfect.</p>
<p><b>A multitude being in the place </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">οχλου οντος εν τω τοπω</span></span>). Genitive absolute and the reason for Christ's departure. </p>
