<p><b> When the water is troubled </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">οταν ταραχθη το υδωρ</span></span>). Indefinite temporal clause with <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">οταν</span></span> and the first aorist passive subjunctive of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ταρασσω</span></span>, old verb to agitate (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mt+2:3">Matthew 2:3</span>). The popular belief was that, at each outflow of this intermittent spring, there was healing power in the water for the first one getting in.</p>
<p><b>To put me into the pool </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ινα βαλη με εις την κολυμβηθραν</span></span>). Final use of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ινα</span></span> and the second aorist active subjunctive of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">βαλλω</span></span>, "that he throw me in" quickly before any one else. For this use of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">βαλλω</span></span> see <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mr+7:30">Mark 7:30</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="lu+16:20">Luke 16:20</span>.</p>
<p><b>But while I am coming </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εν ω δε ερχομα</span></span>). Temporal use of the relative, "in which time" (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">χρονω</span></span> or <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">καιρω</span></span> understood). <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">Εγω</span></span> (I) is emphatic. </p>
