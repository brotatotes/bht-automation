<p><b> Every one that committeth sin is the bondservant of sin </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">πας ο ποιων την αμαρτιαν δουλος εστιν [της αμαρτιασ]</span></span>). The Western class omits <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">της αμαρτιας</span></span> (sin), but that is the idea anyhow. Note the use of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ποιων</span></span> (present active participle, continuous habit or practice), not <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ποιησας</span></span> (aorist active participle for single act), precisely as in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="1jo+3:4-8">1 John 3:4-8</span>. Note also <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="joh+3:21">John 3:21</span> for <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ο ποιων την αληθειαν</span></span> (the one who practises the truth). Sin, like the worst narcotic, is habit forming. Hence the problem today for criminologists for paroled or pardoned criminals nearly always go back to crime, sink again into sin, the slaves of sin. Xenophon has this notion of the slavery of sin (<i>Memor</i>. IV. 5. 3). So Paul clearly in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ro+6:17">Romans 6:17</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ro+6:20">Romans 6:20</span> "slaves of sin" (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">δουλο της αμαρτιας</span></span>). </p>
