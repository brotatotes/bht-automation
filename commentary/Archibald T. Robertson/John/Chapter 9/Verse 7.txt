<p><b> Wash </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">νιψα</span></span>). First aorist middle imperative second person singular of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">νιπτω</span></span>, later form of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">νιζω</span></span>, to wash, especially parts of the body. Certainly bathing the eyes is good for eye trouble, and yet we are not to infer that the cure was due to the use of the clay or to the washing.</p>
<p><b>In the pool of Siloam </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εις την κολυμβηθραν του Σιλωαμ</span></span>). The word <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">κολυμβηθρα</span></span> (from <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">κολυμβαω</span></span>, to swim) is a common word for swimming-pool, in N.T. only here and <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="joh+5:2">John 5:2</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="joh+5:7">John 5:7</span>. The name <i>Siloam</i> is Hebrew (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="isa+8:6">Isaiah 8:6</span>) and means "sent" (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">απεσταλμενος</span></span>, perfect passive participle of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">αποστελλω</span></span>). It was situated south of the temple area and was apparently connected by a subterranean tunnel with the Virgin's Well (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="joh+5:2">John 5:2</span>) according to Bernard. The water was conducted artificially to the pool of Siloam.</p>
<p><b>Washed </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ενιψατο</span></span>). First aorist direct middle (cf. <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">νιψα</span></span>), apparently bathing and not merely washing his eyes.</p>
<p><b>Came seeing </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ηλθεν βλεπων</span></span>). Jesus had healed him. He was tested by the demand to bathe his eyes. </p>
