<p><b> Hidden rocks </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">σπιλαδες</span></span>). Old word for rocks in the sea (covered by the water), as in Homer, here only in N.T. <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="2pe+2:13">2 Peter 2:13</span> has <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">σπιλο</span></span>.</p>
<p><b>Love-feasts </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">αγαπαις</span></span>). Undoubtedly the correct text here, though A C have <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">απαταις</span></span> as in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="2pe+2:14">2 Peter 2:14</span>. For disorder at the Lord's Supper (and love-feasts?) see <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="1co+11:17-34">1 Corinthians 11:17-34</span>. The Gnostics made it worse, so that the love-feasts were discontinued.</p>
<p><b>When they feast with you </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">συνευωχουμενο</span></span>). See <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="2pe+2:13">2 Peter 2:13</span> for this very word and form. Masculine gender with <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ουτο ο</span></span> rather than with the feminine <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">σπιλαδες</span></span>. Cf. <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="re+11:4">Revelation 11:4</span>. Construction according to sense.</p>
<p><b>Shepherds that feed themselves </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εαυτους ποιμαινοντες</span></span>). "Shepherding themselves." Cf. <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="re+7:17">Revelation 7:17</span> for this use of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ποιμαινω</span></span>. Clouds without water (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">νεφελα ανυδρο</span></span>). <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">Νεφελη</span></span> common word for cloud (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mt+24:30">Matthew 24:30</span>). <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="2pe+2:17">2 Peter 2:17</span> has <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">πηγα ανυδρο</span></span> (springs without water) and then <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ομιχλα</span></span> (mists) and <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ελαυνομενα</span></span> (driven) rather than <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">περιφερομενα</span></span> here (borne around, whirled around, present passive participle of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">περιφερω</span></span> to bear around), a powerful picture of disappointed hopes.</p>
<p><b>Autumn trees </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">δενδρα φθινοπωρινα</span></span>). Late adjective (Aristotle, Polybius, Strabo) from <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">φθινω</span></span>, to waste away, and <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">οπωρα</span></span>, autumn, here only in N.T. For <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ακαρπα</span></span> (without fruit) see <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="2pe+1:8">2 Peter 1:8</span>.</p>
<p><b>Twice dead </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">δις αποθανοντα</span></span>). Second aorist active participle of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">αποθνησκω</span></span>. Fruitless and having died. Having died and also "uprooted" (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εκριζωθεντα</span></span>). First aorist passive participle of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εκριζοω</span></span>, late compound, to root out, to pluck up by the roots, as in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mt+13:29">Matthew 13:29</span>. </p>
