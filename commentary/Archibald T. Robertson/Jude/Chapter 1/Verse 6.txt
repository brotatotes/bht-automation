<p><b> And angels </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">αγγελους δε</span></span>). The second example in Jude, the fallen angels, accusative case after <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">τετηρηκεν</span></span> (perfect active indicative of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">τηρεω</span></span>, for which verb see <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="2pe+2:4">2 Peter 2:4</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="2pe+2:7">2 Peter 2:7</span>) at the end of the verse (two emphatic positions, beginning and end of the clause).</p>
<p><b>Kept not </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">μη τηρησαντας</span></span>). First aorist active participle with negative <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">μη</span></span>, with play on "kept not" and "he hath kept."</p>
<p><b>Principality </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">αρχην</span></span>). Literally, "beginning," "rule," (first place of power as in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="1co+15:24">1 Corinthians 15:24</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ro+8:38">Romans 8:38</span>). In <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ac+10:11">Acts 10:11</span> it is used for "corners" (beginnings) of the sheet. In <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="eph+6:12">Ephesians 6:12</span> the word is used for evil angels. See <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="de+32:8">Deuteronomy 32:8</span>. Both Enoch and Philo (and Milton) discuss the fallen angels.</p>
<p><b>But left </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">αλλα απολιποντας</span></span>). Second aorist active participle of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">απολειπω</span></span>, old verb, to leave behind (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="2ti+4:13">2 Timothy 4:13</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="2ti+4:20">2 Timothy 4:20</span>).</p>
<p><b>Their own proper habitation </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">το ιδιον οικητηριον</span></span>). Old word for dwelling-place (from <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">οικητηρ</span></span>, dweller at home, from <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">οικος</span></span>), in N.T. only here and <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="2co+5:2">2 Corinthians 5:2</span> (the body as the abode of the spirit).</p>
<p><b>In everlasting bonds </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">δεσμοις αιδιοις</span></span>). Either locative (in) or instrumental (by, with). <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">Αιδιος</span></span> (from <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">αε</span></span>, always), old adjective, in N.T. only here and <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ro+1:20">Romans 1:20</span> (of God's power and deity). It is synonymous with <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">αιωνιος</span></span> (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mt+25:46">Matthew 25:46</span>). Mayor terms <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">αιδιος</span></span> an Aristotelian word, while <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">αιωνιος</span></span> is Platonic.</p>
<p><b>Under darkness </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">υπο ζοφον</span></span>). See <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="2pe+2:4">2 Peter 2:4</span> for <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ζοφος</span></span>. In Wisd. 17:2 we find <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">δεσμιο σκοτους</span></span> (prisoners of darkness).</p>
<p><b>Great </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">μεγαλης</span></span>). Not in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="2pe+2:9">2 Peter 2:9</span>, which see for discussion. </p>
