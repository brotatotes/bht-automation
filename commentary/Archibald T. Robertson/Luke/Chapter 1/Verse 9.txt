<p><b> His lot was </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ελαχε</span></span>). Literally,</p>
<p><b>he obtained the lot </b>. Second aorist active indicative of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">λαγχανω</span></span>, to obtain by lot, a very old verb from Homer on. It is used either with the genitive as here, or the accusative as in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ac+1:17">Acts 1:17</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="2pe+1:1">2 Peter 1:1</span>. Papyri show examples with the accusative. It was only once in a lifetime that a priest obtained the lot of going (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εισελθων</span></span>, here nominative aorist active participle agreeing with the subject of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ελαχε</span></span>) into the sanctuary (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">τον ναον</span></span>, not <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">το ιερον</span></span>, the outer courts) and burning incense on the golden altar. "It was the great moment of Zacharias's life, and his heart was no doubt alert for the supernatural" (Ragg). The fortunate lot was "a white stone" to which <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="re+2:17">Revelation 2:17</span> may refer.</p>
<p><b>Burn incense </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">του θυμιασα</span></span>). Here only in the N.T. Occurs on inscriptions. Hobart finds it used by medical writers for fumigating herbs. "Ascending the steps to the Holy Place, the priests spread the coals on the golden altar, and arranged the incense, and the chief operating priest was then left alone within the Holy Place to await the signal of the president to burn the incense. It was probably at this time that the angel appeared to Zacharias" (Vincent). </p>
