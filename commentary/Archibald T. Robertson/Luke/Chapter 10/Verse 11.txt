<p><b> Even the dust </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">κα τον κονιορτον</span></span>). Old word from <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">κονις</span></span>, dust, and <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ορνυμ</span></span>, to stir up. We have seen it already in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mt+10:14">Matthew 10:14</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="lu+9:5">Luke 9:5</span>. Dust is a plague in the east. Shake off even that.</p>
<p><b>Cleaveth </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">κολληθεντα</span></span>). First aorist passive participle of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">κολλαω</span></span>, to cling as dust and mud do to shoes. Hence the orientals took off the sandals on entering a house.</p>
<p><b>We wipe off </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">απομασσομεθα</span></span>). Middle voice of an old verb <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">απομασσω</span></span>, to rub off with the hands. Nowhere else in the N.T. But <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εκμασσω</span></span>, occurs in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="lu+7:38">Luke 7:38</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="lu+7:44">Luke 7:44</span>.</p>
<p><b>Against you </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">Hυμιν</span></span>). Fine example of the dative of disadvantage (the case of personal interest, the dative). </p>
