<p><b> Unto you my friends </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">υμιν τοις φιλοις</span></span>). As opposed to the Pharisees and lawyers in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="lu+11:43">Luke 11:43</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="lu+11:46">Luke 11:46</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="lu+11:53">Luke 11:53</span>.</p>
<p><b>Be not afraid of </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">μη φοβηθητε απο</span></span>). First aorist passive subjunctive with <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">μη</span></span>, ingressive aorist, do not become afraid of, with <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">απο</span></span> and the ablative like the Hebrew <i>min</i> and the English "be afraid of," a translation Hebraism as in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mt+10:28">Matthew 10:28</span> (Moulton, <i>Prolegomena</i>, p. 102).</p>
<p><b>Have no more that they can do </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">μη εχοντων περισσοτερον τ ποιησα</span></span>). Luke often uses the infinitive thus with <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εχω</span></span>, a classic idiom (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="lu+7:40">Luke 7:40</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="lu+7:42">Luke 7:42</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="lu+12:4">Luke 12:4</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="lu+12:50">Luke 12:50</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="lu+14:14">Luke 14:14</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ac+4:14">Acts 4:14</span>, etc.). </p>
