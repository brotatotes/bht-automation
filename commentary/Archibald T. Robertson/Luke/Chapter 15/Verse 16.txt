<p><b> He would fain have been filled </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">επεθυμε χορτασθηνα</span></span>). Literally, he was desiring (longing) to be filled. Imperfect indicative and first aorist passive infinitive. <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">Χορτασθηνα</span></span> is from <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">χορταζω</span></span> and that from <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">χορτος</span></span> (grass), and so to feed with grass or with anything. Westcott and Hort put <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">γεμισα την κοιλιαν αυτου</span></span> in the margin (the Textus Receptus).</p>
<p><b>With the husks </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εκ των κερατιων</span></span>). The word occurs here alone in the N.T. and is a diminutive of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">κερας</span></span> (horn) and so means little horn. It is used in various senses, but here refers to the pods of the carob tree or locust tree still common in Palestine and around the Mediterannean, so called from the shape of the pods like little horns, <i>Bockshornbaum</i> in German or goat's-horn tree. The gelatinous substance inside has a sweetish taste and is used for feeding swine and even for food by the lower classes. It is sometimes called Saint John's Bread from the notion that the Baptist ate it in the wilderness.</p>
<p><b>No man gave unto him </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ουδεις εδιδου αυτω</span></span>). Imperfect active. Continued refusal of anyone to allow him even the food of the hogs. </p>
