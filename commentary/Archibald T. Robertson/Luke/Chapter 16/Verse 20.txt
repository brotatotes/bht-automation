<p><b> Beggar </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">πτωχος</span></span>). Original meaning of this old word. See on <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mt+5:3">Matthew 5:3</span>. The name Lazarus is from <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">Ελεαζαρος</span></span>, "God a help," and was a common one. <i>Lazar</i> in English means one afflicted with a pestilential disease.</p>
<p><b>Was laid </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εβεβλητο</span></span>). Past perfect passive of the common verb <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">βαλλω</span></span>. He had been flung there and was still there, "as if contemptuous roughness is implied" (Plummer).</p>
<p><b>At his gate </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">προς τον πυλωνα αυτου</span></span>). Right in front of the large portico or gateway, not necessarily a part of the grand house, porch in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mt+26:71">Matthew 26:71</span>.</p>
<p><b>Full of sores </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ειλκωμενος</span></span>). Perfect passive participle of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ελκοω</span></span>, to make sore, to ulcerate, from <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ελκος</span></span>, ulcer (Latin <i>ulcus</i>). See use of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ελκος</span></span> in verse <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="lu+16:21">Luke 16:21</span>. Common in Hippocrates and other medical writers. Here only in the N.T. </p>
