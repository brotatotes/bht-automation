<p><b> I kept </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ειχον</span></span>). Imperfect active of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εχω</span></span>. I kept on keeping.</p>
<p><b>Laid up </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">αποκειμενην</span></span>). Present passive participle agreeing with <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ην</span></span> (which), used often as perfect passive of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">τιθημ</span></span> as here, laid away or off (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">απο</span></span>). It is not the periphrastic construction, but two separate verbs, each with its own force.</p>
<p><b>In a napkin </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εν σουδαριω</span></span>). A Latin word <i>sudarium</i> from <i>sudor</i> (sweat) transliterated into Greek, a sweatcloth handkerchief or napkin. Found in papyrus marriage contracts as part of the dowry (second and third centuries A.D., Deissmann, <i>Bible Studies</i>, p. 223). Used also for swathing the head of the dead (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="joh+11:44">John 11:44</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="joh+20:7">John 20:7</span>). </p>
