<p><b> It had been revealed unto him </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ην αυτω κεχρηματισμενον</span></span>). Periphrastic past perfect passive indicative. Common Greek verb. First to transact business from <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">χρημα</span></span> and that from <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">χραομα</span></span>, to use, make use of; then to do business with public officials, to give advice (judges, rulers, kings), then to get the advice of the Delphic and other oracles (Diodorus, Plutarch). The LXX and Josephus use it of God's commands. A Fayum papyrus of 257 B.C. has the substantive <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">χρημαστισμος</span></span> for a divine response (cf. <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ro+11:4">Romans 11:4</span>). See Deissmann, <i>Light From the Ancient East</i>, p. 153.</p>
<p><b>Before </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">πριν η</span></span>). Classic Greek idiom after a negative to have subjunctive as here (only example in the N.T.) or the optative after past tense as in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ac+25:16">Acts 25:16</span> (subjunctive changed to optative in indirect discourse). Elsewhere in the N.T. the infinitive follows <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">πριν</span></span> as in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mt+1:18">Matthew 1:18</span>. </p>
