<p><b> When the parents brought in the child Jesus </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εν τω εισαγαγειν τους γονεις το παιδιον Ιησουν</span></span>). A neat Greek and Hebrew idiom difficult to render into English, very common in the LXX;</p>
<p><b>In the bringing the Child Jesus as to the parents </b>. The articular infinitive and two accusatives (one the object, the other accusative of general reference).</p>
<p><b>After the custom of the law </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">κατα το ειθισμενον του νομου</span></span>). Here the perfect passive participle <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ειθισμενον</span></span>, neuter singular from <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εθιζω</span></span> (common Greek verb, to accustom) is used as a virtual substantive like <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">το εθος</span></span> in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="lu+1:8">Luke 1:8</span>. Luke alone in the N.T. uses either word save <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εθος</span></span> in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="joh+19:40">John 19:40</span>, though <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ειωθα</span></span> from <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εθω</span></span>, occurs also in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mt+27:15">Matthew 27:15</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mr+10:1">Mark 10:1</span>. </p>
