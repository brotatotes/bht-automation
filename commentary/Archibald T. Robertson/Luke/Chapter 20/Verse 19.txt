<p><b> To lay hands on him </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">επιβαλειν επ' αυτον τας χειρας</span></span>). Second aorist active infinitive of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">επιβαλλω</span></span>, an old verb and either transitively as here or intransitively as in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mr+4:37">Mark 4:37</span>. Vivid picture here where <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mr+12:12">Mark 12:12</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mt+21:46">Matthew 21:46</span> has "to seize" (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">κρατησα</span></span>).</p>
<p><b>In that very hour </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εν αυτη τη ωρα</span></span>). Luke's favourite idiom, in the hour itself. Not in Mark or Matthew and shows that the Sanhedrin were angry enough to force the climax then.</p>
<p><b>And they feared </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">κα εφοβηθησαν</span></span>). Adversative use of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">κα</span></span> = but they feared. Hence they refrained.</p>
<p><b>For they perceived </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εγνωσαν γαρ</span></span>). The reason for their rage. Second aorist active indicative of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">γινωσκω</span></span>.</p>
<p><b>Against them </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">προς αυτους</span></span>). As in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mr+12:12">Mark 12:12</span>. The cap fitted them and they saw it. </p>
