<p><b> As it is written </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ως γεγραπτα</span></span>). The regular formula for quotation, perfect passive indicative of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">γραφω</span></span>.</p>
<p><b>Isaiah the prophet </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">Εσαιου του προφητου</span></span>). The same phrase in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mr+1:2">Mark 1:2</span> (correct text) and <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mt+3:3">Matthew 3:3</span>. Mark, as we have seen, adds a quotation from <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mal+3:1">Malachi 3:1</span> and Luke gives verses 4 and 5 of <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="lu+3:40">Luke 3:40</span> not in Matthew or Mark (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="lu+3:5">Luke 3:5</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="lu+3:6">Luke 3:6</span>). See <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mt+3:3">Matthew 3:3</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mr+1:3">Mark 1:3</span> for discussion of Luke 4:4. </p>
