<p><b> They rose up and cast him forth </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ανασταντες εξεβαλον</span></span>). Second aorist ingressive active participle and second aorist effective active indicative. A movement towards lynching Jesus.</p>
<p><b>Unto the brow of the hill </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ηος οφρυος του ορους</span></span>). Eyebrow (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">οφρυς</span></span>), in Homer, then any jutting prominence. Only here in the N.T. Hippocrates speaks of the eyebrow hanging over.</p>
<p><b>Was built </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ωικοδομητο</span></span>). Past perfect indicative, stood built.</p>
<p><b>That they might throw him down headlong </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ωστε κατακρημνισα αυτον</span></span>). Neat Greek idiom with <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ωστε</span></span> for intended result, "so as to cast him down the precipice." The infinitive alone can convey the same meaning (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mt+2:2">Matthew 2:2</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mt+20:28">Matthew 20:28</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="lu+2:23">Luke 2:23</span>). <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">Κρημνος</span></span> is an overhanging bank or precipice from <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">κρεμαννυμ</span></span>, to hang. <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">Κατα</span></span> is down. The verb occurs in Xenophon, Demosthenes, LXX, Josephus. Here only in the N.T. At the southwest corner of the town of Nazareth such a cliff today exists overhanging the Maronite convent. Murder was in the hearts of the people. By pushing him over they hoped to escape technical guilt. </p>
