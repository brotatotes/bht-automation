<p><b> Two boats </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">πλοια δυο</span></span>). Some MSS. have <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">πλοιαρια</span></span>, little boats, but <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">πλοια</span></span> was used of boats of various sizes, even of ships like <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">νηες</span></span>.</p>
<p><b>The fishermen </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ο αλεεις</span></span>). It is an old Homeric word that has come back to common use in the <i>Koine</i>. It means "sea-folk" from <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">αλς</span></span>, sea.</p>
<p><b>Were washing </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">επλυνον</span></span>). Imperfect active, though some MSS. have aorist <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">επλυναν</span></span>. Vincent comments on Luke's use of five verbs for washing: this one for cleaning, <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">απομασσω</span></span> for wiping the dust from one's feet (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="lu+10:11">Luke 10:11</span>), <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εκμασσω</span></span> of the sinful woman wiping Christ's feet with her hair (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="lu+7:38">Luke 7:38</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="lu+7:44">Luke 7:44</span>), <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">απολουω</span></span> of washing away sins (symbolically, of course) as in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ac+22:16">Acts 22:16</span>, and <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">λουω</span></span> of washing the body of Dorcas (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ac+9:37">Acts 9:37</span>) and the stripes of the prisoners (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ac+16:33">Acts 16:33</span>). On "nets" see on <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mt+4:20">Matthew 4:20</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mr+1:18">Mark 1:18</span>. </p>
