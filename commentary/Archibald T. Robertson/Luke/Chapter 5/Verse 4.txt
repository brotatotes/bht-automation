<p><b> Had left speaking </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">επαυσατο λαλων</span></span>). He ceased speaking (aorist middle indicative and present active participle, regular Greek idiom).</p>
<p><b>Put out into the deep </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">επαναγαγε εις το βαθος</span></span>). The same double compound verb as in verse <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="lu+5:3">Luke 5:3</span>, only here second aorist active imperative second person singular.</p>
<p><b>Let down </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">χαλασατε</span></span>). Peter was master of the craft and so he was addressed first. First aorist active imperative second person plural. Here the whole crew are addressed. The verb is the regular nautical term for lowering cargo or boats (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ac+27:17">Acts 27:17</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ac+27:30">Acts 27:30</span>). But it was used for lowering anything from a higher place (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mr+2:4">Mark 2:4</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ac+9:25">Acts 9:25</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="2co+11:33">2 Corinthians 11:33</span>). For a catch (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εις αγραν</span></span>). This purpose was the startling thing that stirred up Simon. </p>
