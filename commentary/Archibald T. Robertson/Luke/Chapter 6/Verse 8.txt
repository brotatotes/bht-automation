<p><b> But he knew their thoughts </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">αυτος δε ηιδε τους διαλογισμους αυτων</span></span>). In Luke alone. Imperfect in sense, second past perfect in form <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ηιδε</span></span> from <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">οιδα</span></span>. Jesus, in contrast to these spies (Plummer), read their intellectual processes like an open book.</p>
<p><b>His hand withered </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ξηραν την χειρα</span></span>). Predicate position of the adjective. So in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mr+3:3">Mark 3:3</span>.</p>
<p><b>Stand forth </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">στηθ</span></span>). Luke alone has this verb, second aorist active imperative. <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mr+3:3">Mark 3:3</span> has</p>
<p><b>Arise into the midst </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εγειρε εις το μεσον</span></span>). Luke has</p>
<p><b>Arise and step forth into the midst </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εγειρε κα στηθ εις το μεσον</span></span>). Christ worked right out in the open where all could see. It was a moment of excitement when the man stepped forth (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εστη</span></span>) there before them all. </p>
