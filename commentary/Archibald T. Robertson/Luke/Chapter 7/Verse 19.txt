<p><b> Calling unto him </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">προσκαλεσαμενος</span></span>). First aorist middle (indirect) participle.</p>
<p><b>Two </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">δυο τινας</span></span>). Certain two. Not in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mt+11:2">Matthew 11:2</span>.</p>
<p><b>Saying </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">λεγων</span></span>). John saying by the two messengers. The message is given precisely alike in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mt+11:3">Matthew 11:3</span>, which see. In both we have <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ετερον</span></span> for "another," either a second or a different kind. In verse <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="lu+7:20">Luke 7:20</span> Westcott and Hort read <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">αλλον</span></span> in the text, <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ετερον</span></span> in the margin. <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">Προσδοκωμεν</span></span>, may be present indicative or present subjunctive (deliberative), the same contract form (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">αο= ω, αω ω</span></span>). </p>
