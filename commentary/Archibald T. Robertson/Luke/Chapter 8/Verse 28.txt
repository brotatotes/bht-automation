<p><b> Fell down </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">προσεπεσεν</span></span>). Second aorist active of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">προσπιπτω</span></span>, to fall forward, towards, prostrate before one as here. Common verb. <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mr+5:6">Mark 5:6</span> has <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">προσεκυνησεν</span></span> (worshipped).</p>
<p><b>The Most High God </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">του θεου του υψιστου</span></span>). Uncertain whether <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">του θεου</span></span> genuine or not. But "the Most High" clearly means God as already seen (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="lu+1:32">Luke 1:32</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="lu+1:35">Luke 1:35</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="lu+1:36">Luke 1:36</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="lu+6:35">Luke 6:35</span>). The phrase is common among heathen (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="nu+24:16">Numbers 24:16</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mic+6:6">Micah 6:6</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="isa+14:14">Isaiah 14:14</span>). The demoniac may have been a Gentile, but it is the demon here speaking. See on <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mr+2:7">Mark 2:7</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mt+8:29">Matthew 8:29</span> for the Greek idiom (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">τ εμο κα σο</span></span>). "What have I to do with thee?" See there also for "Torment me not." </p>
