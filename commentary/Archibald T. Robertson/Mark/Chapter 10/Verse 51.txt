<p><b> That I should do </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ποιησω</span></span>). Neat Greek idiom with aorist subjunctive without <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ινα</span></span> after <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">θελεις</span></span>. For this asyndeton (or parataxis) see Robertson, <i>Grammar</i>, p. 430.</p>
<p><b>Rabboni </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">Ραββουνε</span></span>). The Aramaic word translated Lord (Kurie) in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mt+20:33">Matthew 20:33</span> and <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="lu+18:41">Luke 18:41</span>. This very form occurs again in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="joh+20:16">John 20:16</span>.</p>
<p><b>That I may receive my sight </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ινα αναβλεψω</span></span>). To recover sight (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ανα-</span></span>), see again. Apparently he had once been able to see. Here <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ινα</span></span> is used though <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">θελω</span></span> is not (cf. <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mr+10:35">Mark 10:35</span>). The Messiah was expected to give sight to the blind (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="isa+61:1">Isaiah 61:1</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="lu+4:18">Luke 4:18</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="lu+7:22">Luke 7:22</span>). </p>
