<p><b> To the eleven themselves </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">αυτοις τοις ενδεκα</span></span>). Both terms, eleven and twelve (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="joh+20:24">John 20:24</span>), occur after the death of Judas. There were others present on this first Sunday evening according to <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="lu+24:33">Luke 24:33</span>.</p>
<p><b>Afterward </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">υστερον</span></span>) is here alone in Mark, though common in Matthew.</p>
<p><b>Upbraided </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ωνειδισεν</span></span>). They were guilty of unbelief (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">απιστιαν</span></span>) and hardness of heart (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">σκληροκαρδιαν</span></span>). Doubt is not necessarily a mark of intellectual superiority. One must steer between credulity and doubt. That problem is a vital one today in all educated circles. Some of the highest men of science today are devout believers in the Risen Christ. Luke explains how the disciples were upset by the sudden appearance of Christ and were unable to believe the evidence of their own senses (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="lu+24:38-43">Luke 24:38-43</span>). </p>
