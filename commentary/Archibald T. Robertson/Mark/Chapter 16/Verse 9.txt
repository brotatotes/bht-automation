<p><b> When he had risen early on the first day of the week </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">αναστας πρω πρωτη σαββατου</span></span>). It is probable that this note of time goes with "risen" (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">αναστας</span></span>), though it makes good sense with "appeared" (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εφανη</span></span>). Jesus is not mentioned by name here, though he is clearly the one meant. Mark uses <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">μια</span></span> in verse <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mr+16:2">Mark 16:2</span>, but <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">πρωτη</span></span> in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mr+14:12">Mark 14:12</span> and the plural <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">σαββατων</span></span> in verse <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mr+16:2">Mark 16:2</span>, though the singular here.</p>
<p><b>First </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">πρωτον</span></span>). Definite statement that Jesus</p>
<p><b>appeared </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εφανη</span></span>) to Mary Magdalene first of all. The verb <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εφανη</span></span> (second aorist passive of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">φαινω</span></span>) is here alone of the Risen Christ (cf. <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">Ελειας εφανη</span></span>, <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="lu+9:8">Luke 9:8</span>), the usual verb being <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ωφθη</span></span> (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="lu+24:34">Luke 24:34</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="1co+15:5">1 Corinthians 15:5</span>).</p>
<p><b>From whom </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">παρ' ης</span></span>). Only instance of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">παρα</span></span> with the casting out of demons, <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εκ</span></span> being usual (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mr+1:25">Mark 1:25</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mr+1:26">Mark 1:26</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mr+5:8">Mark 5:8</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mr+7:26">Mark 7:26</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mr+7:29">Mark 7:29</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mr+9:25">Mark 9:25</span>). <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">Εκβεβληκε</span></span> is past perfect indicative without augment. This description of Mary Magdalene is like that in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="lu+8:2">Luke 8:2</span> and seems strange in Mark at this point, described as a new character here, though mentioned by Mark three times just before (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mr+15:40">Mark 15:40</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mr+15:47">Mark 15:47</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mr+16:1">Mark 16:1</span>). The appearance to Mary Magdalene is given in full by <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="joh+20:11-18">John 20:11-18</span>. </p>
