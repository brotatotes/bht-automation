<p><b> Often bound </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">πολλακις δεδεσθα</span></span>). Perfect passive infinitive, state of completion. With fetters (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">πεδαις</span></span>, from <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">πεζα</span></span>, foot, instep) and chains, bound hand and foot, but all to no purpose. The English plural of foot is feet (Anglo-Saxon <i>fot</i>, <i>fet</i>) and fetter is <i>feeter</i>.</p>
<p><b>Rent asunder </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">διεσπασθα</span></span>). Drawn (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">σπαω</span></span>) in two (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">δια-</span></span> same root as <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">δυο</span></span>, two). Perfect passive infinitive.</p>
<p><b>Broken in pieces </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">συντετριφθα</span></span>.) Perfect passive infinitive again, from <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">συντριβω</span></span>, to rub together. Rubbed together, crushed together. Perhaps the neighbours who told the story could point to broken fragments of chains and fetters. The fetters may have been cords, or even wooden stocks and not chains.</p>
<p><b>No man had strength to tame him </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ουδεις ισχυεν αυτον δαμασα</span></span>). Imperfect tense. He roamed at will like a lion in the jungle. </p>
