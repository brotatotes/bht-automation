<p><b> They were as sheep not having a shepherd </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ησαν ως προβατα μη εχοντα ποιμενα</span></span>). Matthew has these words in another context (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mt+9:26">Matthew 9:26</span>), but Mark alone has them here. <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">Μη</span></span> is the usual negative for the participle in the <i>Koine</i>. These excited and exciting people (Bruce) greatly needed teaching. <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mt+14:14">Matthew 14:14</span> mentions healing as does <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="lu+9:11">Luke 9:11</span> (both preaching and healing). But a vigorous crowd of runners would not have many sick. The people had plenty of official leaders but these rabbis were for spiritual matters blind leaders of the blind. Jesus had come over for rest, but his heart was touched by the pathos of this situation. So "he began to teach them many things" (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ηρξατο διδασκειν αυτους πολλα</span></span>). Two accusatives with the verb of teaching and the present tense of the infinitive. He kept it up. </p>
