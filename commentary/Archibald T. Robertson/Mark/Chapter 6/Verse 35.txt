<p><b> When the day was now far spent </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ηδη ωρας πολλης γενομενης</span></span>). Genitive absolute. <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">Hωρα</span></span> used here for day-time (so <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mt+14:15">Matthew 14:15</span>) as in Polybius and late Greek.</p>
<p><b>Much day-time already gone </b>. <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="lu+9:12">Luke 9:12</span> has it began to</p>
<p><b>incline </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">κλινειν</span></span>) or wear away. It was after 3 P.M., the first evening. Note second evening or sunset in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mr+6:47">Mark 6:47</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mt+14:23">Matthew 14:23</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="joh+6:16">John 6:16</span>. The turn of the afternoon had come and sunset was approaching. The idiom is repeated at the close of the verse. See on <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mt+14:15">Matthew 14:15</span>. </p>
