<p><b> Shall lose it </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">απολεσε αυτην</span></span>). This paradox appears in four forms according to Allen (I) <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mt+10:39">Matthew 10:39</span> (2) <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mr+8:35">Mark 8:35</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mt+16:25">Matthew 16:25</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="lu+9:24">Luke 9:24</span> (3) <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="lu+17:33">Luke 17:33</span> (4) <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="joh+12:25">John 12:25</span>. <i>The Wisdom of Sirach</i> (Hebrew text) in 51:26 has: "He that giveth his life findeth her (wisdom)." It is one of the profound sayings of Christ that he repeated many times. Plato (<i>Gorgias</i> 512) has language somewhat similar though not so sharply put. The article and aorist participles here (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ο ευρων, ο απολεσας</span></span>) are timeless in themselves just like <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ο δεχομενος</span></span> in verses <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mt+10:40">Matthew 10:40</span> and <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mt+10:41">Matthew 10:41</span>. </p>
