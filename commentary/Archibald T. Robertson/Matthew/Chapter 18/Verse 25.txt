<p><b> Had not wherewith to pay </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">μη εχοντος αυτου αποδουνα</span></span>). There is no "wherewith" in the Greek. This idiom is seen in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="lu+7:42">Luke 7:42</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="lu+14:14">Luke 14:14</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="heb+6:13">Hebrews 6:13</span>. Genitive absolute though <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">αυτον</span></span> in the same clause as often in the N.T.</p>
<p><b>To be sold </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">πραθηνα</span></span>). First aorist passive infinitive of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">πιπρασκω</span></span>. This was according to the law (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ex+22:3">Exodus 22:3</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="le+25:39">Leviticus 25:39</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="le+25:47">Leviticus 25:47</span>). Wife and children were treated as property in those primitive times. </p>
