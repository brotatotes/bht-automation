<p><b> And they said unto him </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ο δε ειπαν αυτω</span></span>). Whether the ecclesiastics had to search their scriptures or not, they give the answer that is in accord with the common Jewish opinion that the Messiah was to come from Bethlehem and of the seed of David (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="joh+7:42">John 7:42</span>). So they quote <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mic+5:2">Micah 5:2</span>, "a free paraphrase" Alford calls it, for it is not precisely like the Hebrew text or like the Septuagint. It may have come from a collection of <i>testimonia</i> with which J. Rendel Harris has made the world familiar. He had consulted the experts and now he has their answer. Bethlehem of Judah is the place. The use of the perfect passive indicative (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">γεγραπτα</span></span>) is the common form in quoting scripture. It stands written.</p>
<p><b> Shall be shepherd </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ποιμανε</span></span>). The Authorized Version had "shall rule," but "shepherd" is correct. "Homer calls kings 'the shepherds of the people'" (Vincent). In <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="heb+13:20">Hebrews 13:20</span> Jesus is called "the great shepherd of the sheep." Jesus calls himself "the good shepherd" (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="joh+10:11">John 10:11</span>). Peter calls Christ "the chief shepherd" (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="1pe+2:25">1 Peter 2:25</span>). "The Lamb which is in the midst of the throne shall be their shepherd" (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="re+7:17">Revelation 7:17</span>). Jesus told Peter to "shepherd" the lambs (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="joh+21:16">John 21:16</span>). Our word pastor means shepherd. </p>
