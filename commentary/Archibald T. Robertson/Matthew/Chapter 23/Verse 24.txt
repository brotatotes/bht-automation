<p><b> Strain out the gnat </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">διυλιζοντες τον κωνωπα</span></span>). By filtering through (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">δια</span></span>), not the "straining at" in swallowing so crudely suggested by the misprint in the A.V.</p>
<p><b>Swallow the camel </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">την δε καμηλον καταπινοντες</span></span>). Gulping or drinking down the camel. An oriental hyperbole like that in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mt+19:24">Matthew 19:24</span>. See also <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mt+5:29">Matthew 5:29</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mt+5:30">Matthew 5:30</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mt+17:20">Matthew 17:20</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mt+21:21">Matthew 21:21</span>. Both insects and camels were ceremonially unclean (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="le+11:4">Leviticus 11:4</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="le+11:20">Leviticus 11:20</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="le+11:23">Leviticus 11:23</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="le+11:42">Leviticus 11:42</span>). "He that kills a flea on the Sabbath is as guilty as if he killed a camel" (Jer. <i>Shabb.</i> 107). </p>
