<p><b> Beware </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">βλεπετε</span></span>). Three times for urgency and with different epithet for the Judaizers each time.</p>
<p><b>The dogs </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">τους κυνας</span></span>). The Jews so termed the Gentiles which Jesus uses in a playful mood (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">κυναριοις</span></span>, little dogs) to the Syro-Phoenician woman (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mt+15:26">Matthew 15:26</span>). Paul here turns the phrase on the Judaizers themselves.</p>
<p><b>The evil workers </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">τους κακους εργατας</span></span>). He had already called the Judaizers "deceitful workers" (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εργατα δολιο</span></span>) in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="2co+11:13">2 Corinthians 11:13</span>.</p>
<p><b>The concision </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">την κατατομην</span></span>). Late word for incision, mutilation (in contrast with <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">περιτομη</span></span>, circumcision). In Symmachus and an inscription. The verb <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">κατατεμνω</span></span> is used in the LXX only of mutilations (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="le+21:5">Leviticus 21:5</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="1ki+18:28">1 Kings 18:28</span>). </p>
