<p><b> I know how </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">οιδα</span></span>). Followed by the infinitive <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">οιδα</span></span> has this sense. So here twice, with <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ταπεινουσθα</span></span>, to be humbled, from <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ταπεινος</span></span>, and with <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">περισσευειν</span></span>, to overflow.</p>
<p><b>Have I learned the secret </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">μεμυημα</span></span>). Perfect passive indicative of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">μυεω</span></span>, old and common word from <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">μυω</span></span>, to close (Latin <i>mutus</i>), and so to initiate with secret rites, here only in N.T. The common word <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">μυστηριον</span></span> (mystery) is from <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">μυστης</span></span> (one initiated) and this from <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">μυεω</span></span>, to initiate, to instruct in secrets. Paul draws this metaphor from the initiatory rites of the pagan mystery-religions.</p>
<p><b>To be filled </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">χορταζεσθα</span></span>). Old verb from <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">χορτος</span></span> (grass, hay) and so to fatten like an animal.</p>
<p><b>To be hungry </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">πειναιν</span></span>). Old verb from <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">πεινα</span></span> (hunger) and kin to <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">πενης</span></span>, poor man who has to work for his living (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">πενομα</span></span>). </p>
