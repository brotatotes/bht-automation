<p><b> One like unto a son of man </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ομοιον υιον ανθρωπου</span></span>). Note accusative here with <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ομοιον</span></span> (object of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ειδον</span></span>) as in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="re+14:14">Revelation 14:14</span> and not the associative-instrumental as is usual (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="re+1:15">Revelation 1:15</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="re+4:3">Revelation 4:3</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="re+4:6">Revelation 4:6</span>). Charles holds that <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ομοιον</span></span> here has the sense of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ως</span></span> (as) and compares <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="re+4:6">Revelation 4:6</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="re+22:1">Revelation 22:1</span> for proof. The absence of the article here shows also (Charles) that the idea is not "like the Son of man" for Christ is the Son of man. He is like "a son of man," but not a man.</p>
<p><b>Clothed </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ενδεδυμενον</span></span>). Perfect passive participle of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ενδυω</span></span>, accusative case agreeing with <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ομοιον</span></span>.</p>
<p><b>A garment down to the foot </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ποδηρη</span></span>). Old adjective <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ποδηρης</span></span> (from <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">πους</span></span>, foot, and <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">αιρω</span></span>), here only in N.T., accusative singular retained with the passive participle as often with verbs of clothing. Supply <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">χιτωνα</span></span> or <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εσθητα</span></span> (garment).</p>
<p><b>Girt about </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">περιεζωσμενον</span></span>). Perfect passive participle of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">περιζωννυμ</span></span>, accusative singular agreeing with <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ομοιον</span></span>.</p>
<p><b>At the breasts </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">προς τοις μαστοις</span></span>). Old word for breasts of a woman (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="lu+11:27">Luke 11:27</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="lu+23:29">Luke 23:29</span>) and nipples of a man, as here. High girding like this was a mark of dignity as of the high priest (Josephus, <i>Ant</i>. III. 7. 2). For <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">προς</span></span> with the locative see <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mr+5:11">Mark 5:11</span>.</p>
<p><b>With a golden girdle </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ζωνην χρυσαν</span></span>). Accusative case again retained with the passive participle (verb of clothing). Note also <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">χρυσαν</span></span> (vernacular <i>Koine</i>) rather than the old form, <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">χρυσην</span></span>. </p>
