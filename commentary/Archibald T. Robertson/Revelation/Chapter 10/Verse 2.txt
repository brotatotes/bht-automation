<p><b> And he had </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">κα εχων</span></span>). This use of the participle in place of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ειχεν</span></span> (imperfect) is like that in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="re+4:7">Revelation 4:7</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="re+12:2">Revelation 12:2</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="re+19:12">Revelation 19:12</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="re+21:12">Revelation 21:12</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="re+21:14">Revelation 21:14</span>, a Semitic idiom (Charles), or as if <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">καταβαινων</span></span> (nominative) had preceded in place of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">καταβαινοντα</span></span>.</p>
<p><b>A little book </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">βιβλαριδιον</span></span>). A diminutive of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">βιβλαριον</span></span> (papyri), itself a diminutive of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">βιβλιον</span></span> (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="re+5:1">Revelation 5:1</span>) and perhaps in contrast with it, a rare form in Hermas and <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="re+10:2">Revelation 10:2</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="re+10:9">Revelation 10:9</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="re+10:10">Revelation 10:10</span>. In <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="re+10:8">Revelation 10:8</span> Tischendorf reads <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">βιβλιδαριον</span></span>, diminutive of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">βιβλιδιον</span></span> (Aristophanes) instead of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">βιβλιον</span></span> (Westcott and Hort). The contents of this little book are found in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="re+11:1-13">Revelation 11:1-13</span>.</p>
<p><b>Open </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ηνεωιγμενον</span></span>). See <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="eze+2:9">Ezekiel 2:9</span>. Perfect (triple reduplication) passive participle of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ανοιγω</span></span>, in contrast to the closed book in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="re+5:1">Revelation 5:1</span>. There also we have <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">επ</span></span> (upon) <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">την δεξιαν</span></span> (the right hand), for it was a large roll, but here the little open roll is held in the hand (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εν τη χειρ</span></span>), apparently the left hand (verse <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="re+10:5">Revelation 10:5</span>).</p>
<p><b>He set </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εθηκεν</span></span>). First aorist active indicative of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">τιθημ</span></span>. The size of the angel is colossal, for he bestrides both land and sea. Apparently there is no special point in the right foot (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">τον ποδα τον δεξιον</span></span>) being on the sea (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">επ της θαλασσης</span></span>) and the left (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">τον ευωνυμον</span></span>) upon the land (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">επ της γης</span></span>). It makes a bold and graphic picture.</p>
<p><b>As a lion roareth </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ωσπερ λεων μυκατα</span></span>). Only instance of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ωσπερ</span></span> in the Apocalypse, but <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ως</span></span> in the same sense several times. Present middle indicative of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">μυκαομα</span></span>, an old onomatopoetic word from <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">μυ</span></span> or <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">μοο</span></span> (the sound which a cow utters), common for the lowing and bellowing of cattle, Latin <i>mugire</i>, but in Theocritus for the roaring of a lion as here, though in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="1pe+5:8">1 Peter 5:8</span> we have <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ωρυομα</span></span>. Homer uses <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">μυκαομα</span></span> for the clangour of the shield and Aristophanes for thunder. It occurs here alone in the N.T. It does not mean that what the angel said was unintelligible, only loud. Cf. <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="re+1:10">Revelation 1:10</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="re+5:2">Revelation 5:2</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="re+5:12">Revelation 5:12</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="re+6:10">Revelation 6:10</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="re+7:2">Revelation 7:2</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="re+7:10">Revelation 7:10</span>, etc. </p>
