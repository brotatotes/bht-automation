<p><b> Were wroth </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ωργισθησαν</span></span>). Ingressive first aorist active indicative of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">οργιζομα</span></span>, "became angry." The culmination of wrath against God (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="re+16:13">Revelation 16:13</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="re+20:8">Revelation 20:8</span>). Cf. <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ps+2:1">Psalms 2:1</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ps+2:5">Psalms 2:5</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ps+2:12">Psalms 2:12</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ps+99:1">Psalms 99:1</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ac+4:25">Acts 4:25</span>. John sees the hostility of the world against Christ.</p>
<p><b>Thy wrath came </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ηλθεν η οργη σου</span></span>). Second aorist active indicative of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ερχομα</span></span>, the prophetic aorist again. The <i>Dies Irae</i> is conceived as already come.</p>
<p><b>The time of the dead to be judged </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ο καιρος των νεκρων κριθηνα</span></span>). For this use of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">καιρος</span></span> see <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mr+11:13">Mark 11:13</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="lu+21:24">Luke 21:24</span>. By "the dead" John apparently means both good and bad (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="joh+5:25">John 5:25</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ac+24:21">Acts 24:21</span>), coincident with the resurrection and judgment (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mr+4:29">Mark 4:29</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="re+14:15">Revelation 14:15</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="re+20:1-15">Revelation 20:1-15</span>). The infinitive <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">κριθηνα</span></span> is the first aorist passive of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">κρινω</span></span>, epexegetic use with the preceding clause, as is true also of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">δουνα</span></span> (second aorist active infinitive of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">διδωμ</span></span>), to give.</p>
<p><b>Their reward </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">τον μισθον</span></span>). This will come in the end of the day (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mt+20:8">Matthew 20:8</span>), from God (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mt+6:1">Matthew 6:1</span>), at the Lord's return (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="re+22:12">Revelation 22:12</span>), according to each one's work (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="1co+3:8">1 Corinthians 3:8</span>).</p>
<p><b>The small and the great </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">τους μικρους κα τους μεγαλους</span></span>). The accusative here is an anacoluthon and fails to agree in case with the preceding datives after <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">δουνα τον μισθον</span></span>, though some MSS. have the dative <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">τοις μικροις</span></span>, etc. John is fond of this phrase "the small and the great" (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="re+13:16">Revelation 13:16</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="re+19:5">Revelation 19:5</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="re+19:18">Revelation 19:18</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="re+20:12">Revelation 20:12</span>).</p>
<p><b>To destroy </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">διαφθειρα</span></span>). First aorist active infinitive of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">διαφθειρω</span></span>, carrying on the construction with <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">καιρος</span></span>. Note <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">τους διαφθειροντας</span></span>, "those destroying" the earth (corrupting the earth). There is a double sense in <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">διαφθειρω</span></span> that justifies this play on the word. See <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="re+19:2">Revelation 19:2</span>. In <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="1ti+6:5">1 Timothy 6:5</span> we have those "corrupted in mind" (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">διαφθαρμενο τον νουν</span></span>). God will destroy the destroyers (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="1co+3:16">1 Corinthians 3:16</span>). </p>
