<p><b> I will give </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">δωσω</span></span>). Future active of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">διδωμ</span></span>. The speaker may be God (Beckwith) or Christ (Swete) as in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="re+2:13">Revelation 2:13</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="re+21:6">Revelation 21:6</span> or his angel representative (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="re+22:7">Revelation 22:7</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="re+22:12">Revelation 22:12</span>). The idiom that follows is Hebraic instead of either the infinitive after <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">διδωμ</span></span> as in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="re+2:7">Revelation 2:7</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="re+3:21">Revelation 3:21</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="re+6:4">Revelation 6:4</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="re+7:2">Revelation 7:2</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="re+13:7">Revelation 13:7</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="re+13:15">Revelation 13:15</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="re+16:8">Revelation 16:8</span> or <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ινα</span></span> with the subjunctive (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="re+9:5">Revelation 9:5</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="re+19:8">Revelation 19:8</span>) we have <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">κα προφητευσουσιν</span></span> (and they shall prophesy).</p>
<p><b>Unto my two witnesses </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">τοις δυσιν μαρτυσιν μου</span></span>). Dative case after <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">δωσω</span></span>. The article seems to point to two well-known characters, like Elijah, Elisha, but there is no possible way to determine who they are. All sorts of identifications have been attempted.</p>
<p><b>Clothed </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">περιβλημενους</span></span>). Perfect passive participle of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">περιβαλλω</span></span> as often before (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="re+7:9">Revelation 7:9</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="re+7:13">Revelation 7:13</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="re+10:1">Revelation 10:1</span>, etc.). But Aleph A P Q here read the accusative plural in <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">-ους</span></span>, while C has the nominative in <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">-ο</span></span>. Charles suggests a mere slip for the nominative, but Hort suggests a primitive error in early MSS. for the dative <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">περιβεβλεμενοις</span></span> agreeing with <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">μαρτυσιν</span></span>.</p>
<p><b>In sackcloth </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">σακκους</span></span>). Accusative retained with this passive verb as in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="re+7:9">Revelation 7:9</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="re+7:13">Revelation 7:13</span>. See <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="re+6:12">Revelation 6:12</span> for <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">σακκος</span></span> and also <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mt+3:4">Matthew 3:4</span>. The dress suited the message (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mt+11:21">Matthew 11:21</span>). </p>
