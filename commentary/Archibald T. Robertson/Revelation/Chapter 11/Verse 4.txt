<p><b> The two olive trees </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">α δυο ελαια</span></span>). The article seems to point to what is known. For this original use of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ελαια</span></span> see <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ro+11:17">Romans 11:17</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ro+11:24">Romans 11:24</span>. In <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="zec+4:2">Zechariah 4:2</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="zec+4:3">Zechariah 4:3</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="zec+4:14">Zechariah 4:14</span> the lampstand or candlestick (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">λυχνια</span></span>) is Israel, and the two olive trees apparently Joshua and Zerubbabel, but John makes his own use of this symbolism. Here the two olive trees and the candlesticks are identical.</p>
<p><b>Standing </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εστωτες</span></span>). Masculine perfect active participle agreeing with <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ουτο</span></span> instead of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εστωσα</span></span> (read by P and cursives) agreeing with <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ελαια κα λυχνια</span></span>, even though <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">α</span></span> (feminine plural article) be accepted before <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ενωπιον του κυριου</span></span> (before the Lord). </p>
