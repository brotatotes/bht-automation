<p><b> There was war in heaven </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εγενετο πολεμος εν τω ουρανω</span></span>). "There came to be war in heaven" (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εγενετο</span></span>, not <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ην</span></span>). "Another <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ταβλεαυ</span></span>, not a <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">σημειον</span></span> (vv. <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="re+12:1">Revelation 12:1</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="re+12:3">Revelation 12:3</span>), but consequent upon the two <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">σημεια</span></span> which precede it. The birth and rapture of the Woman's Son issue in a war which invades the <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">επουρανια</span></span>" (Swete). The reference is not to the original rebellion of Satan, as Andreas held. As the coming of Christ brought on fresh manifestations of diabolic power (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mr+1:13">Mark 1:13</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="lu+22:3">Luke 22:3</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="lu+22:31">Luke 22:31</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="joh+12:31">John 12:31</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="joh+14:30">John 14:30</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="joh+16:11">John 16:11</span>), just so Christ's return to heaven is pictured as being the occasion of renewed attacks there. We are not to visualize it too literally, but certainly modern airplanes help us to grasp the notion of battles in the sky even more than the phalanxes of storm-clouds (Swete). John even describes this last conflict as in heaven itself. Cf. <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="lu+10:18">Luke 10:18</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="1ki+22:1">1 Kings 22:1</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="1ki+22:1">1 Kings 22:1</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="1ki+22:2">1 Kings 22:2</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="zec+3:1">Zechariah 3:1</span>.</p>
<p><b>Michael and his angels </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ο Μιχαηλ κα ο αγγελο αυτου</span></span>). The nominative here may be in apposition with <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">πολεμος</span></span>, but it is an abnormal construction with no verb, though <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εγενετο</span></span> (arose) can be understood as repeated. Michael is the champion of the Jewish people (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="da+10:13">Daniel 10:13</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="da+10:21">Daniel 10:21</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="da+12:1">Daniel 12:1</span>) and is called the archangel in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="jude+1:9">Jude 1:9</span>.</p>
<p><b>Going forth to war </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">του πολεμησα</span></span>). This genitive articular infinitive is another grammatical problem in this sentence. If <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εγενετο</span></span> (arose) is repeated as above, then we have the infinitive for purpose, a common enough idiom. Otherwise it is anomalous, not even like <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ac+10:25">Acts 10:25</span>.</p>
<p><b>With the dragon </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">μετα του δρακοντος</span></span>). On the use of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">μετα</span></span> with <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">πολεμεω</span></span> see <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="re+2:16">Revelation 2:16</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="re+13:4">Revelation 13:4</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="re+17:14">Revelation 17:14</span> (nowhere else in N.T.). The devil has angels under his command (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mt+25:41">Matthew 25:41</span>) and preachers also (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="2co+11:14">2 Corinthians 11:14</span>).</p>
<p><b>Warred </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">επολεμησεν</span></span>). Constative aorist active indicative of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">πολεμεω</span></span>, picturing the whole battle in one glimpse. </p>
