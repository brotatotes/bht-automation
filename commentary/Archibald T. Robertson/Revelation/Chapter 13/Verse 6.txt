<p><b> For blasphemies </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εις βλασφημιας</span></span>). "For the purpose of blasphemies."</p>
<p><b>Against God </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">προς τον θεον</span></span>). "Face to face with God" in sheer defiance, like Milton's picture of Satan in <i>Paradise Lost</i>. See <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="da+7:25">Daniel 7:25</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="da+8:10">Daniel 8:10</span>. The aorist <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ηνοιξεν</span></span> is probably constative, for he repeated the blasphemies, though the phrase (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ανοιγω</span></span> to stoma, to open the mouth) is normally ingressive of the beginning of an utterance (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mt+5:2">Matthew 5:2</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ac+8:35">Acts 8:35</span>). This verse explains verse <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="re+13:5">Revelation 13:5</span>. The Roman emperors blasphemously assumed divine names in public documents. They directed their blasphemy against heaven itself ("his tabernacle," <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">την σκηνην αυτου</span></span>, <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="re+7:15">Revelation 7:15</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="re+12:12">Revelation 12:12</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="re+21:3">Revelation 21:3</span>) and against "them that dwell in the heaven" (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">τους εν τω ουρανω σκηνουντας</span></span>), the same phrase of <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="re+12:12">Revelation 12:12</span> (either angels or the redeemed or both). </p>
