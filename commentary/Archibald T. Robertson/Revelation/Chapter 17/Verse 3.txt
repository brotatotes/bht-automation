<p><b> He carried me away </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">απηνεγκεν με</span></span>). Second aorist active indicative of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">αποφερω</span></span>, to bear away, prophetic aorist. This verb is used of angels at death (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="lu+16:22">Luke 16:22</span>) or in an ecstasy (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="re+21:10">Revelation 21:10</span> and here).</p>
<p><b>In the Spirit </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εν πνευματ</span></span>). Probably his own spirit, though the Holy Spirit is possible (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="re+1:10">Revelation 1:10</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="re+4:2">Revelation 4:2</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="re+21:10">Revelation 21:10</span>), without Paul's uncertainty (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="2co+12:2">2 Corinthians 12:2</span>). Cf. <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="eze+3:14">Ezekiel 3:14</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="eze+8:3">Ezekiel 8:3</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="eze+11:24">Ezekiel 11:24</span>.</p>
<p><b>Into a wilderness </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εις ερημον</span></span>). In <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="isa+21:1">Isaiah 21:1</span> there is <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">το οραμα της ερημου</span></span> (the vision of the deserted one, Babylon), and in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="isa+14:23">Isaiah 14:23</span> Babylon is called <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ερημον</span></span>. John may here picture this to be the fate of Rome or it may be that he himself, in the wilderness (desert) this side of Babylon, sees her fate. In <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="re+21:10">Revelation 21:10</span> he sees the New Jerusalem from a high mountain.</p>
<p><b>Sitting </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">καθημενην</span></span>). Present middle participle of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">καθημα</span></span> as in verse <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="re+17:1">Revelation 17:1</span>. "To manage and guide the beast" (Vincent).</p>
<p><b>Upon a scarlet-coloured beast </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">επ θηριον κοκκινον</span></span>). Accusative with <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">επ</span></span> here, though genitive in verse <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="re+17:1">Revelation 17:1</span>. Late adjective (from <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">κοκκος</span></span>, a parasite of the <i>ilex coccifera</i>), a crimson tint for splendour, in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="re+17:3">Revelation 17:3</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="re+17:4">Revelation 17:4</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="re+18:12">Revelation 18:12</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="re+18:16">Revelation 18:16</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mt+27:28">Matthew 27:28</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="heb+9:19">Hebrews 9:19</span>.</p>
<p><b>Full of names of blasphemy </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">γεμοντα ονοματα βλασφημιας</span></span>). See <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="re+13:1">Revelation 13:1</span> for "names of blasphemy" on the seven heads of the beast, but here they cover the whole body of the beast (the first beast of <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="re+13:1">Revelation 13:1</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="re+19:20">Revelation 19:20</span>). The harlot city (Rome) sits astride this beast with seven heads and ten horns (Roman world power). The beast is here personified with masculine participles instead of neuter, like <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">θηριον</span></span> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">γεμοντα</span></span> accusative singular, <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εχων</span></span> nominative singular, though some MSS. read <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εχοντα</span></span>), construction according to sense in both instances. The verb <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">γεμω</span></span> always has the genitive after it in the Apocalypse (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="re+4:6">Revelation 4:6</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="re+4:8">Revelation 4:8</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="re+5:8">Revelation 5:8</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="re+15:7">Revelation 15:7</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="re+17:4">Revelation 17:4</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="re+21:9">Revelation 21:9</span>) save here and apparently once in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="re+17:4">Revelation 17:4</span>. </p>
