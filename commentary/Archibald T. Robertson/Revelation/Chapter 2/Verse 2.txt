<p><b> I know </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">οιδα</span></span>). Rather than <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">γινωσκω</span></span> and so "emphasizes better the absolute clearness of mental vision which photographs all the facts of life as they pass" (Swete). So also in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="re+2:9">Revelation 2:9</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="re+2:13">Revelation 2:13</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="re+2:19">Revelation 2:19</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="re+3:1">Revelation 3:1</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="re+3:8">Revelation 3:8</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="re+3:15">Revelation 3:15</span>. For the distinction see <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="joh+21:17">John 21:17</span>, "where the universal knowledge passes into the field of special observation."</p>
<p><b>Works </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εργα</span></span>). The whole life and conduct as in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="joh+6:29">John 6:29</span>.</p>
<p><b>And thy toil and patience </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">κα τον κοπον κα την υπομονην σου</span></span>). "Both thy toil and patience," in explanation of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εργα</span></span>, and see <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="1th+1:3">1 Thessalonians 1:3</span>, where all three words (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εργον, κοποσ, υπομονη</span></span>) occur together as here. See <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="re+14:13">Revelation 14:13</span> for sharp distinction between <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εργα</span></span> (activities) and <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">κοπο</span></span> (toils, with weariness). Endurance (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">υπομονη</span></span>) in hard toil (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">κοπος</span></span>).</p>
<p><b>And that </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">κα οτ</span></span>). Further explanation of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">κοπος</span></span> (hard toil).</p>
<p><b>Not able </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ου δυνη</span></span>). This <i>Koine</i> form for the Attic <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">δυνασα</span></span> (second person singular indicative middle) occurs also in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mr+9:22">Mark 9:22</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="lu+16:2">Luke 16:2</span>.</p>
<p><b>Bear </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">βαστασα</span></span>). First aorist active infinitive of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">βασταζω</span></span>, for which verb see <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="joh+10:31">John 10:31</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="joh+12:6">John 12:6</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ga+6:2">Galatians 6:2</span>. These evil men were indeed a heavy burden.</p>
<p><b>And didst try </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">κα επειρασας</span></span>). First aorist active indicative of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">πειραζω</span></span>, to test, a reference to a recent crisis when these Nicolaitans (verse <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="re+2:6">Revelation 2:6</span>) were condemned. The present tenses (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">δυνηι, εχεις</span></span>) indicate the continuance of this attitude. Cf. <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="1jo+4:1">1 John 4:1</span>.</p>
<p><b>Which call themselves apostles </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">τους λεγοντας εαυτους αποστολους</span></span>). Perhaps itinerant missionaries of these Nicolaitans who posed as equal to or even superior to the original apostles, like the Judaizers so described by Paul (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="2co+11:5">2 Corinthians 11:5</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="2co+11:13">2 Corinthians 11:13</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="2co+12:11">2 Corinthians 12:11</span>). Paul had foretold such false teachers (Gnostics), grievous wolves, in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ac+20:29">Acts 20:29</span>; in sheep's clothing, Jesus had said (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mt+7:15">Matthew 7:15</span>).</p>
<p><b>And they are not </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">κα ουκ εισιν</span></span>). A parenthesis in Johannine style (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="joh+2:9">John 2:9</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="joh+3:9">John 3:9</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="1jo+3:1">1 John 3:1</span>) for <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">κα ουκ οντας</span></span> to correspond to <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">λεγοντας</span></span>.</p>
<p><b>And didst find </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">κα ευρες</span></span>). Second aorist active indicative of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ευρισκω</span></span>. Dropping back to the regular structure parallel with <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">επειρασας</span></span>.</p>
<p><b>False </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ψευδεις</span></span>). Predicate accusative plural of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ψευδης</span></span>, self-deceived deceivers as in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="re+21:8">Revelation 21:8</span>. </p>
