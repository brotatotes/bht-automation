<p>Sardonyx (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">σαρδονυξ</span></span>), here only in N.T., white with layers of red, from sardion (red carnelian) and onyx (white); for sardius (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">σαρδιον</span></span>) see <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="re+4:3">Revelation 4:3</span>; chrysolite (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">χρυσολιθος</span></span>), here only in N.T. (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ex+28:20">Exodus 28:20</span>), stone of a golden colour like our topaz or amber or a yellow beryl or golden jasper; beryl (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">βηρυλλος</span></span>), again here only in N.T. (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ex+28:20">Exodus 28:20</span>), note the difficulty of identification, much like the emerald according to Pliny; for topaz (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">τοπαζιον</span></span>), here only in N.T. (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ex+28:17">Exodus 28:17</span>), a golden-greenish stolle; chrysoprase (chrusoprasos), here only in N.T. (not in LXX), in colour like a teek, translucent golden-green; jacinth (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">υακινθος</span></span>), of the colour of the hyacinth, a violet colour (Pliny), already in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="re+9:17">Revelation 9:17</span> like blue smoke, like achates in LXX; amethyst (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">αμεθυστος</span></span>), only here in N.T. (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ex+28:19">Exodus 28:19</span>), of a violet and purple colour, more brilliant than the <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">υακινθος</span></span>. Swete sums up the colours thus: blue (sapphire, jacinth, amethyst), green (jasper, chalcedony, emerald, beryl, topaz, chrysoprase), red (sardonyx, sardius), yellow (chrysolite). But even so there is great variety in hue and brilliancy and in the reaction on each other. Clement of Alexandria argues that this variety illustrates the variety of gifts and graces in the twelve apostles. Possibly so. </p>
