<p><b> I have set </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">δεδωκα</span></span>). Perfect active indicative of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">διδωμ</span></span>, "I have given" (a gift of Christ, this open door). See <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="lu+12:51">Luke 12:51</span> for a like use of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">διδωμ</span></span>.</p>
<p><b>A door opened </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">θυραν ηνεωιγμενην</span></span>). Perfect (triple reduplication) passive predicate participle of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ανοιγω</span></span> (verse <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="re+3:7">Revelation 3:7</span>) accusative feminine singular. The metaphor of the open door was a common one (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="joh+10:7-9">John 10:7-9</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ac+14:27">Acts 14:27</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="1co+16:9">1 Corinthians 16:9</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="2co+2:12">2 Corinthians 2:12</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="col+4:3">Colossians 4:3</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="re+3:20">Revelation 3:20</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="re+4:1">Revelation 4:1</span>). Probably it means here a good opportunity for missionary effort in spite of the Jewish hostility.</p>
<p><b>Which </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ην--αυτην</span></span>). Pleonastic vernacular and Hebrew repetition of the personal pronoun <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">αυτην</span></span> (it) after the relative <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ην</span></span> (which). Direct reference to the statement in verse <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="re+3:7">Revelation 3:7</span>.</p>
<p><b>That </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">οτ</span></span>). This conjunction resumes the construction of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">οιδα σου τα εργα</span></span> (I know thy works) after the parenthesis (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ιδου--αυτην</span></span>, Behold--shut).</p>
<p><b>A little power </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">μικραν δυναμιν</span></span>). Probably "little power," little influence or weight in Philadelphia, the members probably from the lower classes (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="1co+1:26">1 Corinthians 1:26</span>).</p>
<p><b>And didst keep </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">κα ετηρησας</span></span>). "And yet (adversative use of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">κα</span></span>) didst keep" (first aorist active indicative of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">τηρεω</span></span>) my word in some crisis of trial. See <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="joh+17:6">John 17:6</span> for the phrase "keeping the word."</p>
<p><b>Didst not deny </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ουκ ηρνησω</span></span>). First aorist middle indicative second person singular of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">αρνεομα</span></span>. The issue was probably forced by the Jews (cf. <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="re+2:9">Revelation 2:9</span>), but they stood true. </p>
