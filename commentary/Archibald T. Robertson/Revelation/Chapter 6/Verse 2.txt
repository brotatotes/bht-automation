<p><b> And I saw and behold </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">κα ειδον κα ιδου</span></span>). This combination is frequent in the Apocalypse (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="re+4:1">Revelation 4:1</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="re+6:2">Revelation 6:2</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="re+6:5">Revelation 6:5</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="re+6:8">Revelation 6:8</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="re+14:1">Revelation 14:1</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="re+14:14">Revelation 14:14</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="re+19:11">Revelation 19:11</span>).</p>
<p><b>A white horse </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ιππος λευκος</span></span>). In <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="zec+6:1-8">Zechariah 6:1-8</span> we have red, black, white, and grizzled bay horses like the four winds of heaven, ministers to do God's will. White seems to be the colour of victory (cf. the white horse of the Persian Kings) like the white horse ridden by the Roman conqueror in a triumphant procession.</p>
<p><b>Had </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εχων</span></span>). Agreeing in gender and case with <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ο καθημενος</span></span>.</p>
<p><b>A bow </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">τοξον</span></span>). Old word (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="zec+9:13">Zechariah 9:13</span> of a great bow), here only in N.T.</p>
<p><b>Was given </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εδοθη</span></span>). First aorist passive indicative of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">διδωμ</span></span>.</p>
<p><b>A crown </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">στεφανος</span></span>). See on <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="re+4:4">Revelation 4:4</span> for this word.</p>
<p><b>He came forth </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εξηλθεν</span></span>). Second aorist active indicative of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εξερχομα</span></span>, either to come out or to go out (went forth).</p>
<p><b>Conquering </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">νικων</span></span>). Present active participle of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">νικαω</span></span>.</p>
<p><b>And to conquer </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">κα ινα νικηση</span></span>). Purpose clause with <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ινα</span></span> and the first aorist active subjunctive of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">νικαω</span></span>. Here <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ως νικησων</span></span> (future active participle with <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ως</span></span>) could have been used. The aorist tense here points to ultimate victory. Commentators have been busy identifying the rider of the white horse according to their various theories. "It is tempting to identify him with the Rider on the white horse in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="re+19:11">Revelation 19:11</span>, whose name is 'the Word of God'" (Swete). Tempting, "but the two riders have nothing in common beyond the white horse." </p>
