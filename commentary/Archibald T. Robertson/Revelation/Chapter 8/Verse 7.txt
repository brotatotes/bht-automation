<p><b> Sounded </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εσαλπισεν</span></span>). First aorist active indicative of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">σαλπιζω</span></span>, repeated with each angel in turn (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="re+8:8">Revelation 8:8</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="re+8:10">Revelation 8:10</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="re+8:12">Revelation 8:12</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="re+9:1">Revelation 9:1</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="re+9:13">Revelation 9:13</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="re+11:15">Revelation 11:15</span>).</p>
<p><b>Hail and fire mingled with blood </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">χαλαζα κα πυρ μεμιγμενα εν αιματ</span></span>). Like the plague of hail and fire in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ex+9:24">Exodus 9:24</span>. The first four trumpets are very much like the plagues in Egypt, this one like a semitropical thunderstorm (Swete) with blood like the first plague (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ex+7:17">Exodus 7:17</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ps+106:35">Psalms 106:35</span>). The old feminine word <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">χαλαζα</span></span> (hail) is from the verb <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">χαλαω</span></span>, to let down (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mr+2:4">Mark 2:4</span>), in N.T. only in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="re+8:7">Revelation 8:7</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="re+11:19">Revelation 11:19</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="re+16:21">Revelation 16:21</span>. The perfect passive participle <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">μεμιγμενα</span></span> (from <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">μιγνυμ</span></span>, to mix) is neuter plural because of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">πυρ</span></span> (fire).</p>
<p><b>Were cast </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εβληθη</span></span>). First aorist passive singular because <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">χαλαζα</span></span> and <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">πυρ</span></span> treated as neuter plural. "The storm flung itself on the earth" (Swete).</p>
<p><b>Was burnt up </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">κατεκαη</span></span>). Second aorist (effective) passive indicative of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">κατακαιω</span></span>, old verb to burn down (effective use of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">κατα</span></span>, up, we say). Repeated here three times for dramatic effect. See <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="re+7:1-3">Revelation 7:1-3</span> about the trees and <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="re+9:4">Revelation 9:4</span> where the locusts are forbidden to injure the grass. </p>
