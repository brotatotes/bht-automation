<p><b> The invisible things of him </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">τα αορατα αυτου</span></span>). Another verbal adjective (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">α</span></span> privative and <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">οραω</span></span>, to see), old word, either unseen or invisible as here and elsewhere in N.T. (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="col+1:15">Colossians 1:15</span>, etc.). The attributes of God's nature defined here as "his everlasting power and divinity" (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">η τε αιδιος αυτου δυναμις κα θειοτης</span></span>). <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">Αιδιος</span></span> is for <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">αειδιος</span></span> from <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">αε</span></span> (always), old word, in N.T. only here and <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="jude+1:6">Jude 1:6</span>, common in Philo (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ζωη αιδιος</span></span>), elsewhere <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">αιωνιος</span></span>. <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">Θειοτης</span></span> is from <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">θειος</span></span> (from <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">θεος</span></span>) quality of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">θεος</span></span> and corresponds more to Latin <i>divinitas</i> from <i>divus</i>, divine. In <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="col+2:9">Colossians 2:9</span> Paul uses <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">θεοτης</span></span> (Latin <i>deitas</i> from <i>deus</i>)</p>
<p><b>deity </b>, both old words and nowhere else in the N.T. <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">Θεοτης</span></span> is Divine Personality, <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">θειοτης</span></span>, Divine Nature and properties (Sanday and Headlam).</p>
<p><b>Since the creation of the world </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">απο κτισεως κοσμου</span></span>). He means by God and unto God as antecedent to and superior to the world (cf. <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="col+1:15">Colossians 1:15</span>. about Christ).</p>
<p><b>Are clearly seen </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">καθορατα</span></span>). Present passive indicative of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">καθοραω</span></span> (perfective use of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">κατα-</span></span>), old word, only here in N.T., with direct reference to <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">αορατα</span></span>.</p>
<p><b>Being perceived </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">νοουμενα</span></span>). Present passive participle of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">νοεω</span></span>, to use the <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">νους</span></span> (intellect).</p>
<p><b>That they may be without excuse </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εις το εινα αυτους αναπολογητους</span></span>). More likely, "so that they are without excuse." The use of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εις το</span></span> and the infinitive (with accusative of general reference) for result like <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ωστε</span></span> is reasonably clear in the N.T. (Moulton, <i>Prolegomena</i>, p. 219; Robertson, <i>Grammar</i>, p. 1003). <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">Αναπολογητους</span></span> is another verbal with <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">αν</span></span> from <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">απολογεομα</span></span>. Old word, in N.T. only here and <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ro+2:1">Romans 2:1</span> ("inexcusable" here). </p>
