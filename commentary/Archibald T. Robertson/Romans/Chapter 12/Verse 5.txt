<p><b> And severally </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">το δε καθ' εις</span></span>). A difficult late idiom where the preposition <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">καθ'</span></span> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">κατα</span></span>) is treated adverbially with no effect on the nominative case <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εις</span></span> like <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">υπερ εγω</span></span> (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="2co+11:23">2 Corinthians 11:23</span>). So <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εις καθ' εις</span></span> (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mr+14:19">Mark 14:19</span>) and in Modern Greek <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">καθεις</span></span> as a distributive pronoun. But we have <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">καθ' ενα</span></span> in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="1co+14:31">1 Corinthians 14:31</span>. The use of the neuter article here <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">το</span></span> with <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">καθ' εις</span></span> is probably the accusative of general reference, "as to each one." </p>
