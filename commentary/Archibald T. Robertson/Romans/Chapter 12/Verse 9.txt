<p><b> Without hypocrisy </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ανυποκριτος</span></span>). Late double compound adjective for which see <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="2co+6:6">2 Corinthians 6:6</span>. Hypocritical or pretended love is no love at all as Paul describes <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">αγαπη</span></span> in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ro+12:1">Romans 12:1</span>.</p>
<p><b>Abhor </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">αποστυγουντες</span></span>). Old verb with intensive (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">απο</span></span>) dislike, only here in N.T. The present active participle is here employed in the sense of the present active indicative as sometimes happens with the independent participle (Robertson, <i>Grammar</i>, pp. 1132ff.). This same idiom appears with <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">κολλωμενο</span></span> (cleaving) for which verb see on <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="1co+6:17">1 Corinthians 6:17</span>, with <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">προηγουμενο</span></span> (preferring) in verse <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ro+12:10">Romans 12:10</span> (old verb here only in N.T.), and with the participles in verses <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ro+12:11-13">Romans 12:11-13</span> and again in verses <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ro+12:16-18">Romans 12:16-18</span>. One can supply <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εστε</span></span> if he prefers. </p>
