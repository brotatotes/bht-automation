<p><b> For this </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">το γαρ</span></span>). For the article (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">το</span></span>) pointing to a sentence see <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ro+8:26">Romans 8:26</span>, here to the quotation. The order of the commandments here is like that in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="lu+18:20">Luke 18:20</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="jas+2:11">James 2:11</span> and in B for <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ro+13:5">Romans 13:5</span>, but different from that of the Hebrew in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ro+13:20">Romans 13:20</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ro+13:5">Romans 13:5</span>. The use of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ου</span></span> with the volitive future in prohibitions in place of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">μη</span></span> and the imperative or subjunctive is a regular Greek idiom.</p>
<p><b>And if there be any other </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">κα ε τις ετερα</span></span>). Paul does not attempt to give them all.</p>
<p><b>It is summed up </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ανακεφαλαιουτα</span></span>). Present passive indicative of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ανακεφαλαιοω</span></span>, late literary word or "rhetorical term" (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ανα, κεφαλαιον</span></span>, head or chief as in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="heb+8:1">Hebrews 8:1</span>). Not in the papyri, but <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">κεφαλαιον</span></span>, quite common for sum or summary. In N.T. only here and <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="eph+1:10">Ephesians 1:10</span>.</p>
<p><b>Namely </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εν τω</span></span>). See <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">το γαρ</span></span> at the beginning of the verse, though omitted by B F. The quotation is from <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="le+19:18">Leviticus 19:18</span>. Quoted in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mt+5:43">Matthew 5:43</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mt+22:39">Matthew 22:39</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mr+12:31">Mark 12:31</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="lu+10:27">Luke 10:27</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ga+5:14">Galatians 5:14</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="jas+2:8">James 2:8</span> it is called <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">βασιλικος νομος</span></span> (royal law).</p>
<p><b>Thy neighbour </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">τον πλησιον σου</span></span>). <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">Πλησιον</span></span> is an adverb and with the article it means "the one near thee." See on <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mt+5:43">Matthew 5:43</span>. </p>
