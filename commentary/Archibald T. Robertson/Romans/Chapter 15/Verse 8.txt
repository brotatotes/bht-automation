<p><b> A minister of the circumcision </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">διακονον περιτομης</span></span>). Objective genitive, "a minister to the circumcision." <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">Διακονον</span></span> is predicate accusative with <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">γεγενησθα</span></span> (perfect passive infinitive of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">γινομα</span></span> in indirect assertion after <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">λεγω</span></span>, I say) and in apposition with <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">Χριστον</span></span>, accusative of general reference with the infinitive. See <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ga+4:4">Galatians 4:4</span>.</p>
<p><b>That he might confirm </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εις το βεβαιωσα</span></span>). Purpose clause with <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εις το</span></span> and the infinitive <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">βεβαιωσα</span></span> (first aorist active of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">βεβαιοω</span></span>, to make stand).</p>
<p><b>The promises given unto the fathers </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">τας επαγγελιας των πατερων</span></span>). No "given" in the Greek, just the objective genitive, "the promises to the fathers." See <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ro+9:4">Romans 9:4</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ro+9:5">Romans 9:5</span>. </p>
