<p><b> What then shall we say? </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">τ ουν ερουμεν?</span></span>). Paul is fond of this rhetorical question (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ro+4:1">Romans 4:1</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ro+6:1">Romans 6:1</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ro+7:7">Romans 7:7</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ro+8:31">Romans 8:31</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ro+9:14">Romans 9:14</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ro+9:30">Romans 9:30</span>).</p>
<p><b>Forefather </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">προπατορα</span></span>). Old word, only here in N.T. Accusative case in apposition with <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">Αβρααμ</span></span> (accusative of general reference with the infinitive).</p>
<p><b>Hath found </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ευρηκενα</span></span>). Westcott and Hort put <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ευρηκενα</span></span> in the margin because B omits it, a needless precaution. It is the perfect active infinitive of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ευρισκω</span></span> in indirect discourse after <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ερουμεν</span></span>. The MSS. differ in the position of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">κατα σαρκα</span></span>. </p>
