<p><b> I speak after the manner of men </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ανθρωπινον λεγω</span></span>). "I speak a human word." He begs pardon for using "slaving" in connection with righteousness. But it is a good word, especially for our times when self-assertiveness and personal liberty bulk so large in modern speech. See <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ro+3:5">Romans 3:5</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ga+3:15">Galatians 3:15</span> where he uses <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">κατα ανθρωπον</span></span>.</p>
<p><b>Because of the infirmity of your flesh </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">δια την ασθενειαν της σαρκος υμων</span></span>). Because of defective spiritual insight largely due to moral defects also.</p>
<p><b>Servants to uncleanness </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">δουλα τη ακαθαρσια</span></span>). Neuter plural form of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">δουλος</span></span> to agree with <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">μελη</span></span> (members). Patently true in sexual sins, in drunkenness, and all fleshly sins, absolutely slaves like narcotic fiends.</p>
<p><b>So now </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ουτως νυν</span></span>). Now that you are born again in Christ. Paul uses twice again the same verb <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">παριστημ</span></span>, to present (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">παρεστησατε, παραστησατε</span></span>).</p>
<p><b>Servants to righteousness </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">δουλα τη δικαιοσυνη</span></span>). Repeats the idea of verse <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ro+6:18">Romans 6:18</span>.</p>
<p><b>Unto sanctification </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εις αγιασμον</span></span>). This the goal, the blessed consummation that demands and deserves the new slavery without occasional lapses or sprees (verse <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ro+6:15">Romans 6:15</span>). This late word appears only in LXX, N.T., and ecclesiastical writers so far. See on <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="1th+4:3">1 Thessalonians 4:3</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="1co+1:30">1 Corinthians 1:30</span>. Paul includes sanctification in his conception of the God-kind (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ro+1:17">Romans 1:17</span>) of righteousness (both justification, <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ro+1:18-5">Romans 1:18-5</span> and sanctification, chapters <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ro+6:6-8">Romans 6:6-8</span>). It is a life process of consecration, not an instantaneous act. Paul shows that we ought to be sanctified (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ro+6:1-7">Romans 6:1-7</span>) and illustrates the obligation by death (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ro+6:1-14">Romans 6:1-14</span>), by slavery (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ro+6:15-23">Romans 6:15-23</span>), and by marriage (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ro+7:1-6">Romans 7:1-6</span>). </p>
