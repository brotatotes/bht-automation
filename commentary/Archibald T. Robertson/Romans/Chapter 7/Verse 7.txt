<p><b> Is the law sin? </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ο νομος αμαρτια?</span></span>). A pertinent query in view of what he had said. Some people today oppose all inhibitions and prohibitions because they stimulate violations. That is half-baked thinking.</p>
<p><b>I had not known sin </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">την αμαρτιαν ουκ εγνων</span></span>). Second aorist indicative of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">γινωσκω</span></span>, to know. It is a conclusion of a second class condition, determined as unfulfilled. Usually <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">αν</span></span> is used in the conclusion to make it plain that it is second class condition instead of first class, but occasionally it is not employed when it is plain enough without as here (<span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="joh+16:22">John 16:22</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="joh+16:24">John 16:24</span>). See on <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ga+4:15">Galatians 4:15</span>. So as to</p>
<p><b>I had not known coveting </b> (lust), <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">επιθυμιαν ουκ ηιδειν</span></span>. But all the same the law is not itself sin nor the cause of sin. Men with their sinful natures turn law into an occasion for sinful acts. </p>
