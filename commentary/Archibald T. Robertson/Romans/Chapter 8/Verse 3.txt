<p><b> That the law could not do </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">το αδυνατον του νομου</span></span>). Literally, "the impossibility of the law" as shown in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="ro+7:7-24">Romans 7:7-24</span>, either nominative absolute or accusative of general reference. No syntactical connection with the rest of the sentence.</p>
<p><b>In that </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εν ω</span></span>). "Wherein."</p>
<p><b>It was weak </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ησθενε</span></span>). Imperfect active, continued weak as already shown.</p>
<p><b>In the likeness of sinful flesh </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εν ομοιωματ σαρκος αμαρτιας</span></span>). For "likeness" see <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="php+2:7">Philippians 2:7</span>, a real man, but more than man for God's "own Son." Two genitives "of flesh of sin" (marked by sin), that is the flesh of man is, but not the flesh of Jesus.</p>
<p><b>And for sin </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">κα περ αμαρτιας</span></span>). Condensed phrase, God sent his Son also concerning sin (our sin).</p>
<p><b>Condemned sin in the flesh </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">κατεκρινε την αμαρτιαν εν τη σαρκ</span></span>). First aorist active indicative of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">κατακρινω</span></span>. He condemned the sin of men and the condemnation took place in the flesh of Jesus. If the article <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">την</span></span> had been repeated before <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εν τη σαρκ</span></span> Paul would have affirmed sin in the flesh of Jesus, but he carefully avoided that (Robertson, <i>Grammar</i>, p. 784). </p>
