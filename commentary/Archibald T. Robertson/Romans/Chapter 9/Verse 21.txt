<p><b> Or hath not the potter a right over the clay? </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">η ουκ εχε εξουσιαν ο κεραμευς του πηλου?</span></span>). This question, expecting an affirmative answer, is Paul's reply to the previous one, "Why didst thou make me thus?" <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">Πηλος</span></span>, old word for clay, is mud or wet clay in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="joh+9:6">John 9:6</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="joh+9:11">John 9:11</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="joh+9:14">John 9:14</span>. The old word for potter (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">κεραμευς</span></span>) in N.T. only here and <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mt+27:7">Matthew 27:7</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mt+27:10">Matthew 27:10</span>.</p>
<p><b>Lump </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">φυραματος</span></span>). Late word from <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">φυραω</span></span>, to mix (clay, dough, etc.).</p>
<p><b>One part </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ο μεν</span></span>)</p>
<p><b>--another </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ο δε</span></span>). Regular idiom for contrast (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">μεν--δε</span></span>) with the old demonstrative <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ο</span></span> (this), "this vessel (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">σκευος</span></span>, old word as in <span class="scriptRef" com="rwp" lang="eng" ref="mr+11:16">Mark 11:16</span>) for honour, that for dishonour." Paul thus claims clearly God's sovereign right (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εξουσιαν</span></span>, power, right, authority, from <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εξεστ</span></span>) to use men (already sinners) for his own purpose. </p>
