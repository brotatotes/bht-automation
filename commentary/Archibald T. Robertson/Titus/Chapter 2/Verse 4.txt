<p><b> That they may train </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ινα σωφρονιζωσιν</span></span>). Purpose clause, <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ινα</span></span> and present active subjunctive of <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">σωφρονιζω</span></span>, old verb (from <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">σωφρων</span></span>, sound in mind, <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">σαοσ, φρην</span></span>, as in this verse), to make sane, to restore to one's senses, to discipline, only here in N.T.</p>
<p><b>To love their husbands </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">φιλανδρους εινα</span></span>). Predicate accusative with <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εινα</span></span> of old adjective <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">φιλανδρος</span></span> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">φιλοσ, ανηρ</span></span>, fond of one's husband), only here in N.T. <span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">Ανηρ</span></span> means man, of course, as well as husband, but only husband here, not "fond of men" (other men than their own).</p>
<p><b>To love their children </b> (<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">φιλοτεκνους</span></span>). Another old compound, here only in N.T. This exhortation is still needed where some married women prefer poodle-dogs to children. </p>
