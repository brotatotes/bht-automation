<p><strong> 32.</strong> ] <strong> and</strong> (not, <em> for</em> : but a parallel assertion to the last, ‘ye have power, &amp;c. <em> and</em> ’) <strong> spirits of prophets</strong> (i.e. <em> their own spirits</em> , filled with the Holy Spirit: so Meyer, and rightly: not, as De Wette, the Spirit of God within each: and so <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="1co+14:12">1 Corinthians 14:12</span> ; the inspired spirit being regarded as a <strong> πνεῦμα</strong> <em> in a peculiar sense</em> from God, or otherwise. See the distinction plainly made <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="1jo+4:2">1 John 4:2</span> ; ἐν τούτῳ γινώσκετε τὸ <strong> πνεῦμα τοῦ θεοῦ</strong> . πᾶν <strong> πνεῦμα</strong> κ . τ . λ . The omission of the art. generalizes the assertion, making it applicable to all genuine Christian prophets) <strong> are subject to prophets</strong> (i.e. to the men whose spirits they are. But very many Commentators, e.g. Theophyl. (alt.), Calvin, Estius, and more recently Bleek and Rückert, take προφήταις to signify <em> other prophets</em> τὸ ἔν σοι χάρισμα , καὶ ἡ ἐνέργεια τοῦ ἔν σοι πνεύματος , ὑποτάσσεται τῷ χαρίσματι τοῦ ἑτέρου τοῦ κινηθέντος εἰς τὸ προφητεύειν (Theophyl.). But the command ὁ πρῶτος σιγάτω would be superfluous, if his gift was in subjection to <em> another</em> ).</p>
