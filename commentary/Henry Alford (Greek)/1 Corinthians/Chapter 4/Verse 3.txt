<p><strong> 3.</strong> ] <strong> But to me</strong> (contrast to the case of the stewards into whose faithfulness enquiry is made ὧδε , here on earth) <strong> it is</strong> (amounts to) <strong> very</strong> little (Meyer compares ἐς χάριν τέλλεται , Pind. Ol. i. 122, and Theognis, 162, οἷς τὸ κακὸν δοκέον γίγνεται εἰς ἀγαθόν <strong> that I</strong> [ <strong> should</strong> ] <strong> be</strong> (the ἵνα , here and always, is more or less the conj. of <em> purpose</em> . The construction is a mixed one in such clauses as this, compounded of ἐλάχιστόν ἐστιν ἀνακριθῆναι , and ἐλαχίστου ἂν πριαίμην , ἵνα ἀνακριθῶ ) <strong> judged</strong> (enquired into, as to my faithfulness) <strong> by you, or by the day of man</strong> ([i.e., <strong> of man’s judgment</strong> ,] in reference to ὧδε above, and contrast to the ἡμέρα κυρίου , to which his appeal is presently made, <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="1co+4:5">1 Corinthians 4:5</span> , and of which, as testing the worth of the labour of teachers, he spoke so fully ch. <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="1co+3:13-15">1 Corinthians 3:13-15</span> . Jerome, Quæstiones ad Algasiam, Ep. xxxi. (cli.) 10, vol. i. p. 879, numbers the expression among the <em> cilicisms</em> of the Apostle. Estius, al., suppose it to be a Hebraism, referring to <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="jer+17:16">Jeremiah 17:16</span> , which is irrelevant. All these are probably wrong, and the expression <em> chosen purposely</em> by the Apostle. Grot. compares ‘diem dicere,’ ‘to cite to trial;’ to which Stanley adds the English ‘daysman’ for arbiter (see Job 9:33 ), and the Dutch ‘dagh vaerden’ and ‘daghen,’ to ‘summon’), <strong> nay, I do not judge even</strong> (hold not an enquiry on: lit. ‘ <em> but neither do I</em> ,’ &amp;c.) <strong> myself:</strong></p>
