<p><strong> 8</strong> .] <em> Reason why we should accommodate ourselves to the prejudices of the weak in this matter: because</em> it is not one in which any spiritual advantage is to be gained, but one perfectly indifferent: not, with Calv., al., an objection of the <em> strong</em> among the Corinthians: no such assumption must be made, without a <em> plain indication in words</em> that the <em> saying of another is being cited</em> : see <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="ro+9:19">Romans 9:19</span> ; <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="ro+11:19">Romans 11:19</span> ; and as Meyer well remarks, if the <em> eaters</em> had said this, they would have expressed it, οὔτε ἐὰν <strong> μὴ</strong> φάγωμεν <strong> περισς</strong> <strong> .</strong> , οὔτε ἐὰν φάγ ., ὑστερ ., as it has actually been corrected (see var. readd.) in some MSS., and adopted by Lachm. in his last edn.
	</p>
<p>The δέ carries on the argument.
	</p>
<p>Bengel remarks (against the ordinary rendering, which takes παρίστημι = συνίστημι , ‘commendo,’ which meaning it will not bear) that παραστήσει is a verbum μέσον , after which may follow a good or a bad predicate: <strong> will not affect our</strong> (future) <strong> standing before God</strong> ; and to this indifferent meaning of παραστήσει answers the antithetic alternative which follows.</p>
