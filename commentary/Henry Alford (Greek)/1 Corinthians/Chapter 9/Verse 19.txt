<p><strong> 19 ff.</strong> ] <strong> He</strong> now proceeds to <em> answer</em> the question, ‘ <em> What prospect of reward could induce me to do this</em> ?’
	</p>
<p>[ <strong> Yea</strong> (literally] <strong> For</strong> , q. d. the reward must have been great and glorious in prospect) <strong> being free from</strong> ( <strong> the power of</strong> ) <strong> all men, I enslaved myself</strong> (when I made this determination: and have continued to do so) <strong> to all, that I might gain</strong> (not τοὺς πάντας , which he could not exactly say, but) <strong> the largest number</strong> ( <em> of any</em> : that hereafter Paul’s converts might be found to be οἱ πλείονες : see below on 1Co 9:24 ).
	</p>
<p>Bengel has remarked on <strong> κερδήσω</strong> , ‘congruit hoc verbum cum consideratione <em> mercedis</em> :’ but ‘ <em> congruit</em> ’ is not enough: it is <em> actually</em> THE ANSWER <em> to the question</em> τίς μού ἐστιν ὁ μισθός ; This <em> ‘lucrifecisse’ the greater number</em> is distinctly referred to by him elsewhere, as <em> his reward</em> in the day of the Lord: τίς γὰρ ἡμῶν ἐλπὶς ἢ χαρὰ ἢ στέφανος καυχήσεως ; ἢ οὐχὶ καὶ ὑμεῖς , ἔμπροσθεν τοῦ κυρίου ἡμῶν Ἰησοῦ ἐν τῇ αὐτοῦ παρουσίᾳ ; ὑμεῖς γάρ ἐστε ἡ δόξα ἡμῶν καὶ ἡ χαρά . <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="1th+2:19-20">1 Thessalonians 2:19-20</span> . And it is for this reason that ἵνα .… κερδ . is <em> three times repeated</em> : and, as we shall presently see, that the similitude at the end of the chapter is chosen.</p>
