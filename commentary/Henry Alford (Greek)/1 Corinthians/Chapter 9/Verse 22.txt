<p><strong> 22.</strong> ] The <strong> ἀσθενεῖς</strong> here can hardly be the <em> weak Christians</em> of ch. 8 and <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="ro+14:0">Romans 14:0</span> , who were <em> already won</em> , but as in ref., <em> those who had not strength to believe and receive the Gospel</em> . This sentence then does not bring out a new form of condescension, but recapitulates the preceding two classes, τοῖς ὑπὸ νόμον .… τοῖς ἀνόμοις .
	</p>
<p><strong> τοῖς πᾶσιν</strong> <strong> …</strong> ] This sums up the above, and others not enumerated, in one general rule, and the various <em> occasions</em> of his practising the condescension (aorists) in one general result (perfect). <strong> To all men I am become all things</strong> (i.e. to each according to his situation and prejudices) <strong> that by all means</strong> (‘omnino:’ or perhaps as Meyer, <em> in all ways</em> : but I prefer the other) <strong> I may save some</strong> ( <strong> τινάς</strong> is emphatic: <em> some</em> , out of each class in the πάντες . It is said, as is the following verse, in extreme humility, and distrust of even an Apostle’s confidence, to shew them the immense importance of the μισθός for which he thus denied and submitted himself).</p>
