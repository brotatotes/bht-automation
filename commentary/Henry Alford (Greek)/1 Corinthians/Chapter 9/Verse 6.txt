<p><strong> 6.</strong> ] <strong> Or</strong> (implying what the consequence would then be, see ch. 1Co 6:2 ; <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="1co+6:9">1 Corinthians 6:9</span> ; does not introduce a new ἐξουσία , but a consequence of the denial of the last two) <strong> have only I and Barnabas</strong> (why Barnabas? Perhaps on account of his former connexion with Paul, <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="ac+11:30">Acts 11:30</span> ; <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="ac+12:25">Acts 12:25</span> ; Act 13:1 to <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="ac+15:39">Acts 15:39</span> ; but this seems hardly enough reason for his being here introduced. It is not improbable that having been at first associated with Paul, who appears <em> from the first</em> to have abstained from receiving sustenance from those among whom he was preaching, Barnabas, after his separation from our Apostle, may have retained the same self-denying practice. “This is the only time when he is mentioned in conjunction with St. Paul, since the date of the quarrel in <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="ac+15:39">Acts 15:39</span> .” Stanley) <strong> not power to abstain from working</strong> (i.e. power to look for our maintenance from the churches, without manual labour of our own. The Vulg. has ‘ <em> hoc operandi</em> ,’ so also Tertull., Ambrose, al., omitting μή , and against the usage of ἐργάζεσθαι , see reff.)?</p>
