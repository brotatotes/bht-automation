<p><strong> 15</strong> .] And recognition of this fact is a condition and proof of the life of God. <strong> Whosoever confesseth</strong> (the aorist can only be given by the English present and an exegesis, viz. that this present betokens not a repeated act and habit, but a great act once for all introducing the man into a state of ὁμολογῆσαι . All futures, “ <em> shall confess</em> ,” and futuri exacti, “ <em> shall have confessed</em> ,” are objectionable; the one as losing the retrospective tinge, the other as making it unduly prominent, and indeed imparting a slight hue of transitoriness, which least of all belongs to the word.
	</p>
<p>The same remark holds good of this confessing, as before with regard to denying, ch. <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="1jo+2:23">1 John 2:23</span> ; viz., that we must not bring into it more than the Apostle intends by it: it is not the “confession of the <em> life</em> ” which is here spoken of, but that of the lips only. Of course it would be self-evident that this is taken by the Apostle as ruling the life: but simply <em> as</em> a matter of course. He speaks of the ideal realized) <strong> that Jesus is the Son of God</strong> (i. e. receives the testimony in the last verse as true), <strong> God abideth in him, and he in God</strong> .</p>
