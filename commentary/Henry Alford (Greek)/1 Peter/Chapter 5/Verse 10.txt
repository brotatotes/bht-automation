<p><strong> 10</strong> .] <strong> But</strong> (q. d. however you may be able to apprehend the consolation which I have last propounded to you, one thing is sure: or as Bengel, “vos tantum vigilate et resistite hosti: cætera Deus præstabit”) <strong> the God of all grace</strong> (who is the Source of all spiritual help for every occasion: see reff.) <strong> who called you</strong> (which was the first proof of His grace towards you) <strong> unto</strong> (with a view to; ‘consolationis argumentum:’ He who has begun grace with a view to glory, will not cut off grace till it be perfected in glory. Cf. <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="1th+2:12">1 Thessalonians 2:12</span> ; 2Th 2:14 ) <strong> His eternal glory in Christ Jesus</strong> ( <strong> ἐν χρ</strong> <strong> . </strong> <strong> Ἰησ</strong> <strong> .</strong> belongs to καλέσας , which has since been defined by ὑμᾶς εἰς τ . αἰ . αὐ . δόξ . Christ Jesus is the element in which that calling took place. The words cannot, as Calov., al., be joined with what follows), <strong> when ye have suffered a little while</strong> (these words belong to what has gone before, ὁ καλέσας ὑμᾶς κ . τ . λ ., not to what follows, as is decisively shewn by the consideration that all four verbs must belong to acts of God on them <em> in this life</em> , while these sufferings would be still going on. The <strong> ὀλίγον παθόντας</strong> expresses the <em> condition</em> of their calling to glory in Christ, viz. after having suffered for a short time. <strong> παθόντας</strong> , as in all cases of an aor. part. connected with a future verb, is to be taken in the strictness of its aoristic meaning as a futurus exactus: the παθήματα are over when the δόξα comes in), <strong> shall Himself</strong> ( <strong> αὐτός</strong> , solemn and emphatic: “ostendit enim Apostolus ex eodem gratiæ fonte et primam ad gloriam cœlestem vocationem et ultimam hujus beneficii consummationem provenire,” Gerhard) <strong> perfect</strong> ( <strong> you</strong> ) (see ref. Heb. and note: “ne remaneat in vobis defectus,” Beng.), <strong> shall confirm</strong> (establish you firmly, so as to be στερεοὶ τῇ πίστει : “ne quid vos labefactet,” Beng.), <strong> shall strengthen</strong> (the word <strong> σθενόω</strong> belongs to later Greek), <strong> shall ground</strong> ( <strong> you</strong> ) (fix you as on a foundation, “ut superetis omnem vim adversam. Digna <em> Petro</em> oratio. Confirmat fratres suos,” Bengel.
