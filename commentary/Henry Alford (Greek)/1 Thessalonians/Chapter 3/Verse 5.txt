<p><strong> 5</strong> .] <strong> διὰ τοῦτο</strong> , because tribulation had verily begun among you ( καθὼς καὶ ἐγένετο ).
	</p>
<p><strong> κἀγώ</strong> seems to convey a delicate hint that Timotheus also was anxious respecting them: or it may have the same reference as καὶ ἡμεῖς , ch. <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="1th+2:13">1 Thessalonians 2:13</span> , viz. to the other Christians who had heard of their tribulation. De W. would render, not, ‘ <em> therefore I also &amp;c.</em> ’ but ‘ <em> therefore also, I &amp;c.</em> ’ But this would require (as Lün.) διὰ καὶ τοῦτο or καὶ διὰ τ .
	</p>
<p><strong> εἰς τὸ γν</strong> <strong> .</strong> ] that I (not ‘ <em> he</em> ’) <strong> might know</strong> (be informed about): belongs to the subject of the verb ἔπεμψα .
	</p>
<p><strong> μή πως</strong> κ . τ . λ .] <strong> lest perchance the tempter</strong> (ref.) <strong> have tempted</strong> (not, as Whitby, al., ‘ <em> seduced</em> ’) <strong> you</strong> (indicative betokening the fact absolute), <strong> and our labour might be</strong> (subjunctive, betokening the fact conditional) <strong> to no purpose</strong> (reff.). Fritz. and De W. rather harshly take μή πως in two different meanings, with the first clause as ‘ <em> an</em> forte,’ and with the second as ‘ <em> ne</em> forte.’</p>
