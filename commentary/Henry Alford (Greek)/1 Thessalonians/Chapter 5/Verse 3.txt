<p><strong> 3</strong> .] Following out of the comparison ὡς κλ . ἐν νυκτί , into detail.
	</p>
<p><strong> λέγωσιν</strong> , viz. men in general the children of the world, as opposed to the people of God: cf. ὄλεθρος below. The vivid description dispenses with any copula.
	</p>
<p><strong> εἰρ</strong> <strong> . </strong> <strong> κ</strong> <strong> . </strong> <strong> ἀσφ</strong> <strong> .</strong> , scil. ἐστιν , see ref. Ezek.
	</p>
<p><strong> αἰφνίδ</strong> <strong> .</strong> has the emphasis, becoming a kind of predicate.
	</p>
<p><strong> ἐφίσταται</strong> , generally used of any sudden unexpected appearance: see reff., and <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="ac+4:1">Acts 4:1</span> .
	</p>
<p>It is pressing too close the comparison ὥσπερ ἡ ὠδὶν κ . τ . λ ., when De W. says that it “assumes the day to be <em> near</em> , for that such a woman, though she does not know the day and the hour, yet has a definite knowledge of the <em> period</em> :” for it is not the woman, nor her condition, that is the subject of comparison, but the <em> unexpected pang</em> of labour which comes on her.</p>
