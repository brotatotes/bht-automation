<p><strong> 17.</strong> ] <strong> So that</strong> (additional inference from what has gone before: hardly as Meyer, from 2Co 5:16 <em> only</em> : the <em> death</em> of <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="2co+5:15">2 Corinthians 5:15</span> , as well as the new knowledge of <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="2co+5:16">2 Corinthians 5:16</span> , going to make up the καινὴ κτίσις ) <strong> if any man is in Christ</strong> (far better than ‘whoever is in Christ.’ See note on <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="php+4:8">Philippians 4:8</span> . ‘ <em> In Christ</em> ,’ i.e. in union with Him: Christ being ‘the element in which by faith we live and move,’ as Meyer), <strong> he is a new creature</strong> ( κτίσις , ‘ <em> creation</em> ,’ the act, implying here the result of the act. See ref. and <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="col+3:10-11">Colossians 3:10-11</span> ; <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="eph+2:10">Ephesians 2:10</span> ; <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="eph+4:23">Ephesians 4:23</span> .

<p>‘He has received,’ ‘passed into,’ ‘a new life,’ Joh 3:3 ): <strong> the old things</strong> (of his former life ‘all the old selfish and impure motives, views, and prejudices,’ De Wette) <strong> have passed away</strong> (there does not appear to be any allusion, as in Chrys., Theophyl., to the passing away of Judaism, but only to the <em> new birth</em> , the antiquation of the former unconverted state, with all that belonged to it); <strong> behold</strong> (a reminiscence of Isa 43:18-19 μὴ μνημονεύετε τὰ πρῶτα , καὶ τὰ ἀρχαῖα μὴ συλλογίζεσθε · ἰδοὺ , ἐγὼ ποιῶ καινά ), <strong> they have become new</strong> (see var. readd.). The arrangement of the sentence followed by the Vulg., al., ‘Si qua ergo in Christo nova creatura, vetera transierunt,’ is inadmissible, because the second member would be a mere reassertion of the first.</p>
