<p><strong> 5</strong> .] <strong> And now</strong> (so <strong> καὶ νῦν</strong> , coupling to what has gone before, <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="1jo+2:28">1 John 2:28</span> . It has also a force of breaking off, and passing to that which is the main subject, or most in the Writer’s thoughts, which here is, that this walking in truth is a walking after God’s commandments in love) <strong> I entreat thee</strong> (see on ἐρωτάω and αἰτέω , <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="1jo+5:15-16">1 John 5:15-16</span> . Here ἐρωτῶ carries, as Schlichting, “blandior quædam admonendi ratio:” with the assumed fact of a right thus to entreat) <strong> lady, not as writing to thee a new commandment, but</strong> (as writing to thee …: the construction is not strictly logical) <strong> that which we had from the beginning</strong> (see on this, 1Jn 2:7 ), <strong> that</strong> ( <strong> ἵνα</strong> here is not epexegetic of ἐντολή , as so often in St. John, but is to be taken in its proper sense, as the aim of ἐρωτῶ , and dependent on it) <strong> we love one another</strong> (the expression of the commandment in the first person is a mark of gentleness and delicacy: a sign that he who wrote it kept the commandment himself.)</p>
