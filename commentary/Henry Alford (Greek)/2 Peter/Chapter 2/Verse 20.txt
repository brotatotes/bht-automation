<p><strong> 20</strong> .] <strong> For if, having escaped</strong> (it might seem at first sight as if the ἀποφεύγοντας of 2Pe 2:18 were meant: but on close inspection it is plain that this is not so, but that we are continuing the description of the δοῦλοι τῆς φθορᾶς , viz. the deceivers themselves: the ἥττηται and ἡττῶνται marking the identity) <strong> the pollutions</strong> (reff.) <strong> of the world, in</strong> (element and condition of their escape) <strong> knowledge</strong> ( <strong> ἐπιγνώσει</strong> , genuine and accurate knowledge: shewing that he is treating of men who have not been mere professors of spiritual grace, but real possessors of it) <strong> of the Lord and Saviour Jesus Christ</strong> (expressed at length, to set forth more solemnly that from which they fall), <strong> but having again become entangled in these, they are overcome</strong> (the construction is ordinarily regarded as broken by the <strong> δέ</strong> , placed as if ἀποφύγωσιν and not ἀποφυγόντες had preceded: “ <em> if, after they have, &amp;c., they are again entangled and overcome</em> ,” as E. V. But it is better to regard ἡττῶνται as the apodosis to both the participial clauses, and δέ as coupling them to each other), <strong> their last state is</strong> (we cannot say in English “has become,” for we thereby convey an idea that it was not always so, but has undergone a change) <strong> worse than the first</strong> ( <strong> αὐτοῖς</strong> , dat. incommodi. The saying is our Lord’s own: see reff. Matt. [20] L.).
	</p>

Mk.,’ &amp;c., it is signified that the word occurs <em> in the parallel place in that Gospel or Gospels, but not in the other or others</em> .</p>
