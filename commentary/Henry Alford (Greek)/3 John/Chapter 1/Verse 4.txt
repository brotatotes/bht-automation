<p><strong> 4</strong> .] Explains ἐχάρην λίαν above. <strong> I have no greater</strong> (the form μειζότερος is condemned by some (Phryn. Lob. p. 136, Œc. h. l.) as barbarous. But these comparatives of comparatives and superlatives are found both in classical and in N. T. Greek: see <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="eph+3:8">Ephesians 3:8</span> , and note) <strong> joy than this</strong> (lit. “than these things:” following the usage by which ταῦτα is so often put where one thing only is intended: cf. the formula, καὶ ταῦτα , “idque:” so Plato, Phæd. p. 62, D, ἀλλ ʼ ὁ ἀνόητος ἄνθρωπος τάχ ʼ ἂν οἰηθείη ταῦτα , φευκτέον εἶναι ἀπὸ τοῦ δεσπότου . See Kühner, Gr. ii. p. 48), <strong> that</strong> (explicative, as constantly in St. John after the demonstrative pronoun) <strong> I hear of my children walking in the truth</strong> (on the participial construction, see note on <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="2jo+1:7">2 John 1:7</span> . The expression τέκνα here seems rather to favour the idea that the κυρία of the 2nd Epistle is a Church; but see prolegg. to 2 John).</p>
