	</p>
<p><strong> προφῆται</strong> ] Inspired teachers in the early Christian church, referred to in the Acts, and in the Epistles of Paul (see reff. and ch. <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="ac+19:6">Acts 19:6</span> ; <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="ac+21:9">Acts 21:9</span> ; Rom 12:6 ; <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="1co+12:10">1 Corinthians 12:10</span> ; <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="1co+13:2">1 Corinthians 13:2</span> ; 1Co 13:8 ; <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="1co+14:6">1 Corinthians 14:6</span> ; 1Th 5:20 ). They might be of either sex (ch. Act 21:9 ). The foretelling of future events was not the usual form which their inspiration took, but that of an <em> exalted and superhuman teaching</em> , ranked by St. Paul above ‘speaking with tongues,’ in being the <em> utterance of their own conscious intelligence informed by the Holy Spirit</em> . This inspiration was however, occasionally, as here, and ch. <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="ac+21:10">Acts 21:10</span> , made the vehicle of <em> prophecy</em> , properly so called.</p>
