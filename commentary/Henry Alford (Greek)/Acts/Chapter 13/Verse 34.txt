<p><strong> 34. </strong> <strong> μηκέτι μέλλ</strong> <strong> .</strong> ] Compare <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="ro+6:9">Romans 6:9</span> , χριστὸς ἐγερθεὶς ἐκ νεκρῶν οὐκέτι ἀποθνήσκει · θἁνατος αὐτοῦ οὐκέτι κυριεύει . It is interesting to trace the same shades of thought in the speeches and epistles of Paul; and abundant opportunity of doing so will occur as we proceed.
	</p>
<p>But here the ὑποστρ . εἰς διαφθ . does not merely imply <em> death</em> , so that Jesus should have once undergone it, and no more hereafter, as the E. V. seems to imply: but we must supply ‘to die, and in consequence to’ before the words, understanding them as the <em> result of death, if it had dominion over him</em> : thus the clause answers even more remarkably to <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="ro+6:9">Romans 6:9</span> .
	</p>
<p><strong> τὰ ὅσια</strong> is the LXX rendering of חַסְדֵי , ref. Isa., which in <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="2ch+6:42">2 Chronicles 6:42</span> , they have translated τὰ ἐλέη . The word ‘holy’ should have been preserved in the E. V., as answering to τὸν ὅσιόν σου below; <strong> the mercies of David, holy and sure</strong> : or <strong> my holy promises which I made sore unto David.</strong></p>
