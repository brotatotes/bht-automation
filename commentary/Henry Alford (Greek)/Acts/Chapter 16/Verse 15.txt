<p><strong> 15. </strong> <strong> ἐβαπτ</strong> <strong> ., </strong> <strong> κ</strong> <strong> . </strong> <strong> ὁ οἶκος αὐτ</strong> <strong> .</strong> ] It <em> may be</em> (as Meyer maintains) that no inference for infant-baptism is hence deducible. The practice, however, does not rest on <em> inference</em> , but on the <em> continuity and identity of the covenant of grace to Jew and Christian</em> , the <em> sign only</em> of admission being altered. The Apostles, <em> as Jews</em> , would have proposed to administer baptism to the children, and Jewish or proselyte converts would, <em> as matter of course</em> , have acceded to the proposal; and that the practice thus by universal consent, tacitly (because at first unquestioned) pervaded the universal church, can hardly with any reason be doubted. See note on <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="1co+7:14">1 Corinthians 7:14</span> .
	</p>
<p><strong> εἰ κεκρίκατε</strong> ] <strong> If ye have judged me</strong> ; modestly alluding to the decision respecting her faithfulness implied by their baptizing her, and assuming that such a judgment <em> had been passed</em> . Similarly εἰ ἡμεῖς ἀνακρινόμεθα , ch. <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="ac+4:9">Acts 4:9</span> .</p>
