<p><strong> 8 11</strong> .] This question is broken, in construction, by the enumeration of <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="ac+2:9-10">Acts 2:9-10</span> , and then Act 2:11 takes up the construction again from <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="ac+2:8">Acts 2:8</span> . As regards the <em> catalogue itself</em> , of course it cannot have been thus delivered as <em> part of a speech by any hearer on the occasion</em> , but is inserted into a speech expressing the general sense of what was said, and put, according to the usage of all narrative, into the mouths of all. The words <strong> τῇ ἰδίᾳ διαλ</strong> <strong> . </strong> <strong> ἡμ</strong> <strong> . </strong> <strong> ἐν ᾗ ἐγεννήθημεν</strong> are very decisive as to the nature of the miracle. The hearers could not have thus spoken, had <em> they</em> been <em> spiritually uplifted</em> into the comprehension of some <em> ecstatic language</em> spoken by the disciples. <em> They</em> were not spiritually acted on at all, but <em> spoke the matter of fact</em> : they were surprised at each recognizing, so far from his country, and in the mouths of Galilæans, his own native tongue.</p>
