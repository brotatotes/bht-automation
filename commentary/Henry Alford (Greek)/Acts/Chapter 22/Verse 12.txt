<p><strong> 12.</strong> ] That Ananias was a <em> Christian</em> , is not <em> here</em> mentioned, and ἀνὴρ … Ἰουδαίων is added: both, as addressed to a <em> Jewish audience</em> . Before the <em> Roman governor</em> in ch. 26, he <em> does not mention him at all</em> , but compresses the whole substance of the command given to Ananias into the words spoken by the Lord to himself. A heathen moralist could teach, ‘Quid de quoque viro, et cui dicas, sæpe videto’ (Hor. <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="eph+1:18">Ephesians 1:18</span> , 68): and a Christian Apostle was not unmindful of the necessary caution. Such features in his speeches are highly instructive and valuable to those who would gather from Scripture itself its own real character: and be, not slaves to its letter, but disciples of its spirit.</p>
