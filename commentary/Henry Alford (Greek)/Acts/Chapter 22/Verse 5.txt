<p><strong> 5. </strong> <strong> ὁ ἀρχ</strong> <strong> .</strong> ] ‘ <strong> The High Priest</strong> <em> of that day, who is still living</em> :’ i.e. Theophilus, see on ch. <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="ac+9:1">Acts 9:1</span> . Similarly, <strong> the whole Sanhedrim</strong> = ‘ <em> those who were then members, and now survive</em> .’
	</p>
<p><strong> παρ</strong> <strong> ʼ </strong> <strong> ὧν καί</strong> ] <strong> from whom, moreover.
	</strong></p>
<p><strong> πρὸς τοὺς ἀδελφ</strong> <strong> .] to the Jewish</strong> ( <em> their</em> ) <strong> brethren</strong> (see ch. Act 28:21 ). Bornemann’s rendering, ‘ <em> against the</em> (Christian) <em> brethren</em> ,’ is altogether inadmissible. If ever Paul spoke to the Jews <em> as a Jew</em> , it was on this occasion.
	</p>
<p><strong> καὶ τοὺς ἐκ</strong> <strong> .</strong> ] <strong> even those who were there.
	</strong></p>
<p><strong> ἐκεῖσε</strong> ] if resolved, would be εἰς Δαμασκόν , a similar construction to εἰς οἶκόν ἐστιν , <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="mr+2:1">Mark 2:1</span> , ‘ <em> those who had settled at Damascus and were then there</em> .’</p>
