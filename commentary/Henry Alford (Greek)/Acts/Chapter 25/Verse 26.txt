<p><strong> 26.</strong> <strong> ἀσφαλές</strong> ] <strong> fixed, definite</strong> . The whole matter had been hitherto obscured by the exaggerations and fictions of the Jews.
	</p>
<p><strong> τῷ κυρίῳ</strong> ] viz. Nero. Augustus and Tiberius refused this title; Caligula and (apparently) all following bore it. “Thus Tertullian, Apol. xxxiv. vol. i. p. 450: ‘Augustus imperii formator ne dominum quidem dici se volebat;’ and Suet. Aug [151] 53: ‘Dominum se appellari ne a liberis quidem aut nepotibus vel serio vel joco passus est;’ ami Tib. 27: ‘Dominus appellatus a quodam denuntiavit ne se amplius contumeliæ causa nominaret.’ <em> Caligula</em> accepted the title, according to Victor, ap. Eckhel, viii. 364. Herod Agrippa had applied it to <em> Claudius</em> (Philo ap. Spanheim. Numism. ii. 482); but it was not a <em> recognized</em> title of any emperor before <em> Domitian</em> . Suet. <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="de+13:0">Deuteronomy 13:0</span> : ‘Martial, Edictum Domini Deique nostri.’ ” Mr. Humphry.
