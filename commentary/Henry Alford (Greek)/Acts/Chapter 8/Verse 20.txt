<p><strong> 20.</strong> ] The solemn denunciation of Peter, like the declaration of Paul, <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="1co+6:13">1 Corinthians 6:13</span> , has reference to the perishableness of all worldly good, and of those with it, whose chief end is the use of it (see Col 2:22 ), ‘Thy gold and thou are equally on the way to corruption:’ <em> thy gold</em> , as its nature is: <em> thou with it</em> , as having no higher life than thy natural corrupt one: as being bound in the σύνδεσμος τῆς ἀδικίας . The <em> expression of Peter</em> , <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="1pe+1:7">1 Peter 1:7</span> , χρυσίου τοῦ ἀπολλυμένου , is remarkably parallel with this (see too 1Pe 1:18 ).
	</p>
<p><strong> ἐνόμισας</strong> ] aor. <strong> thou thoughtest</strong> : not ‘ <em> thou hast thought</em> ,’ as E. V. The historic force of the tense is to be kept here: the Apostle uses it as looking forward to the day of ἀπώλεια , ‘Let thy lot be ἀπ ., and that because thou thoughtest,’ &amp;c.
	</p>
<p><strong> κτᾶσθ</strong> .] <strong> to acquire</strong> , not pass. as E. V., ungrammatically.</p>
