<p><strong> 23.</strong> ] <strong> γάρ</strong> gives the reasons, not why it would be <em> difficult for forgiveness to take place</em> , but why <em> he had such extreme need of repentance and prayer</em> , as being tied and bound by the chain of sin.
	</p>
<p><strong> ὄντα εἰς</strong> ] a pregnant construction <strong> having fallen into and abiding in</strong> : not to be taken (as Kuin., &amp;c.) as ‘ <em> amounting to</em> ,’ ‘totus quantus es, nil nisi venenum amarum es et colligatio iniquitatis,’ which is very harsh, and improbable: nor (as Stier) is it prophetic, as to what <em> would be</em> the consequence, if he did not repent: ‘ <em> I see that thou wilt come to,’ &amp;c.</em> Least of all must it be said, here or any where else, that εἰς is <em> put for</em> ἐν . I cannot too often remind my younger readers, that it is a fundamental maxim of all sound scholarship, that <em> no word is ever put for another</em> .
	</p>
