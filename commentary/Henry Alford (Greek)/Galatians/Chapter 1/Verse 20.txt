<p><strong> 20</strong> .] This asseveration (cf. 2Co 11:31 ) applies most naturally to the important fact just asserted his short visit to Jerusalem, and his having seen only Peter and James, rather than to the whole subject of the chapter. If a report had been spread in Galatia that after his conversion he spent years at Jerusalem and received regular institution in Christianity at the hands of the Apostles, this last fact would naturally cause amazement, and need a strong confirmatory asseveration.
	</p>
<p>As regards the construction, ἃ … ὑμῖν stands alone, ( <strong> with regard to</strong> ) <strong> the things which I am writing to you</strong> , and the word necessary to be supplied to carry on the sense from ἰδοὺ ἐνώπ . τ . θεοῦ to ὅτι , lies under the ἰδού , which here answers to such words as διαμαρτύρομαι , <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="1ti+5:21">1 Timothy 5:21</span> ; 2Ti 2:14 ; <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="2ti+4:1">2 Timothy 4:1</span> , παραγγέλλω , <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="1ti+6:13">1 Timothy 6:13</span> . Meyer would supply γράφω , which seems harsh: others take ὅτι as ‘ <em> for</em> ,’ which is worse still (cf. <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="2co+11:21">2 Corinthians 11:21</span> , ὁ θεὸς οἶδεν … <strong> ὅτι</strong> οὐ ψεύδομαι ), and this too, understanding ἐστίν after θεοῦ (Bengel).</p>
