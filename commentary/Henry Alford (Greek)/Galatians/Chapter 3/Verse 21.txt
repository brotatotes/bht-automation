<p><strong> 21</strong> .] The Law being thus set over against the promises, being given through a mediator between two, the promises by the one God, it might seem as if there were an inconsistency between them. The nature of the contrariety must not (as De W.) be deduced from the following disproof of it: this disproof proceeds on τῶν παραβάσεων χάριν προσετέθη , which is <em> not</em> the ground of the apparent contrariety, but its explanation. The appearance of inconsistency lay in the whole paragraph preceding the οὐκ ἀκυροῖ of <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="ga+3:17">Galatians 3:17</span> , the εἰ ἐκ νόμου , οὐκέτι ἐξ ἐπαγγελίας of <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="ga+3:18">Galatians 3:18</span> , and the contrast between the giving of the two in <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="ga+3:20">Galatians 3:20</span> . “ τοῦ θεοῦ is not without emphasis: the promises which rest immediately on God, and were attested (? sic still in <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="ex+2:0">Exodus 2:0</span> ) by no mediator.” Ellic. <strong> εἰ γάρ</strong> ] Notwithstanding all the above features of contrast between the Law and the promises, it is not against them, for it does not pretend to perform the same office; <em> if it did</em> , then there would be this rivalry, which now does not exist.
	</p>
<p><strong> νόμος ὁ δυν</strong> <strong> .</strong> is best expressed in English, as in E. V., <strong> a law which could</strong> … for the article circumscribes the νόμος to some particular quality indicated in the defining participle which follows: see reff. Peile’s rendering, “if that which ( ὁ δυνάμενος !) should have power to give life had been given in the form of law,” is in the highest degree ungrammatical.
	</p>
<p><strong> ζωοποιῆσαι</strong> takes for granted that we by nature are <em> dead</em> in trespasses and sins.
	</p>
<p><strong> ὄντως</strong> has the emphasis: <strong> in very truth</strong> , and not only in the fancy of some, <strong> by the law</strong> (as its ground) <strong> would have been righteousness</strong> (which is the condition of life eternal, ὁ <strong> δίκαιος</strong> <strong> … </strong> <strong> ζήσεται</strong> .
	</p>
<p>If life, the result, had been given by the law, then righteousness, the <em> condition</em> of life, must have been by it also: reasoning from the whole to its part).</p>
