<p><strong> 26</strong> .] <strong> But</strong> (opposes to the last sentence, not to μία μέν , <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="ga+4:24">Galatians 4:24</span> , which, as Meyer observes, is left without an apodosis, the reader supplying that the other covenant is Sara, &amp;c.) <strong> the Jerusalem above</strong> (i.e. the heavenly Jerusalem = Ἱερ . ἐπουράνιος <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="heb+12:22">Hebrews 12:22</span> , ἡ καινὴ Ἱερ . <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="re+3:12">Revelation 3:12</span> ; <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="re+21:2">Revelation 21:2</span> , and see reff. on ἄνω . Michaelis, al., suppose <em> ancient Jerusalem</em> (Melchisedek’s) to be meant.
	</p>
<p>Vitringa, al., <em> Mount Zion</em> , as ἡ ἄνω πόλις means the Acropolis. But Rabbinical usage, as Schöttgen has abundantly proved in his Dissertation de Hierosolyma cœlesti (Hor. Heb. vol. i. Diss. v.), was familiar with the idea of a Jerusalem in heaven. See also citations in Wetst. This latter quotes a very remarkable parallel from Plato, Rep. ix. end, ἐν ᾗ νῦν δὴ διήλθομεν οἰκίζοντες πόλει λέγεις , τῇ ἐν λόγοις κειμένῃ , ἐπεὶ γῆς γε οὐδαμοῦ οἶμαι αὐτὴν εἶναι . Ἀλλ ʼ ἦν δ ʼ ἐγώ ἐν οὐρανῷ ἴσως παράδειγμα ἀνάκειται τῷ βουλομένῳ ὁρᾷν καὶ ὁρῶντι ἑαυτὸν κατοικίζειν . διαφέρει δὲ οὐδὲν εἴτε που ἐστὶν εἴτε ἔσται · τὰ γὰρ ταύτης μόνης ἂν πράξειεν , ἄλλης δὲ οὐδεμιᾶς . Εἰκός γ ʼ, ἔφη .
	</p>

The rendering adopted by Mr. Bagge, “ <em> which</em> (Jerusalem the free) <em> is</em> (answers to, as ἥτις ἐστὶν Ἄγαρ above) <em> our mother</em> (viz.
