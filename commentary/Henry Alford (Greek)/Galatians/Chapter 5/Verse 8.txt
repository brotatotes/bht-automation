<p><strong> 8</strong> .] <strong> The persuasion</strong> (to which you are yielding active; not <em> your persuasion</em> , passive. <strong> πεισμονή</strong> may mean either. Ellic. says: “As the similar form πλησμονή means both <em> satietas</em> (the state) and also <em> expletio</em> (the act), <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="col+2:23">Colossians 2:23</span> ; Plato, Sympos. 186 c. πλ . καὶ κένωσις , so πεισμονή may mean the state of being persuaded, i.e. conviction, or the act of persuading, ‘persuadendi sollertia’ (Schött.): cf. Chrys. on <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="1th+1:3">1 Thessalonians 1:3</span> , οὐ πεισμονὴ ἀνθρωπίνη … ἦν ἡ πείθουσα .” But here, ἡ πεισμ . being connected with ὁ καλῶν ὑμᾶς , and answering to the act of ἐγκόπτειν in the last verse, is better taken actively) <strong> is not from</strong> (does not come from, is not originated by) <strong> Him who calleth you</strong> (i.e. God: see ch. Gal 1:6 and note).
	</p>
<p><strong> 9</strong> ] <strong> ζύμη</strong> may allude either to men (Jer., Aug., Grot., Est., Beng., De W., al.), or to doctrine. In the parallel place in <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="1co+5:6">1 Corinthians 5:6</span> , it is moral influence; so also where our Lord uses the same figure, <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="mt+16:12">Matthew 16:12</span> , where ζύμη = διδαχή . Nor can there be any objection to taking it as abstract, and φύραμα concrete: a little false doctrine corrupts the whole mass (of Christians). So Chrys. ( οὕτω καὶ ὑμᾶς ἰσχύει τὸ μικρὸν τοῦτο κακόν , μὴ διορθωθέν , καὶ εἰς τέλειον ἰουδαϊσμὸν ἀγαγεῖν ), Thl., Luth., Calv., all.</p>
