<p><strong> 31</strong> .] The <em> last example</em> is one connected with the taking of Jericho, just mentioned. <strong> By faith</strong> (shewn in her confession <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="jos+2:9">Joshua 2:9</span> , “I know that Jehovah hath given you the land:” and <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="jos+2:11">Joshua 2:11</span> , “Jehovah your God, He is God in heaven above and in earth beneath”) <strong> Rahab the harlot</strong> (not to be softened into “ <em> cauponaria</em> ,” as Valcknaer, al. Clement of Rome devotes to her a whole chapter of his Epistle to the Corinthians, and has no idea of her other than as an harlot. Calvin says well, “Hoc (epitheton) ad anteactam vitam referri certum est: resipiscentiæ enim testis est fides.” See note, Mat 1:5 ) <strong> did not perish with them who were disobedient</strong> (on the word <strong> ἀπειθέω</strong> , see note ch. <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="heb+3:18">Hebrews 3:18</span> . The inhabitants of Jericho were disobedient to the will of God manifested by the signs and wonders which he had wrought for Israel: as is implied by Rahab’s speech, Jos 2:9-12 ), <strong> having received</strong> (viz. to her house: κατέλυσαν ἐκεῖ , Jos 2:1 ) <strong> the spies</strong> (sent by Joshua to Jericho: ἀπέστειλεν Ἰησοῦς δύο νεανίσκους κατασκοπεῦσαι , Jos 2:1 ) <strong> with peace</strong> (reff.: so that they had nothing hostile to fear from her). On the introduction of Rahab in <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="jas+2:25">James 2:25</span> , as an example of justification by works, see note there.</p>
