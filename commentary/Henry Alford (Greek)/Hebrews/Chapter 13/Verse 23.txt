<p><strong> 23</strong> .] <strong> Know</strong> ( <strong> γινώσκετε</strong> can hardly but be imperative, standing as it does at the beginning of the sentence. In τὴν δὲ δοκιμὴν αὐτοῦ γινώσκετε , <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="php+2:22">Philippians 2:22</span> , it is otherwise arranged. When the knowledge already exists, the <em> fact</em> is the prominent thing: when the knowledge is first conveyed, the <em> information</em> ) <strong> that our brother Timotheus is dismissed</strong> (the construction is good Greek: Del. gives as instances ἤκουσε τὴν χώραν δῃουμένην , Xen. Anab. v. 5. 7: πυθόμενοι βασιλέα τεθνηκότα , Thuc. iv. 50: γνῶτε ἀναγκαῖον ὂν ὑμῖν ἀνδράσιν ἀγαθοῖ γενέσθαι , ib. vii. 77. It is in fact the original government of the accus. and inf. with a participial predicate substituted for the infinitive: ‘Know him being,’ for ‘know him to be.’ <strong> ἀπολύειν</strong> , on which see Prolegg. § ii. 24, does not occur in St. Paul, but is frequent in St. Luke; e. g. <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="lu+22:68">Luke 22:68</span> ; <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="lu+23:16">Luke 23:16</span> ff.: <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="ac+3:13">Acts 3:13</span> ; <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="ac+4:21">Acts 4:21</span> , of dismissal from prison or custody; <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="ac+13:3">Acts 13:3</span> ; <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="ac+15:30">Acts 15:30</span> , of official sending away; <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="ac+15:33">Acts 15:33</span> , of solemn dismissal, and <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="ac+19:41">Acts 19:41</span> ; <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="ac+23:22">Acts 23:22</span> , of simple dismissal), <strong> with whom, if he come</strong> ( πρός με … εἰκὸς γὰρ ἦν , ἀπολελύσθαι μὲν αὐτόν , μήπω δὲ ἀπεληλυθέναι πρὸς τὸν Παῦλον . Œc.) <strong> soon</strong> (Luther, Schulz, al. take this in the Attic sense of ἐὰν θᾶττον or ἐπειδὰν θᾶττον , “as soon as,” “simul atque:” but such can hardly be the sense here), <strong> I will see you</strong> ( πρὸς ὑμᾶς ἐρχόμενος . Œc.).</p>
