<p><strong> 19</strong> .] <strong> And</strong> ( <strong> thus</strong> ) <strong> we see</strong> (Grot., al. give it, “ex historia cognoscimus:” but Bleek quotes from Seb. Schmidt, and it seems the correcter view, “ <strong> βλέπομεν</strong> non de lectione aut cognitione historiæ, sed de convictione animi e disputatione seu doctrina præmissa”) <strong> that they were not able to enter in</strong> (however much they desired it: they were incapacitated by not fulfilling the condition of inheriting all God’s promises, belief and resulting obedience) <strong> on account of unbelief</strong> (see above on <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="heb+3:12">Hebrews 3:12</span> . This verse forms a kind of ‘quod erat demonstrandum’ (as Ebrard), clenching the argument which has been proceeding since <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="heb+3:12">Hebrews 3:12</span> . The Writer now proceeds to make another use of the example on which he has been so long dwelling).</p>
