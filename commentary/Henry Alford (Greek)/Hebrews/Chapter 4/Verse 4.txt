<p><strong> 4</strong> .] <em> Substantiation of the last assertion</em> . <strong> For he</strong> (God, not Moses, nor ἡ γραφή : see above on εἴρηκεν : see ch. Heb 13:5 ) <strong> hath spoken somewhere</strong> (see above on ch. Heb 2:6 ) <strong> concerning the seventh day</strong> (so in Hellenistic Greek constantly for the Sabbath: as e. g. in the title of one of Philo’s treatises, περὶ τῆς ἑβδόμης : and elsewhere: see Bleek. In 2Ma 15:1 , the Sabbath is called ἡ τῆς καταπαύσεως ἡμέρα ) <strong> on this wise, And God rested</strong> (in classical Greek καταπαύω is transitive, with an accusative of the person and a genitive of the thing: so Xen. Cyr. viii. 5. 25, ἤν τις ἀρχῆς Κῦρον ἐπιχειρῇ καταπαύειν . For this other usage, see <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="heb+4:10">Hebrews 4:10</span> , and reff. LXX. The rest here spoken of must not be understood only as that of one day after the completion of creation; but as an enduring rest, commencing then and still going on, into which God’s people shall hereafter enter. Still less must we find here any discrepancy with such passages as <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="joh+5:17">John 5:17</span> ; <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="isa+40:28">Isaiah 40:28</span> ; God’s rest is not a rest necessitated by fatigue, nor conditioned by idleness: but it is, in fact, the very continuance in that upholding and governing, of which the Creation was the beginning) <strong> on the seventh day from all His works:</strong></p>
