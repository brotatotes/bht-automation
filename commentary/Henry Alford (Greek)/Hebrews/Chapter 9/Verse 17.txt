<p><strong> 17</strong> .] <strong> for</strong> (renders a fresh reason within the domain of the former γάρ , explaining the axiom of Heb 9:16 ) <strong> a testament is of force</strong> ( <strong> βεβαία</strong> , see on ch. <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="heb+2:2">Hebrews 2:2</span> , and Rom 4:16 ) <strong> in the case of the dead</strong> ( <strong> ἐπί</strong> , over, the thing predicated being the substratum or condition of the subject. Doubtless in choosing the plural, and indeed the word itself, the Writer has in his mind the transition which he is about to make from the death of the New Testament to the typical deaths of the Old, which were of animals, between which and men, <strong> νεκρά</strong> , not ἀποθανόντα , would be the common term), <strong> seeing that it</strong> (a διαθήκη ) <strong> is never</strong> (we should expect οὔποτε here, the assertion being absolute and of matter of fact: but it appears to be a habit of later writers after ἐπεί to use the subjective, not the objective negation. So Ælian xii. 63, ἐπεὶ μὴ πάνυ ἦν πλούσιος : Lucian, Hermot. 47, ἐπεὶ μηδενὸς ἡγεμόνος τοιούτου ἔς γε τὸ παρὸν εὐποροῦμεν : Ptol. Geogr. viii., ἐπεὶ μηδὲν εἶχε τοιοῦτον … ἀντιπαραγράφειν . But we must not render μήποτε as = μήπω , which vulg., Faber Stap., Erasm., Luther, Calv., Böhme have done. Many expositors take it interrogatively: “ <em> surely it is not?</em> ” &amp;c. So Œc., Thl., De Dieu, Bengel, Lachmann, and even Delitzsch: but quite unnecessarily, as the above usage is undoubted, and the question introduces an unnecessary harshness) <strong> availing when</strong> ( <strong> ὅτε</strong> corresponding to μή <strong> ποτε</strong> ) <strong> he that made it is alive</strong> .</p>
