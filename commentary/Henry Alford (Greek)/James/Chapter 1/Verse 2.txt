<p><strong> 2</strong> .] <strong> Think it all joy</strong> <strong> (</strong> <strong> χαράν</strong> , following up <strong> χαίρειν</strong> , a characteristic of the style of this Epistle: so ὑπομονήν · ἡ δὲ ὑπομονή , <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="jas+1:3">James 1:3</span> ; λειπόμενοι · εἰ δέ τις λείπεται , <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="jas+1:4">James 1:4</span> f.; διακρινόμενος · ὁ δὲ διακρινόμενος , <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="jas+1:6">James 1:6</span> ; ἀπεἰραστός ἐστι … πειράζει δέ , <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="jas+1:13">James 1:13</span> ; βραδὺς εἰς ὀργήν · ὀργὴ γάρ , <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="jas+1:19">James 1:19</span> f.; τὸν ἔμφυτον λόγον .… γίνεσθε δὲ ποιηταὶ λόγου , <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="jas+1:21">James 1:21</span> f.; τούτου μάταιος ἡ θρησκεία · θρησκεία καθαρὰ κ . τ . λ ., <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="jas+1:26">James 1:26</span> f.; yea, and that when &amp;c. <strong> πᾶσαν</strong> , as in reff., not “ <em> all</em> ( <em> of it</em> ) <em> joy</em> ,” eitel Freude, as Luther: but “ <em> all sorts of</em> ,” “ <em> every kind of</em> ,” “ <em> all conceivable</em> ,” “rem revera omnique ex parte lætam,” as Theile, in Huther. Bengel’s idea is good, that ‘all’ is used as applying to all kinds of temptations; transferred from the subject to the predicate), <strong> my brethren</strong> (this is the constant address in our Epistle. It betokens community of origin and of faith), <strong> whensoever ye fall into</strong> ( <strong> περιπίπτειν</strong> is used of becoming unexpectedly surrounded by adverse circumstances of any kind: so in reff.: so ὅστις ἂν τοιαύταις ξυμφοραῖς περιπέσῃ , Plato, Legg. ix. p. 877 C: μεγάλοις ἀτυχήμασιν ὑπ ʼ Αἰτωλῶν , καὶ μεγάλαις συμφοραῖς περιπεσόντες , Polyb. iv. 19. 13: περιπεσὼν βιαίοις πληγαῖς , ib. iii. 116. 9. Herodotus also uses the expression, cf. vi. 16, and Thuc. ii. 54) <strong> various temptations</strong> (the <strong> πειρασμοί</strong> here are not only what we properly call <em> temptations</em> , but any kind of distresses which happen to us, from without or from within, which in God’s purpose serve as <em> trials</em> of us: the latter word being, in this its now common general meaning, a word derived from the Christian life. See ref. 1 Pet., which is strictly parallel. Œc. says, after Chrys. (in Catena), τὴν κατὰ θεὸν λύπην καὶ τοὺς πειρασμοὺς τούτους καὶ ἐπαινετοὺς οἶδε καὶ χαρᾶς ἀξίους · δεσμὸς γὰρ οὗτοί εἰσιν ἀῤῥαγής , καὶ αὔξησις ἀγάπης καὶ κατανύξεως .
