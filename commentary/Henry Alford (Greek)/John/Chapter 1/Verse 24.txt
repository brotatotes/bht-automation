<p><strong> 24.</strong> ] The reason of this explanation being added is not very clear. Lücke, with whom De Wette agrees, refers it to the apparent hostility of the next enquiry: but I confess I cannot see that it is more hostile than the preceding. Luthardt thinks that it imports, there were some ἀπεσταλμένοι present, who belonged to the sect of the Pharisees ( ἦσαν δὲ καὶ ἐκ τῶν Φαρ . ἀπεσταλμένοι ), which the words will hardly bear: see below. Might it not be to throw light on their question about <em> baptizing</em> , as the Pharisees were the most precise about all ceremonies, lustrations, &amp;c.? Origen makes this <em> a new deputation:</em> but he is plainly wrong: see the <strong> οὖν</strong> below. Euthymius gives another reason yet: ἐπεσημήνατο καὶ τὴν αἵρεσιν αὐτῶν , ἐμφαίνων τὸ περίεργον τούτων καὶ σκολιόν .
	</p>
<p>Abandoning the οἱ (see var. readd.), we must render, <strong> And they</strong> (i.e. the whole deputation) <strong> were</strong> (or <strong> had been</strong> ) <strong> sent by the Pharisees;</strong> which will make it more probable that the explanation refers to the <em> nature</em> of the following question. ἀποστέλλομαι … ἐκ has occurred above, <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="joh+1:19">John 1:19</span> , which gives additional probability to the reading of the text.</p>
