<p><strong> 12.</strong> ] The imagery is here again somewhat changed. The false shepherds are here compared to hirelings, i.e. those who serve <em> merely</em> for gain; the μισθωτός who fulfils the character implied by the word. The idea is brought in by τὴν ψυχ . αὐτ . τίθ . ὑπὲρ τ . πρ ., which introduces a time of danger, when the true and false shepherds are distinguished.
	</p>
<p><strong> τ</strong> <strong> . </strong> <strong> λύκον</strong> ] The purposes of this <strong> wolf</strong> are the same as those of the thief in <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="joh+10:10">John 10:10</span> , and in the allegory he is the same; <em> the great Foe of the sheep of Christ</em> . Lücke and De Wette deny this, and hold ‘any enemies of the theocracy’ to be meant; but no deep view of the parable will be content with this, see <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="mt+7:15">Matthew 7:15</span> , where the λύκοι ἅρπαγες are ψευδοπροφῆται , the κλέπται κ . λῃσταί of <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="joh+10:8">John 10:8</span> ; and their chief and father would therefore be <strong> ὁ λύκος</strong> <strong> ,</strong> just as <strong> ὁ ποιμήν</strong> is <em> the</em> Shepherd.</p>
