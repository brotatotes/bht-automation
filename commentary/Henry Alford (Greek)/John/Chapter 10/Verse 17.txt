<p><strong> 17.</strong> ] The λαλεῖν ἐν παροιμίαις is now over, and He speaks <em> plainly</em> , <strong> My Father.</strong> In this wonderful verse lies the mystery of the love of the Father for the Son; <em> because</em> the Son has condescended to the work of humiliation, and to earn the crown through the cross (see <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="php+2:8-9">Philippians 2:8-9</span> , <strong> διό</strong> ). The <strong> ἵνα</strong> here is strictly τελικόν , <strong> in order that.</strong> “Without this purpose in view,” says Stier (iv. 504, edn. 2), “the Death of Christ would neither be lawful nor possible.”</p>
