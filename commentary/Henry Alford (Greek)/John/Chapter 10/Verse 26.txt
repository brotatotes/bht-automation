<p><strong> 26.</strong> ] The difficulty of καθὼς εἶπον ὑμῖν is considerable warrant for its genuineness: and it comes much more naturally with this than with the following verse. I believe it to refer more to the <em> whole allegory</em> , than to any explicit saying of this kind; and this is shewn to my mind by the following words in <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="joh+10:27">John 10:27</span> : the minor proposition, ‘ <em> but ye hear not my voice</em> ,’ being understood. This was a corollary from the allegory, and thus it might be said <strong> καθὼς εἶπον ὑμῖν</strong> <strong> .</strong> This reference to the allegory some two months after it was spoken, has been used by the rationalists as an argument against the authenticity of the narrative. But, as Meyer observes, it in reality implies that the conflict with the Jewish authorities is here again taken up after that interval, during which it had not broken out.</p>
