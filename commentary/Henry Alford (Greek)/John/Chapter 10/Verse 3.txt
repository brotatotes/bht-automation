<p><strong> 3.</strong> ] Perhaps the <strong> θυρωρός</strong> should not be too much pressed as significant; but certainly <em> the Holy Spirit</em> is especially He who opens the door to the shepherds: see frequent uses of this symbolism by the Apostles, <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="ac+14:27">Acts 14:27</span> ; 1Co 16:9 ; <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="2co+2:12">2 Corinthians 2:12</span> ; <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="col+4:3">Colossians 4:3</span> ; and instances of the θυρωρός shutting the door, <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="ac+16:6-7">Acts 16:6-7</span> . (So Theodorus Heracleota, and Stier, iv. 482, edn. 2.)
	</p>
<p><strong> τὰ πρόβ</strong> <strong> . </strong> <strong> τ</strong> <strong> . </strong> <strong> φων</strong> <strong> . </strong> <strong> αὐτ</strong> <strong> . </strong> <strong> ἀκ</strong> <strong> .</strong> ] The voice of <em> every such true shepherd</em> is heard (heeded, understood) by <em> the sheep</em> (generally): and he calls by name <em> his own</em> sheep, that portion of the great flock entrusted to him, and leads them out to pasture, as his office is.
	</p>
<p>This distinction between τὰ πρόβ . and <strong> τὰ ἴδια</strong> πρόβ . has given rise to exegetical and doctrinal mistakes, from not observing ποιμήν above. It has been imagined that Christ is here spoken of, and that therefore these two descriptions of sheep must be different, and so the whole exposition has been confused. Even Stier has fallen into this mistake.</p>
