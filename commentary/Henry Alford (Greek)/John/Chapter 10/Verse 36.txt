<p><strong> 36.</strong> ] The argument is <em> à minori ad majus</em> . If in any sense they could be called <strong> gods,</strong> how much more properly He, whom &amp;c. They were only officially so <em> called</em> , only <strong> λεγόμενοι</strong> θεοί but He, the only One, sealed and hallowed by the Father, and sent into the world (the aorists refer to the time of the Incarnation), is essentially <strong> θεός</strong> inasmuch as He is <strong> υἱὸς τοῦ θεοῦ</strong> <strong> .
	</strong></p>
<p>The deeper aim of this argument is, to shew them that the idea of <em> man and God being one</em> , was not alien from their O.T. spirit, but set forth there in types and shadows of Him, the real God-Man.
	</p>
<p>Observe <strong> ὑμεῖς</strong> <strong> ,</strong> set in emphatic contrast to the authority of Scripture, as <strong> ὃν ὁ πατὴρ ἡγίασεν</strong> <strong> …</strong> is to <strong> ἐκείνους</strong> above.</p>
