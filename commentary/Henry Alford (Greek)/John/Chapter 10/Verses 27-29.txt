<p><strong> 27 29.</strong> ] This leads to a further description of these sheep. The form of the sentence is a climax; rising through the <strong> ἐγὼ</strong> δίδωμι and ἐκ τ . χ . <strong> μου</strong> <strong> ,</strong> to <strong> ὁ πατήρ μου</strong> ὃ δέδωκέν μοι and ἐκ τ . χ . <strong> τοῦ πατρός</strong> . Then the apparent diversity of the two expressions, ἐκ τ . χ . <strong> μου</strong> and ἐκ τ . χ . <strong> τοῦ πατ</strong> <strong> . </strong> <strong> μου</strong> <strong> ,</strong> gives occasion to the assertion in <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="joh+10:30">John 10:30</span> <strong> ,</strong> that Christ and the Father are ONE; one in <em> essence</em> primarily, but therefore also one in <em> working</em> , and POWER, and in <em> will</em> . ἓν κατὰ δύναμιν , ἤγουν ταυτοδύναμοι , Euthym [152] ; who adds, εἰ δὲ ἓν κατὰ δύναμιν , ἓν ἄρα καὶ κατὰ τὴν θεότητα καὶ οὐσίαν καὶ φύσιν . This certainly is <em> implied</em> in the words, and so the Jews understood them, <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="joh+10:33">John 10:33</span> . Bengel remarks after Augustine, “per <em> sumus</em> refutatur Sabellius, per <em> unum</em> , Arius.” It is perhaps more than is actually contained in the words: but, as Meyer says, they are <em> founded on</em> the unity of essence of the Son and the Father, and so <em> presuppose</em> the homousian doctrine.
