<p><strong> 23.</strong> ] <em> Did the Greeks see</em> (i.e. speak with) <em> Jesus, or not?</em> Certainly <em> not</em> , if I understand His discourse rightly. But they may have been present at, and have understood it. The substance of His answer ( <strong> αὐτοῖς</strong> <strong> ,</strong> to Philip and Andrew, not to the Greeks) is, that the time was now come for His glorification, which should draw all nations to Him: but that glorification must be accomplished by His Death. The very appearance of these Greeks is to Him a token that His glorification is at hand. Stier strikingly says, “These men from the West at the end of the Life of Jesus, set forth the same as the Magi from the East at its beginning; but they come to the <em> Cross</em> of the King, as those to His <em> cradle</em> .” (R. J. ver. 69, edn. 2.) The rejection of the Jews for their unbelief is the secondary subject, and is commented on by the Evangelist, <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="joh+12:37-43">John 12:37-43</span> .
	</p>
<p><strong> ἵνα</strong> <strong> ,</strong> not ‘eventual,’ nor ‘for’ any thing, but <em> most strictly</em> of the <em> purpose</em> <strong> the hour has come, that</strong> (whose object of preparation, and aim, in the eternal counsels, it has been, that) <strong> the Son of Man should be glorified.</strong></p>
