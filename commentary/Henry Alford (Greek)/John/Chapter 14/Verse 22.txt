<p><strong> 22.</strong> ] <strong> Ἰούδας</strong> <strong> , </strong> <strong> οὐχ ὁ Ἰσκ</strong> <strong> .</strong> = Ἰούδας Ἰακώβου of <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="lu+6:16">Luke 6:16</span> ; see note on <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="mt+10:2">Matthew 10:2</span> ff. Meyer remarks that the <strong> οὐχ ὁ Ἰσκαριώτης</strong> is <em> pragmatically</em> superfluous, after ch. <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="joh+13:30">John 13:30</span> , but is added by St. John from his deep horror of the Traitor who bore the same name.
	</p>
<p>The question seems to be put with the Jewish idea, that the Messiah, the King and Judge of the nations, must necessarily <em> manifest himself to the world</em> .
	</p>
<p>[ <strong> καί</strong> preceding an interrogation, expresses astonishment at what has just been said, and, assuming it, connects to it a conclusion which appears to refute or cast doubt on it. So Eur. Med. 1388, ὦ τέκνα φίλτατα ! “ μητρί γε , σοὶ δ ʼ οὔ .” κἄπειτ ʼ ἔκτας ; See more examples in Hartung, i. p. 146, and cf. Kühner on Xen. Mem. p. 117.]
