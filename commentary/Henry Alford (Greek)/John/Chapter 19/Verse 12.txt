<p><strong> 12.</strong> ] <strong> ἐκ τ</strong> <strong> ., from this time;</strong> so De W., Lücke, &amp;c.: Meyer, Stier, and Luthardt render it “ <em> on this account;</em> ” arguing that Pilate had before been endeavouring to deliver Him: but the words imply that from this time, he <em> entirely set</em> <em> himself</em> to deliver Him.
	</p>
<p>Pilate himself was deeply struck by these words of majesty and mildness, and almost sympathy for his [own] weakness, and made a last, and, as <strong> ἐκ τ</strong> <strong> .</strong> seems to imply, a somewhat longer attempt than before, to deliver Him.
	</p>
<p><strong> φίλος τ</strong> <strong> . </strong> <strong> Κ</strong> <strong> .</strong> ] There does not seem to be any allusion to a title of honour, <em> amicus Cæsaris</em> ; indeed, to judge from the citations in Wetstein, a good deal of fancy has been employed in making out the fact of such a title having been in use, any further than that the appellation would naturally arise and be accounted honourable.
	</p>
<p><strong> φίλ</strong> <strong> . </strong> <strong> τ</strong> <strong> . </strong> <strong> Κ</strong> <strong> .</strong> here is ‘well affected to Cæsar.’
	</p>
<p>This was a terrible saying, especially under Tiberius, with whom (Tacit. Ann. iii. 38) “majestatis crimen omnium accusationum complementum erat.”
	</p>
<p><strong> πᾶς ὁ β</strong> <strong> .…</strong> ] This was true: their application of it to Christ a lie. But <em> words</em> , not <em> facts</em> , are taken into account by tyrants, and this Pilate knew.</p>
