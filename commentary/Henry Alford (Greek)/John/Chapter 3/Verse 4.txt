<p><strong> 4.</strong> ] It is impossible that Nicodemus can have so entirely and stupidly <em> misunderstood</em> our Lord’s words, as his question here would seem to imply. The idea of new birth was by no means alien from the Rabbinical views. They described a proselyte when baptized as “sicut parvulus jam natus.” Lightfoot in loc. I agree with Stier in thinking that there was something of the spirit that <em> would not</em> understand, and the disposition to turn to ridicule what he heard. But together with this there was also considerable <em> real ignorance</em> . The proselyte might be regarded as born again, when he became one of the seed of Abraham: this figure would be easily explained on the Judaical view: but that <em> every</em> man should need this, was beyond Nicodemus’s comprehension. He therefore rebuts the assertion with a reductio ad absurdum, which in spirit expresses, as in ch. <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="joh+6:60">John 6:60</span> , ‘This is an hard saying; who can hear it?’
	</p>
