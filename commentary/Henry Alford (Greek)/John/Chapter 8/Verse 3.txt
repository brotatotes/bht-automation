<p><strong> 3.</strong> ] John does not usually connect with δέ , more commonly with οὖν : but δέ is found thus used <em> here</em> , <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="joh+8:1-3">John 8:1-3</span> , (5, where the conjunction of δὲ … δέ is not in St. John’s manner, see <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="ga+2:20">Galatians 2:20</span> ,) 6 (twice v. r.), 7, 9, 10, 11 (twice v. r.). Thence, there is not one δέ of <em> mere connexion</em> ( Joh 8:35 is no exception) through the remaining forty-eight verses of the chapter. Nor does he ever mention οἱ γραμματεῖς elsewhere, but usually calls the opponents of Jesus οἱ Ἰουδαῖοι , or οἱ ἄρχοντες . οἱ γρ . κ . οἱ Φ . is a very common expression in the synoptic narrative.
	</p>
<p>The account gives no light as to the <em> capacity in which</em> these Scribes and Pharisees acted when they brought the woman. Probably, <em> only</em> as tempting Jesus, and not in the course of any legal proceedings against her. Such would have required (<span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="le+20:10">Leviticus 20:10</span> ; Deu 22:22 ) that the <em> man also</em> should have been put to death.</p>
