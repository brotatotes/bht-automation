<p><strong> 10.</strong> ] This shews the sense of the parable, as applying to our own thoughts of ourselves, and the impossibility of any <em> claim</em> for our services to God.
	</p>
<p>In <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="ro+6:23">Romans 6:23</span> (see also the foregoing verses) we have the true ground on which we look for eternal life set before us; viz. as the <em> gift</em> of God <em> whose servants we are</em> , not the <em> wages</em> , as in the case of sin, <em> whose we are not</em> . In the case of <em> men</em> this is different; a good servant is εὔχρηστος ( Phm 1:11 ), not ἀχρεῖος , i.e. οὗ μὴ ἔχει τις χρείαν , Etym. Mag. See <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="ac+17:25">Acts 17:25</span> .
	</p>
<p>The case supposed introduces an argument à fortiori: ‘ <em> how much more, when ye have failed in so many respects</em> .’ ‘Miser est quem Dominus <em> servum inutilem</em> appellat, <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="mt+25:30">Matthew 25:30</span> ; beatus qui se ipse.’ Bengel.
	</p>
<p>Thus closes the series of discourses which began with ch. <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="lu+15:1">Luke 15:1</span> .</p>
