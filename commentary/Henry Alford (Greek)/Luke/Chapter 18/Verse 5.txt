<p><strong> 5.</strong> ] <strong> εἰς τέλος</strong> belongs to <strong> ἐρχομένη</strong> <strong> ,</strong> as in E. V., but has a stronger force than there <strong> lest coming for ever, she …
	</strong></p>
<p><strong> ὑπωπιάζῃ</strong> <strong> ,</strong> from ὑπώπιον , the part of the cheek immediately beneath the eyes, signifies literally <strong> to smite in the face;</strong> and proverbially (see reff.), <strong> to mortify</strong> or <strong> incessantly annoy.</strong> It answers exactly to the Latin <em> obtundo</em> , which Terence has in this sense, ‘ <em> Ne me obtundas hac de re sæpius</em> ,’ Adelph. i. 2. 33; and alli [103] . fr. Livy, ‘ <em> Neque ego obtundam, sæpius eadem nequicquam agendo</em> ,’ ii. 15. The Greek word does not appear to be any where used in this sense; so that the use of it here may be a Latinism, as Grotius thought. Meyer interprets it literally ‘ <em> lest at last she should become desperate and come and strike me in the face</em> .’ It has been observed that the Apostles acted from this very motive when they besought the Lord to send away the Syrophœnician woman, ‘for she <em> cried after them</em> .’ <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="mt+15:23">Matthew 15:23</span> .
	</p>
<p style="margin-left:3em;"> [103] alli= some cursive mss.</p>
