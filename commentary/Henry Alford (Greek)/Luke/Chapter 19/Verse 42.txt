<p><strong> 42.</strong> ] <strong> εἰ ἔγνως</strong> εἰώθασιν οἱ κλαίοντες ἐπικόπτεσθαι τοὺς λόγους ὑπὸ τῆς τοῦ πάθους σφοδρότητος , Euthym [107] Perhaps in the <em> actual words</em> spoken by the Lord there may have been an allusion to the <em> name Jerusalem:</em> ‘Utinam quæ diceris Jerusalem re ipsa esses Jerusalem, ac videres ea, quæ pacem tibi præstare possent.’ Wetstein.
	</p>
<p style="margin-left:3em;"> [107] Euthymius Zigabenus, 1116
	</p>
<p><strong> καὶ σύ</strong> <strong> , thou also,</strong> as well as these My disciples.

But in classic Greek the emphatic word always intervenes between καί and <strong> γε</strong> <strong> ,</strong> so καί σέ γε ἐν τούτοις λέγω , Æsch. Prom. 1009: whereas in Latin <em> et quidem</em> is usually found undivided. <strong> ]</strong></p>
