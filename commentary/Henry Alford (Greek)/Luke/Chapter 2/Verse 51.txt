<p><strong> 51.</strong> ] The high consciousness which had manifested itself in Luk 2:49 did not interfere with His self-humiliation, nor render Him independent of his parents. This voluntary subjection probably shewed itself in working at his reputed father’s trade: see Mar 6:2 and note.
	</p>
<p>From this time we have <em> no more mention of Joseph</em> (ch. Luk 4:22 is not to the point): the next we hear is of <em> His mother and brethren</em> ( Joh 2:12 ): whence it is inferred that, between this time and the commencement of our Lord’s public life, <em> Joseph died</em> .
	</p>
<p><strong> καὶ ἡ μήτ</strong> <strong> .</strong> ] These words tend to confirm the common belief that these opening chapters, or at least <em> this</em> narrative, may have been derived from the testimony of <em> the mother of the Lord herself</em> . She <em> kept</em> them, as in wonderful coincidence with the remarkable circumstances of His birth, and its announcement, and His presentation in the temple, and the offerings of the Magi; but in what way, or by what one great revelation all these things were to be gathered in one, did not yet appear, but was doubtless manifested to her afterwards: see <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="ac+1:14">Acts 1:14</span> ; <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="ac+2:1">Acts 2:1</span> .</p>
