<p><strong> 1</strong> . <strong> ἀρχὴ</strong> κ . τ . λ .] This is probably a title to what follows, as <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="mt+1:1">Matthew 1:1</span> , and not connected with <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="mr+1:4">Mark 1:4</span> , as Fritzsche and Lachm., nor with <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="mr+1:2">Mark 1:2</span> , as Meyer. It is simpler and gives more majesty to the exordium, to put a period at the end of <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="mr+1:1">Mark 1:1</span> , and make the citation from the Prophet a new and confirmatory title.
	</p>
<p><strong> Ἰης</strong> <strong> . </strong> <strong> χρ</strong> <strong> .</strong> ] <strong> of,</strong> as its author, or its subject, as the context may determine. “If the genit. after εὐαγγ . is <em> not</em> a person, it is always that of the <em> object</em> , as εὐαγγ . τῆς βασιλείας , τῆς σωτηρίας κ . τ . λ . (<span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="mt+4:23">Matthew 4:23</span> ; <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="eph+1:13">Ephesians 1:13</span> ; Eph 6:15 alli [1] .). If θεοῦ follows, the genit. is one of the <em> subject</em> (ch. <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="mr+1:15">Mark 1:15</span> .<span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="ro+1:1">Romans 1:1</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="ro+1:1">Romans 1:1</span> ; <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="ro+1:15-16">Romans 1:15-16</span> , alli [2] .), as also when μου follows (<span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="ro+2:16">Romans 2:16</span> ; Rom 16:25 ; <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="1th+1:5">1 Thessalonians 1:5</span> , alli [3] .). But if χριστοῦ follows (<span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="ro+1:9">Romans 1:9</span> ; <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="ro+15:19">Romans 15:19</span> ; <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="1co+9:12">1 Corinthians 9:12</span> , alli [4] .), it may be either genit. of the <em> subject</em> ( <em> auctoris</em> ) or of the <em> object</em> : and only the context can determine. Here it decides for the latter ( Mar 1:2-8 ). Render therefore, <strong> the glad tidings concerning Jesus Christ.</strong> ” Meyer.
