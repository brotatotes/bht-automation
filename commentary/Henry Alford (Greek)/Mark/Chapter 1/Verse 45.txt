<p><strong> 45.</strong> ] <strong> ἤρξατο</strong> <strong> ,</strong> he lost no time in doing it.
	</p>
<p><strong> τὸν λόγον</strong> ] not, ‘ <em> what Jesus had said to him</em> ,’ but <strong> the account</strong> , of his healing.
	</p>
<p>ἤρχοντο tells us more than ἦλθον would have done. Our Lord did not wish to put a stop to the multitudes seeking Him, but only to avoid that kind of concourse which would have beset Him in the <em> towns</em> : the seeking to Him for teaching and healing <em> still went or</em> and that from all parts.
	</p>

The three are evidently independent accounts; Mark’s, as usual, the most precise in details; e.g. “ <em> borne of four</em> ;” Luke’s also bearing marks of an eye-witness (see <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="mr+1:19">Mark 1:19</span> , end); Matthew’s apparently at second hand.</p>
