<p><strong> 1, 2.</strong> ] As far as εὑρήσετε , the agreement in Matt., Mark, and Luke is nearly verbal; after that Mark and Luke only mention the <em> foal</em> , and add, <strong> on which never man sat.</strong> Compare with this <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="lu+23:53">Luke 23:53</span> . Our Lord’s birth, triumph, and burial were to be, in this, alike. ‘A later tradition, sprung from the <em> sacred destination</em> of the beast (for beasts never yet worked were used for sacred purposes, <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="nu+19:2">Numbers 19:2</span> ; Deu 21:3 ; 1Sa 6:7 ).’ Meyer. But does it never strike such annotators, that this very usage would lead not only to the <em> narrative being so constructed</em> , but to the <em> command itself having been so given?</em></p>
