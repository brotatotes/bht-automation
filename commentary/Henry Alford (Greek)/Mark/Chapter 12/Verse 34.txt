<p><strong> 34.</strong> ] <strong> νουνεχῶς</strong> Attice νουνεχόντως , opposed to ἀφρόνως , Isocr. <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="mr+12:7">Mark 12:7</span> (Meyer).
	</p>
<p><strong> οὐ μακρὰν</strong> <strong> …</strong> ] This man had hold of that principle in which Law and Gospel are one: he stood as it were <em> at the door</em> of the Kingdom of God. He only wanted (but the want was indeed a serious one) repentance and faith to be <em> within it</em> . The Lord shews us here that even outside His flock, those who can answer νουνεχῶς who have knowledge of the <em> spirit</em> of the great command of Law and Gospel, are <em> nearer</em> to being of his flock, than the <em> formalists:</em> but then, as Bengel adds, ‘Si non procul es, intra: alias <em> prœstiterit, procul fuisse</em> .’
	</p>
<p><strong> καὶ οὐδεὶς</strong> <strong> …</strong> ] This is apparently out of its place here, as it is <em> after the question which now follows</em> , that Matt. relates this discomfiture of his adversaries. We must not however conclude too hastily, especially where the minute accuracy of Mark is at stake. The question just asked <em> was the last put to our</em> <em> Lord</em> , and therefore the notice of its <em> being the last</em> comes in fitly here. The enquiry which follows did more than silence their <em> questioning:</em> it silenced their <em> answering too:</em> both which things Matt. combines as the result of this day, in his <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="mt+22:46">Matthew 22:46</span> .
	</p>
<p><strong> ἐπερωτῆσαι</strong> <strong> ,</strong> not, ‘to ask him any <em> more</em> questions:’ see on ch. <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="mr+11:29">Mark 11:29</span> .</p>
