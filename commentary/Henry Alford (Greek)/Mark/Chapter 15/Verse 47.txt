<p><strong> 47.</strong> ] <strong> Μ</strong> <strong> . </strong> <strong> ἡ Ἰωσῆτος</strong> understand, <em> mother:</em> see <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="mr+15:40">Mark 15:40</span> . That she is so called here, and <strong> Μαρία ἡ τοῦ Ἰακώβου</strong> in the next verse, points to a <em> difference of origin</em> in the two accounts here, of the <em> Crucifixion</em> and <em> Resurrection</em> .
	</p>
<p>The mother of the Lord had in all probability previously departed: see notes on Mat 27:56 and <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="joh+19:27">John 19:27</span> .
	</p>
<p>Luke generalizes, and says, <em> the women who came with Him from Galilee</em> .
	</p>
<p>Some have understood by <strong> Μ</strong> <strong> . </strong> <strong> Ἰωσῆτος</strong> or <strong> Ἰωσῆ</strong> or <strong> Ἰωσήφ</strong> , the <em> wife</em> or <em> daughter</em> of Joseph of Arimathæa some, the <em> mother of the Lord:</em> but both unnecessarily, and without proof. The perf. τεθεῖται is to shew that they came up after the burial had taken place; the pres. ( τίθεται , re [61] .) would imply that they were present at the entombment. So Meyer.
	</p>
<p style="margin-left:3em;"> [61] The <em> Textus Receptus</em> or received text of the Greek Testament. Used in this Edition when elz and Steph agree</p>
