<p><strong> 35. </strong> <strong> ὅπως πλ</strong> <strong> .</strong> ] <strong> in order that</strong> &amp;c., not ‘ <em> so that thus</em> ,’ or ‘ <em> and in this way</em> ’ (?) as Webst. and Wilk., here, or any where else. See note on ch. <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="mt+1:22">Matthew 1:22</span> . The <em> prophet</em> , according to the superscription of <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="ps+78:1-72">Psalms 78:1-72</span> , is Asaph, so called <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="2ch+29:30">2 Chronicles 29:30</span> , LXX. The former clause of the citation is identical with the LXX; the latter = φθέγξομαι προβλήματα ἀπ ʼ ἀρχῆς , LXX. When we find De Wette, &amp;c. maintaining that the Psalm <em> contains no parable</em> , and that consequently these words can only be cited <em> out of</em> their context, we must remember that such a view is wholly inconsistent with any deep insight into the meaning of the Scripture record: for the whole Psalm consists of a recounting of events which St. Paul assures us τύποι ἡμῶν ἐγενήθησαν … τυπικῶς συνέβαινον ἐκείνοις , ἐγράφη δὲ πρὸς νουθεσίαν ἡμῶν . <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="1co+10:6">1 Corinthians 10:6</span> ; <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="1co+10:11">1 Corinthians 10:11</span> .</p>
