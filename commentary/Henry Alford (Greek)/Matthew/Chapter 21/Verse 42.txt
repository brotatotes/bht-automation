<p><strong> 42.</strong> ] A citation from <em> the same Psalm of triumph from which the multitudes had taken their Hosannas</em> . This verse is quoted with the same signification in <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="ac+4:11">Acts 4:11</span> ; <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="1pe+2:6-7">1 Peter 2:6-7</span> , where also the cognate passage Isa 28:16 is quoted, as in <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="ro+9:33">Romans 9:33</span> . The words here are those of the LXX.
	</p>
<p><strong> αὕτη</strong> <strong> … </strong> <strong> θαυμαστὴ</strong> <strong> …</strong> are feminine by a Hebraism, in which idiom the fem. is used as the neuter, there being no neuter. Meyer takes it as agreeing with κεφ . γωνίας , but surely with the examples in the reff. before us, it is simpler and better to understand the construction as above.
	</p>
<p>The <strong> οἰκοδομοῦντες</strong> answer to the <em> husbandmen</em> , and the addition is made in this changed similitude to shew them that <em> though they might reject and kill the Son, yet He will be victorious in the end</em> .
	</p>
<p><strong> εἰς κεφ</strong> <strong> . </strong> <strong> γων</strong> <strong> .</strong> ] The <em> corner-stone</em> binds together both walls of the building; so Christ unites Jews and Gentiles in Himself. See the comparison beautifully followed into detail, <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="eph+2:20-22">Ephesians 2:20-22</span> .
	</p>
