<p><strong> 40, 41.</strong> ] From this point (or perhaps even from <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="mt+24:37">Matthew 24:37</span> , as historic resemblance is itself parabolic) the discourse begins to assume a parabolic form, and gradually passes into a series of formal parables in the next chapter.
	</p>
<p>These verses set forth that, as in the times of Noah, men and women shall be employed in their ordinary work: see <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="ex+11:5">Exodus 11:5</span> (LXX), <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="isa+47:2">Isaiah 47:2</span> . They also shew us that the elect of God will <em> to the last be mingled in companionship and partnership with the children of this world</em> (see Mar 1:19-20 ). We may notice, that these verses do not refer to the same as <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="mt+24:16-18">Matthew 24:16-18</span> . <em> Then</em> it is a question of <em> voluntary flight; now</em> of <em> being taken</em> (by the angels, <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="mt+24:31">Matthew 24:31</span> : the <em> present</em> graphically sets the incident before us; or perhaps describes the <em> rule</em> of proceeding. See on the sense of <strong> παραλαμβ</strong> <strong> .</strong> especially ref. John), <em> or left</em> . Nor again do they refer to the great judgment of ch. <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="mt+25:31">Matthew 25:31</span> , for then ( Mat 24:32 ) <em> all</em> shall be summoned: but they refer to the millennial dispensation, and the gathering of the elect to the Lord <em> then</em> . The “women grinding at the mill” has been abundantly illustrated by travellers, as even now seen in the East. See especially ‘The Land and the Book,’ pp. 526, 7.
	</p>
<p><strong> ἐν</strong> <strong> ,</strong> either because the pair of stones is the element in which the act of grinding takes place, or, more probably, because that which is ground is <em> within</em> , between the stones.</p>
