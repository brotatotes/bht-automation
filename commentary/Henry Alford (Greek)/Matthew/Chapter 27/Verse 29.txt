<p><strong> 29.</strong> ] It does not appear whether the purpose of the crown was <em> to wound</em> , or simply <em> for mockery</em> and equally uncertain is it, of what kind of thorns it was composed. The <em> acanthus</em> itself, with its large succulent leaves, is singularly unfit for such a purpose: as is the plant with very long sharp thorns commonly known as <em> spina Christi</em> , being a <em> brittle</em> acacia (robinia), and the very length of the thorns, which would meet in the middle if it were bent into a wreath, precluding it. Some <em> flexile</em> shrub or plant must be understood possibly some variety of the cactus or prickly pear. ‘Hasselquist, a Swedish naturalist, supposes a very common plant, <em> naba</em> or <em> nubka</em> of the Arabs, with many small and sharp spines; soft, round, and pliant branches; leaves much resembling ivy, of a very deep green, as if in designed mockery of a victor’s wreath,’ Travels, 288. 1766 (cited by F. M).
	</p>
