<p><strong> 9</strong> . <strong> τὴν ἀγάπην</strong> ] is not to be restricted to ‘ <em> this thy love</em> ’ (of <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="phm+1:7">Philemon 1:7</span> ; so Calv., al.), or ‘ <em> our mutual love</em> ’ (Grot., al.), but is quite general ‘that Christian love, of which thou shewest so bright an example:’ <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="phm+1:7">Philemon 1:7</span> .
	</p>
<p><strong> τοιοῦτος ὤν</strong> ] reason for the μᾶλλον ‘I prefer this way, as the more efficacious, being such an one, &amp;c.’ The ‘cum <em> sis</em> talis’ of the Vulgate is evidently a mistake. I believe Meyer is right in maintaining that τοιοῦτος cannot be taken as preparatory to ὡς , ‘ <em> such an one, as …</em> ’ as in E. V., and commonly. I have therefore punctuated accordingly, as has Ellic. The rendering will be: <strong> Being such an one</strong> (as declared in διὸ .… παρακαλῶ ), <strong> as</strong> (1) <strong> Paul the aged and</strong> (2) <strong> now a prisoner also of Christ Jesus</strong> ( <em> two</em> points are made, and not <em> three</em> as Chrys., all. <strong> Παῦλος πρεσβύτης</strong> going together, and the fact of his being a prisoner, adding weight ( <strong> καί</strong> ). The fact of <strong> πρεσβύτης</strong> is interesting, as connected with the date of this Epistle and those to Eph. and Col.: see Prolegg. to Eph. § iv.), <strong> I beseech thee</strong> , &amp;c.
	</p>
<p>If we read ἐγώ before ἐγέννησα , the repetition of ἐμοῦ ἐγώ will serve, as Meyer remarks, to mark more forcibly the character of his <em> own</em> child, and ἐν τοῖς δεσμοίς gives more weight still to the entreaty.
	</p>
<p><strong> Ὀνήσιμον</strong> is not (with Erasm.-Schmid) to be treated as if it were a play on the name <strong> ὃν ἐγένν</strong> <strong> … </strong> <strong> ὀνήσιμον</strong> , ‘ <em> profitable to me</em> :’ but simply to be regarded as an accusative by attraction.</p>
