<p><strong> 15</strong> .] <strong> ἄμεμπτοι</strong> , <strong> without blame</strong> , <strong> ἀκέραιοι</strong> , “ <strong> pure</strong> , <em> simplices</em> , vulg æth: <em> sinceres</em> ( <em> i</em> ), Clarom.: ὁ μὴ κεκραμένος κακοῖς , ἀλλ ʼ ἁπλοῦς καὶ ἀποίκιλος , Etym. Mag.… For the distinction between ἀκέραιος , ἁπλοῦς , and ἄκακος , see Tittm. Synon. i. p. 27.” Ellicott. On <strong> τέκνα θεοῦ</strong> , see especially <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="ro+8:14-15">Romans 8:14-15</span> .
	</p>
<p><strong> ἄμωμα</strong> , <strong> blameless</strong> : unblamed, and unblamable: Herod. uses it, ii. 177, of a law: τῷ ἐκεῖνοι ἐς αἰεὶ χρέωνται , εόντι ἀμώμῳ νόμῳ . The whole clause is a reminiscence of ref. Deut., where we have τέκνα μωμητά , γενεὰ σκολιὰ κ . διεστραμμένη .
	</p>
<p>For the figurative meaning of <strong> σκολιός</strong> , cf. reff. Acts and 1 Pet., and Plato, Legg. xii. p. 945 B, ἄν τίς τι εἴπῃ σκολιὸν αὐτῶν ἢ πραξῃ , Gorg. p. 525 A, πάντα σκολιὰ ὑπὸ ψεύδους κ . ἀλαζονείας , κ . οὐδὸν εὐθὺ διὰ τὸ ἅνευ ἀληθείας τεθράφθαι : and on <strong> διεστραμμένη</strong> , διεστρέφετο ὑπὸ κόλακος , Polyb. viii. 24. 3.
	</p>
<p><strong> ἐν οἷς</strong> , the masculine referring to those included in <strong> γενεά</strong> : so Thuc. i. 136, φεύγει ἐς Κέρκυραν , ὢν αὐτῶν εὐεργέτης . See more examples in Kühner, ii. p. 43.
	</p>
<p><strong> φαίνεσθε</strong> , not <em> imperative</em> , as most of the Fathers, Erasm., Calvin, Grot., al., but <em> indicative</em> , for this <em> is</em> the position of Christians in the world: see <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="mt+5:14">Matthew 5:14</span> ; <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="eph+5:8">Ephesians 5:8</span> . So De W., Meyer, Wiesinger, &amp;c. &amp;c. It has been said (Mey., Wies., al.) that we must not render <strong> φαίνεσθε</strong> ‘ <em> shine</em> ,’ which would be φαίν <strong> ετε</strong> : but surely there is but very little difference between ‘ <em> appear</em> ’ and ‘ <em> shine</em> ’ here, and only St. John and St. Peter use φαίνω for ‘ <em> to shine</em> ,’ <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="joh+1:5">John 1:5</span> ; <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="joh+5:35">John 5:35</span> ; <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="1jo+2:8">1 John 2:8</span> ; Rev 1:16 ; <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="2pe+1:19">2 Peter 1:19</span> , not St. Paul, for whom in such a matter their usage is no rule. Ellic. 1) objects that this must not be alleged against the simple meaning of the word, and 2) wishes to give the middle a special use in connexion with the <em> appearance</em> or rising of the heavenly bodies. But we may answer 1) by such examples as δεινοὶ δέ οἱ ὄσσε φάανθεν , where Rost and Palm translate the passive ‘ <em> leuchteten</em> :’ and 2) by urging that such a reference seems here to lay too much pregnancy of meaning on the word.
	</p>
<p><strong> φωστῆρες</strong> , not ‘ <em> lights</em> ’ merely, but <strong> luminaries</strong> , ‘ <em> heavenly bodies</em> :’ see ref. Gen.: and Sir 43:7 , Wis 13:2 .
	</p>
<p><strong> ἐπέχοντες</strong> ] probably as E. V. <strong> holding forth</strong> (hardly, as Ellic., “ <em> seeing ye hold forth</em> ,” but “ <em> in that ye hold forth</em> :” the participle being rather explicative than causal) to them, <strong> applying</strong> to them, which is the one of the commonest meanings of ἐπέχειν , see reff. Various senses have been given, e.g. ‘ <em> holding fast</em> ,’ Luther, Estius, Bengel, De Wette, al.: ‘ <em> in vertice tenentes</em> ,’ Erasm.: ‘ <em> sustinentes</em> ,’ Calv.: ‘ <em> possessing</em> ,’ Meyer, who quotes for this meaning Herod. i. 104, οἱ δὲ Σκύθαι τὴν Ἀσίαν πᾶσαν ἐπέσχον , and Thuc. ii. 101, ὁ δὲ τήν τε Χαλκιδικὴν κ . Βοττικὴν κ . Μακεδονίαν ἅμα ἐπέχων ἔφθειρε , neither of which justify it: for in both these places it is ‘ <em> to occupy</em> ,’ not ‘ <em> to possess</em> :’ as also in Polyb. iii. 112. 8, εὐχαὶ κ . θυσίαι κ . τ . λ .… ἐπεῖχον τὴν πόλιν . And this sense would manifestly be inapplicable. His objection to the ordinary rendering, that the subjects of the sentence themselves shine by means of the λόγος τῆς ζωῆς , surely is irrelevant: for may not the stars be said ‘præbere,’ ‘prætendere,’ their light, notwithstanding that that light is <em> in</em> them? Chrys., Œc., Thl., interpret it, μέλλοντες ζήσεσθαι , τῶν σωζομένων ὄντες · and Chrys. continues οἱ φωστῆρές , φησι , λόγον φωτὸς ἐπέχουσιν · ὑμεῖς λόγον ζωῆς . τί ἐστι , λόγον ζωῆς · σπέρμα ζωῆς ἔχοντες , τουτέστιν , ἐνέχυρα ζωῆς ἔχοντες , αὐτὴν κατέχοντες τὴν ζωήν , τουτέστι σπέρμα ζωῆς ἐν ὑμῖν ἔχοντες : Thdrt., ἀντὶ τοῦ τῷ λόγῳ προσέχοντες τῆς ζωῆς , ungrammatically, for this would be λόγῳ ζωῆς ἐπέχοντες , as ὁ δὲ ἐπεῖχεν αὐτοῖς , <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="ac+3:5">Acts 3:5</span> ; cf. also ref. 1 Tim.
	</p>
<p><strong> εἰς καύχ</strong> <strong> . </strong> <strong> ἐμοί</strong> ] <strong> for</strong> (result of your thus walking, <em> as concerns myself</em> ) <strong> a matter of boasting for me against</strong> (temporal: reserved for) <strong> the day of Christ, that</strong> ( ὅτι οὐ μάτην τὴν ὑπὲρ ὑμῶν ἀνεδεξάμην σπουδήν , Thdrt.) <strong> I did not run</strong> (the past tense is from the point of view of that day. On ἔδραμον , see reff.) <strong> for nothing, nor labour for nothing</strong> (cf. ref. Job).</p>
