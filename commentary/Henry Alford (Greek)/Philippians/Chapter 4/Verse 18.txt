<p><strong> 18</strong> .] <strong> But</strong> (notwithstanding that the gift is not that which I <em> desire</em> , I have received it, and been sufficiently supplied by it) <strong> I have</strong> (emphatic, and exactly as in ἀπέχειν τὸν μισθόν ‘I have no more to ask from you, but have enough:’ not as Erasm., Beza, Grot., &amp;c. ‘I have duly received all you sent’) <strong> all</strong> ( <strong> I want</strong> ), <strong> and abound</strong> (over and above): <strong> I am filled</strong> (repetition and intensification of περισσεύω ), <strong> having received at the hands of Epaphroditus the remittance from you, a savour of fragrance</strong> (a clause in apposition, expressing a judgment, so frequently in poetry, especially in tragedians, Il. ω . 735, ἤ τις Ἀχαιῶν ῥίψει , χειρὸς ἑλών , ἀπὸ πύργου , λυγρὸν ὄλεθρον : Eur. Orest. 950, τιθεῖσα λευκὸν ὄνυχα διὰ παρηΐδων , αἱματηρὸν ἄταν . See Kühner, ii. 146. On <strong> ὀσμὴ εὐωδίας</strong> see <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="eph+5:2">Ephesians 5:2</span> , note), <strong> a sacrifice acceptable, well pleasing to God</strong> (see Heb 13:16 ; 1Pe 2:5 ).</p>
