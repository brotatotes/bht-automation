<p><strong> 15.</strong> ] The reading γάρ , besides the overwhelming authority in its favour, is the more difficult and characteristic. It can hardly (as Meyer and Tholuck) depend on the <strong> εἰ μὴ</strong> κ . τ . λ ., for thus an awkwardness would be introduced into the connexion of the clauses: but I believe it to be elliptical, depending on the suppressed restatement of the precept of <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="ro+14:13">Romans 14:13</span> ; q. d. ‘But this knowledge is not to be your rule in practice, but rather, &amp;c., as in <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="ro+14:13">Romans 14:13</span> ; ‘ <em> for</em> if,’ &amp;c.
	</p>
<p><strong> βρῶμα</strong> , barely put, to make the contrast greater between the <em> slight occasion</em> , and the <em> great mischief done</em> . The mere λυπεῖν your brother, is an offence against <em> love</em> : how much greater an offence then, <strong> if</strong> this λυπεῖν end in ἀπολλύειν in ruining (causing to act against his conscience, and so to commit sin and be in danger of quenching God’s Spirit within him) by a MEAL of thine, a brother, for whom Christ died! “Ne pluris feceris tuum cibum, quam Christus vitam suam.” Bengel. See an exact parallel in <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="1co+8:10-11">1 Corinthians 8:10-11</span> .</p>
