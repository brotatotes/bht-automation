<p><strong> 22.</strong> ] <strong> The faith which thou hast</strong> (this reading, which is the more probable on critical grounds, was perhaps changed into the σὺ πίστιν ἔχεις of the rec. on account of the position of the σύ . But this is quite in St. Paul’s manner: cf. <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="ro+14:4">Romans 14:4</span> ; 1Co 15:36 ; <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="2co+2:10">2 Corinthians 2:10</span> . However, the other reading is very ancient, and it is impossible to decide positively between them. If it is taken, the interrogative rendering, “ <strong> Hast thou faith?</strong> ” better suits the lively character of the address than the affirmative, “ <em> Thou hast faith</em> ”) <strong> have (it) to thyself</strong> (reff.) <strong> before God</strong> . Chrys., who does not read the last words ( ἐν . τ . θ .), says, πίστιν ἐνταῦθα οὐ τὴν περὶ δογμάτων , ἀλλὰ τὴν περὶ τῆς προκειμένης ὑποθέσεως λέγει …, ἐκείνη μὲν γὰρ μὴ ὁμολογουμένη καταστρέφει , αὕτη δὲ ὁμολογουμένη ἀκαίρως . Hom. xxvi. p. 714. ‘ <em> Before God</em> ,’ because He is the object of faith: hardly, as Erasm., “comprimens inanem gloriam quæ solet esse comes scientiæ,” for there is <em> no trace of a depreciation</em> of the strong in faith in the chapter, only a <em> caution as to their conduct</em> in regard of their weaker brethren.
	</p>
<p>With μακάριος begins the closing and general sentence of the Apostle with regard to <em> both</em> : it is a blessed thing to <em> have no scruples</em> (the <em> strong in faith</em> is in a situation to be envied) about things in which we allow ourselves (Olsh. refers to the addition in the Codex Bezæ at <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="lu+6:4">Luke 6:4</span> , where our Lord is related to have seen a man tilling his land on the Sabbath, and to have said to him, εἰ μὲν οἶδας τί ποιεῖς , μακάριος εἶ , εἰ δὲ μὴ οἶδας , ἐπικατάρατος , καὶ παραβάτης εἶ τοῦ νόμου ):</p>
