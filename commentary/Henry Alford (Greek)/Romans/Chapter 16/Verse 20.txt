<p><strong> 20.</strong> ] ἐπειδὴ γὰρ εἶπε τοὺς τὰς διχοστασίας κ . τὰ σκάνδαλα ποιοῦντας , εἶπεν εἰρήνης θεόν , ἵνα θαρσήσωσι περὶ τῆς τούτων ἀπαλλαγῆς . Chrys. Hom. xxxii. p. 755: and so most Commentators. De W. prefers taking ὁ θ . τῆς εἰρ . more generally as ‘the God of <em> salvation</em> ;’ and the usage of the expression (see reff.) seems to favour this.
	</p>
<p><strong> συντρ</strong> <strong> . </strong> <strong> τ</strong> <strong> . </strong> <strong> σατ</strong> <strong> .</strong> is a similitude from <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="ge+3:15">Genesis 3:15</span> .
	</p>
<p><strong> συντρίψει</strong> , not as Stuart, ‘for optative,’ nor does it express any <em> wish</em> , but a prophetic assurance and encouragement in bearing up against all adversaries, that it would not be long before the great Adversary himself would be bruised under their feet.
	</p>
<p><strong> ἡ χάρις</strong> κ . τ . λ .] It appears as if the Epistle was intended to conclude with this usual benediction, but the Apostle found occasion to add more. This he does also in other Epistles: see <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="1co+16:23-24">1 Corinthians 16:23-24</span> ; similarly <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="php+4:20">Philippians 4:20</span> , and Php 4:21-23 after the doxology, <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="2th+3:16-18">2 Thessalonians 3:16-18</span> : <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="1ti+6:16-17">1 Timothy 6:16-17</span> ff.: <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="2ti+4:18-19">2 Timothy 4:18-19</span> ff.</p>
