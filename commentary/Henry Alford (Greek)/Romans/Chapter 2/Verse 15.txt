<p><strong> 15.</strong> ] <strong> ἐνδείκν</strong> <strong> ., by their conduct shew forth</strong> , give an example of.
	</p>
<p><strong> τὸ ἔργ</strong> <strong> . </strong> <strong> τοῦ νόμου</strong> = τὰ τοῦ νόμου above: but sing. as applying to each of the particular cases supposed in the ὅταν .… ποιῶσιν . If it had here been τὰ ἔργα τοῦ νόμου , it might have been understood to mean <em> the whole works of the law</em> , which the indefinite ὅταν prevents above.
	</p>
<p><strong> γραπτὸν ἐν τ</strong> <strong> . </strong> <strong> κ</strong> <strong> . </strong> <strong> αὐτ</strong> <strong> .</strong> ] Alluding to the tables of stone on which the law was written: see a similar figure <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="2co+3:3">2 Corinthians 3:3</span> .
	</p>
<p><strong> συμμαρτ</strong> <strong> . </strong> <strong> αὐτ</strong> <strong> . </strong> <strong> τ</strong> <strong> . </strong> <strong> συνειδ</strong> <strong> .</strong> ] This is <em> a new</em> argument, not a mere continuation of the ἔνδειξις above. Besides their giving this example by actions consonant with the law, their <em> own conscience</em> , reflecting on the thing done, <em> bears witness to it as good</em> .
	</p>
<p><strong> συμμ</strong> ., not merely = μαρτ ., as Grot., Thol., nor = <em> una testatur</em> , viz. as well as their practice, but <strong> confirming by its testimony</strong> , the συν signifying the agreement of the witness with the deed, as <em> con</em> in <em> contestari, confirmare</em> : perhaps also the συν may be partly induced by the συν in συνειδήσεως , referring to the reflective process, in which a man confers, so to speak, with himself.
	</p>
<p><strong> καὶ μετ</strong> <strong> . </strong> <strong> ἀλλ</strong> <strong> .</strong> κ . τ . λ .] <strong> and their thoughts</strong> ( <em> judgments</em> or <em> reflections</em> , the self-judging voices of the conscience, which being corrupted by sinful desires are often <em> divided</em> ) <strong> among one another</strong> (i.e. thought against thought in inner strife) <strong> accusing, or perhaps excusing</strong> (these two participles are <em> absolute</em> , describing the office of these judgments, and nothing need be supplied, as ‘ <em> them</em> ,’ or ‘ <em> their deeds</em> ’). Notice the similarity of this strife of conscience, and its testimony, as here described, to the higher and more detailed form of the same conflict in the Christian man, ch. <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="ro+7:16">Romans 7:16</span> .</p>
