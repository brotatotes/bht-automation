<p><strong> 4.</strong> ] <strong> ἤ</strong> <strong> , or</strong> (introducing a new error or objection, see ch. <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="ro+3:29">Romans 3:29</span> ; <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="ro+6:3">Romans 6:3</span> ; Rom 11:2 ), ‘ <em> inasmuch as God spares thee day by day</em> (see Ecc 8:11 ), <em> dost thou set light by His long-suffering, ignorant that His intent in it is to lead thee to repentance</em> ?’
	</p>
<p><strong> πλούτου</strong> , a favourite word with the Apostle (see reff.), the fulness, ‘abundance.’
	</p>
<p><strong> χρηστ</strong> ., as shewn by His <strong> ἀνοχή</strong> and <strong> μακροθ</strong> <strong> .</strong> (reff.)
	</p>
<p><strong> ἀγνοῶν</strong> <strong> , not knowing</strong> , being blind to the truth, <strong> that</strong> … Grot., Thol., al. would render it ‘ <em> not considering</em> :’ but as De Wette remarks, it is a <em> wilful and guilty ignorance</em> , not merely an inconsiderateness, which is blamed in the question.
	</p>
<p><strong> ἄγει</strong> <strong> , is leading thee</strong> : this is its intent and legitimate course, which thy blindness will frustrate, ‘Malo <em> deducit</em> quam <em> invitat</em> ; quia illud plus quiddam significat. Neque tamen pro adigere accipio, sed pro manu ducere.’ Calvin.</p>
