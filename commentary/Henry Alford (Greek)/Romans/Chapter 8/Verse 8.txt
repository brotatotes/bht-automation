<p><strong> 8.</strong> ] <strong> but</strong> (takes up the other and inferential member of the proposition, answering to a suppressed μέν preceding, τὸ μὲν φρόνημα κ . τ . λ . [bringing in a further consequence: if the mind of the flesh cannot be subject to God’s law, then they who are in the flesh, and are led by that mind, cannot please God]. Calv., Beza, al. render it ‘ <em> therefore</em> ,’ and so E. V., ‘ <em> so then</em> ,’ erroneously) <strong> they who are in the flesh</strong> (as their <em> element of life and thought</em> : nearly κατὰ σάρκα ὄντες above, which however denotes the rule which they follow. In <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="2co+10:3">2 Corinthians 10:3</span> , the two are distinguished: ἐν σαρκὶ γὰρ περιπατοῦντες οὐ κατὰ σάρκα στρατευόμεθα ) <strong> cannot please God</strong> .
