<p><strong> 23.</strong> ] <strong> For I</strong> (see ch. <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="1co+7:28">1 Corinthians 7:28</span> ; Php 4:11 ) <strong> received from the Lord</strong> ( <em> by special revelation</em> , see <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="ga+1:12">Galatians 1:12</span> . Meyer attempts to deny that this revelation was made to Paul himself, on the strength of ἀπό meaning ‘ <em> indirect</em> ,’ παρά ‘direct’ reception from any one: but this distinction is fallacious: e.g. <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="1jo+1:5">1 John 1:5</span> , αὕτη ἐστὶν ἡ ἐπαγγελία ἣν ἀκηκόαμεν ἀπ ʼ αὐτοῦ . He supposes that it was made to Ananias or some other, and communicated to Paul. But the sole reason for this somewhat clumsy hypothesis is the supposed force of the preposition, which has no existence. If the Apostle had referred only to the Evangelic tradition or writings (?) he would not have used the first person <em> singular</em> , but παρελάβομεν . I may remark, that the similarity between this account of the Institution and that in Luke’s Gospel, is only what might be expected on the supposition of a special revelation made to Paul, of which that Evangelist, being Paul’s companion, in certain parts of his history availed himself) <strong> that which I also delivered</strong> (in my apostolic testimony) <strong> to you</strong> , (viz.) <strong> that the Lord Jesus, &amp;c.
	</strong></p>
