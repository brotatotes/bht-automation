<b> 23.</b>]<b> therefore</b> following up the axiom just laid down, by supposing a case =<b> if then....</b>—The first case put answers to the former half of ver. 22: the second, to the latter.—The supposition is this: that all the (Corinthian) church is assembled, and all its members speak with tongues (not in a<i> tumultuary</i> manner—that is not part of the present hypothesis, for if it were, it must apply equally to ver. 24, which it clearly cannot: but that all have the gift, and are<i> in turn</i> exercising it):—then<b> private persons</b>, ‘<i>plain</i> believers,’ persons unacquainted with the gift and its exercise, come in. It is obvious that the hypothesis of<i> all</i> being assembled, and<i> all</i> having the gift, must not be pressed. to infer that no such<i> private person</i> could be found: no one hypothesizes thus rigidly. If any will have it so, then, as Meyer, we may suppose the<i> private persons</i> to come<i> from another congregation:</i> but the whole difficulty seems to me mere trifling. The<i> private person</i> plainly cannot be an<i> unbeliever,</i> for<i> his</i> case is separately mentioned. Such<b> plain men</b>, or perhaps a company of unbelievers, have come in:—they have no understanding of what is going on: the ‘<i>tongues</i>’ sound to them an unmeaning jargon; and they come to the conclusion, ‘These men are mad;’ just as men<i> did infer</i>, on the day of Pentecost, that the speakers were<i> drunken</i>.
