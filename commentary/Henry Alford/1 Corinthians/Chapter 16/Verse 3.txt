<p><strong> 3</strong> .] “Vide quomodo vir tantus nullam suspicioni rimam aperire voluerit.” Grot.
	</p>
<p><strong> δι</strong> <strong> ʼ </strong> <strong> ἐπιστολῶν</strong> cannot belong to δοκιμάσητε (as Beza, Calv., Wetst., E. V., for what need of letters <em> from them</em> ὅταν παραγένωμαι , or before his coming, if the person recommended were <em> not to be sent off before his arrival</em> ?), but is emphatically prefixed, as the safe and proper way of giving credentials to those <em> sent</em> ; τούτους πέμψω , the alternative which follows, of himself accompanying them, being already in the mind of the Apostle.
	</p>
<p><strong> ἐπιστολῶν</strong> , plur., not of the category merely, meaning <em> one letter</em> , but meaning, either that each should have his letter of credentials, or more probably, that Paul would give them letters to several persons in Jerusalem.
	</p>
<p>Meyer well remarks: “Hence we see how common in Paul’s practice was the writing of Epistles. Who knows how many private letters of his, not addressed to churches have been lost? The only letter of the kind which remains to us (except the Pastoral Epistles), viz. that to Philemon, owes its preservation perhaps to the mere circumstance, that it is at the same time addressed <em> to the church in the house</em> of Philemon. See <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="1co+16:2">1 Corinthians 16:2</span> .”
	</p>
