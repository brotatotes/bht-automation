<b> 18.] Flee</b> might be followed by<i> therefore</i>, but is more forcible in this disconnected form.<br><br><b> Every sin...]</b> The assertion, which has surprised many of the Commentators, is nevertheless<i> strictly true</i>. Drunkenness and gluttony, e.g. are sins done<i> in</i> and<i> by</i> the body, and are sins<i> by abuse</i> of the body. but they are still<b> outside the body</b>,—introduced<i> from without</i>, sinful<i> not</i> in their<i> act</i>, but in their<i> effect</i>, which effect it is each man’s duty to foresee and avoid. But fornication is the<i> alienating that body which is the Lord’s, and making it a harlot’s body</i>—it is sin against a<i> man’s own body</i>, in its very nature,—against<i> the verity and nature</i> of his body; not an<i> effect on</i> the body from participation of things without, but a<i> contradiction of the truth</i> of the body, wrought<i> within itself</i>.
