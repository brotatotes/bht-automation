<b> 5.]</b><i> assertion of the other alternative,</i> not put as before, but, as usual, carried further and differently expressed:<i> an opposition with an accession</i>, as Grotins calls it):<b>—but whoso keepeth His word</b> (synonymous with<i> His commandments</i>, considered as a whole: on the mode of expression, see above),<b> of a truth in him is the love of God perfected</b> (why should this transition be made from knowledge to love? Love presupposes knowledge, and is a further step in the same<i> fellowship</i> with God: not indeed that the former step is passed through and done with, but that true knowledge and love increase together, and the former is the measure of the latter, just as keeping God’s commandments is the test and measure of true knowledge of Him. And thus in the final and perfect ideal, the two are coincident: the perfect observation of His commandments is the perfection of love to Him. It is manifest, from what has been said, that this<b> love of God</b> must be our love towards God, not His love towards us).<b> In this</b> (in the fact of our progress towards this ideal state of perfection of obedience and therefore of love:—thus assured that the germ of the state is in us and unfolding)<b> we know that we are in Him</b> (this completes the logical period which began in ver. 3, by reasserting that verse, carrying however that assertion yet deeper, by substituting “<i>we are in Him</i>” for “<i>we have the knowledge of Him</i>.” This “being in Him” is in fact the Christian life in its central depth of<i> fellowship</i>
