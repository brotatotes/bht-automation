<p><strong> 10</strong> .] <strong> Concerning which salvation</strong> (its time especially, as explained below, but its manner and issue also) <strong> sought earnestly and examined earnestly</strong> (the prep. <strong> ἐξ</strong> both times strengthens the verb) <strong> prophets</strong> ( <strong> προφῆται</strong> <strong> </strong> <strong> ἄγγελοι</strong> , both times generic, to exalt the greatness of the σωτηρία . The <strong> οἱ περὶ</strong> <strong> …</strong> limits the assertion and defines the Prophets intended. Some take προφῆται as = οἱ προφ ., as in ch. <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="1pe+5:1">1 Peter 5:1</span> (rec.), πρεσβυτέρους τοὺς ἐν ὑμῖν : but placed as the word is here parallel with ἄγγελοι , the other way seems better. So Bengel, “Articulus hic prætermissus grandem facit orationem, nam auditorem a determinata individuorum consideratione ad ipsum genus spectandum traducit: sic, <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="1pe+1:12">1 Peter 1:12</span> , angeli”), <strong> they who prophesied concerning the grace that was</strong> (destined) <strong> for you</strong> (we cannot fill up <strong> τῆς εἰς</strong> in English without defining the tense of the verb substantive, which here may be twofold: as above, or ‘that hath come unto you.’ The specification of <strong> ὑμᾶς</strong> makes this latter more probable: the whole cast of the sentence, the former. For we are considering what the <em> Prophets</em> felt, and looking forward with them: and the ὑμᾶς is not inconsistent with this. In matter of fact, in God’s purposes it was <em> you, for whom</em> the salvation was destined, though you as individuals were not in their view),</p>
