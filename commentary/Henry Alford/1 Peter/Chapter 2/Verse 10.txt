<p><strong> 10</strong> .] <strong> who</strong> (contrast between their former and present states) <strong> were once no people</strong> (the Apostle is again citing, or rather clothing that which he has to write in, O. T. words. In Hos 2:23 A, we read ἐλεήσω τὴν οὐκ ἠλεημένην , καὶ ἐρῶ τῷ οὐ λαῷ μου λαός μου εἶ σύ ), <strong> but</strong> ( <strong> are</strong> ) <strong> now the people of God</strong> (these words, as Wies. maintains, apply most properly to Gentile Christians, although spoken in the prophecy of Jews, St. Paul thus uses them, <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="ro+9:25">Romans 9:25</span> ; and it is not impossible that that passage may have been in St. Peter’s mind), <strong> who were unpitied</strong> (of God: the <strong> οὑκ</strong> here and above, not merely negatives, but contraries: not “who had not obtained mercy, but now have obtained mercy,” as E. V., indicating a mere change of time in order of progress, but who were unpitied, objects of aversion and wrath), <strong> but now compassionated</strong> (the aor. part. has a fine and delicate force which cannot be given in a version: q. d. who were men who (have received no pity), but now men who (received pity), viz. when God called you by Christ).</p>
