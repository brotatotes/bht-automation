<p><strong> 13</strong> .] <strong> Be subjected</strong> (aor. pass. with a quasimiddle sense, given by the aorist coupled with the fact of the command: be in a condition of having been subjected: on the medial signification of aorists passive in N. T. see on ch. 1Pe 5:6 ) <strong> to every human institution</strong> (“quod creat et condit homo,” Luth. Such, and not “ <em> every human creature</em> ,” as Syr., Erasm., Estius, Pott, De Wette, is the meaning. The latter would stultify what follows: for it is not to the king as a man, but to the king as a human institution, that we are to be subject. And so Œc., κτίσιν ἀνθρωπίνην τὰς ἀρχὰς λέγει τὰς χειροτονητὰς ὑπὸ τῶν βασιλέων , ἢ καὶ αὐτοὺς τοὺς βασιλεῖς , καθότι καὶ αὐτοὶ ὑπὸ ἀνθρώπων ἐτάχθησαν ἤτοι ἐτέθησαν . It is no objection to this, that all powers are ordained of God: for that consideration does not come into notice in these words, but in those which follow, διὰ τ . κύριον . <em> Here</em> , it is the <em> lower side</em> of such institutions, the fact of their being ordained and upheld by men, that is brought into sight) <strong> for the Lord’s sake</strong> (i. e. Christ’s: <strong> κύριος</strong> with St. Peter, except in O. T. citations, is always our Lord. And here there is additional reason, for that He, the Head of all principality and power, is yet in us his members subject to them, until the day when all shall be put under His feet): <strong> whether to king</strong> (general, but, from the nature of the case as regarded those to whom the Epistle is addressed, here the Roman Emperor) <strong> as supereminent</strong> (“qui ita imperat, ut ab aliis hominibus ipsi non imperetur,” Gerh.),</p>
