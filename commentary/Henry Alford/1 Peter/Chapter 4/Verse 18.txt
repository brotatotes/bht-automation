<p><strong> 18</strong> .] <strong> and</strong> (the question of the last verse is again repeated under a well-known form, taken from the O. T., which however casts solemn light on both members of the interrogation: explaining what is meant by judgment on God’s people and also by the end of the disobedient. The citation is verbatim from the LXX, except that μέν is omitted between ὁ and δίκαιος . The LXX departs from the Heb. text, which is as the E. V., “Behold the righteous shall be recompensed in the earth: much more the wicked and the sinner”) <strong> if the righteous is</strong> ( <em> is being</em> , see reff.: or rather perhaps the pres., of that which is to be) <strong> with difficulty saved</strong> (on account of the sharpness of the trial, and his own weakness. “Hoc μόλις temperatur 2Pe 1:11 prolixe.” Bengel. Cf. <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="re+5:4-5">Revelation 5:4-5</span> . The <strong> μόλις</strong> does not induce any <em> doubt</em> as to the issue, only <em> wonder</em> : if we be δίκαιοι by faith in Christ, our salvation, however difficult and apparently impossible, is as certain as Christ’s own triumph), <strong> the ungodly</strong> ( <strong> ἀσεβής</strong> , ‘impius,’ the man who in his innermost heart cares not for God and turns not to Him) <strong> and sinner</strong> (he that is devoted to sin. The absence of a second article, and the singular verb, both shew, that the same person is meant by both), <strong> where shall he appear</strong> (so in <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="ps+1:5">Psalms 1:5</span> ; where shall he stand and find an abiding place in the judgment?) <strong> ?</strong></p>
