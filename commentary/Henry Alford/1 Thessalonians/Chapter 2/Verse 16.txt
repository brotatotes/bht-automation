<p><strong> 16</strong> . <strong> εἰς τό</strong> ] not of the result merely, ‘ <em> so that</em> ,’ but of the <em> intention</em> , not of the Jews themselves, but of their course of conduct, viewed as having an intent in the divine purposes: as so often in St. Paul.
	</p>
<p><strong> ἀναπλ</strong> <strong> .</strong> ] to bring up the measure of their sins to the prescribed point.
	</p>
<p><strong> πάντοτε</strong> ] ταῦτα δὲ καὶ πάλαι ἐπὶ τῶν προφητῶν κ . νῦν ἐπὶ τοῦ χριστοῦ κ . ἐφ ʼ ἡμῶν ἔπραξαν , ἵνα πάντοτε ἀναπληρωθῶσιν αἱ ἁμαρτίαι αὐτῶν , Œcum. The idea is, not of a new measure having to be filled πάντοτε , but of their being πάντοτε employed in filling up <em> the measure</em> .
	</p>
<p><strong> But</strong> (this their opposition to God and men shall not avail them: for) <strong> the</strong> (predestined, or predicted, or merited) <strong> wrath</strong> (of God) <strong> came upon them</strong> (he looks <em> back</em> on the fact in the divine counsels as a thing in past time, q. d. ‘was appointed to come:’ not ‘has come.’ No sense of <em> anticipation</em> need be sought in ἔφθασεν in later Greek, except when it governs an accusative of the person, as ch. <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="1th+4:15">1 Thessalonians 4:15</span> ; see reff.) <strong> to the utmost</strong> (to the end of it, i.e. the wrath: so that it shall exhaust all its force on them: not ‘at last’ Wahl, al.: nor to be taken with ἡ ὀργή , the wrath which shall endure to the end ( ἡ εἰς τ .?), as Thl., Œc., al.: nor to be referred to the Jews, ‘so as to make an end of them,’ De W.).</p>
