<p><strong> 1.</strong> ] <strong> δέ</strong> marks the transition to a new subject, and <strong> αὐτός</strong> points on to the personal characteristics mentioned below, ‘Ego idem Paulus, qui …;’ the words <strong> ἐγὼ Παῦλος</strong> setting his Apostolic dignity in contrast with the depreciation which follows. Sometimes however we have αὐτός used, where the only object seems to be to <em> bring out the personality more strongly</em> : so <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="1th+3:11">1 Thessalonians 3:11</span> ; 1Th 4:16 ; <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="1th+5:23">1 Thessalonians 5:23</span> ; <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="2th+2:16">2 Thessalonians 2:16</span> ; <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="2th+3:16">2 Thessalonians 3:16</span> . See also <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="ro+7:25">Romans 7:25</span> ; and ch. <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="2co+12:13">2 Corinthians 12:13</span> : and such may be the case here: but the ὅς rather favours the former interpretation.
