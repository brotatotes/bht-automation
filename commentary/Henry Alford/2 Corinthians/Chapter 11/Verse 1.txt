<p><strong> 1.</strong> ] <strong> ἀνείχεσθε</strong> is the Hellenistic form, ἠνείχ . the Attic, not ‘utinam <em> tolerassetis</em> ,’ as Calv., al.: the imperfect is put after εἴθε , αἰ , ὄφελον , &amp;c., ‘ubi optamus eam rerum conditionem, quam non esse sentimus:’ Klotz ad Devar. p. 516, cited by Meyer.
	</p>
<p><strong> μου</strong> and <strong> ἀφροσύνης</strong> are not both genitives after μικρόν τι , as Meyer: nor is it so in the passage quoted by him, <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="job+6:26">Job 6:26</span> , LXX: οὐδὲ γὰρ ὑμῶν φθέγμα ῥήματος ( φθέγματος ῥήματος ὑμῶν , Α ) ἀνέξομαι . In both cases the personal pronoun is <em> governed by the verb</em> , as indeed here in ἀνέχεσθέ μου immediately following and μικρόν τι ἀφροσύνης is the accusative of remote reference, as in the double accus. construction.
	</p>
<p><strong> ἀλλὰ κ</strong> .] <strong> But</strong> (why need I request this? for ( <strong> you really</strong> (see note, ch. 2Co 5:3 ) <strong> do bear with me</strong> . The indicative is much better than the imperative rendering (as Vulg., Beza, Calvin, Grot., Estius, Bengel, al.), which, after ὄφελον ἀνείχ ., is very flat, and gives no account of the καί . He says it, to shew them that he does not express the wish as supposing them void of tolerance for his weakness, but as having <em> experienced some</em> at their hands, and now <em> requiring more</em> .</p>
