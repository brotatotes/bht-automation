<p><strong> 12.</strong> ] <em> He shews them that to bring out this zeal in them was the real motive of his writing to them, and no private considerations.
	</em></p>
<p><strong> ἄρα</strong> , <strong> accordingly</strong> , ‘in accordance with the result just mentioned.’
	</p>
<p><strong> εἰ καὶ ἔγραψα ὑμ</strong> . is parallel with εἰ καὶ ἐλύπησα ὑμᾶς , <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="2co+7:8">2 Corinthians 7:8</span> , <strong> though</strong> (i.e. <em> assumed that</em> ) <strong> I wrote</strong> (severely) <strong> to you.
	</strong></p>
<p>The <strong> ἀδικηθείς</strong> would be the <em> father</em> of the incestuous person, who γυναῖκα τοῦ πατρὸς εἶχεν , <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="1co+5:1">1 Corinthians 5:1</span> .
	</p>
<p>Theodoret imagines it to mean the <em> stepmother,</em> who was the adulteress; and thinks that the <em> father</em> was dead. But there is no ground for this in <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="1co+5:0">1 Corinthians 5:0</span> , and the <em> masculine</em> participle, though not decisive against it, is at least more naturally explained on the other view. Others (as Wolf, Bleek, al.) suppose <em> Paul himself</em> to be meant, which however would be in direct contradiction to ch. <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="2co+2:5">2 Corinthians 2:5</span> ; Bengel, al., the <em> Corinthians,</em> ‘singularis pro plurali, per euphemiam,’ which is forced: Theophyl., al., both the persons concerned ( ἀμφότεροι γὰρ ἀλλήλους ἠδίκησαν ): and Neander, al., take τοῦ ἀδικηθέντος as = τοῦ ἀδικήματος , <em> ‘the fault committed:</em> ’ which however would not be true, for the Apostle certainly <em> did</em> write <em> on account of the committal</em> of the fault.
	</p>
<p>It would be easy for any of the Apostle’s adversaries to maintain that the reproof had been administered from private and interested motives.
	</p>
<p><strong> ἀλλ</strong> <strong> ʼ </strong> <strong> ἕνεκεν</strong> …] <em> But he wrote, in order to bring out their zeal on his behalf</em> (i.e. to obey his command), <em> and make it manifest to themselves in God’s sight.</em> The other reading, ἡμῶν τὴν ὑπὲρ ὑμῶν , has been an alteration owing to not understanding τ . σπουδ . ὑμ . τ . ὑπ . ἡμ ., and is <em> inconsistent with the fact:</em> it was not to exhibit to them <em> his</em> zeal for them that he wrote, but to make manifest to ( πρός <em> ‘among,’ ‘chez’</em> them, to bring out among them, <em> their zeal to regard and obey him.</em></p>
