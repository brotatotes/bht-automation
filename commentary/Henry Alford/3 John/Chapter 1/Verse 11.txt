<p><strong> 11</strong> .] Upon occasion of the hostility just mentioned, St. John <em> exhorts Caius to imitate not the evil but the good</em> , probably as shewn in the praises of Demetrius which follow. <strong> Beloved, imitate not evil</strong> ( <strong> τὸ κακόν</strong> , abstract), <strong> but good</strong> (abstract also). <strong> He that doeth good, is from God</strong> (is born of God, and has his mission and power from Him: as so often in the first Epistle): <strong> he that doeth evil, hath not seen God</strong> (so in ref., πᾶς ὁ ἁμαρτάνων οὐχ ἑώρακεν αὐτόν , where see note. And yet this expression is called by Lücke and De Wette “unjohanneisch,” and <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="1jo+4:20">1 John 4:20</span> , adduced to prove it, where the word ἑώρακεν is used in its literal physical sense).</p>
