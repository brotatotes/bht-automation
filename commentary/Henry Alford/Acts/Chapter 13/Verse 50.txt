<p><strong> 50. </strong> <strong> τὰς σεβ</strong> <strong> . </strong> <strong> γυν</strong> <strong> .</strong> ] Women had a strong religious influence both for and against Christianity: see for the former ch. <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="ac+16:14">Acts 16:14</span> ; <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="ac+17:4">Acts 17:4</span> ; Php 4:3 ; <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="1co+7:16">1 Corinthians 7:16</span> ; for the latter, compare Josephus’s statement (B. J. ii. 20.2), that the majority of the wives of the Damascenes were proselytes, with ch. <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="ac+9:22-25">Acts 9:22-25</span> . Strabo (vii. 3: C. and H. i. p. 219) says, ἅπαντες τῆς δεισιδαιμονίας ἀρχηγοὺς οἴονται τὰς γυναῖκας αὗται δὲ καὶ τοὺς ἄνδρας προκαλοῦνται πρὸς τὰς ἐπὶ πλέον θεραπείας τῶν θεῶν καὶ ἑορτὰς καὶ ποτνιασμούς .
	</p>
<p>These were proselytes of the gate, or at least inclined to Judaism.
	</p>
<p><strong> ἐξέβαλον</strong> ] Though the πρῶτοι τῆς πόλεως , at the instigation, probably, of their wives, were concerned, this seems to have been no <em> legal</em> expulsion: for we find them revisiting Antioch on their return, ch. <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="ac+14:21">Acts 14:21</span> ; but only a compulsory retirement for peace, and their own safety’s sake.</p>
