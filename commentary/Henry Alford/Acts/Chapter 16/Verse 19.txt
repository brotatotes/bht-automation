<p><strong> 19.</strong> ] Her masters (a partnership of persons, not plur. for sing.
	</p>
<p>They may have been the hæredes of some one to whom she had belonged) perceived that the hope of their gain had gone out ( <em> with the dœmon</em> ).
	</p>
<p><strong> ἐπιλ</strong> <strong> .… </strong> <strong> εἵλκ</strong> <strong> .</strong> gives the idea of force having been used. So we have ‘obtorto collo ad prætorem trahor,’ Plaut. Pœn. iii. 5. 45.
	</p>
<p>Paul and Silas only are apprehended as having been the principal persons in the company. When De Wette says that, if Luke here were the narrator, he must say something of Timotheus, as he mentions him ch. <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="ac+17:14">Acts 17:14</span> , <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="ac+18:5">Acts 18:5</span> , and yet holds (on Act 16:10 ) that Timotheus himself is the narrator, he forgets that the same reasoning will apply to <em> him also</em> , if it applies at all, which I much doubt. When two persons of a company are described as being apprehended, we do not need an express assertion to assure us that the rest were not.
	</p>
