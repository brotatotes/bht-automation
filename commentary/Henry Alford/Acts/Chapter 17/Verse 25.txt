As the assertion of Creation contradicted the Epicurean<i> error</i>, so this laid hold of that portion of truth, which, however disguised, that school had apprehended: viz. that the Deity does not stand in need of us, nor can gain aught from us. There is a verse in 2 Macc. xiv. 35, remarkable, as compared with the thoughts and words of Paul here: “<i>Thou, O Lord of all things, who hast need of nothing, wast pleased that the temple of thine habitation should be among us.</i>’<br><br><b> life, and breath, and all things</b>] He is the<i> Preserver</i>, as well as the Creator, of all; and all things come to us<i> from Him</i>. Compare, on<b> all things</b>, David’s words, 1 Chron. xxix. 14, “<i>Thine are all things, and of Thine own have we given Thee.</i>”
