<p><strong> 10.</strong> ] From the use of θεωρῶ here, and from the saying itself, it seems clear to me that Paul was not uttering <em> at present</em> any prophetic intimation, but simply his own sound judgment on the difficult question at issue. It is otherwise at <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="ac+27:22-24">Acts 27:22-24</span> . As Smith remarks, “The event justified St. Paul’s advice. At the same time it may be observed, that a bay, open to nearly one half the compass, could not have been a good winter harbour.” (p. 47.)
	</p>
<p><strong> μετὰ ὕβρεως</strong> is interpreted by Meyer as subjective ‘ <em> accompanied with presumption on our part</em> :’ but not to mention that this would be a very unusual sense, <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="ac+27:21">Acts 27:21</span> , κερδῆσαι τὴν ὕβριν ταύτ . κ . τ . ζημίαν , is decisive (De W.) against it.
	</p>
<p><strong> ὅτι</strong> <strong> … </strong> <strong> μέλλειν</strong> ] A mixing of two constructions, see Winer, edn. 6, § 44. 8, remark 2. This is most flagrant in later writers, as Pausanias and Arrian, see Bernhardy, Syntax, p. 369; but is also found earlier, e.g. Plato, Charm., p. 165: οὐκ ἂν αἰσχυνθείην ὅτι μὴ οὐχὶ ὀρθῶς φάναι εἰρηκέναι . Isæus, περὶ τοῦ φιλοκτ . κληρ . p. 57: ἐπειδὴ δὲ προσδιαμεμαρτύρηκεν ὡς υἱὸν εἶναι γνήσιον Εὐκτήμονος τοῦτον … See other references in Winer, 1. c.</p>
