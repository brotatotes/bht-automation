<p><strong> 4.</strong> ] The natives, who were sure to know, here positively declared it to have been a <em> venomous</em> serpent. I make these remarks to guard against the disingenuous shifts of rationalists and semi-rationalists, who will have us believe either that the viper <em> did not bite</em> , or that if it did, it was <em> not venomous</em> .

<p>The idea of his being a murderer is not to be accounted for (as Elsner, Wolf, Kuin.) by the <em> member</em> which was bitten (for this would fit any crime which the hand could commit), nor by supposing (Heinsius) the bite of a serpent to have been the Maltese punishment for murder; it is accounted for by the obviousness of the crime as belonging to the most notorious delinquents, and the aptness of the assumed punishment, death for death.
	</p>
<p><strong> ἡ δίκη</strong> ] <strong> Justice</strong> , or <em> Nemesis</em> . What the Phœnician islanders called her, does not appear; but the <em> idea</em> is common to all religions.</p>
