<p><strong> 18. </strong> <strong> ἰδών</strong> ] Its effects were therefore <em> visible</em> (see above), and consequently the effect of the laying on of the Apostles’ hands was not the <em> inward</em> but the <em> outward miraculous</em> gifts of the Spirit.
	</p>
<p><strong> προσήν</strong> <strong> . </strong> <strong> αὐτ</strong> <strong> . </strong> <strong> χρήματα</strong> ] De W. excellently remarks, ‘He regarded the capability of imparting the Holy Spirit, <em> rightly</em> , as <em> something conferred</em> , as a <em> derived power</em> (see ref. Matt.), but <em> wrongly</em> , as one to be obtained by an <em> external method</em> , without an <em> inward disposition</em> : and, since in external commerce every thing may be had for gold, <em> he wanted to buy it</em> . This is the essence of the sin of <em> Simony</em> , which is intimately connected with unbelief in the power and signification of the Spirit, and with materialism.’
	</p>
<p>Clearly, from the narrative, Simon himself <em> did not receive the Spirit by the laying on of hands</em> . His nefarious attempt to treat with the Apostles was <em> before he himself had been presented to them for this purpose</em> .</p>
