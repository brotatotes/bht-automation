<p><strong> 33</strong> .] <strong> Nevertheless</strong> (not to go further into the mystical bearings of the subject so Meyer) <strong> you also</strong> (as well as Christ) <strong> every one</strong> (see reff. and <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="1co+14:27">1 Corinthians 14:27</span> ; <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="ac+15:21">Acts 15:21</span> ; Heb 9:25 ), <strong> let each</strong> (the construction is changed and the verb put into concord with <strong> ἕκαστος</strong> instead of <strong> ὑμεῖς</strong> : so Plato, Gorg. p. 503, ὥς περ κ . οἱ ἄλλοι πάντες δημιουργοὶ βλέποντες πρὸς τὸ ἑκάστου ἔργον ἕκαστος οὐκ εἰκῆ ἐκλεγόμενος προσφέρει κ . τ . λ .; Rep. p. 346, αἱ ἄλλαι πᾶσαι ( τέχναι ) οὕτω τὸ αὑτῆς ἑκάστη ἔργον ἐργάζεται , κ . τ . λ . Cic. de Off. i. 41, ‘poetæ suum quisque opus a vulgo considerari vult’) <strong> so love his own wife as himself, and the wife</strong> (best taken as a nominative absolute, as Mey. Otherwise we should rather expect ἵνα δὲ ἡ γυνὴ κ . τ . λ . It is no objection to this (Eadie) that in the resolution of the idiom a verb must be supplied: but the wife, for her part, ‘ <em> I order</em> ,’ or, ‘ <em> let her see</em> ,’ cf. note on 2Co 8:7 ), <strong> that she fear</strong> ( ὡς πρέπει γυναῖκα φοβεῖσθαι , μὴ δουλοπρεπῶς , Œc.) <strong> her husband</strong> .</p>
