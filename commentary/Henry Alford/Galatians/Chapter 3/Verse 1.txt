<b> CH. III. 1—V. 12.]</b> SECOND, OR POLEMICAL PART OF THE EPISTLE.<br><br><b> 1.]</b> The Apostle exclaims indignantly, moved by the fervour and truth of his rebuke of Peter, against the folly of the Galatians, for suffering themselves to be bewitched out of their former vivid apprehension of Christ’s work and Person.<br><br><b> foolish</b> must not, with Jerome, be taken as an allusion to any supposed national stupidity of the Galatians (for they appear to have been ‘very apt to learn, and of ready wit’): it merely springs out of the occasion.<br><br><b> you</b> has the emphasis— ‘YOU,<b> to whom</b>,’&c.<br><br><b> was evidently set forth]</b> We must understand this to refer to the time when he preached Christ among them, which he represents as a previous description in writing of Christ, in their hearts and before their eyes.<br><br><b> crucified</b>, as expressing the whole mystery of redemption by grace, and of freedom from legal obligation. “It has an echo of the being<i> crucified with Christ,</i> in ch. ii. 20.” Jowett.<b></b><br><br><b> 14] to the end that</b> (the intent of<i> Christ’s becoming a curse</i>)<b> the blessing of Abraham</b> (promised to Abraham: i.e. justification by faith; ver. 9)<b> might be</b> (come)<b> upon the Gentiles</b> (or, nations: but here not<i> all</i> nations, but strictly the Gentiles: see above on ver. 13)<b> in</b> (in and by,<i> conditional element</i>)<b> Christ Jesus; that we</b> (not emphatic: no longer the Jews, but all Christians)<b> might receive</b> (in full, as fulfilled)<b> through the</b> (or, but not so usually,<i> our</i>: perhaps best expressed in English simply by “<i>through faith</i>”)<b> faith the promise of the Spirit</b> (viz. that made Joel ii. 28.—See Acts ii. 17, 33; Luke xxiv. 49,—THE PROMISE of the new covenant). This ‘receiving the promise of the Spirit’ distinctly refers back to ver. 2, where he asked them whether they received the Spirit by the works of the law, or by the hearing of faith? “Here is a pause, at which the indignant feeling of the Apostle softens, and he begins the new train of thought which follows with words of wilder character, and proceeds more quietly with his argument.” Windischmann.
