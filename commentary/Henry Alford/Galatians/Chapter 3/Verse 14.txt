<b> 14] to the end that</b> (the intent of<i> Christ’s becoming a curse</i>)<b> the blessing of Abraham</b> (promised to Abraham: i.e. justification by faith; ver. 9)<b> might be</b> (come)<b> upon the Gentiles</b> (or, nations: but here not<i> all</i> nations, but strictly the Gentiles: see above on ver. 13)<b> in</b> (in and by,<i> conditional element</i>)<b> Christ Jesus; that we</b> (not emphatic: no longer the Jews, but all Christians)<b> might receive</b> (in full, as fulfilled)<b> through the</b> (or, but not so usually,<i> our</i>: perhaps best expressed in English simply by “<i>through faith</i>”)<b> faith the promise of the Spirit</b> (viz. that made Joel ii. 28.—See Acts ii. 17, 33; Luke xxiv. 49,—THE PROMISE of the new covenant). This ‘receiving the promise of the Spirit’ distinctly refers back to ver. 2, where he asked them whether they received the Spirit by the works of the law, or by the hearing of faith? “Here is a pause, at which the indignant feeling of the Apostle softens, and he begins the new train of thought which follows with words of wilder character, and proceeds more quietly with his argument.” Windischmann.
