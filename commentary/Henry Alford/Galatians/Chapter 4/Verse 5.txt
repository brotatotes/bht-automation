Christ, being born under the law, a Jewish child, subject to its ordinances,—by His perfect fulfilment of it, and by enduring, as the Head and in the root of our nature, its curse on the tree, bought off (from its curse and power, but see on ch. iii. 18) those who were under the law: and if them, then the rest of mankind, whose nature He had upon Him. Thus in buying off<i> those under the law</i>, He effected that<b> we</b>, all men,<b> should receive</b> (not<i> ‘recover</i>,’ as Augustine and others: there is no allusion to the innocence which we lost in Adam, nor was redemption by Christ in any sense a<i> recovery</i> of the state before the fall, but a far more glorious thing, the bestowal of an adoption which Adam never had)<b> the adoption</b> (the place, and privileges)<b> of sons.</b>
