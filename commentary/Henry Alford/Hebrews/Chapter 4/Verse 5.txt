<b> 5.] And in this</b> (place: our present passage)<b> again</b> (i. e.<i> on the other hand:</i> a citation which shall qualify and explain that other, making it impossible that men should have already entered into it),<b> If they shall enter into my rest</b> (these words are to be taken exactly as before, in a strong negative sense. The point raised is, that in the days of Moses, nay, long after, of David, men had not yet, in the full sense at least, entered into that rest, because it was spoken of as yet<i> future</i>: it being of no import to the present argument, whether that, future is of an affirmative or negative proposition: the negative denunciation in fact implying in itself the fact, that<i> some would</i> enter therein).
