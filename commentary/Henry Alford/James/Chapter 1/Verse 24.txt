<p><strong> 24</strong> .] <strong> for</strong> (this seems to stamp the example as a general one, applying to all, not merely taking some possible man who may do this: see above) <strong> he contemplated himself</strong> (on the aorr. see above, Jam 1:11 ), <strong> and has departed</strong> (the perfect in the midst of aorr. is to be noted. We might have had all aorr.: but seeing that the <em> departing</em> begins a permanent state of absence from the mirror, that is chosen to be designated by a perfect. The <em> forgetting</em> is also a permanent state; but the Apostle rather chooses in this case to bring out the act itself, as one immediately ( εὐθύς ) and suddenly taking place. For similar conjunctions of perfects with aorr., cf. <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="lu+4:18">Luke 4:18</span> ; <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="mr+15:44">Mark 15:44</span> ; Heb 2:14 and note: <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="1co+15:4">1 Corinthians 15:4</span> ; and Winer, § 40. 4), <strong> and immediately forgot of what appearance he was</strong> (viz. in the mirror. It is to be observed, that the <strong> κατανοεῖν</strong> answers to the hearing of the word: the <strong> ἀπεληλυθέναι</strong> to the relaxing the attention after hearing letting the mind go elsewhere, and the interest of the thing heard pass away: and then the forgetfulness in both cases follows. In the next verse we pass to one who looks and does not depart).</p>
