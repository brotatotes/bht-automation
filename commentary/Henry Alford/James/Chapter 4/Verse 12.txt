<p><strong> 12</strong> .] <strong> One</strong> (God) <strong> is the lawgiver and judge</strong> (unites these two offices in His own person: the latter of them depending on the former), <strong> He who is able to save and destroy</strong> (this second clause, <strong> ὁ δυνάμ</strong> <strong> .</strong> κ . τ . λ ., is an epexegesis of <strong> εἷς</strong> , and belongs closely to the subject, not to the predicative part of the sentence, as De Wette gives it, <em> Einer ist der Gesessgeber und Richter, der da vermag zu retten und zu verderben</em> . <strong> ὁ δυνάμενος</strong> , because He alone has the power to carry out His judgment when pronounced: “Nostrum non est judicare, præsertim cum exequi non possimus,” Bengel. On <strong> σῶσαι</strong> , see on ch. Jam 1:21 and <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="jas+2:14">James 2:14</span> , as relating to ultimate salvation: and on <strong> κ</strong> <strong> . </strong> <strong> ἀπολέσαι</strong> , ref. Matt., to which this is the key text, fixing the reference there to God, and not to God’s Enemy): <strong> but thou, who art thou</strong> (thou, feeble man, who hast no such power, and who art not the lawgiver) <strong> that judgest thy neighbour</strong> (see ref. Rom., the influence of which on our readings here it is, as usual in such cases, very difficult to estimate)?</p>
