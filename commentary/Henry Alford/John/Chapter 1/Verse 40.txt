<p><strong> 40.</strong> ] They ask <strong> ποῦ μ</strong> <strong> .,</strong> βουλόμενοι καταμόνας ἐντυχεῖν αὐτῷ καὶ μεθ ʼ ἡσυχίας . Euthym [35] They enquire after <em> His place of lodging for the night</em> , intending to visit Him there; or perhaps He was then apparently going thither, as it was late in the day. But He furthers their wish by inviting them to follow, and they will see.
	</p>
<p style="margin-left:3em;"> [35] Euthymius Zigabenus, 1116
	</p>
<p><strong> ὡς δεκάτη</strong> ] i.e. 4 P.M., according to the Jewish reckoning; not, as some have thought, 10 A.M., according to that of the Romans. Our Evangelist appears always to reckon according to the Jewish method, see ch. <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="joh+4:6">John 4:6</span> ; <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="joh+4:52">John 4:52</span> ; <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="joh+19:14">John 19:14</span> , and notes, but especially ch. <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="joh+11:9">John 11:9</span> . And as Lücke remarks (i. 446), even among the Romans, the division of the day into twelve equal hours was, though not the <em> civil</em> , the popular way of computing time. So Persius, Sat. iii. 3: “Stertimus … quinta dum linea tangitur umbra.”
	</p>
<p>They remained with Him <em> the rest of that day</em> , which would be four or five hours, and need not strictly be limited by sunset.</p>
