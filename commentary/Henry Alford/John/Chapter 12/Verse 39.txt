<p><strong> 39.</strong> ] <strong> διὰ τοῦτο</strong> refers to the last verse, and <strong> ὅτι</strong> sets forth the reason more in detail: see ch. <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="joh+5:16">John 5:16</span> : <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="1jo+3:1">1 John 3:1</span> ; <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="mt+24:44">Matthew 24:44</span> . The common interpretation (Theophyl., Vulg., Lampe, Tholuck, Olsh., alli [171] .), by which διὰ τοῦτο is referred forward to ὅτι , would require some particle, καί , or δέ , to denote a transition to the fresh subject. <p><strong> οὐκ ἠδύν</strong> <strong> .</strong> ] <strong> could not</strong> i.e. it was otherwise ordained in the divine counsels. No attempt to escape this meaning (as “ <em> nolebant</em> ,” Chr [173] , Thl. &amp;c.) will agree with the prophecy cited <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="joh+12:40">John 12:40</span> . But the <em> inability</em> , as thus stated, is coincident with the fullest freedom of the human will: compare οὐ θέλετε , ch. <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="joh+5:40">John 5:40</span> .
