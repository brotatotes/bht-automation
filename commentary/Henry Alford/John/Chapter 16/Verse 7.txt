<b> 7.</b>]<b> Nevertheless</b> refers to the last clause,—notwithstanding that no one of you asks me, I<i> Myself</i> will tell you the real state of the case.<br><br><b> It is expedient for you</b>, implies that the dispensation of the Spirit is a more blessed manifestation of God than was even the bodily presence of the risen Saviour.<br><br> Every rendering of this verse ought to keep the distinction between the two verbs which our Lord uses in speaking of His departure; which is not accurately done in the A.V.<b> Depart</b> and go seem to be the best words: the first expressing merely the<i> leaving them</i>, the second, the<i> going up</i> to the Father.<br><br> The I before<b> depart</b> is again emphatic: ‘that I, for my part, should leave you.’<br><br> This saying of our Lord, that the Comforter<b> will not come</b>, except He himself depart, is a convincing proof, if one more were needed,<i> that the gift of the Spirit at and since the day of</i><i> Pentecost, was and is something</i> TOTALLY DISTINCT<i> from any thing before that time: a new and loftier dispensation</i>.
