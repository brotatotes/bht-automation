<b> 10.]</b> The saying of the<i> ruler of the feast</i> is a general one, not applicable to the company then present.<br><br> We may be sure that the Lord would not have sanctioned, nor ministered to,<i> actual drunkenness</i>. Only those who can conceive<i> this</i>, will find any difficulty here; and they will find difficulties every where.<br><br> The account of the practice referred to is, that the palutes of men become after a while dull, and cannot distinguish between good wine and bad. Pliny speaks of persons “who even give their guests other wine than they drink themselves, or<i> bring it in as the banquet proceeds</i>.” But the practice<i> here</i> described is not precisely that of which Pliny speaks, nor is there any meanness to be charged on it: it is only that, when a man has some kinds of wine choicer than others, he naturally produces the choicest, to suit the most discriminating taste. The word rendered<b> have freely drank</b>, in its common meaning, implies, “<i>are intoxicated</i>,” “<i>are drunken</i>:” but while there is no reason here to<i> press</i> its ordinary meaning, so neither is there any to shrink from it, as uttered by the ruler of the feast. ‘The safest rendering is that of Tyndall and Cranmer, “<i>when men be dronke</i>: and so it is in the Vulgate also.
