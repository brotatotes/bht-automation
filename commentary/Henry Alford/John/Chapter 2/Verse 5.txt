<b> 5.]</b> There certainly seems beneath this narrative to lie<i> some incident which is not told us</i>. For not only is Mary<i> not repelled</i> by the answer just given, but she is<i> convinced that the miracle will be wrought</i>, and she is not without an anticipation of the<i> method of working it</i>: for how should He require the aid of the servants, except the miracle were to take place according to the form here related? I believe we shall find, when all things are opened to us, that there had been a previous hint given her,—where or how I would not presume to say,—by our Lord, of His intention and the manner of performing it, and that her fault was,<i> the too rash hastening on of what had been His fixed purpose.</i>
