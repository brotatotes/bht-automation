<b> 18.</b>] The end of his pastoral office is announced to him:—a proof of the<i> knowledge of all things</i> which he had just confessed to be in his Lord;—a contrast to the denial of which he had just been reminded;—a proof to be hereatter given of the here recognized genuineness of that love which he had been professing.<br><br><b> When thou wast young</b>] This may be said merely in contrast to<b> when thou shalt be old</b>. Or it perhaps includes his life up to the time prophesied of.<br><br><b> thou girdedst</b><b> thyself</b>] As in ver. 7, he had girt his fisher’s coat to him: but not confined in its reference to that girding alone,— ‘thou girdedst thyself up for My work, and wentest hither and thither—but hereafter there shall be a service for thee harder and more strictly confined.’<br><br><b> thou shalt stretch forth thy hands</b>] ‘but not as just now, in swimming;—in a more painful manner, on the transverse beam of the cross; and another—the executioner—shall gird thee,—with the cords binding to the cross.’ Such is the traditionary account of the death of Peter. Jerome says, that “he was crowned with martyrdom under Nero, being crucified with his head downwards and his feet upwards, because he alleged himself to he unworthy of being crucified in the same manner as his Lord.”<br><br><b> shall carry thee</b>] viz. in the lifting up after the fastening to the cross—or perhaps in making thee go the way to death, bearing thy cross.<br><br><b> whither thou wouldest not</b>] “For,” says Augustine, “who wishes to die? Truly no one: and so universal is this feeling, that it was said to St. Peter, Another shall gird thee and carry thee whither thou wouldest not.”
