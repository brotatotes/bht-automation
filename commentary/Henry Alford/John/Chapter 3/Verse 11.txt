<p><strong> 11.</strong> ] Henceforward the discourse is an answer to the <em> unbelief</em> , and in answering that, to the <em> question</em> ( <strong> πῶς δύν</strong> <strong> . </strong> <strong> τ</strong> <strong> . </strong> <strong> γεν</strong> <strong> .</strong> ) of Nicodemus: by shewing him the appointed means of this new birth, and of being upheld in the life to which it is the entrance, viz. <em> faith in the Son of God</em> .
	</p>
<p><strong> ὃ οἴδαμεν λ</strong> <strong> .…</strong> ] <em> Why these plurals?</em> Various interpretations have been given: ἢ περὶ ἑαυτοῦ <strong> καὶ τοῦ πατρὸς</strong> τοῦτό φησιν , ἢ περὶ ἑαυτοῦ μόνομ (Euthym [48] ); “Loquitur de se et <em> de Spiritu</em> ” (Bengel); of Himself and <em> the Prophets</em> (Beza, Tholuck); of Himself and <em> John the Baptist</em> (Knapp); of <em> Teachers like Himself</em> (Meyer); of <em> all the born of the Spirit</em> (Lange, Wesley); of the <em> three Persons in the Holy Trinity</em> (Stier); or, the plural is <em> only rhetorical</em> (Lücke, De Wette). I had rather take it as a <em> proverbial</em> saying; q. d. ‘I am one of those who,’ &amp;c. Our Lord thereby brings out the unreasonableness of that unbelief which would not receive <em> His witness</em> , but made <em> it</em> an exception to the general proverbial rule.
	</p>
<p style="margin-left:3em;"> [48] Euthymius Zigabenus, 1116
	</p>
