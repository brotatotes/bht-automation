<p><strong> παρὰ τοῦ μόνου θεοῦ</strong> <strong> ,</strong> not ‘ <em> from God only</em> ’ (E. V. and De Wette), which is ungrammatical (requiring μόνου to be either <em> after</em> θεοῦ , see <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="mt+4:4">Matthew 4:4</span> ; <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="mt+12:4">Matthew 12:4</span> ; <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="mt+17:8">Matthew 17:8</span> , or <em> before</em> τοῦ θεοῦ , <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="lu+5:21">Luke 5:21</span> ; <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="lu+6:4">Luke 6:4</span> ; <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="heb+9:7">Hebrews 9:7</span> . Lücke); but <strong> from the only God:</strong> in contradistinction to the <em> idolatry of the natural heart</em> , which is ever setting up for itself other sources of honour, worshipping <em> man</em> , or <em> self</em> , or even, as in the case alluded to in the last verse, <em> Satan</em> , instead of God. The words τοῦ μόνου θεοῦ are very important, because they form the point of passage to the next verses; in which the Jews are accused of not believing the <em> writings of Moses</em> , the very pith and kernel of which was <em> the unity of God</em> , and <em> the having no other gods but Him</em> .</p>
