<b> 60.]</b> It has been shewn by Lampe, that the word rendered<b> hard</b> signifies not so much<i> absurdity as impiety</i>. It seems clear that it was not the<i> difficulty</i>, so much as the<i> strangeness</i> of the saying, which scandalized them. It is the whole discourse,—the turn given to it,—the doctrine of the Bread of Life,—the giving His Flesh and Blood to eat,—at which they take offence.<br><br><b> who can hear it?</b> i.e.<b> who can listen to it?</b>— ‘Who can stay and hear such sayings as this?’ not,<i> Who can understand it?</i>
