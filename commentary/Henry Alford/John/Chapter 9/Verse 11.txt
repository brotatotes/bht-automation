<p><strong> 11.</strong> ] <strong> ἀνέβλ</strong> <strong> .,</strong> strictly speaking, is in-appropriate in the case of one <em> born</em> blind. Lücke refers to Aristotle as using the word thus, and cites Pausanias, who speaks of Ὀφιονέα … τὸν ἐκ γενετῆς τυφλόν , whom ἐπέλαβε τῆς κεφαλῆς ἄλγημα ἰσχυρόν , καὶ <strong> ἀνέβλεψεν</strong> ἀπ ʼ αὐτοῦ . Sight being natural to men, the deprivation of it is regarded as a <em> loss</em> , and the reception of it, though never enjoyed before, as a <em> recovery</em> . So Grotius: “nec male <em> recipere</em> quis dicitur, quod communiter tributum humanæ naturæ ipsi abfuit.” There is no emphasis on μου here (as Bp. Wordsw.) nor in <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="joh+9:15">John 9:15</span> ; <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="joh+9:30">John 9:30</span> : nor on σου in <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="joh+9:10">John 9:10</span> ; <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="joh+9:17">John 9:17</span> ; <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="joh+9:26">John 9:26</span> .
