<b> 13.]</b> The man was evidently<i> not a disciple</i>, nor preparing to be one, but<i> some hearer in the crowd</i>, whose mind had been working in him during our Lord’s last sayings about the care of Providence for His friends, and he thought this was<i> just the care his circumstances wanted;</i> being, as appears, oppressed by his brother in the matter of his patrimony. Possibly too he had an idea that the Messias, or the great Rabbi to whom he was listening, was come to set all things right;—and with that feeling which we all have of the surpassing injustice of<i> our own</i> wrongs, broke out with this inopportune request.
