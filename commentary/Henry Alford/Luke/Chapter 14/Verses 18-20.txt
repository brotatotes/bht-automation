	The  temper of these self-excusers is threefold; the  excuses themselves are threefold; their  spirit is one . The first alleges an ἀνάγκη , he  must go and see his land: the second not so much as this, only his own plan and purpose πορεύομαι : the third not so much as either of these, but rudely asserts οὐ δύναμαι (i.e. οὐ βούλομαι ) ἐλθεῖν . Also the  excuses themselves are threefold. The first has his  worldly possession (‘one to his farm,’ Mat 22:5 ) to go and see: the second his purchase (‘another to his merchandise,’ ibid.) of stock to prove: the third his home engagements and his lust to satisfy.  All are detained by  worldliness , in however varied forms.
