<p><strong> 19.</strong> ] <strong> δέ</strong> connects this directly with what goes before; being an answer, not immediately to <em> any thing said</em> by the Pharisees, but to their <em> scoffs</em> at Him; q. d. ‘ <em> hear now a parable</em> .’
	</p>
<p><strong> ἄνθρ</strong> <strong> . </strong> <strong> πλ</strong> <strong> .</strong> ] Tertullian thought (l. c.) that <em> Herod</em> was meant, and by Lazarus <em> John;</em> and this view has been taken by Paulus and Schleiermacher also: but surely with no probability. Our Lord might hint with stern rebuke at the present notorious crime of Herod, but can hardly be thought to have spoken thus of him. That the circumstances <em> will in some measure apply to these two</em> , is owing, as above in ch. 15, to the parable taking the <em> general case</em> , of which theirs was a particular instance. Zeller (refuted by Bleek in loc.) thinks that the rich man sets forth the Jews and the poor man the Gentiles. In my view, the very name of the poor man (see below) is a sufficient answer to this.
	</p>
<p>Observe, that this rich man is <em> not accused of any flagrant crimes:</em> he lives, as the world would say, <em> as became his means and station;</em> he does not oppress nor spoil other men: he is simply a υἱὸς τοῦ αἰῶνος τούτου , in the highest form.
	</p>
