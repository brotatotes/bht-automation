 3.] The steward sets before himself the certainty of poverty and misery. He has not by his waste of his lord’s property been laying up any store<i> for himself</i>;—that is not the point of the parable;—he has lived softly and effeminately, and cannot do an honest day’s work:—<b>dig</b> is used for<i> all manual labours</i>. This speech, of digging and begging, must not be sought for in the interpretation; it belongs to the truth of the parable itself, as introducing the scheme which follows, but has no ulterior meaning.
