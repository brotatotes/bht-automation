<p><strong> 28.</strong> ] <strong> στραφείς</strong> after He was relieved from the burden of the cross. This word comes from an eye-witness.
	</p>
<p><strong> ἐπ</strong> <strong> ʼ </strong> <strong> ἐμέ</strong> <em> His</em> future course was not one to be bewailed see especially on this saying, <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="heb+12:2">Hebrews 12:2</span> , ὃς ἀντὶ τῆς προκειμένης αὐτῷ χαρᾶς ὑπέμεινεν σταυρόν , αἰσχύνης καταφρονήσας . Nor again were His sacred sufferings a mere popular tragedy for street-bewailing; the sinners should weep for <em> themselves</em> , not for <em> Him</em> .
	</p>
<p><strong> ἐφ</strong> <strong> ʼ </strong> <strong> ἑαυτὰς</strong> <strong> … </strong> <strong> καὶ ἐπὶ τὰ τέκνα ὑμῶν</strong> ] See <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="mt+27:25">Matthew 27:25</span> , where the people called down the vengeance of His blood on themselves καὶ ἐπὶ <strong> τὰ τέκνα</strong> ἡμῶν . <em> Many of those who now bewailed Him perished in the siege of Jerusalem</em> . Those who now were young wives, would not be more than <em> sixty</em> when (A.D. 70) the city was taken. But to <em> their children</em> more especially belonged the miseries of which the Lord here speaks.</p>
