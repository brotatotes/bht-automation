<p><strong> 15.</strong> ] <strong> καὶ ἐγέν</strong> <strong> .… </strong> <strong> καὶ</strong> <strong> …,</strong> the ordinary construction. The last καί does not mean ‘ <em> also</em> .’
	</p>
<p><strong> αὐτὸς Ἰη</strong> <strong> .</strong> ] <strong> Jesus Himself,</strong> of whom they had been speaking. But this expression forbids the supposition that He was here, <em> strictly speaking</em> , ἐν ἑτέρᾳ μορφῇ , as we find it less precisely expressed in <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="mr+16:12">Mark 16:12</span> . The reason why they did not know Him was ( Luk 24:16 ), that <em> their eyes were supernaturally influenced, so that they could not:</em> see also <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="lu+24:31">Luke 24:31</span> . No change took place in <em> Him</em> nor apparently in <em> them</em> , beyond a power upon them, which prevented the recognition just so much as to delay it till aroused by the well-known action and manner of His breaking the bread. The <em> cause</em> of this was the will of the Lord himself, who would not be seen by them till the time when He saw fit.
	</p>
<p><strong> ἐγγίσας</strong> from <em> behind:</em> see <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="lu+24:18">Luke 24:18</span> , where they take Him for an inhabitant of Jerusalem.</p>
