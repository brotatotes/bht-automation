<p><strong> 50.</strong> ] The Ascension appears to be related as taking place <em> after the above words were spoken</em> but there is an uncertainty and want of specification about the narrative, which forbids us to conclude that it is intended as following immediately upon them. This however can only be said as taking the other Gospels and <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="ac+1:0">Acts 1:0</span> into account: if we had <em> none but the Gospel of Luke</em> we should certainly say that the Lord ascended <em> after the appearance to the Apostles and others on the evening of the day of His resurrection</em> .
	</p>

Mark: but the ἔξω might only apply to the house in which they were, see the other reff., and <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="mt+26:75">Matthew 26:75</span> .
	</p>
<p><strong> ἕως πρὸς Β</strong> <strong> .</strong> ] Not quite to the village itself, but over the brow of the Mount of Olives where it descends on Bethany: see <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="ac+1:12">Acts 1:12</span> . (The synonymousness of these two expressions may shew that the same is meant, when, <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="mr+11:11">Mark 11:11</span> , our Lord is said to have gone out at night <em> to Bethany</em> , and, <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="lu+21:37">Luke 21:37</span> , <em> to the Mount of Olives</em> .)</p>
