<p><strong> 23.</strong> ] Notice the affectionate diminutive <strong> θυγάτριον</strong> <strong> ,</strong> peculiar to Mark.
	</p>
<p><strong> ἐσχ</strong> <strong> . </strong> <strong> ἔχει</strong> = ἄρτι ἐτελεύτησεν Matt. It is branded as an idiom of lower Greek by Phrynichus: ἐσχάτως ἔχει ἐπὶ τοῦ μοχθηρῶς ἔχει καὶ σφαλερῶς τάττουσιν οἱ σύρφακες , ed. Lobeck, p. 389, where see Lobeck’s note.
	</p>
<p>Before <strong> ἵνα</strong> understand πάρειμι , or αἰτῶ σε : or as Meyer suggests, connect it with the fact just announced: ‘this tidings I bring, in order that,’ &amp;c. To do this <em> without any</em> filling up, ‘My daughter is, &amp;c., in order that,’ &amp;c., is far-fetched, and savours too much of the <em> sentimental</em> . Or, it has been suggested that ἵνα might, by a mixture of constructions, depend on the foregoing παρεκάλει .</p>
