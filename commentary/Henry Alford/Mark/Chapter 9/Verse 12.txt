<p><strong> 12.</strong> ] Meyer and others place the interrogation after <strong> τοῦ ἀνθρώπου</strong> <strong> ,</strong> and regard <strong> ἵνα πολ</strong> <strong> .…</strong> as its answer. But not to mention that such a sentence would be without example in our Lord’s discourses, the sense given by it is meagre in the extreme. As it stands in the text, it forms a counter-question to that of the Apostles in <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="mr+9:11">Mark 9:11</span> . <em> They asked</em> , <strong> How say the Scribes that Elias must first come?</strong> Our Lord answers it by telling them that it is even so; and returns the question by another: <strong> And how is it (also) written of the Son of Man, that He,</strong> &amp;c.? then comes the conclusion in Mar 9:13 with <strong> ἀλλὰ λέγω ὑμῖν</strong> <strong> ,</strong> stating that Elias <em> has come</em> , and leaving it therefore to be inferred that the sufferings of the Son of Man were close at hand. Notice how the γέγρ . <strong> ἐπ</strong> <strong> ʼ </strong> <strong> αὐτόν</strong> binds both together. Just as the first coming of the Son of Man is to suffer and to die, so has the first coming of Elias been as it was written of him; but there is a future coming of Elias <strong> ἀποκαθιστάνειν πάντα</strong> <strong> ,</strong> and of the Son of Man in glory. See further in notes on Matt. The first <strong> καί</strong> in Mar 9:13 is <strong> also,</strong> binding what is said of Elias to that which has been said of the Son of Man: the second <strong> καί</strong> is simply <strong> and.
	</strong></p>
