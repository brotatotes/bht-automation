<p><strong> 22.</strong> ] <strong> ἀπὸ τ</strong> <strong> . </strong> <strong> ὁρ</strong> <strong> . </strong> <strong> ἐκ</strong> <strong> …</strong> does not belong to ἐξελθ ., but means <strong> of</strong> or <strong> from</strong> those parts.
	</p>
<p><strong> ἐξελθ</strong> <strong> .</strong> ] <strong> coming out</strong> (they were going by the way, see Mat 15:23 ): i.e. from her house, or town, or village.
	</p>
<p>The inhabitants of these parts are called Canaanites, <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="nu+13:29">Numbers 13:29</span> ; <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="jdg+1:30">Judges 1:30</span> ; <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="jdg+1:32-33">Judges 1:32-33</span> ; and Phœnicians, <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="ex+6:15">Exodus 6:15</span> (LXX): <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="jos+5:1">Joshua 5:1</span> (LXX). Mark calls her Ἑλληνίς , i.e. a heathen by religion, and Σύρα Φοινίκισσα τῷ γένει : and describes her only as having come to our Lord <em> in the house</em> . But by the account in our text, she had been crying after the Lord and the disciples by the way previously; and Mark’s account must be understood to begin at our <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="mt+15:25">Matthew 15:25</span> . From <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="mr+3:8">Mark 3:8</span> , <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="lu+6:17">Luke 6:17</span> , we learn that the fame of our Lord had been spread in these parts, and multitudes from thence had come to Him for healing. It was not this woman’s dwelling-place, but her <em> descent</em> , which placed the bar between her and our Lord’s ministrations.
	</p>
<p>The expression υἱὸς Δαυείδ shews her acquaintance with Jewish expressions and expectations; but the whole narrative is against De Wette’s supposition, that she may have been a proselyte of the gate.</p>
