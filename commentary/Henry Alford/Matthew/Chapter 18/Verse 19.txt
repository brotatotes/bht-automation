<p><strong> 19. </strong> <strong> παντὸς πρ</strong> <strong> .</strong> ] ‘ <em> every thing:</em> ’ but the construction is an instance of attraction: πᾶν πρᾶγμα , the subject of the sentence, is thrown into government after the verb: the plain construction would be ὅτι πᾶν πρ ., ἐὰν δύο ὑμ . συμφ . ἐπὶ τ . γ . περὶ αὐτοῦ , οὗ ἐὰν αἰτήσωνται , γενήσεται κ . τ . λ .: so that παντὸς πρ . amounts in English to <strong> any thing.</strong> This refers to that entire accordance of hearty faith, which could hardly have place except also in accordance with the divine will.
	</p>
<p>It was apparently misunderstood by the Apostles James and John; see St. Mark’s account, ch. <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="mt+10:35">Matthew 10:35</span> , in which they nearly repeat these words. Notice again the <strong> ἀμὴν λέγω ὑμῖν</strong> <strong> :</strong> see on ch. 16 ult.</p>
