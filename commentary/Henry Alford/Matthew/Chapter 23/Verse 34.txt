 34.] From the similar place in the former discourse (Luke xi. 49, see notes there) it would appear that the<b> wherefore</b> refers to the<i> whole</i> last denunciation:— “since ye are bent upon filling up the iniquities of your fathers, in God’s inscrutable purposes ye shall go on rejecting His messengers.’ Notice the difference between “the<i> wisdom of God</i>” in Luke xi. 49, and I, with its emphasis, here.<br><br> These words are nowhere written in Scripture, nor is it necessary to suppose that to be our Lord’s meaning. He speaks this as Head of His Church, of those whom He was about to send: see Acts xiii. 1: 1 Cor. xii. 8: Eph. iii. 5. He cannot, as some think, include<i> Himself</i> among those whom He sends—the Jews may have<i> crucified</i> many Christian teachers before the destruction of Jerusalem. And Eusebius relates from Hegesippus the crucifixion of Symeon son of Clopas, in the reign of Trajan. The and takes out the “crucify,” the special, from the “kill,” the general; with, of course, somewhat of emphasis: “<i>yea, and even crucify.</i>” The prophets were the Apostles, who, in relation to the Jews, were such—the<b> wise men</b>, Stephen and such like, men full of the Holy Ghost—the scribes, Apollos, Paul (who indeed was all of these together), and such. On<b> scourge</b> in your<b> synagogues</b>, see Acts v. 40; xxii. 19; xxvi. 11.
