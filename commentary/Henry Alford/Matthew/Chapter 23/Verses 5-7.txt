<p><strong> 5 7.</strong> ] But whatever they <em> do</em> perform, has but one motive.
	</p>
<p><strong> φυλακ</strong> <strong> .,</strong> Heb. Totaphoth, or subsequently and more generally, Tephillin (see Gesen. Thes. Hebr., and Buxtorf, Lex. Rabbin.), were strips of parchment with certain passages of Scripture, viz. <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="ex+13:11-16">Exodus 13:11-16</span> ; <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="ex+13:1-10">Exodus 13:1-10</span> ; <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="de+11:13-21">Deuteronomy 11:13-21</span> ; <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="de+6:4-9">Deuteronomy 6:4-9</span> , written on them, and worn on the forehead between the eyes, on the left side next the heart, and on the left arm. The name in the text was given because they were considered as charms. They appear not to have been worn till after the captivity; and are still in use among the Rabbinical Jews. Their use appears to have arisen from a superstitious interpretation of <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="ex+13:9">Exodus 13:9</span> ; <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="de+6:8-9">Deuteronomy 6:8-9</span> . See Jos. Antt. iv. 8. 13. The fringes were commanded to be worn for a memorial, <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="nu+15:38">Numbers 15:38</span> . See note on ch. <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="mt+9:20">Matthew 9:20</span> .</p>
