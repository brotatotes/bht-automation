<p><strong> 19, 20.</strong> ] It will be most important that so sudden a flight should not be encumbered, by <em> personal</em> hindrances ( <strong> τ</strong> <strong> . </strong> <strong> ἐν γ</strong> <strong> . </strong> <strong> ἐχ</strong> <strong> .</strong> ), or by hindrances of <em> accompaniment</em> ( <strong> τ</strong> <strong> . </strong> <strong> θηλ</strong> <strong> .</strong> ), see <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="1co+7:26">1 Corinthians 7:26</span> ; and that those things which are <em> out of our power to arrange</em> , should be propitious, weather, and freedom from legal prohibition. The words <strong> μηδὲ σαβ</strong> <strong> .</strong> are peculiar to Matthew, and shew the strong Jewish tint which caused <em> him alone</em> to preserve such portions of our Lord’s sayings. That they were not said as any <em> sanction</em> of observance of the Jewish Sabbath, is most certain: but merely as referring to the <em> positive impediments</em> which might meet them on that day, the shutting of the gates of cities, &amp;c., and their own scruples about travelling further than the ordinary Sabbath-day’s journey (about a mile English); for the Jewish Christians adhered to the law and customary observances till the destruction of Jerusalem.</p>
