<p><strong> 9 13.</strong> ] <strong> τότε</strong> <strong> , at this time, during this period,</strong> not ‘after these things have happened.’ De Wette presses this latter meaning, that he may find a contradiction to <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="lu+21:12">Luke 21:12</span> , πρὸ δὲ τούτων πάντων .… These words serve only definitely to fix the time of the indefinite τότε , here and in <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="mt+24:10">Matthew 24:10</span> . The τότε in Mat 24:14 is, from the construction of the sentence, more definite. For <strong> ἀποκτ</strong> <strong> . </strong> <strong> ὑμ</strong> <strong> .</strong> Luke has θανατώσουσιν <strong> ἐξ ὑμῶν</strong> <strong> ,</strong> viz. the Apostles. This sign was early given. <em> James the brother of John</em> was put to death, A.D. 44: <em> Peter and Paul</em> (traditionally, Euseb. H. E. ii. 25) and <em> James the Lord’s brother</em> , before the destruction of Jerusalem: and possibly others.
	</p>
<p><strong> ἔσεσθε μισ</strong> <strong> .</strong> ] see <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="ac+28:22">Acts 28:22</span> , ἡ αἵρεσις αὕτη .… πανταχοῦ ἀντιλέγεται : also Tacitus, Ann. xv. 44, where Nero, for the conflagration of Rome, persecutes ‘Christianos, genus hominum ob flagitia invisos:’ also see <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="1pe+2:12">1 Peter 2:12</span> ; <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="1pe+3:16">1 Peter 3:16</span> ; <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="1pe+4:14-16">1 Peter 4:14-16</span> . In chap. <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="mt+10:22">Matthew 10:22</span> , from which these verses are repeated, we have only ὑπὸ πάντων here τῶν ἐθνῶν is added, giving particularity to the prophecy.</p>
