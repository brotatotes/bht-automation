<p><strong> 20. </strong> <strong> ὁ υἱὸς τοῦ ἀνθρώπου</strong> ] “It is thought that this phrase was taken from <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="da+7:13">Daniel 7:13</span> , to which passage our Saviour seems to allude in ch. <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="mt+26:64">Matthew 26:64</span> , and probably Stephen in <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="ac+7:56">Acts 7:56</span> . It appears from <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="joh+12:34">John 12:34</span> , that the Jews understood it to mean the Messiah: and from <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="lu+22:69-70">Luke 22:69-70</span> , that they considered <em> the Son of Man</em> to mean the same as <em> the Son of God</em> .” Dr. Burton. It is the name by which the Lord ordinarily in one pregnant word designates Himself as the Messiah the <em> Son of God manifested in the flesh of man</em> the <em> second Adam</em> . And to it belong all those conditions, of humiliation, suffering, and exaltation, which it behoved the Son of Man to go through.</p>
