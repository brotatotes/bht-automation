<p><strong> 22</strong> . <strong> ἅμα δὲ κιά</strong> ] <strong> But at the same time</strong> (as thou fulfillest my request), <strong> also</strong> .… We may, perhaps, take this direction as serving to secure the favourable reception of Onesimus: for the Apostle would himself come and see how his request had fared: πολλὴ γὰρ ἦν ἡ χάρις κ . ἡ τιμὴ Παύλου ἐνδημοῦντος , Παύλου μετὰ ἡλικίαν , Παύλου μετὰ δεσμούς , Chrys. Or it may be, as Ellic., that Philemon was not to consider the Epistle as a mere petition for Onesimus, but as containing special messages on other matters to himself. <strong> ὑμῶν</strong> and <strong> ὑμῖν</strong> refer to those names in <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="phm+1:1-2">Philemon 1:1-2</span> .</p>
