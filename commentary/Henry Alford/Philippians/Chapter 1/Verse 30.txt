<p><strong> 30</strong> .] <strong> having</strong> (the nominative instead of the dative, the subjective ὑμεῖς being before the Apostle’s mind: so <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="eph+4:2">Ephesians 4:2</span> , Thuc. iii. 36, ἔδοξεν αὐτοῖς … ἐπικαλοῦντες : ib. vi. 24, καὶ ἔρως ἐνέπεσε πᾶσιν … εὐέλπιδες ὄντες : Sallust, Jug. 112, ‘ <em> populo Romano melius visum … rati</em> :’ see other examples in Kühner, ii. p. 377. This is far better than with Lachm., al., to parenthesize ἥτις … πάσχειν , which unnecessarily breaks the flow of the sentence) <strong> the same conflict</strong> (one in its nature and object) <strong> as ye saw</strong> (viz. when I was with you, <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="ac+16:16">Acts 16:16</span> ff.) <strong> in me</strong> (in my case as its example), <strong> and now hear of in me</strong> ( <strong> ἐν ἐμοί</strong> , as before, not ‘ <em> de</em> me.’ He means, by report of others, and by this Epistle).</p>
