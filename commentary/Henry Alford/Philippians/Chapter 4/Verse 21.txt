<p><strong> 21</strong> .] <strong> πάντα ἅγιον</strong> , <strong> every</strong> individual <strong> saint</strong> . The singular has love and affection, and should not be lost as in Conyb., ‘Salute all <em> God’s people</em> .’
	</p>
<p><strong> ἐν χριστῷ Ἰησοῦ</strong> ] belongs more probably to <strong> ἀσπάσασθε</strong> , see <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="ro+16:22">Romans 16:22</span> ; <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="1co+16:19">1 Corinthians 16:19</span> , than to <strong> ἅγιον</strong> , as in ch. <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="php+1:1">Philippians 1:1</span> , where, as Meyer observes, the expression has a diplomatic formality, whereas here there is no reason for so formal an adjunct.
	</p>
<p><strong> οἱ σὺν ἐμοὶ ἀδελφοί</strong> ] These must, on account of the next verse, have been his closer friends, perhaps his colleagues in the ministry, such as Aristarchus, Epaphras, Demas, Timotheus. But there has arisen a question, how to reconcile this with ch. Php 2:20 ? And it may be answered, that the lack of ἰσοψυχία there predicated of his companions, did not exclude them from the title ἀδελφοί , nor from sending greeting to the Philippians: see also ch. <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="php+1:14">Philippians 1:14</span> .</p>
