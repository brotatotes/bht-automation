<b> 4.</b>]<b> the sword,</b> perhaps in allusion to the dagger worn by the Cæsars, which was regarded as a symbol of the power of life and death. In ancient and modern times, the sword has been carried before sovereigns. It betokens the power of capital punishment: and the reference to it here is among the many testimonies borne by Scripture against the attempt to abolish the infliction of the penalty of death for crime in Christian states.<br><br><b> for wrath</b> seems to be inserted for the sake of parallelism with “<i>for good</i>” above: it betokens the<i> character</i> of the vengeance,—that it<i> issues in wrath.</i> The wrath is referred to in “<i>the wrath,</i>” ver. 5.
