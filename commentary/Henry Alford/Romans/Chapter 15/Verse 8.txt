<b> 8.</b>]<b> For</b> (reason for the above exhortation)<b> I say that Christ hath been made</b> (lias come as: the effects still enduring)<b> a minister</b> (He came<i> to minister</i>, Matt. xx. 28)<b> of the circumcision</b> (an expression nowhere else found, and doubtless here used by Paul to humble the pride of the<i> strong</i>, the Gentile Christians, by exalting God’s covenant people to their true dignity),<b> for the sake of God’s truth</b> (i. e. for the fulfilment of the Divine pledges given under the covenant of circumcision)<b>, in order to confirm the promises made unto the fathers</b> (literally,<b> the promises of the fathers;</b> so “<i>the blessing of Abraham,</i>” Gal. iii. 14, Christ came to the<i> Jews</i> in virtue of a<i> long-sealed compact, to the fulfilment of which God’s truth was pledged</i>):<b> and</b> (I say)<b> that the Gentiles glorified God</b> (meaning, that ‘<i>each man at his conversion did so.</i>’ These words cannot by any possibility be rendered as in the A.V., “<i>that the Gentiles might glorify God</i>”)<b> on account of</b> [<b>His</b>]<b> mercy</b> (the emphasis is on<b> mercy:</b> the Gentiles have no<i> covenant</i><i> promise</i> to claim,—they have nothing but the pure mercy of God in grafting them in to allege—therefore the Jew has an advantage)<b>, &c.</b>—The citations are from the Law, the Prophets, and the Psalms. The first, originally spoken by David of his joy after his deliverances and triumphs, is prophetically said of Christ in His own Person. It is adduced to shew that among the Gentiles Christ’s triumphs were to take place, as well as among the Jews.
