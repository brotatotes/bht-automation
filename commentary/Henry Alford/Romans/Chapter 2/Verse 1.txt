<p><strong> 1.</strong> ] The address passes gradually to the Jews. They were the people who <em> judged</em> who pronounced all Gentiles to be born in sin and under condemnation: doubtless there were also proud and censorious men among the Gentiles, to whom the rebuke might apply, but these are hardly in the Apostle’s mind. This is evident by comparing τὰ γὰρ αὐτὰ πράσσεις ὁ κρίνων with <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="ro+2:21-23">Romans 2:21-23</span> , where the same charge is implied in a direct address to the Jew.
	</p>
<p><strong> διό</strong> , on account of this δικαίωμα θεοῦ decreeing death against the doers of these things FOR <em> thou doest them thyself</em> . Therefore thy setting thyself up as a judge, is unjustifiable.
	</p>
<p><strong> πᾶς ὁ κρίνων</strong> ] The Jew is not yet named, but hinted at (see above): not in order to conciliate the Jews (Rückert), but on account of the as yet purposely general form of the argument. This verse is in fact the major of a syllogism, the minor of which follows, <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="ro+2:17-20">Romans 2:17-20</span> , where the position here declared to be unjustifiable, is asserted to be assumed by <em> the Jew</em> .
	</p>
<p><strong> ἐν ᾧ</strong> <strong> …</strong> ] <strong> For wherein</strong> (not ‘ <em> in that</em> ’), as E. V. i.e. ‘ <em> in the matter in which</em> .’</p>
