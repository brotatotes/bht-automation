<p><strong> 23.</strong> ] <strong> ἐγράφη</strong> <strong> , was written</strong> , not the more usual γέγραπται , ‘ <em> is written</em> :’ similarly in the parallel, <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="1co+10:11">1 Corinthians 10:11</span> ; and in our ch. <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="ro+15:4">Romans 15:4</span> . The aorist asserts the design of God’s Spirit <em> at the time</em> of penning the words: the perfect may <em> imply</em> that, but more directly <em> asserts</em> the intent of our Scriptures <em> as we now find them</em> . <strong> Now it was not written for his sake alone</strong> (merely to bear testimony to him and his faith) <strong> that it was reckoned unto him, but for our sake also</strong> (for our benefit, to bear testimony to us of the efficacy of faith like his. Observe that διὰ in the two clauses has not exactly the same sense, ‘for <em> his</em> sake’ being = (1) <em> to celebrate his faith</em> , and (2) for <em> our</em> sake = <em> for our profit</em> ; see on Rom 4:25 ), <strong> to whom it</strong> (i.e. τὸ πιστεύειν τῷ θεῷ , as Rom 4:22 ) <strong> shall be reckoned</strong> (for righteousness: μέλλει λογ . is a <em> future</em> , as ch. <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="ro+3:30">Romans 3:30</span> ; <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="ro+5:19">Romans 5:19</span> (Thol.), not, as Olsh. al., spoken as from the time and standing of Abraham), <strong> who believe on</strong> (this specifies the ἡμᾶς : and the belief is not a mere historical but a <em> fiducial</em> belief) <strong> Him who raised Jesus our Lord from the dead</strong> (the central fact in our redemption, as the procreation of the seed of promise was in the performance of the promise to Abraham, see ch. <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="ro+1:4">Romans 1:4</span> ; <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="1co+15:14">1 Corinthians 15:14</span> ff.; and resembling it in the ζωοποιῆσαι τοὺς νεκρούς ).</p>
