<p><strong> 15.</strong> ] <strong> For</strong> (a proof of this πεπράσθαι under sin, viz. not being able to do what I would, Rom 7:15-17 ) <strong> that which I perform</strong> (am in the habit of doing) <strong> I know not</strong> (act blindly, at the dictates of another: which is proper to a slave. σκοτοῦμαι φησί , συναρπάζομαι , ἐπήρειαν ὑπομένω , οὐκ οἶδα πῶς ὑποσκελίζομαι , Chrys. The meaning, ‘ <em> I approve not</em> ,’ introduced by Aug [45] and held by Erasm., Beza, Grot., Estius, Semler, al., is not sanctioned by usage, see note on <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="1co+8:3">1 Corinthians 8:3</span> , and would make the following clause almost a tautology): for (explanation of last assertion, shewing how such blind service comes to pass) <strong> not what I desire, that do I</strong> (this θέλω is not the <em> full determination of the will</em> , the standing with the bow drawn and the arrow aimed; but rather the <em> inclination</em> of the will, the taking up the bow and pointing at the mark, but without power to draw it: -we have θέλω in the sense of <em> to wish</em> , 1Co 7:7 ; <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="1co+7:32">1 Corinthians 7:32</span> ; <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="1co+14:5">1 Corinthians 14:5</span> ; 2Co 12:20 ), <strong> but what I hate</strong> (= οὐ θέλω , <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="ro+7:19">Romans 7:19</span> ; no distinction in intensity between θέλω and μισῶ ), <strong> that I do</strong> (no distinction here between πράσσω and ποιῶ , as apparently in <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="joh+3:20-21">John 3:20-21</span> , where see note: for they are interchanged in Rom 7:19-20 ).
	</p>

<p>The Commentators cite several parallel passages from profane writers: e.g. Seneca, Hippol. 604, ‘Vos testor omnes cœlites, hoc quod volo, me nolle;’ Epictetus, Enchiridion ii. 26, ἐπεὶ γὰρ ὁ ἁμαρτάνων οὐ θέλει ἁμαρτάνειν , ἀλλὰ κατορθῶσαι , δῆλον ὅτι ὃ μὲν θέλει οὐ ποιεῖ , καὶ ὃ μὴ θέλει ποιεῖ : the well-known lines of Ovid, Met. <span class="scriptRef" com="hac" lang="eng" ref="ro+7:19">Romans 7:19</span> , ‘aliudque cupido, Mens aliud suadet: video meliora proboque, Deteriora sequor:’ Plautus, Trinummus iii. 2. 31, ‘Scibam ut esse me deceret, facere non quibam miser:’ &amp;c.</p>
