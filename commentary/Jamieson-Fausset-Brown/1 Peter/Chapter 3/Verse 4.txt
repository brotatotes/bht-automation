<p><b>4. But</b>—"Rather." The
	"outward adornment" of jewelry, c., is forbidden, in so far
	as woman loves such things, not in so far as she uses them from a
	sense of propriety, and does not <i>abuse</i> them. Singularity
	mostly comes from pride and throws needless hindrances to religion in
	the way of others. Under costly attire there may be a humble mind.
	"Great is he who uses his earthenware as if it were plate not
	less great is he who uses his silver as if it were earthenware"
	[SENECA in ALFORD].
	</p>
<p><b>hidden</b>—<i>inner</i>
	man, which the Christian instinctively <i>hides</i> from public view.
	</p>
<p><b>of the heart</b>—<i>consisting
	in the heart</i> regenerated and adorned by the Spirit. This "inner
	man of the heart" is the subject of the verb "be," <span class="scriptRef" com="jfb" lang="eng" ref="+:-"> :-</span>, <i>Greek:</i> "Of whom let the inner man be,"
	namely, the distinction or adornment. 
	</p>
<p><b>in that</b>—consisting or
	standing <i>in that</i> as its element. 
	</p>
<p><b>not corruptible</b>—not
	transitory, nor tainted with corruption, as all earthly adornments. 
	</p>
<p><b>meek and quiet</b>—<i>meek,</i>
	not creating disturbances: <i>quiet,</i> bearing with tranquillity
	the disturbances caused by others. <i>Meek</i> in affections and
	feelings; <i>quiet</i> in words, countenance, and actions [BENGEL].
	</p>
<p><b>in the sight of God</b>—who
	looks to inward, not merely outward things. 
	</p>
<p><b>of great price</b>—The
	results of redemption should correspond to its costly price (<span class="scriptRef" com="jfb" lang="eng" ref="+:-"> :-</span>).</p>
