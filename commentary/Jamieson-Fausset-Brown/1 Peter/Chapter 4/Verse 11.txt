<p><b>11. If any . . . speak</b>—namely,
	as a prophet, or divinely taught <i>teacher</i> in the Church
	assembly. 
	</p>
<p><b>as the,</b> c.—The <i>Greek</i>
	has no article: "as oracles of God." This may be due to
	<i>Greek:</i> "God," having no article, it being a
	principle when a governed noun omits the <i>Greek</i> article that
	the governing noun should omit it, too. In <span class="scriptRef" com="jfb" lang="eng" ref="+:-"> :-</span> also, the <i>Greek</i> article is wanting thus <i>English
	Version,</i> "as the oracles of God," namely, <i>the Old
	Testament,</i> would be "right," and the precept be similar
	to <span class="scriptRef" com="jfb" lang="eng" ref="ro+12:6">Romans 12:6</span>, "prophesy
	according to <i>the analogy of the faith.</i>" But the context
	suits better thus, "Let him speak as (becomes one speaking)
	<i>oracles</i> OF GOD."
	His divinely inspired words are <i>not his own,</i> but <i>God's,</i>
	and as a <i>steward</i> (<span class="scriptRef" com="jfb" lang="eng" ref="1pe+4:10">1 Peter 4:10</span>)
	having them committed to him, he ought so to speak them. Jesus was
	the pattern in this respect (<span class="scriptRef" com="jfb" lang="eng" ref="mt+7:29">Matthew 7:29</span>;
	<span class="scriptRef" com="jfb" lang="eng" ref="joh+12:49">John 12:49</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="jfb" lang="eng" ref="joh+14:10">John 14:10</span>;
	compare Paul, <span class="scriptRef" com="jfb" lang="eng" ref="2co+2:17">2 Corinthians 2:17</span>). Note,
	the very same term as is applied in the only other passages where it
	occurs (<span class="scriptRef" com="jfb" lang="eng" ref="ac+7:38">Acts 7:38</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="jfb" lang="eng" ref="ro+3:2">Romans 3:2</span>;
	<span class="scriptRef" com="jfb" lang="eng" ref="heb+5:12">Hebrews 5:12</span>), to the <i>Old
	Testament</i> inspired writings, is here predicated of the inspired
	<i>words</i> (the substance of which was afterwards committed to
	<i>writing</i>) of the <i>New Testament</i> prophets. 
	</p>
<p><b>minister</b>—in <i>acts;</i>
	the other sphere of spiritual activity besides <i>speaking.</i>
</p>
<p><b>as of</b>—"out of"
	the store of his "strength" (<i>Greek, physical</i> power
	in relation to outward service, rather than moral and intellectual
	"ability"; so in <span class="scriptRef" com="jfb" lang="eng" ref="heb+5:12- :">Hebrews 5:12- :</span>). 
	</p>
<p><b>giveth</b>—<i>Greek,</i>
	"supplieth"; originally said of a <i>choragus,</i> who
	<i>supplied</i> the chorus with all necessaries for performing their
	several parts. 
	</p>
<p><b>that God in all things may be
	glorified</b>—the final end of all a Christian's acts. 
	</p>
<p><b>through Jesus Christ</b>—the
	mediator through whom all our blessings come down to us, and also
	through whom all our praises ascend to God. Through Christ alone can
	God be glorified in us and our sayings and doings. 
	</p>
<p><b>to whom</b>—Christ. 
	</p>
<p><b>be</b>—<i>Greek,</i> "is."
	</p>
<p><b>for ever and ever</b>—<i>Greek,</i>
	"unto the ages of the ages."</p>
