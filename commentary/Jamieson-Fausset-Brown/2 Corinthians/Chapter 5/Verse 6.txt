<p><b>6.</b> Translate as <i>Greek,</i>
	"Being therefore always confident and knowing," c. He had
	intended to have made the verb to this nominative, "we are
	willing" (rather, "well content"), but digressing on
	the word "confident" (<span class="scriptRef" com="jfb" lang="eng" ref="2co+5:6">2 Corinthians 5:6</span>
<span class="scriptRef" com="jfb" lang="eng" ref="2co+5:7">2 Corinthians 5:7</span>), he resumes the word in
	a different form, namely, as an assertion: "We are confident and
	well content." "Being confident . . . we are confident"
	may be the <i>Hebraic</i> idiom of emphasis; as <span class="scriptRef" com="jfb" lang="eng" ref="2co+5:7- :">2 Corinthians 5:7- :</span>, <i>Greek,</i> "Having seen, I have seen," that
	is, I have <i>surely seen.</i>
</p>
<p><b>always</b>—under all
	trials. BENGEL makes the
	contrast between "<i>always</i> confident" and "confident"
	especially at the prospect of being "absent from the body."
	We are confident as well <i>at all times,</i> as also most of all in
	the hope of a blessed departure. 
	</p>
<p><b>whilst . . . at home . . .
	absent</b>—Translate as <i>Greek,</i> "While we sojourn <i>in
	our home</i> in the body, we are <i>away from our home</i> in the
	Lord." The image from a "house" is retained (compare
	<span class="scriptRef" com="jfb" lang="eng" ref="php+3:20">Philippians 3:20</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="jfb" lang="eng" ref="heb+11:13-16">Hebrews 11:13-16</span>;
	<span class="scriptRef" com="jfb" lang="eng" ref="heb+13:14">Hebrews 13:14</span>).</p>
