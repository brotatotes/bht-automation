<p><b>20. some of them were men of Cyprus
	and Cyrene</b>—(see on <span class="scriptRef" com="jfb" lang="eng" ref="+:-"> :-</span>);
	as Lucius, mentioned in <span class="scriptRef" com="jfb" lang="eng" ref="ac+13:1">Acts 13:1</span>.
	</p>
<p><b>spake unto the
	Grecians</b>—rather, "the <i>Greeks,</i>" that is,
	uncircumcised Gentiles (as the true reading beyond doubt is). The
	Gospel had, from the first, been preached to "the Grecians"
	or Greek-speaking <i>Jews,</i> and these "men of Cyprus and
	Cyrene" were themselves "Grecians." How, then, can we
	suppose that the historian would note, as something new and singular
	(<span class="scriptRef" com="jfb" lang="eng" ref="ac+11:22">Acts 11:22</span>), that some of the
	dispersed Christians preached to <i>them?</i></p>
