<p><b>1. For</b>—He explains in what
	respect he "labored <i>striving</i>" (<span class="scriptRef" com="jfb" lang="eng" ref="+:-"> :-</span>). Translate as <i>Greek,</i> "I <i>wish you to know how</i>
	great a conflict (the same <i>Greek</i> word as in <span class="scriptRef" com="jfb" lang="eng" ref="+:-"> :-</span>, "<i>agony</i> of a conflict" of fervent, anxious
	prayer; not conflict with the false teachers, which would have been
	impossible for him now in prison) I have for you." 
	</p>
<p><b>them at Laodicea</b>—exposed
	to the same danger from false teachers as the Colossians (compare <span class="scriptRef" com="jfb" lang="eng" ref="+:-"> :-</span>). This danger was probably the cause of his writing to
	Laodicea, as well as to Colosse. 
	</p>
<p><b>not seen my face in the
	flesh</b>—including those in Hierapolis (<span class="scriptRef" com="jfb" lang="eng" ref="+:-"> :-</span>). Paul considered himself a "debtor" to all the
	Gentiles (<span class="scriptRef" com="jfb" lang="eng" ref="ro+1:14">Romans 1:14</span>). "His
	face" and presence would have been a "comfort"
	(<span class="scriptRef" com="jfb" lang="eng" ref="col+2:2">Colossians 2:2</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="jfb" lang="eng" ref="ac+20:38">Acts 20:38</span>).
	Compare <span class="scriptRef" com="jfb" lang="eng" ref="col+1:4">Colossians 1:4</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="jfb" lang="eng" ref="col+1:7">Colossians 1:7</span>;
	<span class="scriptRef" com="jfb" lang="eng" ref="col+1:8">Colossians 1:8</span>, in proof that he had not
	<i>seen,</i> but only <i>heard of</i> the Colossians. Hence he
	strives by earnest <i>conflict</i> with God in anxious prayer for
	them, to make up for the loss of his bodily presence among them.
	Though "absent in the <i>flesh,</i> I am with you in the <i>Spirit</i>"
	(<span class="scriptRef" com="jfb" lang="eng" ref="col+2:5">Colossians 2:5</span>).</p>
