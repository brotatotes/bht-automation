<p><b>11. Justus</b>—that is, <i>righteous;</i>
	a common name among the Jews; <i>Hebrew,</i> "<i>tzadik</i>"
	(<span class="scriptRef" com="jfb" lang="eng" ref="ac+1:23">Acts 1:23</span>). 
	</p>
<p><b>of the circumcision</b>—This
	implies that Epaphras, Luke, and Demas (<span class="scriptRef" com="jfb" lang="eng" ref="col+4:12">Colossians 4:12</span>;
	<span class="scriptRef" com="jfb" lang="eng" ref="col+4:14">Colossians 4:14</span>) were <i>not</i> of the
	circumcision. This agrees with Luke's Gentile name (the same as
	Lucanus), and the Gentile aspect of his Gospel. 
	</p>
<p><b>These only,</b> c.—namely,
	of the Jews. For the Jewish teachers were generally opposed to the
	apostle of the Gentiles (<span class="scriptRef" com="jfb" lang="eng" ref="php+1:15">Philippians 1:15</span>).
	Epaphras, &amp;c., were also fellow laborers, but Gentiles. 
	</p>
<p><b>unto</b>—that is, in
	promoting the Gospel kingdom. 
	</p>
<p><b>which have been</b>—<i>Greek,</i>
	"which have been <i>made,</i>" or "have become,"
	that is, <i>inasmuch as</i> they have become a comfort to me. The
	<i>Greek</i> implies <i>comfort</i> in forensic dangers a different
	<i>Greek</i> word expresses comfort in domestic affliction [BENGEL].</p>
