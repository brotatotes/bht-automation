<p><b>12-16. And from
	thenceforth</b>—particularly this speech, which seems to have
	filled him with awe, and redoubled his anxiety. 
	</p>
<p><b>Pilate sought to release
	him</b>—that is, to gain their <i>consent</i> to it, for he could
	have done it at once on his authority. 
	</p>
<p><b>but the Jews cried</b>—seeing
	their advantage, and not slow to profit by it. If thou let this man
	go, thou art not Cæsar's friend, &amp;c.—"This was equivalent
	to a threat of <i>impeachment,</i> which we know was much dreaded by
	such officers as the procurators, especially of the character of
	Pilate or Felix. It also consummates the treachery and disgrace of
	the Jewish rulers, who were willing, for the purpose of destroying
	Jesus, to affect a zeal for the supremacy of a foreign prince"
	[WEBSTER and WILKINSON].
	(See <span class="scriptRef" com="jfb" lang="eng" ref="joh+19:15">John 19:15</span>). 
	</p>
<p><b>When Pilate . . . heard that,
	. . . he brought Jesus forth, and sat down in</b>—"upon" 
	</p>
<p><b>the judgment seat</b>—that
	he might pronounce sentence against the Prisoner, on this charge, the
	more solemnly. 
	</p>
<p><b>in a place called the
	Pavement</b>—a tesselated pavement, much used by the Romans. 
	</p>
<p><b>in the Hebrew, Gabbatha</b>—from
	its being <i>raised.</i></p>
