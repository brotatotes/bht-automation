	</p>
<p style="margin-left: 1.25em; "> A charming
	little incident, given only in <span class="scriptRef" com="jfb" lang="eng" ref="lu+11:27">Luke 11:27</span>
<span class="scriptRef" com="jfb" lang="eng" ref="lu+11:28">Luke 11:28</span>, seems to have its
	proper place here. 
	</p>
<p style="margin-left: 1.25em; "> <span class="scriptRef" com="jfb" lang="eng" ref="lu+11:28- :">Luke 11:28- :</span>: 
	</p>
<p style="margin-left: 1.25em; "> <i><b>And
	it came to pass, as He spake these things, a certain woman of</b></i><b>
</b><i><b>the company</b></i><b> </b>—out of the crowd. 
	</p>
<p style="margin-left: 1.25em; "> <i><b>lifted
	up her voice and said unto Him, Blessed is the womb that bare Thee,
	and the paps which Thou hast sucked</b></i><b>
</b>—With true womanly feeling she envies
	the mother of such a wonderful Teacher. And a higher and better than
	she had said as much before her (see on <span class="scriptRef" com="jfb" lang="eng" ref="lu+11:28- :">Luke 11:28- :</span>). How does our Lord, then, treat it? He is far from
	condemning it. He only holds up as "blessed rather" another
	class: <span class="scriptRef" com="jfb" lang="eng" ref="lu+11:28">Luke 11:28</span>: 
	</p>
<p><i><b>But he
	said, Yea rather, blessed are they that hear the word of</b></i><b>
</b><i><b>God, and keep it</b></i><b>
</b>—in other words, the humblest real
	saint of God. How utterly alien is this sentiment from the teaching
	of the Church of Rome, which would doubtless excommunicate any one of
	its members that dared to talk in such a strain! 
	</p>
