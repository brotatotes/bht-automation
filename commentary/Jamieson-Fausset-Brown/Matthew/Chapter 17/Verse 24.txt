<p><b>24. And when they were come to
	Capernaum, they that received tribute money</b>—the double drachma;
	a sum equal to two Attic drachmas, and corresponding to the Jewish
	"half-shekel," payable, towards the maintenance of the
	temple and its services, by every male Jew of twenty years old and
	upward. For the origin of this annual tax, see <span class="scriptRef" com="jfb" lang="eng" ref="ex+30:13">Exodus 30:13</span>;
	<span class="scriptRef" com="jfb" lang="eng" ref="ex+30:14">Exodus 30:14</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="jfb" lang="eng" ref="2ch+24:6">2 Chronicles 24:6</span>;
	<span class="scriptRef" com="jfb" lang="eng" ref="2ch+24:9">2 Chronicles 24:9</span>. Thus, it will be
	observed, it was not a civil, but an ecclesiastical tax. The tax
	mentioned in <span class="scriptRef" com="jfb" lang="eng" ref="mt+17:25">Matthew 17:25</span> was a
	civil one. The whole teaching of this very remarkable scene depends
	upon this distinction. 
	</p>
<p><b>came to Peter</b>—at whose
	house Jesus probably resided while at Capernaum. This explains
	several things in the narrative. 
	</p>
<p><b>and said, Doth not your
	master pay tribute?</b>—The question seems to imply that the
	payment of this tax was <i>voluntary,</i> but <i>expected;</i> or
	what, in modern phrase, would be called a "voluntary
	assessment."</p>
