<p><b>7. finished their testimony</b>—The
	same verb is used of Paul's ending his ministry by a violent death. 
	</p>
<p><b>the beast that ascended out
	of the bottomless pit</b>—<i>Greek,</i> "the wild beast . . .
	the abyss." This beast was not mentioned before, yet he is
	introduced as "<i>the</i> beast," because he had already
	been described by Daniel (<span class="scriptRef" com="jfb" lang="eng" ref="da+7:3">Daniel 7:3</span>;
	<span class="scriptRef" com="jfb" lang="eng" ref="da+7:11">Daniel 7:11</span>), and he is fully so in
	the subsequent part of the Apocalypse, namely, <span class="scriptRef" com="jfb" lang="eng" ref="re+13:1">Revelation 13:1</span>;
	<span class="scriptRef" com="jfb" lang="eng" ref="re+17:8">Revelation 17:8</span>. Thus, John at once
	appropriates the Old Testament prophecies; and also, viewing his
	whole subject at a glance, mentions as familiar things (though not
	yet so to the reader) objects to be described hereafter by himself.
	It is a proof of the unity that pervades all Scripture. 
	</p>
<p><b>make war against
	them</b>—alluding to <span class="scriptRef" com="jfb" lang="eng" ref="da+7:21">Daniel 7:21</span>,
	where the same is said of <i>the little horn</i> that sprang up among
	the ten horns on the fourth beast.</p>
