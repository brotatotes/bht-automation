<p><b>4. Titus, mine own son</b>—<i>Greek,</i>
	"my <i>genuine</i> child" (<span class="scriptRef" com="jfb" lang="eng" ref="+:-"> :-</span>), that is, converted by my instrumentality (<span class="scriptRef" com="jfb" lang="eng" ref="1co+4:17">1 Corinthians 4:17</span>;
	<span class="scriptRef" com="jfb" lang="eng" ref="phm+1:10">Philemon 1:10</span>). 
	</p>
<p><b>after the common faith</b>—a
	genuine son in respect to (in virtue of) the faith <i>common</i> to
	all the people of God, comprising in a common brotherhood Gentiles as
	well as Jews, therefore embracing Titus a Gentile (<span class="scriptRef" com="jfb" lang="eng" ref="2pe+1:1">2 Peter 1:1</span>;
	<span class="scriptRef" com="jfb" lang="eng" ref="jdg+1:3">Judges 1:3</span>). 
	</p>
<p><b>Grace, mercy, and
	peace</b>—"mercy" is omitted in some of the oldest
	manuscripts. But one of the best and oldest manuscripts supports it
	(compare <i>Notes,</i> see on <span class="scriptRef" com="jfb" lang="eng" ref="jdg+1:3- :">Judges 1:3- :</span>;
	<span class="scriptRef" com="jfb" lang="eng" ref="jdg+1:3- :">Judges 1:3- :</span>). There are many
	similarities of phrase in the Pastoral Epistles. 
	</p>
<p><b>the Lord Jesus Christ</b>—The
	oldest manuscripts read only "Christ Jesus." 
	</p>
<p><b>our Saviour</b>—found thus
	added to "Christ" only in Paul's <i>Pastoral Epistles,</i>
	and in <span class="scriptRef" com="jfb" lang="eng" ref="2pe+1:1">2 Peter 1:1</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="jfb" lang="eng" ref="2pe+1:11">2 Peter 1:11</span>;
	<span class="scriptRef" com="jfb" lang="eng" ref="2pe+2:20">2 Peter 2:20</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="jfb" lang="eng" ref="2pe+3:18">2 Peter 3:18</span>.</p>
