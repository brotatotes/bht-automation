<p><b>27.</b><i>If any one of them that believe not invites you.</i> Here follows an exception, to this effect, that if a believer has been warned, that what is set before him has been offered to an idol, and sees that there is a danger of offense being given, he sins against the brethren if he does not abstain. He shows then, in short, that care must be taken not to hurt weak consciences. </p>
<p>When he says — <i>and you are willing to go</i>, he intimates indirectly, that he does not altogether approve of it, and that it would be better if they declined, but as it is a thing indifferent, he does not choose to forbid it absolutely. And, certainly, there could be nothing better than to keep at a distance from such snares — not that those are expressly to be condemned, who accommodate themselves to men only in so far as conscience permits, <span style="color:#800000;font-weight:bold;"><sup>(601)</sup></span> but because it becomes us to proceed with caution, <span style="color:#800000;font-weight:bold;"><sup>(602)</sup></span> where we see that we are in danger of falling. </p>
<p><span style="color:#800000;font-weight:bold;">(601)</span> “<blu><span lang="fr-FR" style="">Seulement autant que faire se pent sans offenser Dieu</span></blu>;” — “Only so far as they can do so without offending God.” </p>
<p><span style="color:#800000;font-weight:bold;">(602)</span> “<blu><span lang="fr-FR" style="">Auec grand auis et prudence</span></blu>;” — “With great care and prudence.” </p>
