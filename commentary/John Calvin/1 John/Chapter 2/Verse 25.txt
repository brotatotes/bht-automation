<p>In the last place, he reminds us that <i>it is real happiness when God dwells in us</i>. The words he uses are ambiguous. They may be rendered, “<i>This is the promise which he has promised to us, even eternal life</i>. ” <span style="color:#800000;font-weight:bold;"><sup>(73)</sup></span> You may, however, adopt either of these renderings, for the meaning is still the same. The sum of what is said is, that we cannot live otherwise than by nourishing to the end the seed of life sown in our hearts. John insists much on this point, that not only the beginning of a blessed life is to be found in the knowledge of Christ, but also its perfection. But no repetition of it can be too much, since it is well known that it has ever been a cause of ruin to men, that being not content with Christ, they have had a hankering to wander beyond the simple doctrine of the gospel. </p>
<p><span style="color:#800000;font-weight:bold;">(73)</span> This, which is our version, is, no doubt, the best construction. “<i>Promise</i> ” is a metonymy for what is promised: “This is the promise, which he hath promised to us, <i>even</i> eternal life.” “Eternal life” is in apposition with “which.” — <i>Ed</i>. </p>
