<p><b>30.</b><i>If he must glory.</i> Here we have the conclusion, drawn from all that has gone before — that Paul is more inclined to boast of those things that are connected with his <i>infirmity,</i> that is, those things which might, in the view of the world, bring him contempt, rather than glory, as, for example, hunger, thirst, imprisonments, stonings, stripes, and the like — those things, in truth, that we are usually as much ashamed of, as of things that incur great dishonor. <span style="color:#800000;font-weight:bold;"><sup>(869)</sup></span> </p>
<p><span style="color:#800000;font-weight:bold;">(869)</span> <sup>“</sup><blu><span lang="fr-FR" style="">De toutes lesquelles nous n’avons point de honte coustumierement, que si nous estions vileinement diffamez</span></blu>;” — “Of all which we feel ordinarily as much ashamed, as if we had been shockingly defamed.” </p>
