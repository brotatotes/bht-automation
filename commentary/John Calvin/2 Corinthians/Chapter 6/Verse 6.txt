<p><i>Knowledge</i> may be taken in two senses — either as meaning doctrine itself, or skill in acting properly and <i>knowingly.</i> The latter appears to me the more likely, as he immediately adds — <i>the word of truth</i> The <i>Spirit</i> is taken by <i>metonymy,</i> to denote spiritual graces. Frivolous, however, is the cavil of Chrysostom, who infers from this, that the other excellences are peculiar to the Apostle, because he makes mention of the <i>Spirit</i> separately, as if <i>kindness, knowledge, pureness, armor of righteousness,</i> were from any other source, than from the Holy Spirit. He makes mention, however, of the Spirit separately, as a general term in the midst of particular instances. <span style="color:#800000;font-weight:bold;"><sup>(592)</sup></span> The <i>power of God</i> showed itself in many things — in magnanimity, in efficacy in the maintaining of the truth, in the propagation of the Gospel, in victory over enemies, and the like. </p>
<p><span style="color:#800000;font-weight:bold;">(592)</span> “<grk><blu><span lang="el-GR" style="">᾿Εν πνεύματι ἁγίω</span></blu></grk> — ’In demonstration of the <i>Holy Spirit</i> — so that I showed that the Holy Spirit wrought by me.’ It is possible, that in these words, Paul makes an allusion to the <grk><blu><span lang="el-GR" style="">χαρίσματα</span></blu></grk>, (<i>gifts</i>,) but it seems better, nevertheless, to suppose with Calvin, that he sets <blu><i>genus</i></blu> and <blu><i>species</i></blu> over against each other.” — <i>Billroth</i>. — <i>Ed.</i> </p>
