<p><i>And pollute the man.</i> Instead of the verb <i>pollute,</i> the Greek term is <grk><blu><span lang="el-GR" style="">κοινοῖ</span></blu></grk>, <i>make common;</i> as Mark, a little before, ( <span class="scriptRef" com="cal" lang="eng" ref="mr+7:2">Mark 7:2</span>,) used the phrase, <grk><blu><span lang="el-GR" style="">κοιναῖς χερσὶ</span></blu></grk>, <i>with</i> common <i>hands,</i> for <i>with</i> unclean <i>hands.</i> <span style="color:#800000;font-weight:bold;"><sup>(412)</sup></span> It is a Hebrew phrase; <span style="color:#800000;font-weight:bold;"><sup>(413)</sup></span> for, since God had set apart the Jews on the condition that they should separate themselves from all the pollutions of the Gentiles, everything that was inconsistent with this holiness was called <i>common,</i> that is, <i>profane.</i> </p>
<p><span style="color:#800000;font-weight:bold;">(412)</span> “<blu><i><span lang="fr-FR" style="">Les mains communes</span></i></blu> <blu><span lang="fr-FR" style="">pour</span></blu> <blu><i><span lang="fr-FR" style="">souillees</span></i></blu> <blu><span lang="fr-FR" style="">et</span></blu> <blu><i><span lang="fr-FR" style="">non lavees</span></i></blu> <i>;</i> ” — “<i>common hands</i> for <i>polluted</i> and <i>not washed.”</i> </p>
<p><span style="color:#800000;font-weight:bold;">(413)</span> “<blu><span lang="fr-FR" style="">C’est une facon de parler propre aux Hebrieux</span></blu>;” — “it is a mode of speaking peculiar to the Hebrews.” </p>
