<p><b>13.</b>It is further added, <i>Their throat is an open grave;</i> <span style="color:#800000;font-weight:bold;"><sup>(100)</sup></span> that is, a gulf to swallow up men. It is more than if he had said, that they were devourers (<grk><blu><span lang="el-GR" style="">ἀνθρωποφάγους</span></blu></grk> — men-eaters;) for it is an intimation of extreme barbarity, when the throat is said to be so great a gulf, that it is sufficient to swallow down and devour men whole and entire. Their<i>tongues are deceitful,</i> and, <i>the poison of asps is under their lips,</i> import the same thing, </p>
<p><span style="color:#800000;font-weight:bold;">(100)</span> This is from <span class="scriptRef" com="cal" lang="eng" ref="ps+5:9">Psalms 5:9</span>, that is, the first part, and is literally the <i>Septuagint,</i> which correctly represents the Hebrew. The last clause is from <span class="scriptRef" com="cal" lang="eng" ref="ps+140:3">Psalms 140:3</span>, and is according to the <i>Septuagint,</i> and the Hebrew, too, except that “asps,” or adders, is in the singular number. [<u>Stuart</u> ] gives the import of this figurative language different from [<u>Calvin</u> ] <i>:</i> “As from the sepulchre,” he says, “issues forth an offensive and pestilential vapor; so from the mouths of slanderous persons issue noisome and pestilential words. Their words are like poison, they utter the poisonous breath of slander.” — <i>Ed.</i> </p>
