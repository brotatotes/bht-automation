<p><strong>And as they went about to kill him</strong>,.... In the manner as zealots did, without bringing him before any court of judicature, without any charge, trial, and condemnation:</p>
<p><strong>tidings came unto the chief captain of the band</strong>; the Roman band of soldiers, who were placed near the temple, to keep the peace of the city, and persons in order; and who were more especially needful, at such a time as the feast of Pentecost, when there was such a great concourse of people in the city, and indeed always were in arms at such times b; this chief captain was Claudius Lysias, as appears from <span class="scriptRef" com="geb" lang="eng" ref="ac+23:26">Acts 23:26</span> to him the report of the disturbance was brought; or as it is in the Greek text, the "fame ascended" to him; who very likely might be in the tower of Antonia, which joined to the temple:</p>
<p><strong>that all Jerusalem was in an uproar</strong>; or in confusion, and therefore it became him, as a Roman officer, to take care to quell it, lest it should issue in sedition and rebellion.</p>
<p>b Joseph. de Bello Jud. l. 2. c. 12. sect. 1. &amp; l. 5. c. 5. sect. 8.</p>
