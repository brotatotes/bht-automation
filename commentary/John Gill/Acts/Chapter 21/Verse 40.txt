<p><strong>And when he had given him licence</strong>,.... To speak to the people, which he could not well deny him, after he had so freely declared who he was, and in so courteous a manner addressed him, and asked leave of him:</p>
<p><strong>Paul stood on the stairs</strong>; on the steps of the ascent to the castle, on the top of them:</p>
<p><strong>and beckoned with the hand unto the people</strong>; to desire silence, which he might be able to do, notwithstanding his chains; for his being bound with a chain to a soldier, did not hinder the moving and lifting up of his hand:</p>
<p><strong>and when there was made a great silence</strong>; either through the authority of the captain, who might command it, or through the desire of the people, to hear what he could say for himself:</p>
<p><strong>he spake unto them in the Hebrew tongue</strong>; which the people he spoke to best understood, and was his own mother tongue; the Alexandrian copy reads, "in his own dialect"; this was not pure Hebrew that was spoke in common in those times, but the Syro-Chaldean language:</p>
<p><strong>saying</strong>; as in the following chapter.</p>
