	 21:15 knowest (a-28) <em> Oida</em> , see Note at <span class="scriptRef" com="dsn" lang="eng" ref="1co+8:1">1 Corinthians 8:1</span> . lovest (b-15) to (b-33) This passage (vers. 15-17) illustrates the force of two Greek words for 'to love,' <em> phileo</em> and <em> agapao</em> . The former signifies the love of friendship, and is more intimate and intense. It is here translated 'I am attached to,' and in ch. 16.27 'have affection for.' <em> Agapao</em> , more often used in the New Testament, is more general, and signifies love as the settled disposition of a person rather than as an emotion. It is used for God's love to man (except in <span class="scriptRef" com="dsn" lang="eng" ref="tit+3:4">Titus 3:4</span> , where a compound word is used which embodies the word <em> phileo</em> ) and for the love of men to God. Both words are used for the love of the Father for the Son, <em> phileo</em> once only, <span class="scriptRef" com="dsn" lang="eng" ref="joh+5:20">John 5:20</span> , and <em> agapao</em> in <span class="scriptRef" com="dsn" lang="eng" ref="joh+3:35">John 3:35</span> , &amp;c.. and for the love of Christ for his own, <em> phileo</em> in <span class="scriptRef" com="dsn" lang="eng" ref="joh+11:3">John 11:3</span> and <em> agapao</em> in <span class="scriptRef" com="dsn" lang="eng" ref="joh+11:5">John 11:5</span> and elsewhere. <em> Phileo</em> is used in <span class="scriptRef" com="dsn" lang="eng" ref="joh+16:27">John 16:27</span> , of the love of the Father for the disciples, and of the love of the disciples for Christ.</p>
