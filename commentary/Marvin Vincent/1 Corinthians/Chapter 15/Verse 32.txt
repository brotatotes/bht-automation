<p>After the manner of men [<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">κατα ανθρωπον] </span></span>. As men ordinarily do, for temporal reward; and not under the influence of any higher principle or hope. </p>
<p>I have fought with beasts [<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εθηριομαχησα] </span></span>. Only here in the New Testament. Figuratively. Paul, as a Roman citizen, would not have been set to fight with beasts in the arena; and such an incident would not have been likely to be passed over by Luke in the Acts. Compare similar metaphors in ch. 4 9, <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="2ti+4:17">2 Timothy 4:17</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="tit+1:12">Titus 1:12</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="ps+22:12">Psalms 22:12</span>, <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="ps+22:13">Psalms 22:13</span>, <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="ps+22:20">Psalms 22:20</span>, <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="ps+22:21">Psalms 22:21</span>. Some, however, think it is to be taken literally. 129 They refer to the presence at Ephesus of the Asiarchs (<span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="ac+19:31">Acts 19:31</span>), who had charge of the public games, as indicating that the tumult took place at the season of the celebration of the games in honor of Diana; to the fact that the young men at Ephesus were famous for their bull - fights; and to the words at Ephesus as indicating a particular incident. On the assumption that he speaks figuratively, the natural reference is to his experience with the ferocious mob at Ephesus. There was a legend that Paul was thrown, first of all, to a lion; then to other beasts, but was left untouched by them all. In the Epistle of Ignatius to the Romans occur these words : "From Syria even unto Rome, I fight with beasts, both by land and sea, both night and day, being bound to ten leopards. I mean a band of soldiers, who, even when they receive benefits, show themselves all the worse" (v.). Compare Epistle to Tralles, 10. : "Why do I pray that I may fight with wild beasts ?" So in the Epistle to Smyrna he says : "I would put you on your guard against these monsters in human shape" [<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">θηριων των ανθρωπομορφων] </span></span>; and in the Antiochene "Acts of Martyrdom" it is said : "He (Ignatius) was seized by a beastly soldiery, to be led away to Rome as a prey for carnivorous beasts" (ii.). </p>
<p>Let us eat and drink, etc. Cited, after the Septuagint, from <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="isa+22:13">Isaiah 22:13</span>. It is the exclamation of the people of Jerusalem during the siege by the Assyrians. The traditional founder of Tarsus was Sardanapalus, who was worshipped, along with Semiramis, with licentious rites which resembled those of the Feast of Tabernacles. Paul had probably witnessed this festival, and had seen, at the neighboring town of Anchiale, the statue of Sardanapalus, represented as snapping his fingers, and with the inscription upon the pedestal, "Eat, drink, enjoy thyself. The rest is nothing." Farrar cites the fable of the Epicurean fly, dying in the honey - pot with the words, "I have eaten and drunk and bathed, and I care nothing if I die." Among the inscriptions from the catacombs, preserved in the Vatican are these : "To the divine shade of Titus, who lived fifty - seven years. Here he enjoys everything. Baths and wine ruin our constitutions, but they make life what it is. Farewell, farewell." " While I lived I lived well. My play is now ended - soon yours will be. Farewell and applaud me. " Compare Wisdom of Solomon, 2 1 - 9.</p>
