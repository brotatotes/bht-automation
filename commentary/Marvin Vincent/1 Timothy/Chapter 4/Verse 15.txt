<p>Meditate [<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">μελετα] </span></span>. Only here and <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="ac+4:25">Acts 4:25</span> (citation). Often in Class. and LXX Most translators reject the A. V. meditate, and substitute be diligent in, or practice, or take care for. Meditate, however, is legitimate, although in Class. the word commonly appears in one of the other senses. The connection between the different meanings is apparent. Exercise or practice applied to the mind becomes thinking or meditation. In LXX it represents seven Hebrew equivalents, and signifies to meditate, talk of, murmur, deligfit one's self in, attend to. Often to meditate, <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="jos+1:8">Joshua 1:8</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="ps+1:9">Psalms 1:9</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="ps+2:1">Psalms 2:1</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="ps+37:12">Psalms 37:12</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="ps+72:6">Psalms 72:6</span>; Sir. 6 7. Meditation is a talking within the mind, and issues in speech; hence to speak, as <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="ps+34:28">Psalms 34:28</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="ps+36:30">Psalms 36:30</span>; Isa 69:3. Similally, logov signifies both reason and discourse. In Lat. meditari, "to reflect," is also "to exercise in," " to practice, " as Virgil, <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="ec+1:2">Ecclesiastes 1:2</span>. In the Vulg. meditabor is the translation of murmur or mourn iu <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="isa+38:14">Isaiah 38:14</span>. The Hebrews hanah means to murmur, whisper; hence the inner whispering of the heart; hence to think, meditate, consider, as <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="ps+63:7">Psalms 63:7</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="ps+78:13">Psalms 78:13</span>. </p>
<p>Give thyself wholly to them [<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εν τουτοις ισθι] </span></span>. Lit. be in these things. The phrase N. T. o. The only parallel in LXX is <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="pr+23:17">Proverbs 23:17</span>. The meaning is that he is to throw himself wholly into his ministry. Comp. "totus in illis," Horace, <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="jas+1:9">James 1:9</span>, <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="jas+1:2">James 1:2</span>. </p>
<p>Profiting [<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">προκοπη] </span></span>. Better, advance or progress. Only here and <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="php+1:12">Philippians 1:12</span>. The verb prokoptein in <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="2ti+2:16">2 Timothy 2:16</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="2ti+3:9">2 Timothy 3:9</span>, <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="2ti+3:13">2 Timothy 3:13</span>. In LXX, see Sir. <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="le+17:0">Leviticus 17:0</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="le+2:0">Leviticus 2:0</span> Macc. 8 8. The figure in the word is uncertain, but is supposed to be that of pioneers cutting [<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">κοπτω</span></span>] a way before [<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">προ</span></span>] an army, and so furthering its advance. The opposite is ejgkoptein to cut into, throw obstacles in the way, and so hinder. See <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="ga+5:7">Galatians 5:7</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="1th+2:18">1 Thessalonians 2:18</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="1pe+3:7">1 Peter 3:7</span>.</p>
