<p>From evil [<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">απο του πονηρου] </span></span>. Possibly, from the evil one. To ponhron evil is found <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="ro+12:9">Romans 12:9</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="mt+5:39">Matthew 5:39</span>; but general N. T. usage favors the masculine, personal sense. See <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="mt+13:19">Matthew 13:19</span>, <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="mt+13:38">Matthew 13:38</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="eph+6:16">Ephesians 6:16</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="eph+1:0">Ephesians 1:0</span> F. ii. 13, 14; iii. 12; 5 18. In LXX, to ponhron evil is very common : oJ ponhrov a few times, but always of men. See <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="de+24:7">Deuteronomy 24:7</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="es+7:6">Esther 7:6</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="job+21:30">Job 21:30</span>. In Job. iii. 8, 17, to ponhron daimonion the wicked demon. The masculine is favored by the Jewish formularies, of which traces appear in the Lord 's prayer; by the unanimous tradition of Greek interpreters; by the interpretations of Tertullian and Cyprian, and by the evidence of the Syriac and Sahidic Versions. 36</p>
