<p>Shun [<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">περιιστασο] </span></span>. P o. In Pastorals, here and <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="tit+3:9">Titus 3:9</span>. Originally, to place round; to stand round. In the middle voice, to turn one's self about, as for the purpose of avoiding something : hence, avoid, shun. Often in Class., but in this sense only in later Greek. </p>
<p>Profane and vain babblings [<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">βεβηλους κενοφωνιας] </span></span>. For profane, see on <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="1ti+1:9">1 Timothy 1:9</span>. Vain is superfluous, being implied in babblings. For babblings, see on <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="1ti+6:20">1 Timothy 6:20</span>. Babble is a word of early origin, an imitative word, formed on the efforts of a young child to speak, and having its counterparts in many languages. It appears very early in English, as in Piers Plowman : </p>
<p>"And so I bablede on my bedes." Vis. 2487. </p>
<p>Bacon : "Who will open himself to a blab or a babbler ?" Ess. vi </p>
<p>Shakespeare : "Leave thy vain bibble babble." Twelfth 10 4 2. </p>
<p>They will increase [<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">προκοψουσιν] </span></span>. See on <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="ro+13:12">Romans 13:12</span>, and <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="ga+1:14">Galatians 1:14</span>. </p>
<p>Ungodiness [<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ασεβειας] </span></span>. The opposite of eujsebeia godliness, for which see on <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="1ti+2:2">1 Timothy 2:2</span>. In Pastorals, <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="tit+2:12">Titus 2:12</span>. In Paul, <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="ro+1:18">Romans 1:18</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="ro+11:26">Romans 11:26</span>, cit.</p>
