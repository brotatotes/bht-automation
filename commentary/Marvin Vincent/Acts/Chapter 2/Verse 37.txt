<p>They were pricked [<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">κατενυγησαν] </span></span>. Only here in New Testament. The word does not occur in profane Greek. It is found in the Septuagint, as <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="ge+34:7">Genesis 34:7</span>, of the grief of the sons of Jacob at the dishonor of Dinah. See, also, <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="ps+109:0">Psalms 109:0</span>. (LXX 108) <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="ps+109:16">Psalms 109:16</span> "broken in heart." The kindred noun katanuxiv occurs <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="ro+11:8">Romans 11:8</span>, in the sense of slumber (Rev., stupor). Compare <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="isa+29:10">Isaiah 29:10</span>. See, also, <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="ps+60:0">Psalms 60:0</span> (LXX 59) <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="ps+60:3">Psalms 60:3</span> oinon katanuxewv, the wine of astonishment (Rev., wine of staggering). The radical idea of the word is given in the simple verb nussw, to prick with a sharp point. So Homer, of the puncture of a spear; of horses dinting the earth with their hoofs, etc. Here, therefore, of the sharp, painful emotion, the sting produced by Peter's words. Cicero, speaking of the oratory of Pericles, says that his speech left stings in the minds of his hearers (" De Oratore, " 3, 34.</p>
