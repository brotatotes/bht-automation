<p>In all the will [<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εν παντι θεληματι] </span></span>. Lit., in every will. Will means the thing willed, as <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="lu+12:47">Luke 12:47</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="jas+5:3">James 5:3</span> <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="jas+5:0">James 5:0</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="1th+5:18">1 Thessalonians 5:18</span>. Hence used sometimes in the plural, as <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="ac+13:22">Acts 13:22</span>, shall do all my will [<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">θεληματα] </span></span>, i e., perform all the things willed by me. <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="eph+2:3">Ephesians 2:3</span>, desires, strictly willings. So here the sense is, everything willed by God. The connection is apparently with staqhte ye may stand. For a similar construction see <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="joh+8:44">John 8:44</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="ro+5:2">Romans 5:2</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="1co+14:1">1 Corinthians 14:1</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="1co+16:13">1 Corinthians 16:13</span>. As Meyer observes, this connection gives stand both a modal definition (perfect and fully assured) and a local definition (in all the will).</p>
