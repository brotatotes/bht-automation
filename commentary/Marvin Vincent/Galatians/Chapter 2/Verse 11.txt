<p>To the face [<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">κατα προσωπον] </span></span>. As <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="ac+3:13">Acts 3:13</span>. The meaning is expressed in the familiar phrase faced him down. It is, however, rarely as strong as this in N. T. Rather before the face, or in the face of, meaning simply in the sight or presence of (<span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="lu+2:31">Luke 2:31</span>), or according to appearance (<span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="2co+1:7">2 Corinthians 1:7</span>). The explanation that Paul withstood Peter only in appearance or semblance (so Jerome, Chrysostom, Theodoret, and other Fathers) is one of the curiosities of exegesis, and was probably adopted out of misplaced consideration for the prestige of Peter. </p>
<p>He was to be blamed [<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">κατεγνωσμενος ην] </span></span>. A. V. is wrong. Rev. </p>
<p>correctly, he stood condemned. Not by the body of Christians at Antioch; rather his act was its own condemnation.</p>
