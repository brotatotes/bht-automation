<p>But the writer refuses to believe that his readers will incur such a fate. Beloved [<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">αγαπητοι] </span></span>. Only here in the epistle. It often suggests an argument. See <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="1co+10:14">1 Corinthians 10:14</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="1co+14:58">1 Corinthians 14:58</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="2co+7:1">2 Corinthians 7:1</span>. </p>
<p>We are persuaded [<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">πεπεισμεθα] </span></span>. We are firmly convinced. The verb indicates a past hesitation overcome. </p>
<p>Better things [<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">τα κρεισσονα] </span></span>. The article gives a collective force, the better state of things, the going on unto perfection (ver. 1). For kreissona better, see on ch. <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="heb+1:4">Hebrews 1:4</span>. </p>
<p>That accompany salvation [<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εχομενα σωτηριας] </span></span>. Ecesqai with a genitive is a common Greek idiom meaning to hold one's self to a person or thing; hence to be closely joined to it. So in a local sense, <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="mr+1:38">Mark 1:38</span>; in a temporal sense, <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="lu+13:33">Luke 13:33</span>, next. He is persuaded that they will give heed to all things which attend the work of salvation and will enjoy all that attaches to a saved condition.</p>
