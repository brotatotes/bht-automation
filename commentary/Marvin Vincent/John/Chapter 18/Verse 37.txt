<p>Art thou then [<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ουκουν ει συ] </span></span>. The interrogative particle oujkoun, not therefore, occurs only here in the New Testament. It is ironical. In ver. 33 the emphasis is on thou : here upon king. So then, after all, thou art a king. </p>
<p>Was I born - came I [<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">γεγεννημαι - εληλυθα] </span></span>. Both perfects. Have I been born - am I come. So Rev. The Greek order is I for this have been born, etc., throwing the emphasis on Christ 's person and destiny. The perfect describes His birth and coming not merely as historical facts, but as abiding in their results. Compare this confession before Pilate (<span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="1ti+6:13">1 Timothy 6:13</span>) with the corresponding confession before the high - priest (<span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="mt+26:64">Matthew 26:64</span>). "The one, addressed to the Jews, is framed in the language of prophecy; the other, addressed to a Roman, appeals to the universal testimony of conscience. The one speaks of a future manifestation of glory, the other speaking of a present manifestation of truth. The one looks forward to the Return, the other looks backward to the Incarnation" (Westcott). </p>
<p>Of the truth [<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εκ της αληθειας] </span></span>. Literally, out of : sprung from : whose life and words issue from the truth. See on 14 6, and compare <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="joh+8:47">John 8:47</span>.</p>
