<p>Messiah cometh. The woman uses the Jewish name, which was known in Samaria. The Samaritans also expected the Messiah, basing their hopes on such Scriptures as <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="ge+3:15">Genesis 3:15</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="ge+49:10">Genesis 49:10</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="nu+24:17">Numbers 24:17</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="de+18:15">Deuteronomy 18:15</span>. They looked for Him to restore the kingdom of Israel and to re - establish the worship on Gerizim, where they supposed that the tabernacle was hidden. They called Him Hushab or Hathab, meaning the Converter, or, according to some, the Returning One. The Samaritan idea was less worldly and political than the Jewish. </p>
<p>Which is called Christ. Added by the Evangelist. Compare <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="joh+1:41">John 1:41</span>. </p>
<p>He is come [<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εκεινος] </span></span>. Emphatic; pointing to Messiah as contrasted with all other teachers. </p>
<p>He will tell [<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">αναγγελει] </span></span>. Literally, proclaim or announce. The compounded preposition ajna, the radical meaning of which is up, signifies throughout, from bottom to top. The verb is used in <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="joh+16:13">John 16:13</span>, of the revelations of the Comforter.</p>
