<p>Covetous [<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">φιλαργυροι] </span></span>. Rev. renders literally, according to the composition of the word, lovers of money. Only here and <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="2ti+3:2">2 Timothy 3:2</span>. Compare the kindred noun, <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="1ti+6:10">1 Timothy 6:10</span>. The usual word for covetous is pleonekthv (<span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="1co+5:10">1 Corinthians 5:10</span>, <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="1co+5:11">1 Corinthians 5:11</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="1co+6:10">1 Corinthians 6:10</span>). </p>
<p>Derided [<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εξεμυκτηριξον] </span></span>. Only here and ch. 23 35. Lit., to turn up the nose at. The Romans had a corresponding phrase, naso adunco suspendere, to hang on the hooked nose : i e., to turn up the nose and make a hook of it, on which (figuratively) to hang the subject of ridicule. Thus Horace, in one of his satires, giving an account of a pretentious banquet at the house of a rich miser, describes on of the guests as hanging everything to his nose; i e., making a joke of everything that occurred. The simple verb occurs at <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="ga+6:7">Galatians 6:7</span>, of mocking God.</p>
