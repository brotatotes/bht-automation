<p>Chastise [<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">παιδευσας] </span></span>. Originally to bring up a child [<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">παις] </span></span>. Hence, to instruct, so <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="ac+7:22">Acts 7:22</span>, of Moses instructed in the wisdom of the Egyptians; and <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="ac+22:3">Acts 22:3</span>, of Paul instructed in the law. To discipline or correct, as <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="heb+12:6">Hebrews 12:6</span>, <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="heb+12:7">Hebrews 12:7</span>. The word is not synonymous with punish, since it always implies an infliction which contemplates the subject's amendment; and hence answers to chastise or chasten. So <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="heb+12:10">Hebrews 12:10</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="re+3:19">Revelation 3:19</span>. In popular speech chastise or punish are often confounded. Chasten is from the Latin castus, "pure," " chaste; " and to chasten is, properly, to purify. This meaning underlies even the use of the word by Pilate, who was not likely to be nice in his choice of words. Instead of punishing him with death, he will chastise him, in order to teach him better. So Wyc., I shall deliver him amended.</p>
