<p>The fame [<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ηχος] </span></span>. Lit., noise. Rev., rumor. Only here, chapter <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="lu+21:25">Luke 21:25</span>, where the correct reading is hcouv, the roaring, and <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="ac+2:2">Acts 2:2</span>.<span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="heb+12:19">Hebrews 12:19</span> is a quotation from the Septuagint. It is the word used in <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="ac+2:2">Acts 2:2</span> of the mighty rushing wind at Pentecost. Mark uses ajkoh, in its earlier sense of a report. The same word occurs in Luke, but always in the sense in which medical writers employed it - hearing or the ears. See chapter <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="lu+7:1">Luke 7:1</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="ac+17:20">Acts 17:20</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="ac+28:26">Acts 28:26</span>. Hcov was the medical tem for sound in the ears or head. Hippocrates uses both words together : " the ears [<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ακοαι</span></span>] are full of sound [<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ηχου] </span></span>; ' and Aretaeus of the noise of the sea, as <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="lu+21:25">Luke 21:25</span>. </p>
