<p>Couch [<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">κλινιδιω] </span></span>. Luke uses four words for the beds of the sick : klinh, as verse 18, the general word for a bed or couch; krabbatov (<span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="ac+5:15">Acts 5:15</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="ac+9:33">Acts 9:33</span>), a rude pallet (see on <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="mr+2:4">Mark 2:4</span>); klinidion, a small couch or litter, as here, a couch so light that a woman could lift and carry it away. Thus, in the "Lysistrata" of Aristophanes, 916, Myrrine says : "Come now, let me carry our couch" [<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">κλινιδιον] </span></span>. The fourth term, klinarion (<span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="ac+5:15">Acts 5:15</span>), cannot be accurately distinguished from the last. The last two are peculiar to Luke. </p>
