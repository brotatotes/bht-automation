<p>Fell down [<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">προσεπεσεν] </span></span>. Mark has prosekunhsen, which often implies religious or superstitious feeling, as <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="mt+4:9">Matthew 4:9</span>, <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="mt+4:10">Matthew 4:10</span>. This is the prostration of abject terror. </p>
<p>Cried out [<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ανακραξας] </span></span>. The compound verb with ajna, up, implies what is conveyed by our phrase, lifting up the voice. See on <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="mr+5:5">Mark 5:5</span>. </p>
<p>What have I to do with thee? See on <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="mr+5:7">Mark 5:7</span>. </p>
<p>Torment [<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">βασανισης] </span></span>. See on <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="mt+4:24">Matthew 4:24</span>. Luke never uses the word of sickness, as <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="mt+8:6">Matthew 8:6</span>. See on ch. <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="lu+4:41">Luke 4:41</span>.</p>
