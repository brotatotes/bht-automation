<p>Was altered [<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εγενετο] </span></span>. Lit., became different. Luke avoids Matthew's word, metamorfwqh, was metamorphosed. He was writing for Greek readers, to whom that word represented the transformations of heathen deities into other forms. See, for instance, the story of the capture of Proteus by Menelaus, in the fourth book of Homer's "Odyssey." See on <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="mt+17:2">Matthew 17:2</span>. </p>
<p>White [<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">λευκος] </span></span>. In classical Greek very indefinite as an expression of color; being used, not only of the whiteness of the snow, but of gray dust. Its original sense is clear. All three evangelists use the word, but combined with different terms. Thus, Matthew, as the light. Mark, stilbonta, glistering (see on <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="mr+9:3">Mark 9:3</span>). Luke, ejxastraptwn (only here in New Testament), flashing as with the brilliance of lightning. Rev., dazzling.</p>
