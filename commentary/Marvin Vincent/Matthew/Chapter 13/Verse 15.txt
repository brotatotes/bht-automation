<p>Is waxed gross [<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">επαχυνθη] </span></span>. Lit., was made fat. Wyc., enfatted. </p>
<p>Are dull of hearing [<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">τοις ωσιν βαρεως ηκουσαν] </span></span>. Lit., They heard heavily with their ears. </p>
<p>They have closed [<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εκαμμυσαν] </span></span>, kata, down, muw, to close, as in musthria above. Our idiom shuts up the eyes. The Greek shuts them down. The Hebrew, in <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="isa+6:10">Isaiah 6:10</span>, is besmear. This insensibility is described as a punishment. Compare <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="isa+29:10">Isaiah 29:10</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="isa+44:18">Isaiah 44:18</span>; in both of which the closing of the eyes is described as a judgment of God. Sealing up the eyes was an oriental punishment. Cheyne (" Isaiah ") cites the case of a son of the Great Mogul, who has his eyes sealed up three years by his father as a punishment. Dante pictures the envious, on the second cornice of Purgatory, with their eyes sewed up : </p>
<p>"For all their lids an iron wire transpierces, And sews them up, as to a sparhawk wild Is done, because it will not quiet stay." Purg., 13 70 - 72. </p>
<p>Be converted [<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">επιστρεψωσιν] </span></span>. Rev., turn again; ejpi, to or toward, strefw, to turn; with the idea of their turning from their evil toward God.</p>
