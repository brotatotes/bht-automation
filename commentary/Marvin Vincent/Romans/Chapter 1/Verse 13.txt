<p>I would not have you ignorant. An emphatic expression calling special attention to what follows. Compare <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="1co+10:1">1 Corinthians 10:1</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="1th+4:13">1 Thessalonians 4:13</span>. </p>
<p>Have some fruit [<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">τινα καρπον σχω] </span></span>. the phrase, compare ch. 6 22. A metaphorical statement of what is stated literally in ver. 11. Not equivalent to bear fruit, but to gather as a harvest. Compare <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="joh+4:36">John 4:36</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="php+1:22">Philippians 1:22</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="col+1:6">Colossians 1:6</span>. Fruit is a favorite metaphor with Paul. He uses it in both a good and a bad sense. See <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="ro+7:4">Romans 7:4</span>, <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="ro+7:5">Romans 7:5</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="ro+6:22">Romans 6:22</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="ga+5:22">Galatians 5:22</span>.</p>
