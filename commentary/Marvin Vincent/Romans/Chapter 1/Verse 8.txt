<p>First [<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">πρωτον μεν] </span></span>. Not above all, but in the first place. The form of the phrase leads us to expect a succeeding clause introduced by secondly or next; but this is omitted in the fullness and rapidity of Paul 's thought, which so often makes him negligent of the balance of his clauses. </p>
<p>Through Jesus Christ. As the medium of his thanksgiving : "As one who is present to his grateful thoughts; in so far, namely, as that for which he thanks God is vividly perceived and felt by him to have been brought about through Christ." Compare <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="ro+7:25">Romans 7:25</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="col+3:17">Colossians 3:17</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="eph+5:20">Ephesians 5:20</span>. In penitence and in thanksgiving alike, Jesus Christ is the one mediator through whom we have access to God. </p>
<p>For you all [<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">περι παντων υμων] </span></span>. The preposition means rather concerning, about. </p>
<p>Is proclaimed [<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">καταγγελλεται] </span></span>. The different compounds of the simple verb ajggellw to announce, are interesting. The simple verb occurs only at <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="joh+20:18">John 20:18</span>. <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="joh+20:15">John 20:15</span> Anaggellein is to report with the additional idea of bringing tidings up to or back to the person receiving them. So <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="joh+5:15">John 5:15</span>. The impotent man brought back information to the Jews. Compare <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="mr+5:14">Mark 5:14</span>. So Christ will send the Comforter, and He will bring back to the disciples tidings of things to come. <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="joh+16:13-15">John 16:13-15</span>. See <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="ac+14:27">Acts 14:27</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="2co+7:7">2 Corinthians 7:7</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="1pe+1:12">1 Peter 1:12</span>. </p>
<p>Apaggellein is to announce with a reference to the source from [<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">απο</span></span>] which the message comes So <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="mt+2:8">Matthew 2:8</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="ac+12:14">Acts 12:14</span>. Compare <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="lu+7:22">Luke 7:22</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="lu+8:34">Luke 8:34</span>, <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="ac+5:22">Acts 5:22</span>. </p>
<p>Kataggellein is to proclaim with authority, as commissioned to spread the tidings throughout, down among those that hear them, with the included idea of celebrating or commending. So here. Compare <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="ac+16:21">Acts 16:21</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="ac+17:3">Acts 17:3</span>. Thus in ajnaggellein the recipient of the news is contemplated; in ajpaggellein the source; in kataggellein the relation of the bearer and hearer of the message. The first is found mostly in John, Mark, and Acts; the second in the Synoptists and Acts; the third only. in the Acts and Paul. </p>
<p>Throughout the whole world. Hyperbolical, but according with the position of the metropolitan church. Compare <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="1th+1:8">1 Thessalonians 1:8</span>.</p>
