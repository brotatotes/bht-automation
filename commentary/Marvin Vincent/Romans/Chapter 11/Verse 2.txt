<p>Or [<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">η] </span></span>. Compare ch. 6 3; <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="ro+7:1">Romans 7:1</span>. Confirming what precedes by presenting the only alternative in the cave. Or is omitted in the A. V. </p>
<p>Wot ye not [<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">ουκ οιδατε] </span></span>. Why should the Revisers have retained the obsolete wot here, when they have rendered elsewhere, know ye not? See <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="ro+6:16">Romans 6:16</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="1co+3:16">1 Corinthians 3:16</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="1co+5:6">1 Corinthians 5:6</span>, <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="1co+6:2">1 Corinthians 6:2</span>, etc. The phrase indicates that this cannot be thought of as true. </p>
<p>Of Elias [<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εν ηλια</span></span>] Wrong; though Rev. has retained it : of Elijah, with in in margin; probably in order to avoid the awkward circumlocution in the passage treating of Elijah, or the ambiguous in Elijah. See on in the bush, <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="mr+12:26">Mark 12:26</span>. Thucydides (1. 9) says : "Homer, in 'The handing down of the sceptre, ' said," etc.; i e., in the passage describing the transmission of the sceptre in the second book of the Iliad. A common form of quotation in the rabbinical writings.
