<p>Righteousness [<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">δικαιωμα] </span></span>. Rev., ordinance. Primarily that which is deemed right, so as to have the force of law; hence an ordinance. Here collectively, of the moral precepts of the law : its righteous requirement. Compare <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="lu+1:6">Luke 1:6</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="ro+2:26">Romans 2:26</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="heb+9:1">Hebrews 9:1</span>. See on ch. <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="ro+5:16">Romans 5:16</span>. </p>
<p>The Spirit [<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">πνευμα] </span></span>. From pnew to breathe or blow. The primary conception is wind or breath. Breath being the sign and condition of life in man, it comes to signify life. In this sense, physiologically considered, it is frequent in the classics. In the psychological sense, never. In the Old Testament it is ordinarily the translation of ruach. It is also used to translate chai life, <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="isa+38:12">Isaiah 38:12</span>; n'shamah breath, <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="1ki+17:17">1 Kings 17:17</span>. In the New Testament it occurs in the sense of wind or breath, <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="joh+3:8">John 3:8</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="2th+2:8">2 Thessalonians 2:8</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="heb+1:7">Hebrews 1:7</span>. Closely related to the physiological sense are such passages as <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="lu+8:55">Luke 8:55</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="jas+2:26">James 2:26</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="re+13:15">Revelation 13:15</span>. </p>
<p>PAULINE USAGE : </p>
<p>1. Breath, <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="2th+2:8">2 Thessalonians 2:8</span>. </p>
<p>2. The spirit or mind of man; the inward, self - conscious principle which feels and thinks and wills (<span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="1co+2:11">1 Corinthians 2:11</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="1co+5:3">1 Corinthians 5:3</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="1co+7:34">1 Corinthians 7:34</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="col+2:5">Colossians 2:5</span>). </p>
<p>In this sense it is distinguished from swma body, or accompanied with a personal pronoun in the genitive, as my, our, his spirit (<span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="ro+1:9">Romans 1:9</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="ro+8:16">Romans 8:16</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="1co+5:4">1 Corinthians 5:4</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="1co+16:18">1 Corinthians 16:18</span>, etc.). It is used as parallel with yuch soul, and kardia heart. See <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="1co+5:3">1 Corinthians 5:3</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="1th+2:17">1 Thessalonians 2:17</span>; and compare <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="joh+13:21">John 13:21</span> and <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="joh+12:27">John 12:27</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="mt+26:38">Matthew 26:38</span> and <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="lu+1:46">Luke 1:46</span>, <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="lu+1:47">Luke 1:47</span>. But while yuch soul, is represented as the subject of life, pneuma spirit, represents the principle of life, having independent activity in all circumstances of the perceptive and emotional life, and never as the subject. </p>
<p>Generally, pneuma spirit, may be described as the principle, yuch soul, as the subject, and kardia heart, as the organ of life. </p>
