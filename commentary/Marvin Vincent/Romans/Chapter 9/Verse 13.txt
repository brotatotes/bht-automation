<p>Jacob - Esau. See <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="ge+25:23">Genesis 25:23</span>. Representing their respective nations, as often in the Old Testament. <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="nu+23:7">Numbers 23:7</span>, <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="nu+23:10">Numbers 23:10</span>, <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="nu+23:23">Numbers 23:23</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="nu+24:5">Numbers 24:5</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="jer+49:10">Jeremiah 49:10</span>; compare also the original of the citation, <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="mal+1:2">Malachi 1:2</span>, <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="mal+1:3">Malachi 1:3</span>, the burden of the word of the Lord to Israel. Compare also Edom in ver. 4, synonymous with Esau in ver. 3; and Israel, ver. 5, synonymous with Jacob, ver. 2. </p>
<p>Hated [<span class="_800000"><span lang="el-GR">εμισησα] </span></span>. The expression is intentionally strong as an expression of moral antipathy. Compare <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="mt+6:24">Matthew 6:24</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="vnt" lang="eng" ref="lu+14:26">Luke 14:26</span>. No idea of malice is implied of course.</p>
