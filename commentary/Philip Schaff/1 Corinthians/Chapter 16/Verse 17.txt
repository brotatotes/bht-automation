<p><span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="1co+16:17">1 Corinthians 16:17</span>. <strong> And I rejoice at the coming of Stephanas</strong> the same, no doubt, as in <span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="1co+16:15">1 Corinthians 16:15</span> and <span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="1co+1:15">1 Corinthians 1:15</span>, <strong> and Fortunatus</strong> [1] <strong> and Achaicus</strong> of whom we only know that they were members of the Corinthian Church who had visited the apostle at Ephesus, and probably were the bearers of the Corinthian letter to him to which he refers in <span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="1co+7:1">1 Corinthians 7:1</span>, and conveyed, probably, the present letter in reply to it, <strong> for</strong> <strong> that what was lacking on your part they supplied</strong> that is, ‘the lack of your own presence has been supplied by theirs as your deputies.’
	</p>
<p style="margin-left:3.6em;"> [1] Bengel conjectures that this Fortunatus was a son of the above, and Stanley thinks him probably a stave who had received his master’s name; but (as Osiander pertinently asks) why not himself?
	</p>
