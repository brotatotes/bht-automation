<p><span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="1co+9:18">1 Corinthians 9:18</span>. <strong> What then is my reward?</strong> <strong> that when I preach the Gospel,</strong> [1] <strong> I may make the Gospel of Christ with, out charge</strong> that for the good of others I forego an undoubted right. On this principle he would have them know that he acted in everything. In particular, <span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="1co+9:20">1 Corinthians 9:20</span>. ... <strong> to the Jews I became as a Jew, that I might gain the Jews</strong> as when he circumcised Timothy (<span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="ac+16:3">Acts 16:3</span>), and when he went through certain Jewish rites during his last visit to Jerusalem (<span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="ac+21:26">Acts 21:26</span>). <strong> to them that are under the law, as under the law, not being myself under the law</strong>, [2] <strong> that I might, </strong> etc.
	</p>
<p style="margin-left:1.8em;"> [1] The received text adds “of Christ,” but with scarce any authority.
	</p>
<p style="margin-left:1.8em;"> [2] The whole of this very important clause is wanting in the received text; but it is found in the five oldest MSS., in most of the Cursives; and in all the oldest and best versions (the Peshito and Memphitic excepted). Beyond doubt, the apostle added it to prevent any misapprehension of his previous statement.
	</p>
