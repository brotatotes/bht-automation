<p><span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="1th+2:3">1 Thessalonians 2:3</span>. <strong> For.</strong> Paul proceeds to show that his manner of preaching was in keeping with the fact that the Gospel he preached was from God. It was not a private idea or invitation of his own, but a message with which God had entrusted him. This they might be sure of, from what he states in the following verses; <span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="1th+2:3-4">1 Thessalonians 2:3-4</span> intimating his ordinary habit, the succeeding verses referring to his practice at Thessalonica.
	</p>
<p><strong> Not of delusion. </strong> Even in these early days, as in our own, there were men who insinuated that the apostles were the victims of a simple-minded credulity, the dupes of ‘cunningly-devised fables.’ Delusion could not have stood the test to which Paul has referred in the preceding verse.
	</p>
<p><strong> Uncleanness </strong> seems here to mean any impure, sordid motives; though the remark of Jowett, that ‘there existed, in the age of the apostles, a connection between the form of spirituality and licentiousness,’ must be kept in mind.
	</p>
<p><strong> Guile.</strong> Paul’s preaching was sincere; he spoke because he believed. He had no ends to serve, for the attainment of which he needed to use deceit (cf. <span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="2co+2:17">2 Corinthians 2:17</span> and <span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="2co+4:2">2 Corinthians 4:2</span>).
	</p>
