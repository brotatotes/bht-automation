<p><span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="2co+3:6">2 Corinthians 3:6</span>. <strong> who also made us sufficient as ministers of the new covenant.</strong> The same word is in the original thrice repeated in different forms, and it might be rendered ‘not we are fit of our-selves. . . but our fitness is from God, who also fitted us to be ministers,’ etc. The expression “able ministers” in the Authorised Version is now unsuitable from its ambiguity, <strong> not of the letter </strong> meaning, not the letter of the law, as opposed to the spirit of the same law; but not of the law itself, considered as a code of duty, to be obeyed on pain of death, <strong> but of the spirit</strong> that word of the Gospel which, instinct with quickening power, “is spirit and life,” <strong> for the letter killeth </strong> cf. <span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="ro+4:14">Romans 4:14</span>, “the law worketh wrath,” and <span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="2co+7:9-10">2 Corinthians 7:9-10</span>, “When the commandment came, gin revived, and I died; and the commandment which was unto life (in its primary intention) I found to be unto death” (through my breach of it).
	</p>
<p>From this the apostle is led into a lengthened contrast, extending to the end of the chapter, between the two dispensations, both in their essential character as <em> killing </em> and as <em> quickening</em> and, as a consequence from this, in that <em> freedom </em> and <em> openness </em> which are distinctive of the Gospel and its ministry, and the reverse of the law.
	</p>
