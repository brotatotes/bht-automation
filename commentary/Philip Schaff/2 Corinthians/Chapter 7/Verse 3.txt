<p><span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="2co+7:3">2 Corinthians 7:3</span>. <strong> </strong> [1] <strong> I say it not to condemn you</strong> ‘I charge none of you with having said this of me; but since it has been insinuated in some quarters, I must repel it as a cruel wrong; against yourselves I have nothing to say,’
	</p>
<p style="margin-left:3.6em;"> [1] Here the apostle resumes the singular in speaking of himself (from chap. <span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="2co+2:13">2 Corinthians 2:13</span>), and after this the singular and the plural alternate to the end of the chapter.
	</p>
<p><strong> for I have said before</strong> (several times in effect, <span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="2co+1:14">2 Corinthians 1:14</span>, <span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="2co+2:4">2 Corinthians 2:4</span>, <span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="2co+3:2">2 Corinthians 3:2</span>, <span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="2co+6:11-12">2 Corinthians 6:11-12</span>), <strong> that ye are in our hearts</strong> (so <span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="php+1:7">Philippians 1:7</span>) <strong> to die together and live together</strong> an expression of enduring affection, not unknown in classical writings (and see <span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="1th+3:8">1 Thessalonians 3:8</span>).
	</p>
