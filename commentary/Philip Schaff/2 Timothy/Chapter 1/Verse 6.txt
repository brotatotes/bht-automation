<p><span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="2ti+1:6">2 Timothy 1:6</span>. <strong> I put thee in remembrance.</strong> The anxiety shows itself again. It is necessary to remind the disciple, shrinking from danger or worry, to ‘stir up’ <em> </em> (literally, <em> ‘to rekindle’) </em> the gift of God, which, as in the phrase ‘quench not the Spirit,’ is thought of as a flame that may dwindle and be extinguished if not cherished and revived.
	</p>
<p><strong> By the laying on of my hands. </strong> As being the chief ordainer (in later ecclesiastical language), he connects the gifts with his own imposition of hands rather than with that of the presbyters who assisted him (<span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="1ti+4:14">1 Timothy 4:14</span>). The passage is not without interest as bearing on the sacramental language of the Church. The outward sign was not only a symbol, but an instrument of the spiritual gift.
	</p>
