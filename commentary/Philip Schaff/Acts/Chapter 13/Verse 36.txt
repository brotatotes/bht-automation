<p><span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="ac+13:36">Acts 13:36</span>. <strong> For David, after he had served his own generation by the will of God, fell on sleep. </strong> The words of the psalm just quoted were spoken certainly by King David, but they cannot possibly find their fulfilment in him, for an everlasting salvation was promised through a Messiah who should reign for ever; but when David had accomplished his allotted work, he died, full of years and honours certainly, the man after Godâ€™s own heart, and with all his errors and shortcomings a great and magnificent sovereign; but, when he reached the usual term of human life, <em> he fell on sleep</em>.
	</p>
<p><strong> And was laid with his fathers. </strong> The word of the original Hebrew and also in the Greek version of the LXX. is a distinct recognition of the existence of the soul after death. The soul went to Sheol, the place where the souls of the departed rest; there the souls of Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob, the fathers of David, already were. It is a different expression to any of those used for death and burial. (See Gesenius on the Hebrew original of this word used <span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="ge+25:8">Genesis 25:8</span>; Gen 35:29 ; <span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="2ki+22:20">2 Kings 22:20</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="jdg+2:10">Judges 2:10</span>.
	</p>
<p><strong> Saw corruption. </strong> That is to say, the body, the mortal part, of King David.
