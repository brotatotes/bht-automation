<p><span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="ac+28:18">Acts 28:18</span>. <strong> Who, when they had examined me, would have let me go, because there was no cause of death in me. </strong> All the great Roman officials, before whose tribunals, at different periods of his career, Paul had been brought, through the enmity of his countrymen, had acquitted him of sedition and wrong-doing. He was thinking of Sergius Paulus (chap. <span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="ac+13:7">Acts 13:7</span>), Gallio (chap. <span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="ac+18:12">Acts 18:12</span>), Claudius Lysias (chap. <span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="ac+23:29">Acts 23:29</span>), Felix (chap. <span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="ac+24:25">Acts 24:25</span>), Festus and Agrippa (chap. <span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="ac+26:32">Acts 26:32</span>), but especially of the last two names, the Roman governor and the Jewish king, who so unwillingly had sent him to Rome to be judged before the imperial tribunal.
	</p>
