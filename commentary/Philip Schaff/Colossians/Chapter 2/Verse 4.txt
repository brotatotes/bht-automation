<p><span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="col+2:4">Colossians 2:4</span>. <strong> And this I say</strong>; referring to <span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="col+2:1-3">Colossians 2:1-3</span>; the remainder of this verse answering to <span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="col+2:2-3">Colossians 2:2-3</span>, and <span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="col+2:5">Colossians 2:5</span> reverting to the sympathy expressed in <span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="col+2:1">Colossians 2:1</span>.
	</p>
<p><strong> That no one may beguile you</strong>, deceive you by sophisms.
	</p>
<p><strong> With </strong> (lit., ‘in’) <strong> persuading speech. </strong> The word here used is compounded of the two occurring in <span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="1co+2:4">1 Corinthians 2:4</span> (‘enticing words’ E. V.); the idea in both cases is that of insinuating sophistical reasoning, but this expression is the stronger of the two. In classical usage the reference was to argument as contrasted with mathematical demonstration (Light-foot).
	</p>
