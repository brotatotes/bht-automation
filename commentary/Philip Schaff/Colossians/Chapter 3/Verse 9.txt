<p><span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="col+3:9">Colossians 3:9</span>. <strong> Lie not one to another.</strong> Comp. <span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="eph+4:25">Ephesians 4:25</span>. The practice of lying is referred to.
	</p>
<p><strong> Seeing that ye have put off</strong>, etc. This participle (‘having put off’) and that in <span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="col+3:10">Colossians 3:10</span> give the motive for the preceding precepts, pointing to a single act (once for all) which occurred in the past. Luther explains the clauses as imperative (so Lightfoot), a view favored by the command in <span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="col+3:12">Colossians 3:12</span>; but the former agrees better with the Apostle’s habit of thought. Comp. <span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="eph+4:22">Ephesians 4:22</span>. The figure is that of putting off and putting away a useless garment. Comp. chap. <span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="col+2:15">Colossians 2:15</span>.
	</p>
<p><strong> The old man with his deeds, or, </strong> ‘ <strong> practices;’</strong> the word usually having a bad sense in the New Testament (comp. <span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="ro+8:13">Romans 8:13</span>). The ‘flesh,’ in its ethical sense, is here personified; see on <span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="eph+4:22">Ephesians 4:22</span>; comp. also <span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="ga+5:24">Galatians 5:24</span>: ‘the flesh with the passions and the lusts thereof.’ </p>
