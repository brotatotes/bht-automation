<p><span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="eph+6:11">Ephesians 6:11</span>. <strong> Put on the whole armour, </strong> lit, panoply, a term then applied to the entire equipment, offensive and defensive (comp. <span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="eph+6:17">Ephesians 6:17</span>), of heavy armed infantry, the choice troops of those days. The several parts are spoken of in <span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="eph+6:14-17">Ephesians 6:14-17</span>. As Paul was bound by a chain to a soldier thus equipped (comp. <span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="eph+6:20">Ephesians 6:20</span>), the figure was literally at hand.
	</p>
<p><strong> Of</strong> <strong> God. </strong> Supplied by God, ‘altogether of a Divine kind, in contrast to the arms of the opponent’ (Braune).
	</p>
<p><strong> That ye may he able to stud; </strong> to stand one’s ground against foes, a military phrase, the opposite of fleeing.
	</p>
<p><strong> Against the wiles of the devil; </strong> in contrast with ‘the whole armor of God.’ ‘Wiles,’ or, stratagems, usually in a bad sense; the plural marking the variety of the attacks. Luther: ‘against the crafty assaults of the devil.’ He is the real enemy against whom we contend (<span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="eph+6:12">Ephesians 6:12</span>), and without the Divine equipment he will be too powerful for us. The existence of a personal devil (not ‘demon’ here) is assumed.
	</p>
