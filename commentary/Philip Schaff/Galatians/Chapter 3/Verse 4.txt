<p><span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="ga+3:4">Galatians 3:4</span>. <strong> Did ye experience so many things in vain? </strong> The usual rendering ‘suffer’ would refer to persecutions which the Galatians had to endure (probably from the Jews); but as we know nothing of them, it seems preferable to take the Greek verb (ἐ πάθετε) <em> </em> in the neutral and wider sense (otherwise not found in the New Testament, except perhaps in <span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="mr+5:26">Mark 5:26</span>), embracing all spiritual experiences (blessings and benefits as well) of the Galatians (comp. <span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="ga+3:3">Galatians 3:3</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="ga+3:5">Galatians 3:5</span>).
	</p>
<p><strong> If it be really in vain. </strong> This leaves room for doubt; the Apostle cannot believe that the Galatians will lose all the benefit of their spiritual experiences and continue in their folly. Others take the words in the sense: ‘if it be only in vain,’ and not much worse; since spiritual expeperiences increase the responsibility and risk. Comp. <span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="lu+12:47-48">Luke 12:47-48</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="2pe+2:21">2 Peter 2:21</span>.
	</p>
