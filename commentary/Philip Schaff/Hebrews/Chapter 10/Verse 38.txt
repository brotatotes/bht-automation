<p><span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="heb+10:38">Hebrews 10:38</span>. <strong> But</strong> (or now) <strong> my righteous one</strong> (he who belongs to God’s people) <strong> by faith shall live.</strong> As it is by faith he first gets life (as is told us in <span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="ro+1:16-17">Romans 1:16-17</span>, and <span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="ga+3:11">Galatians 3:11</span>), so it is by faith that life is preserved in the midst of judgments and of delays that are incident to them.
	</p>
<p><strong> But if he</strong> (A. V. ‘any man’) Owen and Gill, Winer and De Wette, prefer ‘he,’ which is simpler and in harmony with the context; the same person is described in the two clauses <strong> draw back </strong> the rendering of the Septuagint adopts apparently a different reading of the Hebrew text, as it does to a small extent in the following clause. The reference of those two clauses to the same person need create no difficulty. The apostasy of a professed Christian is always possible, or warnings would be needless: not necessarily the apostasy of a true Christian. The perseverance of the elect is one thing; the perseverance of a particular person is to us another.
	</p>
