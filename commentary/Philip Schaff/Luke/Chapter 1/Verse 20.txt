<p><span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="lu+1:20">Luke 1:20</span>. <strong> Thou shalt be silent.</strong> The next clause tells why.
	</p>
<p><strong> And not able to speak.</strong> ‘Dumb’ (E. V.) seems equivalent to ‘not able to speak;’ but the effect is mentioned first, then the cause. This dumbness was miraculous.
	</p>
<p><strong> Because thou didst not believe</strong>. The sign was also a punishment, and a deserved one. Abraham and Sarah went unpunished in a similar case. But Abraham had faith, and Sarah’s subsequent troubles may have been punitive. As the coming of the Messiah drew nigh, the demand for <em> faith</em> was greater; the great condition of the new covenant was thus emphasized. The punishment doubtless became a healing medicine for the soul of Zacharias, thus constrained to silent reflection.
	</p>
<p><strong> Which shall be</strong>, etc. An assertion of the truthfulness of angelic messages in general, and a justification of the punishment of the priest’s unbelief when an angel spoke to him in the holy place.
	</p>
