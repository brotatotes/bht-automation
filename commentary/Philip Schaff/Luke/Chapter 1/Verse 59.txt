<p><span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="lu+1:59">Luke 1:59</span>. <strong> On the eighth day.</strong> The proper time for administering the rite of circumcision (see <span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="ge+21:4">Genesis 21:4</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="lu+2:21">Luke 2:21</span>; comp. <span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="php+3:5">Philippians 3:5</span>).
	</p>
<p style="margin-left:0.12em;"><strong> They were about to call.</strong> The custom of naming a child at circumcision seems to have had its origin in the change of names (Abram, Abraham; Sarai, Sarah) at the institution of the rite; <span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="ge+17:5">Genesis 17:5</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="ge+17:15">Genesis 17:15</span>. Comp, also <span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="ge+21:3-4">Genesis 21:3-4</span>, as a proof that this was the custom from the first. It is said to be the usage in the East, even where circumcision is unknown, to name a child on the seventh or eighth day. Among the Greeks and Romans the name was given on the day of purification.
	</p>
<p style="margin-left:0.12em;"><strong> After the name of his father</strong>. Naming a child after the father or a relative (comp. <span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="lu+1:61">Luke 1:61</span>), was very common among the Greeks, and also among the Jews; but in earlier times a Jewish son rarely bore the name of his father.
	</p>
