<p><span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="lu+1:74">Luke 1:74</span>. <em> </em> <strong> To grant unto us</strong> <em> . </em> This gives the <em> purpose</em> both of the oath and the approaching fulfilment of it.
	</p>
<p style="margin-left:0.06em;"><strong> That we</strong> <em> . </em> This introduces <em> what</em> God purposed to grant.
	</p>
<p style="margin-left:0.06em;"><strong> Being delivered</strong> <em> </em> <strong> from the hand of our enemies</strong> <em> , </em> such as Antiochus Epiphanes and the Romans, who had interfered with the Jews in their worship.
	</p>
<p style="margin-left:0.06em;"><strong> Should serve him</strong>. Since Zacharias was a priest, this probably refers to public religious worship, as the sign of truly serving God.
	</p>
<p style="margin-left:0.06em;"><strong> Without fear</strong>, the emphatic phrase of the sentence. It means: without fear of enemies; the fear of God, which is the Old Testament token of piety, is implied in the next verse.</p>
