<p>Chaps. 1 and 2 forming the <em> first part</em> of the Gospel, narrate ‘the miraculous birth and normal development of the Son of Man.’ Chap. 1 tells of events preceding the birth of Christ, namely, the announcement of the birth of John (<span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="lu+1:5-25">Luke 1:5-25</span>); the announcement of the birth of the Messiah (<span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="lu+1:26-38">Luke 1:26-38</span>); the visit of Mary to Elizabeth (<span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="lu+1:39-56">Luke 1:39-56</span>); the birth of John (<span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="lu+1:57-80">Luke 1:57-80</span>). Both chapters are Hebraistic in style, and hence have been supposed by many to be mainly translations from some <em> document</em> originally existing in the dialect of Palestine. On the poetical compositions, see below. The objections to this part of the narrative have arisen mainly from prejudice against the remarkable facts it states. Yet the wonderful <em> Person of</em> the historical <em> Christ,</em> is the best and only satisfactory explanation of these remarkable antecedents. All
	</p>
<p style="margin-left:0.12em;"> other explanations leave the historical problem greater than ever.
