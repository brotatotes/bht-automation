<p><span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="lu+10:1">Luke 10:1</span>. <strong> These things. </strong> The events related in the last chapter. This opposes the view that the mission of the Seventy <em> preceded </em> the rejection in the Samaritan village.
	</p>
<p style="margin-left:0.06em;"><strong> Other seventy, </strong> or, ‘seventy others,’ either in addition to the Twelve, or to the messengers spoken of in chap. <span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="lu+9:52">Luke 9:52</span>. The former is more probable from the similarity of the instruction given to both. The number <em> seventy </em> may have had reference to the <em> elders </em> of Israel (<span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="ex+24:1">Exodus 24:1</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="nu+11:16">Numbers 11:16</span>), as the number <em> twelve </em> to the tribes. Some ancient authorities read ‘seventy-two’ both here and in <span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="lu+10:17">Luke 10:17</span>. Probably from a desire to conform the number to that of the Jewish Sanhedrin.
	</p>
<p style="margin-left:0.06em;"><strong> Two and two before his face, </strong> etc. The chief purpose was not to train them, as in the case of the Twelve, but actually to prepare the people in these places for His coming. The whole was a final appeal, and also a preparation for the final entry into Jerusalem. That our Lord should follow and actually visit <em> thirty-five </em> places is not remarkable, in view of His great and constant activity.
	</p>
