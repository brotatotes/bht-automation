<p><span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="lu+11:5">Luke 11:5</span>. <strong> Which of you shall have? </strong> The question is: what will happen in these supposed circumstances. The argument of this parable is: ‘If <em> selfish </em> man can be won by prayer and importunity to give,’ ‘much more certainly shall the <strong> </strong> <em> bountiful </em> Lord bestow’ (Trench). The purpose is, as in the similar parable of the unjust judge (chap. <span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="lu+18:1-8">Luke 18:1-8</span>), not only to enjoin and encourage <em> persevering </em> prayer, but to declare the certainty that prayer will be heard (<span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="lu+11:9-13">Luke 11:9-13</span>).
	</p>
<p style="margin-left:0.06em;"><strong> Three loaves. </strong> One for the traveller, one for himself, to eat with his guest, and one that there might be abundance. Allegorical interpretations abound, but must be accepted with caution. A reference to the Bread of Life is most probable.
	</p>
