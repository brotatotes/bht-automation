<p><span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="lu+13:25">Luke 13:25</span>. <strong> When once. </strong> The motive urged is, a time will come when it will be <em> altogether impossible to enter. </em></p>
<p style="margin-left:0.06em;"><strong> The master of the house. </strong> The figure is that of an entertainment made by a householder for his family.
	</p>
<p style="margin-left:0.06em;"><strong> Shut the door. </strong> The feast is to begin, and the expected guests, the members of the family, are all there. Comp. <span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="mt+25:10">Matthew 25:10</span>, where a similar thought occurs with the figure of a marriage feast.
	</p>
<p style="margin-left:0.06em;"><strong> Ye begin to stand without, and knock, </strong> etc. Knowing that the door is shut, they still cling to the false hope that they have a right within. Even in this hour the earnestness is not such as it ought to be; still there is a climax in the description of their conduct: standing, knocking, calling, and finally arguing (<span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="lu+13:26">Luke 13:26</span>). </p>
<p style="margin-left:0.06em;"><strong> I know you not whence ye are, </strong> <em> i.e., </em> ye are strangers to me, not members of my family, not expected at my feast.
	</p>
