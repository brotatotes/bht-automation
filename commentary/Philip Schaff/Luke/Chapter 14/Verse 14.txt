<p><span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="lu+14:14">Luke 14:14</span>. <strong> And then shalt be blessed, because they have not wherewith to recompense thee. </strong> This implies that the benevolence has been done without hope of return, excluding the recompense from ‘the praise of men.’ The proof that the blessing will come is added: <strong> for thou</strong> <strong> shalt be recompensed, </strong> etc. Earthly recompense amounts to nothing; it gives no blessing. All outlay with the hope of return is a mere squandering upon self. But providing for the poor, etc., is lending to the Lord; He will repay it, and His promise is the security for the blessedness referred to. Our Lord, of course, does not here encourage charity <em> for the purpose </em> of obtaining a future reward. The reward comes, but it is still of grace.
	</p>
<p style="margin-left:0.06em;"><strong> In</strong> <strong> the resurrection of</strong> <strong> the just. </strong> This refers to the <em> first </em> resurrection, and implies a <em> second </em> one (comp. <span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="1co+15:22">1 Corinthians 15:22</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="1th+4:16">1 Thessalonians 4:16</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="re+20:4-5">Revelation 20:4-5</span>). Our Lord says nothing of an intervening millennium, but the guest who spoke next evidently alludes to it.
	</p>
