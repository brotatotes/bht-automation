<p><span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="lu+14:21">Luke 14:21</span>. <strong> Being angry. </strong> God has ‘wrath’ in such circumstances.
	</p>
<p style="margin-left:0.06em;"><strong> Go</strong> <strong> out quickly. </strong> This substitution of guests took place at once, both in the parable and in fact
	</p>
<p style="margin-left:0.06em;"><strong> Into the broad ways and streets of the city. </strong> Still in the city, <em> i.e., </em> among the Jews.
	</p>
<p style="margin-left:0.06em;"><strong> The poor, </strong> etc. The very same classes as in <span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="lu+14:13">Luke 14:13</span>. From these no excuses were to be feared: ‘the blind had no field to view, the lame could not go behind his oxen, the maimed had no wife who could have hindered him from coming; only the feeling of poverty could have held them back; but this feeling also vanishes, since they must be in a friendly way <em> led in </em> by the servant’ (Van Oosterzee.) They represent the wretched and despised, ‘publicans and sinners,’ whom the ‘servant’ quickly brought in; since already they listened eagerly to the Saviour. But the absence of hindrance did not imply fitness for the feast.
	</p>
