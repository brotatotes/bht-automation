<p><span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="lu+15:24">Luke 15:24</span>. <strong> Was dead, and is alive again. </strong> Even in the parable, the father speaks figuratively of moral death <strong> ;</strong> much more in the application is it true; the state of sin is a moral death, the state of salvation a moral resurrection.
	</p>
<p style="margin-left:0.06em;"><strong> Was lost, and is found. </strong> This expresses the relation to the father. In the application <strong> :</strong> Sin is estrangement from God, salvation fellowship with God.
	</p>
<p style="margin-left:0.06em;"><strong> And they began to be merry. </strong> The same point is now reached as in the other parables; and the eating with penitent sinners (<span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="lu+15:2">Luke 15:2</span>) abundantly justified.
	</p>
