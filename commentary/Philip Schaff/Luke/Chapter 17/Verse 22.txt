<p><span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="lu+17:22">Luke 17:22</span>. <strong> Unto the disciples. </strong> The Pharisees had probably withdrawn. In what follows there is no reference whatever to the destruction of Jerusalem, as in the later discourse. The one subject is the Lord’s future coming, the sudden personal appearance of the Son of man. Some, to escape this view, maintain the groundless conjecture that Luke has inserted here a part of the discourse on the Mount of Olives, which referred to the destruction of Jerusalem.
	</p>
<p style="margin-left:0.06em;"><strong> Days will come, </strong> etc. The connection with the answer to the Pharisees is close. The kingdom has already begun, for the King, the Bridegroom, the Son of man, is here, but He will be taken away. From the answer to the Pharisees the disciples might have inferred, as they were wont to do, that our Lord would <em> now </em> establish a temporal kingdom on earth, but he discourages such false hopes.
	</p>
<p style="margin-left:0.06em;"><strong> When ye shall desire. </strong> They would have tribulation, which would make them long for Christ’s presence.
	</p>
<p style="margin-left:0.06em;"><strong> One of the days of the Son of man. </strong> The future coming or presence of the Lord is meant, since it is implied that at that time He would be absent. They might also long for the former days, for such intercourse with him as they were now enjoying.
	</p>
<p style="margin-left:0.06em;"><strong> Shall not see it. </strong> Because the hour had not yet come, because the Lord still asked for patient waiting. </p>
