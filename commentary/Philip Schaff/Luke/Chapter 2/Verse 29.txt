<p><span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="lu+2:29">Luke 2:29</span>. The words of Simeon are poetic in their form, and even in a translation retain their peculiar beauty. The song is called <em> Nunc Dimittis, </em> from the opening words in the Latin version. Like the <em> Magnificat</em> and <em> Benedictus</em>, it is adapted to the peculiar time and circumstances assigned in the narrative.
	</p>
<p style="margin-left:0.06em;"><strong> Now lettest thou thy servant depart,</strong> ‘now release thy servant’ The word ‘servant’ corresponds with <strong> Lord</strong>, <em> i.e.</em>, ‘master,’ not Jehovah. Death is regarded as the dismissal from honorable service.
	</p>
<p style="margin-left:0.06em;"><strong> According to</strong> <strong> thy word</strong>, <em> i.e.</em>, the revelation mentioned in <span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="lu+2:26">Luke 2:26</span>.
	</p>
<p style="margin-left:0.06em;"><strong> In peace</strong>, in the fullest sense of happiness, blessedness. This is the result of the release asked for.</p>
