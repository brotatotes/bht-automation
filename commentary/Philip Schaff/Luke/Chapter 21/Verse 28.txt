<p><span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="lu+21:28">Luke 21:28</span>. <strong> But when these things</strong>, <em> 1e,, </em> those spoken of in <span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="lu+21:25-26">Luke 21:25-26</span>, since the coming of the Son of man (<span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="lu+21:27">Luke 21:27</span>) would be instantaneous.
	</p>
<p style="margin-left:0.06em;"><strong> Begin to come to pass. </strong> This suggests their continuance, but the close of the verse indicates a brief period.
	</p>
<p style="margin-left:0.06em;"><strong> Look up. </strong> The word means to raise one’s self from a stooping posture, and is here applied to those previously bowed under tribulations. The idea of joyful hope is of course implied, as in the other phrase: <strong> lift up your heads, </strong> which however suggests more strongly the idea of expectation.
	</p>
<p style="margin-left:0.06em;"><strong> Because your redemption</strong> (completed at and by Christ’s appearing) <strong> draweth nigh. </strong> The same events which terrified the world (<span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="lu+21:25-26">Luke 21:25-26</span>) are to awaken these feelings in Christians. This is to be our comfort also during the intervening period, if we are cast down by the prospect, or fact, of a general rejection of Christ.
	</p>
<p style="margin-left:0.06em;"> <span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="lu+21:29-33">Luke 21:29-33</span> are the same as in the parallel passages.
	</p>
