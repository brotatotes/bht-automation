<p><span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="lu+23:11">Luke 23:11</span>. <strong> And Herod. </strong> Failing of his expected entertainment, the monarch seeks amusement in the way here narrated. The motive was resentment at the silence of Jesus, though actual contempt was doubtless felt.
	</p>
<p style="margin-left:0.06em;"><strong> With his soldiery </strong> (a peculiar word), <em> i.e., </em> his attending body guard.
	</p>
<p style="margin-left:0.06em;"><strong> Set him at nought, </strong> treated Him contemptuously, <strong> and mocked him, </strong> with words and actions alike.
	</p>
<p style="margin-left:0.06em;"><strong> And arraying him in gorgeous apparel. </strong> This garment was put on in mockery, and hence brilliant. It may have been the same scarlet cloak which is spoken of in <span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="mt+27:28">Matthew 27:28</span>, and thus indicated contempt of His claims to royalty, or a <em> white robe, </em> such as candidates for office wore. The sneer in the latter case is obvious. Still the word itself does not mean ‘white,’ and the question is an open one.
	</p>
<p style="margin-left:0.06em;"><strong> Sent him back to Pilate. </strong> This may have been designed to conciliate Pilate, but it is in keeping with the frivolous conduct of Herod throughout.
	</p>
