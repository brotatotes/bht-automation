<p><span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="lu+23:13">Luke 23:13</span>. <strong> When he had called together, </strong> etc. After the return from Herod. Matthew (<span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="mt+27:17">Matthew 27:17</span>) alludes to this.
	</p>
<p style="margin-left:0.06em;"><strong> And the people. </strong> The multitude, doubtless now more numerous, was called to hear a proposal in which their wish was concerned.
	</p>
<p style="margin-left:0.06em;"> <span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="lu+23:14">Luke 23:14</span>. <strong> Said unto them. </strong> Luke, who gives the charge most fully (<span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="lu+23:2">Luke 23:2</span>), also states the reply of Pirate more formally.
	</p>
<p style="margin-left:0.06em;"><strong> Perverteth. </strong> Here the word (Pilate’s) is milder than that of <span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="lu+23:2">Luke 23:2</span> (the Sanhedrin’s).
	</p>
<p style="margin-left:0.06em;"><strong> Before you. </strong> John tells of a private interview, which was the main reason of Pilate’s state of mind, but both Matthew and Mark speak of a public questioning in distinction from this.
	</p>
