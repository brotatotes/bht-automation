<p><span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="lu+4:38-41">Luke 4:38-41</span>. HEALING OF SIMON’S WIFE’S MOTHER, ETC. See on <span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="mt+8:14-17">Matthew 8:14-17</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="mr+1:29-34">Mark 1:29-34</span>. The definite language of <span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="mr+1:29">Mark 1:29</span>, as well as that of <span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="lu+4:38">Luke 4:38</span>, show that this miracle occurred immediately after the one last recorded. (The deviation from the chronological order in Matthew’s account can be readily explained.)
	</p>
<p style="margin-left:0.06em;"><strong> With a great fever</strong> (<span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="lu+4:33">Luke 4:33</span>). A technical medical expression, used by Luke only.
	</p>
<p style="margin-left:0.06em;"><strong> And he stood over her</strong> (<span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="lu+4:39">Luke 4:39</span>). Peculiar to Luke, but implied in the other accounts.
	</p>
<p style="margin-left:0.06em;"><strong> Laid his hands on every one of them </strong> (<span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="lu+4:40">Luke 4:40</span>). <strong> </strong> Peculiar to <strong> </strong> Luke. The toilsome nature of our Lord’s activity is thus brought out The crying out of the demons is more distinctly asserted here (<span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="lu+4:41">Luke 4:41</span>), but the prohibition mentioned by Mark includes this. ‘Christ’ (<span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="lu+4:41">Luke 4:41</span>) is to be omitted.
	</p>
