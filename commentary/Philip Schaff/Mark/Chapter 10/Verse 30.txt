<p><span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="mr+10:30">Mark 10:30</span>. <strong> Now in this time.</strong> So Luke. It is implied, though not very plainly, in Matthew’s account.
	</p>
<p><strong> Houses</strong>, etc. This repetition is peculiar to Mark, and characteristic
	</p>
<p><strong> Mothers</strong>. ‘Nature gives us only one, but love, many’(see <span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="ro+16:13">Romans 16:13</span>). <em> We </em> do not find ‘fathers’ here, or ‘wives’ (‘wife’ being of doubtful authority in <span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="mr+10:29">Mark 10:29</span>), the <em> new </em> relations being spiritual. The <em> </em> former is omitted, probably for the reason suggested in <span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="mt+23:9">Matthew 23:9</span> (‘One is your father,’ etc.), and the omission then contains a lesson. Christian love and hospitality literally fulfil this promise. But the hope of such a reward is not the proper motive. The <em> </em> promise is made only to those who do this ‘for my sake and the gospel’s sake.’
	</p>
<p><strong> With persecutions</strong>. According to the gospel the persecutions are a part of our best possessions (<span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="mt+5:12">Matthew 5:12</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="ro+5:3">Romans 5:3</span>, etc.), and really prevent the others from becoming a curse. This phrase not only serves to spiritualize the whole promise, but to guard against its misuse.
	</p>
