<p><span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="mr+10:36-41">Mark 10:36-41</span>. See on <span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="mt+20:21-24">Matthew 20:21-24</span>. This account has some marks of independence.
	</p>
<p><strong> In thy glory</strong> (<span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="mr+10:37">Mark 10:37</span>), instead of ‘in thy kingdom’ (Matthew). Mark also omits ‘by my Father’ after ‘prepared’ (<span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="mr+10:40">Mark 10:40</span>). The two clauses about His baptism (<span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="mr+10:38-39">Mark 10:38-39</span>) are peculiar to this Gospel; the best authorities omit them in Matthew. We find a vividness too in use of the present tense: <em> </em> <strong> that I drink.</strong> <em> ...</em> <strong> that I am baptised with </strong> (<span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="mr+10:38-39">Mark 10:38-39</span>). ‘The Lord had already the cup of His suffering at His lips: was already, so to speak, sprinkled with the first drops of the spray of His baptism of blood’(Alford).
	</p>
<p><strong> Began</strong> (<span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="mr+10:41">Mark 10:41</span>) is peculiar to Mark, intimating at the feeling of the disciples was soon interrupted.
	</p>
