<p><span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="mr+16:12">Mark 16:12</span>. <strong> After these things.</strong> This expression, peculiar to this section, marks definitely a <em> second </em> appearance, after the ‘first’ (<span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="mr+16:9">Mark 16:9</span>). The appearance to Peter is not mentioned; the author is emphasizing the unbelief of the eleven, so that he chooses a revelation to two, not of their number.
	</p>
<p style="margin-left:0.12em;"><strong> Was manifested</strong> (a different word from that used in <span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="mr+16:9">Mark 16:9</span>), etc. See <span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="lu+24:13-35">Luke 24:13-35</span>, where this manifestation is narrated with richness of detail.
	</p>
<p style="margin-left:0.12em;"><strong> In another form,</strong> so that they did not recognize Him. Luke, says: ‘their eyes were holden.’ But there was some actual difference in the bodily appearance of our Lord.
	</p>
<p style="margin-left:0.12em;"><strong> Two of them</strong>, of the disciples in the wider sense (<span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="mr+16:10-11">Mark 16:10-11</span>).
	</p>
<p style="margin-left:0.12em;"><strong> As they walked,</strong> to Emmaus. The manifestation took place at the close of the walk, but this is the language of brevity. Had the account been more explicit, a captious criticism would have asserted that this verse was copied from Luke.
	</p>
