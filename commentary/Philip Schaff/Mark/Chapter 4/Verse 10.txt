<p><span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="mr+4:10">Mark 4:10</span>. <strong> Alone.</strong> This refers to a temporary withdrawal, when His disciples ‘came’ to Him (Matthew), for He evidently spoke further to the multitude (<span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="mt+13:24-35">Matthew 13:24-35</span>).
	</p>
<p style="margin-left:0.12em;"><strong> They that were about him with the twelve.</strong> Matthew and Luke say less definitely: ‘the disciples.’ What follows was spoken neither to the multitude nor to the Twelve alone.
	</p>
<p style="margin-left:0.12em;"><strong> Asked of him the parables</strong> The plural is the more correct form. Matthew says more definitely: ‘Why speakest thou unto them in parables?’ and Luke: ‘What might this parable be?’ The answer in all three accounts is: <em> first</em>, a reason why He thus taught, and, <em> secondly</em>, the exposition of this particular parable. Both questions must have been asked, as is implied in the indefinite statement of this verse. This was precisely the purpose: that those who would seek might know ‘the mystery,’ and those who would not put forth this effort, might not.
	</p>
