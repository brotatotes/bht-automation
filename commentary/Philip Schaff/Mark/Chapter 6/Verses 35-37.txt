See on <span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="mt+14:15-16">Matthew 14:15-16</span>, and especially <span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="joh+6:5-7">John 6:5-7</span>. From the latter account we learn that our Lord put a question to Philip, who had probably been the spokesman, to try him, and that he answered in language more generally stated here.
	</p>
<p style="margin-left:0.12em;"><strong> Two hundred pennyworth.</strong> This sum is mentioned mainly because it was an estimate of how much it would cost to give to each one a little (<span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="joh+6:7">John 6:7</span>). Some have supposed that this was the amount of money they had in their common treasury, but it seems rather to be mentioned as a sum <em> beyond</em> their ability to pay. It was = $ 30 , or <em> £</em> 6 , 5 , a large amount of money then, since a denarius, or ‘penny,’ was the hire of a day’s labor.
	</p>
