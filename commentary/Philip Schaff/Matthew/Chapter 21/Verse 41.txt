<p><span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="mt+21:41">Matthew 21:41</span>. <strong> They say unto him</strong>, <em> i.e.</em>, the rulers. Probably the people joined in the answer, as the parable was spoken to them also (<span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="lu+20:9">Luke 20:9</span>) Mark and Luke seem to put these words in the mouth of our Lord.
	</p>
<p style="margin-left:0.12em;"><strong> He will miserably destroy</strong> <strong> those miserable men</strong>. The order and repetition of the original might be thus reproduced: ‘these wretches will he wretchedly destroy.’ The rulers, whether wittingly or unwittingly, condemn themselves.
	</p>
<p style="margin-left:0.12em;"><strong> To other husbandmen</strong>. An unconscious prophecy, if they did not yet understand the parable; daring hypocrisy, if they did. The destruction of the husbandmen points to the destruction of Jerusalem, which is therefore the coming of the Lord of the vineyard (<span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="mt+21:40">Matthew 21:40</span>). In that case the heir who was killed becomes Himself ‘the lord of the vineyard; ‘comp, what follows with Peter’s citation of the same passage shortly after the day of Pentecost (<span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="ac+3:10">Acts 3:10</span>).
	</p>
