<p><span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="mt+24:1">Matthew 24:1</span>. <strong> From the temple,</strong> <em> i.e..,</em> the exclusively Jewish part, inclosed from the court of the Gentiles. He never returned, and henceforth the temple was virtually desolate. The Apostles returned, holding out mercy still; the last rejection recorded is that of Paul (<span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="ac+21:27">Acts 21:27</span> ff.), who was even accused of polluting it
	</p>
<p style="margin-left:0.18em;"><strong> Was departing.</strong> He lingered for a time.
	</p>
<p style="margin-left:0.18em;"><strong> His disciples. </strong> Mark (<span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="mr+13:1">Mark 13:1</span>): ‘one of his disciples.’
	</p>
<p style="margin-left:0.18em;"><strong> To shew him the buildings of the temple,</strong> <em> i.e..,</em> all the structures in the inclosure (see note on p. 171 ), especially the stones (comp. Mark and Luke), as His answer (<span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="mt+24:2">Matthew 24:2</span>) indicates. The immense stones (some of them forty-five cubits long, five high, and six broad) could be best seen from the court of the Gentiles; so also the great number of outer structures, some of them still in process of erection. The latter fact gives additional point to the prediction.
	</p>
