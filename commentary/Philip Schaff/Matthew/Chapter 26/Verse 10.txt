<p><span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="mt+26:10">Matthew 26:10</span>. <strong> But Jesus knowing it,</strong> <em> i.e.,</em> the whole case, as is evident. <strong> Said unto them. </strong> He answers, not Judas, but the others. Yet this was a rebuke to Judas, and helped to ripen his treacherous design.
	</p>
<p style="margin-left:0.18em;"><strong> Why trouble ye the woman?</strong> The chief concern is for the affectionate Mary. Her noble act of love had been misjudged, and remarks made which would disquiet or confuse her conscience. (See <span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="mr+14:2">Mark 14:2</span>.) She is defended and encouraged first of all. The impulses of genuine love to Christ, or His people, are often thus checked, even by real Christians, who for the time being speak the cold and selfish language of the world.
	</p>
<p style="margin-left:0.18em;"><strong> A good work. </strong> Christ measured the moral quality of the act by the motive, the disciples by its seeming utility. This utilitarian age presents many temptations to follow the lead of Judas.
	</p>
