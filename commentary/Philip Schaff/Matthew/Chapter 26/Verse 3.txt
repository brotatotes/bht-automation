<p><span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="mt+26:3">Matthew 26:3</span>. <strong> Then gathered together.</strong> The uncertainty of His enemies, despite their hostile desire, is in contrast with His clear statement of what would come to pass. ‘And the scribes ‘is probably inserted from <span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="mr+14:1">Mark 14:1</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="lu+22:2">Luke 22:2</span>, Yet ‘the scribes ‘formed a part of the Sanhedrin, which was probably assembled on this occasion. Unto the court. Not the palace, but the court it inclosed; comp. <span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="mt+26:69">Matthew 26:69</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="lu+22:55">Luke 22:55</span>.
	</p>
<p style="margin-left:0.06em;"><strong> Who was called Caiaphas.</strong> Josephus says he was originally called ‘Joseph; ‘the form here used may point to an additional name. John (<span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="joh+11:51">John 11:51</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="joh+18:13">John 18:13</span>) says he was ‘high-priest that same year,’ and son-in-law of Annas, who had also been high priest and was still called so (<span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="ac+4:5">Acts 4:5</span>). The office was hereditary in the family of Aaron, and held for life; but Antiochus Epiphanes (B. C. 160 ) sold it to the highest bidders, and the Romans removed the incumbent at pleasure. Caiaphas was appointed by a Roman proconsul, his predecessor having been deposed, and was removed by a Roman emperor about six years after this time. Though of the party most hostile to the Romans, he and his associates raised the cry: ‘We have no king but Cesar’ (<span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="joh+19:15">John 19:15</span>). The direct connection of this event is probably with the close of chap. 23
