<p><span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="mt+26:31">Matthew 26:31</span>. <strong> All ye.</strong> Not without a contrast to Judas who had gone.
	</p>
<p><strong> Shall be offended; </strong> ‘made to stumble,’ ‘fall away.’
	</p>
<p><strong> In me,</strong> <em> i.e.,</em> His betrayal and sufferings, <strong> this night, </strong> would be made by them an occasion of stumbling, a snare; they would forsake and deny Him.
	</p>
<p><strong> For it is written</strong> (<span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="zec+13:7">Zechariah 13:7</span>). Our Lord, knowing what would come, knew also that it was designed to fulfil this prophecy.
	</p>
<p><strong> I will smite the Shepherd,</strong> etc. In the prophecy: ‘Smite,’ a command. This change suggests that the coming sufferings were not only at the hands of men, but in some proper sense inflicted by God Himself; God smote Him instead of His people (comp. <span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="isa+53:4-10">Isaiah 53:4-10</span>). ‘The Shepherd ‘is Christ, and in the original prophecy meant the Messiah (comp. <span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="zec+11:7-14">Zechariah 11:7-14</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="zec+12:10">Zechariah 12:10</span>).
	</p>
<p><strong> And the sheep of the flock; </strong> the Apostles, but with a wider reference also to the Jewish people.
	</p>
<p><strong> Scattered abroad.</strong> This occurred both in the case of the disciples, and of the Jews, after they had rejected the smitten Shepherd.
	</p>
