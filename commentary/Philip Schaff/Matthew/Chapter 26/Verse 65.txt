<p><span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="mt+26:65">Matthew 26:65</span>. <strong> Then the high-priest rent his clothes, </strong> his upper-garment, not the high-priestly robe, which was worn only in the temple. Rending the clothes was a sign of mourning or of indignation (<span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="ac+14:14">Acts 14:14</span>), but in the former sense was forbidden to the high-priest (<span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="le+10:6">Leviticus 10:6</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="le+21:10">Leviticus 21:10</span>). Instances of the high-priests using this sign of indignation occur in the first Book of the Maccabees and Josephus. The Jews found in <span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="2ki+18:37">2 Kings 18:37</span>, a precedent for rending the clothes on occasions of real or supposed blasphemy. Such an action, at first natural, became a matter of special regulation, hence more theatrical than real.
	</p>
<p><strong> He hath spoken blasphemy. </strong> This implies: ( 1 .) That our Lord had on oath claimed to be Divine, else it could not be called blasphemy; ( 2 .) that the high-priest, while compelling Him to be a witness in His own case at once declared His testimony to be <em> false</em>, else it could not be called blasphemy. Every one who hears of Jesus now must accept either His testimony respecting Himself or the verdict of the high-priest.
	</p>
<p><strong> What further need,</strong> etc. They had difficulty in getting witnesses. The true witness answered; they refused to believe, but found His confession sufficient for their purpose.
	</p>
<p><strong> Behold now ye have heard the blasphemy.</strong> The high-priest assumes that they all agree with him, the whole verdict being spoken in hot haste.
	</p>
