<p><span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="mt+3:3">Matthew 3:3</span>. <strong> For. </strong> He thus preached, because he was sent to fulfil this prophecy.
	</p>
<p style="margin-left:0.12em;"><strong> Is he. </strong> All the Evangelists and John himself thus apply the prophecy, which is more than a typical one. Even if the primary reference was to a return from captivity, the entire fulfilment was in the mission of the Baptist. <strong> Isaiah, <span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="isa+40:3">Isaiah 40:3</span></strong>. Here, as in <span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="mr+1:3">Mark 1:3</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="lu+3:4">Luke 3:4</span>, the Evangelist quotes; in <span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="joh+1:23">John 1:23</span>, the Baptist applies the prophecy to himself.
	</p>
<p style="margin-left:0.12em;"><strong> The voice. </strong> From the Septuagint. Literally ‘a voice.’ Some suppose John is represented as a ‘voice,’ in contrast with Christ as ‘the Word,’ others because his life was vocal,’ the whole man being as it were a sermon,’ perhaps with reference to the long silence since the prophet Malachi.
	</p>
<p style="margin-left:0.12em;"><strong> In the wilderness </strong> is connected, in the Hebrew, with ‘prepare,’ here with ‘crying.’ The sense remains the same. ‘The wilderness’ here (and probably in the original prophecy) refers to the spiritually desolate condition of God’s people.
	</p>
<p style="margin-left:0.12em;"><strong> The way of the Lord, </strong> <em> i.e.,</em> Jehovah. By implication the coming One was Jehovah. An allusion to the Eastern custom of removing obstacles before the approach of a <strong> </strong> royal personage. Hence the prophecy did not primarily refer to the return of the Jews from captivity, when no King was present.</p>
