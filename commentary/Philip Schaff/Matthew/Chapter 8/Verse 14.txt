<p><span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="mt+8:14">Matthew 8:14</span>. <strong> And when Jesus was come into Peter’s house. </strong> At Capernaum (comp. <span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="mr+1:21">Mark 1:21</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="mr+1:29">Mark 1:29</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="lu+4:31">Luke 4:31</span>; <span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="lu+4:35">Luke 4:35</span>). Bethsaida, however, is called (<span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="joh+1:45">John 1:45</span>) ‘the city of Andrew and Peter.’ When or why they removed is unknown. This miracle, together with others in ‘his own city’ (chap. <span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="mt+9:1">Matthew 9:1</span>), occurred quite early in His ministry.
	</p>
<p style="margin-left:0.12em;"><strong> His wife’s mother.</strong> Peter was therefore married. Jerome and modem Romanist expositors infer that the wife was dead from the fact that the mother when healed ‘ministered unto them;’ but were that the case Peter must have married again (comp. <span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="1co+9:5">1 Corinthians 9:5</span>). ‘Legend says that her name was Perpetua or Concordia.’
	</p>
<p style="margin-left:0.12em;"><strong> Lying</strong>, prostrate, confined to bed with fever.
	</p>
