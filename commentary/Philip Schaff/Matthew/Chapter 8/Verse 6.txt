<p><span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="mt+8:6">Matthew 8:6</span>. <strong> Lord.</strong> This word, used by the elders, probably means more than a title of respect and less than an acknowledgment of Messiahship.
	</p>
<p style="margin-left:0.12em;"><strong> My servant,</strong> lit. ‘boy,’ as in many languages. His personal house servant (‘held in honor by him,’ Luke), as distinguished from the soldiers who served under him
	</p>
<p style="margin-left:0.12em;"><strong> Lieth at home, </strong> lit. ‘has been thrown down,’ or ‘prostrated at my house.’ Exceedingly appropriate in describing the effect of the disease.
	</p>
<p style="margin-left:0.12em;"><strong> Sick of the palsy, </strong> grievously tormented. Luke says: ‘ready to die.’ Paralysis or ‘palsy’ was a common disease in those days (comp. <span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="mt+4:24">Matthew 4:24</span>). Alford: ‘The disease in the text may have been an attack of <em> tetanus,</em> which the ancient physicians included under paralysis, and which is more common in hot countries than with us. It can hardly have been apoplexy, which usually deprives of sensation.’
	</p>
