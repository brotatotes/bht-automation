<p><span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="ro+1:23">Romans 1:23</span>. <strong> And changed. </strong> Comp. the strikingly similar passage, <span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="ps+106:20">Psalms 106:20</span>. ‘Exchanged’ is the meaning, as in <span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="ro+1:25">Romans 1:25</span>, where, however, a stronger word is used.
	</p>
<p style="margin-left:0.06em;"><strong> The glory, </strong> etc. God’s majesty, perfection, etc., made known as stated in <span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="ro+1:19-21">Romans 1:19-21</span>.
	</p>
<p style="margin-left:0.06em;"><strong> Incorruptible; </strong> introduced to mark the folly of the exchange.
	</p>
<p style="margin-left:0.06em;"><strong> For a likeness of an image. </strong> This expression refers both to the grosser and the more refined form of idolatry: the common people saw in the idols the gods themselves; the cultivated heathen regarded them as symbolical representations, etc.
	</p>
<p style="margin-left:0.06em;"><strong> Of corruptible man; </strong> so the Greeks universally.
	</p>
<p style="margin-left:0.06em;"><strong> Of birds, </strong> etc. The Egyptians worshipped idols of varied bestial forms, and in Rome this worship prevailed extensively. The order marks a descent to the lowest kind of idolatrous representation; even the images of <strong> reptiles </strong> were worshipped.
	</p>
