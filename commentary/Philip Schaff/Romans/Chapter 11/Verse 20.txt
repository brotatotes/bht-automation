<p><span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="ro+11:20">Romans 11:20</span>. <strong> Well. </strong> Not necessarily ironical; but an admission of both the fact and the purpose of the breaking off of the branches. The Apostle, however, passes immediately to the cause of this state of things, ‘as one which must prevent haughtiness, and inspire fear and anxiety respecting the duration of the state of grace; assigning the reason in <span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="ro+11:21">Romans 11:21</span> ’ (Meyer).
	</p>
<p><strong> By their</strong> <strong> unbelief. </strong> The form is the same as in the other phrase <strong> by thy faith</strong>; the Greek article in each case being equivalent to the possessive pronoun, though both terms may be used abstractly. ‘Thou’ is emphatic, while <strong> standest </strong> refers to the position as a branch, rather than to standing as opposed to falling.
	</p>
<p><strong> Be not highminded:</strong> be not haughty. A few older manuscripts give a slightly different form (answering to that in chap. <span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="ro+12:16">Romans 12:16</span>), which, however must be taken in the same sense.
	</p>
<p><strong> But fear. </strong> ‘Fear is oppposed, not to faith, but to superciliousness and security’ (Bengel). The reason is added in <span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="ro+11:21">Romans 11:21</span>, with which these clauses should be joined.
	</p>
