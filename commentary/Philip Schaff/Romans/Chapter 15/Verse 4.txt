<p><span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="ro+15:4">Romans 15:4</span>. <strong> For. </strong> This introduces a justification of the previous citation, and a preparation for the subject which follows, the duty of being ‘of the same mind one toward another’ (<span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="ro+15:6">Romans 15:6</span>).
	</p>
<p style="margin-left:0.06em;"><strong> Whatsoever things were written aforetime.</strong> Evidently including the whole Old Testament.
	</p>
<p style="margin-left:0.06em;"><strong> Were written for our</strong> <strong> learning; </strong> to instruct us also; the immediate design does not preclude this further and permanent one, a principle which underlies many other citations made by the Apostle.
	</p>
<p style="margin-left:0.06em;"><strong> That we through the</strong> <strong> patience and through the comfort of the Scriptures. </strong> This is the literal rendering of the better established reading. ‘Of the Scriptures’ qualifies both words: ‘the patience and comfort produced by a study of the Scriptures; the repeated ‘through’ does not disconnect them, but gives rhetorical emphasis. ‘Patience’ is especially needed to hold out in not pleasing ourselves (<span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="ro+15:1">Romans 15:1</span>), and ‘comfort’ or ‘consolation,’ that we may find joy therein.
	</p>
<p style="margin-left:0.06em;"><strong> Might have our hope, </strong> lit., ‘the hope,’ the specific hope of the Christian, possessing more and more of it by means of the patience, etc. Those who neglect the <strong> </strong> Old Testament Scriptures may well remember that this expresses the Christian experience of an inspired Apostle.
	</p>
