<p><span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="ro+16:5">Romans 16:5</span>. <strong> And salute</strong>; the verb is properly supplied, but the clause is grammatically connected with <span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="ro+16:3">Romans 16:3</span>, and should form a part of <span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="ro+16:4">Romans 16:4</span>.
	</p>
<p style="margin-left:0.12em;"><strong> The church that is in their house. </strong> The early Christians had, as a rule, no public place of assembly, but probably met in the houses of the more prominent brethren. In larger cities there seem to have been several such places of meeting; and one of these is here referred to. The language of Justin Martyr sustains this view. The same persons were doubtless wont to gather there, forming a household parish of the one Roman congregation. As the city was four miles in circumference, there was a necessity for a number of these assemblies.
	</p>
<p style="margin-left:0.12em;"><strong> Epenetus my beloved. </strong> All the persons named, from this point to the close of the section, are unknown. ‘The legends of the Fathers made the most of them martyrs and bishops, and the <em> Synopsis </em> of Dorotheus misplaces the most of them among the seventy disciples’ (Meyer).
	</p>
<p style="margin-left:0.12em;"><strong> The first-fruits of Asia onto Christ; </strong> <em> i.e.</em>, among the first converts in the Roman province of Asia, of which Ephesus was the chief city. Comp. <span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="1co+16:15">1 Corinthians 16:15</span>, where the same expression occurs. There ‘Achaia’ is the correct reading; here ‘Asia’ is much better supported. The change may have arisen from the fact that this Epistle was written in Corinth, the capital city of Achaia.
	</p>
