<p><span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="ro+4:18">Romans 4:18</span>. <strong> Who. </strong> Abraham; ‘who’ in <span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="ro+4:17">Romans 4:17</span> (referring to God) has no equivalent in the Greek, which does not present the ambiguity of our version. <span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="ro+4:18-22">Romans 4:18-22</span>, which may constitute a separate paragraph, give a more detailed description of the faith of Abraham; grammatically this verse is parallel with ‘who is the father of us all’ (<span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="ro+4:16">Romans 4:16</span>).
	</p>
<p><strong> Against hope believed in hope. </strong> Abraham’s belief rested ‘upon hope’ (the literal sense), but it was also contrary to hope, <em> i.e.</em>, contrary to external hope, to what might naturally be hoped for. A similar antithesis is continued throughout
	</p>
<p><strong> That he might become father, </strong> etc. This was the end of the faith of Abraham in God’s purpose. It is not merely the result, nor is it the purpose of Abraham, nor what he believed.
	</p>
<p><strong> According to, </strong> etc. This qualifies ‘become,’ not, ‘believed.’
	</p>
<p><strong> Had been spoken </strong> (<span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="ge+15:5">Genesis 15:5</span>), before the promise that he should become a father of many nations (<span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="ge+17:5">Genesis 17:5</span>).
	</p>
<p>So, <em> i.e., </em> as the stars of heaven for multitude.
	</p>
