<p><span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="ro+4:24">Romans 4:24</span>. <strong> But for our sake also. </strong> The design was not merely to show how Abraham was justified, but also to show how we should be justified. </p>
<p style="margin-left:0.06em;"><strong> It shall be reckoned. </strong> ‘Shall be’ is not the simple future, but points the purpose of God with respect to what is continuous; the justification of each believer is a single act, but that of believers as a whole is continuous.
	</p>
<p style="margin-left:0.06em;"><strong> Who believe; ‘</strong> since we are such as believe’ fairly presents the sense. </p>
<p style="margin-left:0.06em;"><strong> Him that raised up Jesus our Lord</strong>, etc. This reference to the resurrection of Christ emphasizes the power of God, just as <span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="ro+4:17">Romans 4:17</span> had done. The birth of Isaac was a proof of God’s omnipotence, but Christ’s resurrection is a still higher proof, both of this omnipotence, and, at the same time, of Divine grace, on which the whole argument turns (<span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="ro+4:16">Romans 4:16</span>). When the fact of Christ’s resurrection is denied or ignored by nominal Christians, their faith is weak in every respect.
	</p>
