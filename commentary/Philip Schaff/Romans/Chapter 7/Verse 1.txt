<p><span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="ro+7:1">Romans 7:1</span>. <strong> Or are ye ignorant. </strong> (Comp. chap. <span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="ro+6:3">Romans 6:3</span>.) In thus appealing to experience, it is implied that every believer, whether he can explain it or not, <em> feels </em> that he is in the state described in chap. <span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="ro+6:22-23">Romans 6:22-23</span>, and hence has some knowledge of his freedom from the law. This knowledge the Apostle would bring into clearness and power.
	</p>
<p><strong> Brethren, </strong> etc. Not addressed to the Jewish Christians alone; for in that age, especially, the knowledge of the Old Testament on the part of all Christians was presupposed; the custom of reading the Old Testament probably obtained in their assemblies.
	</p>
<p><strong> Know the law. </strong> The law of Moses is meant, although the article is wanting in the original; for while the argument might hold true when based upon law in general, the subject under discussion is the relation to the Mosaic law.
	</p>
<p><strong> The law hath dominion, </strong> etc. The whole law is meant, not simply the law of marriage: for that has not yet come into view.
	</p>
<p><strong> For as long time, </strong> etc. This is a peculiarity of the Mosaic law, ‘that it cannot, like human laws, have merely temporary validity, or be altered, suspended, nor can one be exempt from it for a time’ (Meyer). But compare the death to the law (<span class="scriptRef" com="scn" lang="eng" ref="ro+7:4">Romans 7:4</span>).
	</p>
